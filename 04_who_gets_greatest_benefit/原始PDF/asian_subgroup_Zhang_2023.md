## Abstract

**Background:** This prespecified subgroup analysis of the FIDELIO-DKD trial aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of finerenone in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) in China.

**Methods:** 372 participants were recruited from 67 centers in China and randomized 1:1 to oral finerenone or placebo with standard therapy for T2DM. The primary composite outcome included kidney failure, sustained decrease of estimated glomerular filtration rate ≥40% from baseline over at least 4 weeks, or renal death. The key secondary composite outcome included death from cardiovascular causes, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure.

**Results:** After a median follow-up of 30 months, the finerenone group showed a relative risk reduction (RRR) of 41% (hazard ratio [HR] = 0.59, 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.39–0.88; p = 0.009) for the primary composite outcome compared with placebo, consistent across its components with treatment benefits with finerenone. Based on an absolute between-group difference of 12.2% after 30 months, the number of patients who needed to be treated with finerenone to prevent one primary outcome event was eight (95% CI: 4–84). For the key secondary composite outcome, the finerenone group showed a RRR of 25% (HR = 0.75, 95% CI, 0.38–1.48; p = 0.408). Adverse events were similar between the two groups. The effects of finerenone on blood pressure were modest. No gynecomastia events were reported in the study. Hyperkalemia leading to discontinuation occurred in eight (4.3%) and two (1.1%) participants in the finerenone and control groups, respectively. The incidence of acute kidney injury was comparable between the two groups (1.6% vs. 1.6%).

**Conclusions:** Finerenone resulted in lower risks of CKD progression than placebo and a balanced safety profile in Chinese patients with CKD and T2DM.

## Introduction

Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is the leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) worldwide, with a prevalence of CKD caused by T2DM of around 130 million in 2019 and nearly 400,000 deaths, imposing heavy medical and economic burden worldwide. About 20–40% of patients with T2DM have CKD. In China, economic growth of the last decades has increased sedentary behaviors and carbohydrate and saturated fat consumption, leading to rising rates of T2DM. The prevalence of diabetes in China was 10.9% in 2013 and 12.4% in 2018, affecting more than 150 million people. The proportion of patients with CKD due to DM has exceeded that of CKD due to glomerulonephritis since 2011. The prevalence of diabetic nephropathy among patients with T2DM in China is about 21.8%. A similar rise in T2DM-associated CKD has also been observed in other Asian regions.

Finerenone is a nonsteroidal selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) that blocks sodium reabsorption and the overactivation of mineralocorticoid receptors (MR). By exerting powerful anti-inflammatory and antifibrotic effects, finerenone protects the kidney and cardiovascular system. FIDELIO-DKD, a multicenter, global phase III randomized controlled trial (NCT02540993) involving 5,734 participants, showed that finerenone reduced the risk of CKD progression and cardiovascular events compared with placebo in patients with T2DM and CKD. Finerenone was approved by the FDA for treating patients with CKD and T2DM, and in China has also been approved by the National Medical Products Administration (NMPA).

Some characteristics are different in Asian and Chinese patients with T2D and CKD compared with other regions, including a high prevalence of a raised urinary albumin/creatinine ratio (UACR), high risk of end-stage renal disease (ESRD), and high dietary sodium intake. The present study is a prespecified subgroup analysis of the Chinese participants' data from the FIDELIO-DKD study, which aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of finerenone in Chinese patients.

## Methods

### Participants and Study Design

The FIDELIO-DKD study (NCT02540993) was a multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase III trial involving 5,734 participants across 48 countries, including 67 centers in China. The present study was a prespecified subgroup analysis and analyzed all Chinese participants (n = 372).

Participants in FIDELIO-DKD were 18 or older, with T2DM and CKD (defined as a UACR of 30 to <300 mg/g, an eGFR of 25 to <60 mL/min/1.73 m2, and a history of diabetic retinopathy; or a UACR of 300–5,000 mg/g and an eGFR of 25 to <75 mL/min/1.73 m2), were uptitrated for at least 4 weeks to the maximally tolerated labeled dose of ACE inhibitors or ARBs before the run-in period, and had serum potassium of ≤4.8 mmol/L at the screening visit.

### Procedure

Eligible patients were randomly assigned to receive oral finerenone or placebo in a 1:1 ratio. A dose of 10 mg once daily was prescribed to participants with an eGFR of <60 mL/min/1.73 m2 at screening, and 20 mg once daily for participants with an eGFR of ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m2. Dose could be increased from 10 to 20 mg after 1 month if serum potassium <4.8 mmol/L and eGFR stable. If serum potassium exceeded 5.5 mmol/L, treatment was withheld; resumed with 10 mg at 5.0 mmol/L or lower. Follow-up visits were conducted at month 1, month 4, and every 4 months thereafter.

### Outcomes

The primary composite kidney outcome included kidney failure, a sustained drop in eGFR of at least 40% from baseline over at least 4 weeks, or death from renal causes. Kidney failure was defined as eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m2 or ESRD. eGFR was calculated using the CKD-EPI formula.

The key secondary cardiovascular outcome was the composite of time to death from cardiovascular causes, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure. The secondary composite kidney outcome included kidney failure, a sustained drop in eGFR of ≥57% from baseline over ≥4 weeks, or death from renal causes.

### Statistical Analysis

SAS 9.4 software was used. Efficacy analyses were conducted on the full analysis set; safety analyses on the safety analysis set. Kaplan-Meier methods and Cox proportional hazard models were used. A two-sided p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Participants

A total of 372 participants from China were enrolled, with 188 (50.5%) in the finerenone group and 184 (49.5%) in the placebo group. Mean age was 60.3 ± 10.1 years, mean duration of diabetes 13.7 ± 7.9 years. Median UACR was 1,270 (IQR: 605, 2,198) mg/g, and mean eGFR was 45.6 ± 11.3 mL/min/1.73 m2. Baseline characteristics and concomitant medications were balanced between groups. Median follow-up was 30 months. 89.2% of participants achieved treatment compliance of 80–120% (88.3% finerenone, 90.2% placebo); mean daily dose was 15.4 mg (finerenone) and 16.5 mg (placebo).

### Efficacy Outcomes

- **Primary composite kidney outcome** (kidney failure, sustained ≥40% eGFR decrease, or renal death): occurred in 40 patients (21.4%) with finerenone vs 61 patients (33.2%) with placebo (**HR = 0.59, 95% CI 0.39–0.88; p = 0.009**). Absolute between-group difference 12.2% after 30 months; **NNT = 8 (95% CI 4–84)**.
- The main driver was the sustained ≥40% eGFR drop component (HR = 0.59, 95% CI 0.39–0.89; p = 0.011), with incidences of 9.99 (95% CI 7.07–13.4) vs 16.21 (95% CI 12.3–20.6) events per 100 patient-years.
- **Key secondary composite CV outcome**: 15 events (8.0%) with finerenone vs 19 events (10.3%) with placebo (**HR = 0.75, 95% CI 0.38–1.48; p = 0.408**).
- **Secondary composite kidney outcome** (kidney failure, sustained ≥57% eGFR decrease, or renal death): 28 patients (14.9%) finerenone vs 39 patients (21.2%) placebo (HR = 0.68, 95% CI 0.42–1.11, p = 0.12).
- No significant between-group differences in death from any cause or hospitalization.
- Finerenone reduced UACR by 26.8% from baseline to month 4 (ratio of least-squares means 0.73; 95% CI 0.66–0.82).

### Safety

Safety profiles were comparable between groups. Serious TEAEs occurred in 48.4% (finerenone) vs 54.3% (placebo). Discontinuation due to TEAEs: 11 (5.9%) finerenone vs 6 (3.3%) placebo. Treatment-related TEAE deaths: 1 (0.5%) finerenone vs 2 (1.1%) placebo.

Hyperkalemia-related TEAEs were higher with finerenone (37.2% vs 25.5%). Serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L: 20.7% (39/188) finerenone vs 10.5% (19/181) placebo; >6.0 mmol/L: 3.7% (7/188) vs 2.2% (4/182). Hyperkalemia leading to treatment discontinuation: 8 (4.3%) finerenone vs 2 (1.1%) placebo. Three (1.6%) finerenone patients had hyperkalemia leading to hospitalization; no hyperkalemia-related death. Acute kidney injury incidence was comparable (1.6% vs 1.6%).

Finerenone had modest effects on blood pressure. Mean SBP changes from baseline to 1 and 12 months were −3.07 and −1.15 mm Hg with finerenone vs 2.86 and 1.82 mm Hg with placebo. No gynecomastia events or breast pain were reported.

## Discussion

In line with the primary endpoint of the original trial, finerenone significantly reduced the risk of CKD progression in Chinese patients with CKD and T2DM. The risk of cardiovascular events was numerically lower in the finerenone group.

Compared with the global results, Chinese subgroup participants were younger (60.3 ± 10.1 vs 65.6 ± 9.1 years), more male (78.0% vs 70.2%), and with shorter T2DM duration (13.7 ± 7.9 vs 16.6 ± 8.8 years). eGFR and SBP were similar. However, Chinese participants had higher baseline UACR (finerenone 1,326.61 [IQR 607.32–2,148.56]; placebo 1,210.41 [IQR 601.73–2,329.66]) vs the original study (833 [IQR 441–1,628] and 867 [IQR 453–1,645]). This suggested that although Chinese participants were younger with shorter diabetes duration and similar eGFR, their conditions might be more severe (higher baseline UACR). This was also reflected in the higher incidence of the primary composite endpoint in the placebo group of the Chinese subpopulation (33.2%) vs the whole study population (21.1%).

In the Chinese subpopulation, finerenone reduced the risk of the primary composite kidney outcome by **41%**, versus **18%** in the overall population. Proposed reasons for the greater benefit:
1. **High salt diet**: The Chinese population consumes 13.3 g of salt daily (vs 8.5 g UK, 9.5 g USA per INTERMAP), more than twice the WHO recommendation. High salt diet blunts the antiproteinuric effect of ACEIs/ARBs and can produce MR activation, so patients may benefit more from MRA. In a post hoc analysis of the EVALUATE trial, eplerenone-treated patients in the highest sodium excretion tertile had significantly greater UACR reduction. In preclinical work, Hirohama et al. found finerenone improved glomerular injury in uninephrectomized db/db mice fed a high salt diet.
2. **Different CKD pathology spectrum**: A large proportion of CKD in China is still caused by glomerulonephritis, idiopathic membranous nephropathy, IgA nephropathy, and lupus nephritis, which might benefit more from the anti-inflammation and anti-fibrosis effects of MRA. The ongoing phase III FIND-CKD (NCT05047263) study of finerenone in non-T2DM-related CKD may provide evidence.

The key secondary cardiovascular outcome trend was consistent with the whole population (original trial HR = 0.86, 95% CI 0.75–0.99, p = 0.03). In the present study there were no statistically significant differences due to the small sample size, but finerenone showed a potential cardiac protection effect.

Finerenone was well tolerated. Investigator-reported hyperkalemia-related AEs were higher for China (finerenone 37.2% vs global 18.3%; placebo 25.5% vs global 9.0%), but this appears to be over-reporting, as central-laboratory serum hyperkalemia >5.5 mmol/L (20.7% China vs 21.4% global) and >6.0 mmol/L (3.7% China vs 4.5% global) were similar between Chinese and global participants.

**Limitations:** Analysis with a limited sample size; more studies and real-world studies are needed.

**Conclusion:** Finerenone resulted in lower risks of CKD progression than placebo and a trend toward cardiovascular benefits, with a balanced safety profile and manageable risk of hyperkalemia in Chinese patients with CKD and T2DM.
