# Finerenone and left ventricular hypertrophy in chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes

**ESC Heart Failure 2025; 12: 185-188** — SHORT COMMUNICATION
Published online 3 September 2024 in Wiley Online Library. DOI: 10.1002/ehf2.14962

Gerasimos Filippatos, Stefan D. Anker, George L. Bakris, Peter Rossing, Luis M. Ruilope, Andrew J.S. Coats, Stephan von Haehling, Piotr Ponikowski, Giuseppe M.C. Rosano, Meike Brinker, Alfredo E. Farjat, Luke Roberts, Bertram Pitt, and on behalf of the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD investigators.

## Abstract

**Aims** Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH) has been associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular (CV) disease and linked to increased morbidity and mortality. In patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes (T2D), hypertension is common, and patients with these co-morbidities additionally have a high prevalence of LVH. This analysis of the prespecified pooled FIDELITY analysis comprising the randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicentre FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD phase III studies aimed to explore the CV and kidney effects of finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, in patients with CKD and T2D stratified by a diagnosis of LVH at baseline.

**Methods and results** A diagnosis of LVH in the FIDELITY patient population was determined at baseline using investigator-reported electrocardiogram (ECG) findings. The two efficacy outcomes, assessed by baseline LVH, were the composite CV outcome of time to CV death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, non-fatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure (HHF), and a composite kidney outcome of time to onset of kidney failure, a sustained decrease in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥57% from baseline over ≥4 weeks, or kidney-related death. Safety outcomes by baseline LVH were reported as treatment-emergent adverse events. At baseline out of 13 026 patients in FIDELITY, 96.5% had hypertension and 9.6% had investigator-reported LVH. The relative risk reduction for the composite CV and kidney outcomes with finerenone versus placebo was lower in the LVH subgroup; however, the treatment effect of finerenone was not modified by baseline LVH for either outcome (Pinteraction = 0.1075 for composite CV outcome and Pinteraction = 0.1782 for composite kidney outcome). Analysis of the composite CV outcome components showed a greater reduction in the risk of HHF versus placebo for patients with baseline LVH compared with those without (Pinteraction = 0.0024). Overall safety events were comparable between the LVH subgroups and treatment arms. Treatment-emergent hyperkalaemia was observed more frequently with finerenone versus placebo, but discontinuation rates were low in both treatment arms and between LVH subgroups.

**Conclusions** In conclusion, the overall CV and kidney benefits of finerenone versus placebo were not modified by the presence of LVH at baseline, with overall safety findings being similar between LVH subgroups. A greater benefit was observed for HHF in patients with versus without LVH, suggesting that LVH may be a predictor of the treatment effect of finerenone on HHF.

**Keywords** Cardiorenal outcomes; Chronic kidney disease; Finerenone; Hospitalization for heart failure; Left ventricular hypertrophy; Type 2 diabetes.

## Introduction

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) and its related morbidity and mortality is associated with left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH). Hypertension is common in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes (T2D); patients with these co-morbidities have a high prevalence of left ventricular (LV) hypertrophy. Finerenone is a selective, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA). In FIDELITY, a prespecified pooled analysis of the randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicentre FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD phase III studies, finerenone demonstrated cardiovascular (CV) and kidney benefits across the spectrum of CKD severity in patients with T2D. The relative risk reduction with finerenone versus placebo for a composite CV outcome of time to CV death, non-fatal myocardial infarction (MI), non-fatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure (HHF) was shown to be 14%. For HHF, a 22% relative risk reduction was observed with finerenone compared with placebo.

## Aims

The aim of this exploratory FIDELITY subgroup analysis was to explore the CV and kidney effects of finerenone in patients stratified by a diagnosis of LVH at baseline, allowing hypothesis development for further research into the effects of finerenone on modifying LVH risk.

## Methods

The design and results of FIDELITY have been previously published. In summary, patients with CKD and T2D on optimized renin–angiotensin system inhibitor (RASi) therapy were randomized 1:1 to finerenone or placebo. Patients with heart failure (HF) with reduced ejection fraction were excluded. Patients were required to have serum potassium levels ≤4.8 mmol/L at the run-in and screening visits. A diagnosis of LVH at baseline was determined based on investigator-reported electrocardiogram (ECG) findings (as per local practice from either manual ECG evaluation or machine-reported results) identified from the run-in visit to randomization; no central adjudication of ECGs was performed, and no assessment criteria were applied. Any patients with LVH identified by ECG in the period from run-in to randomization were included in the LVH patient subgroup. The use of ECG is known to be less sensitive and may limit the interpretation of the results. To mitigate this, baseline LVH was defined as a diagnosis of LVH using all ECG readings from the run-in visit to randomization to ensure improved identification of patients with a finding of LVH, while reducing the possibility of misclassifying patients as without LVH.

The efficacy outcomes, assessed in the full analysis set and by LVH subgroups, were a composite CV outcome of time to CV death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke, or HHF, and a composite kidney outcome of time to onset of kidney failure, a sustained decrease in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥57% from baseline over ≥4 weeks, or kidney-related death. The interaction between treatment and LVH status at baseline was assessed via a stratified Cox proportional hazards model, including treatment, subgroup, and treatment by subgroup interaction terms. Chi-square tests were used to report the P-values of interaction terms. Safety outcomes were reported as treatment-emergent events and determined in the safety analysis set by baseline LVH.

## Results

Of the 13 026 patients in the full analysis set of FIDELITY, 96.5% had hypertension, 9.6% had investigator-reported LVH, and 7.7% had HF at baseline. The median treatment duration for patients with and without LVH was 29.9 and 31.9 months, respectively, with finerenone, and 28.7 and 32.1 months, respectively, with placebo.

In FIDELITY, finerenone significantly reduced the risk of the composite CV and kidney outcomes versus placebo. In this analysis, the relative risk reduction for the composite CV and kidney outcomes was lower in the LVH patient subgroup compared with the subgroup without LVH:

- **Composite CV outcome:** HR 0.72 (95% CI 0.55–0.95) in LVH vs. HR 0.89 (95% CI 0.81–0.98) without LVH.
- **Composite kidney outcome:** HR 0.56 (95% CI 0.38–0.84) in LVH vs. HR 0.80 (95% CI 0.69–0.93) without LVH.

The treatment effect of finerenone was not modified by baseline LVH for either the composite CV (Pinteraction = 0.1075) or kidney (Pinteraction = 0.1782) outcomes.

A significant reduction in the risk of HHF was observed versus placebo for patients with baseline LVH compared with those without:
- **HHF:** HR 0.34 (95% CI 0.19–0.61) in LVH vs. HR 0.86 (95% CI 0.72–1.03) without LVH; **Pinteraction = 0.0024** (Figure 1).

No significant differences were seen in the other components of the CV or kidney composite outcomes.

Treatment-emergent (TE) adverse events were comparable across the LVH subgroups and treatment arms (with LVH: finerenone 85.2% vs. placebo 86.4%; without LVH: finerenone 86.1% vs. placebo 86.4%). TE hyperkalaemia was reported more frequently in the finerenone group versus placebo (with LVH: finerenone 12.3% vs. placebo 5.7%; without LVH: finerenone 14.2% vs. placebo 7.0%), but the incidence that led to treatment discontinuation was low in both treatment arms and LVH subgroups (with LVH: finerenone 1.5% vs. placebo 0.5%; without LVH: finerenone 1.7% vs. placebo 0.6%).

## Conclusions

In patients with CKD and T2D alongside hypertension, impaired LV diastolic function is largely explained by increased LV wall thickness and concentric LVH development. This analysis indicates that LVH was predictive of the treatment effect of finerenone for HHF and suggests its potential role in modifying risks associated with LVH risk in patients optimally treated with RASi. Further investigation is required to assess how finerenone modifies CV risk in these patients, and whether LVH is merely a marker of increased risk or an indicator of heightened mineralocorticoid receptor activation that is responsive specifically to MRAs rather than other treatments.

Limitations to be considered for the interpretation of the data here include the lack of standardized LVH criteria for the diagnosis of LVH using ECG implied possible variation in the definition; LVH regression with finerenone was not fully assessed due to missing ECG data at later timepoints and lack of corroborating echocardiography; and identification of LVH by ECG interpretation was via machine or physician evaluation, without any central adjudication.

In this post hoc analysis, finerenone and optimized RASi reduced the risk of composite CV and kidney outcomes versus placebo in patients with CKD and T2D, irrespective of baseline LVH. Findings suggest that LVH may be a predictor of the treatment effect of finerenone on HHF, with a greater risk reduction seen for HHF in patients with baseline LVH. An increased incidence of TE hyperkalaemia was observed with finerenone versus placebo, but the discontinuation rate was low for both treatment arms and LVH subgroups and the overall safety findings were similar between LVH subgroups.
