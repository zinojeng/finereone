# Finerenone: Potential Clinical Application Across the Spectrum of Cardiovascular Disease and Chronic Kidney Disease

## Abstract

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is the leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and is a risk factor for progression to end-stage kidney disease and cardiovascular morbidity and mortality. Despite pharmacologic treatment, residual risk of disease progression and adverse outcomes remains substantial. Finerenone is a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) approved in the United States for use in patients with CKD associated with T2D. The present review focuses on finerenone use, including its pharmacologic basis, indication and eligibility, and practical aspects of incorporation into routine clinical practice (particularly primary care). Results from the two placebo-controlled phase 3 clinical trials of finerenone (plus maximum tolerated dose of a renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitor) in patients with CKD associated with T2D showed a significantly lower risk of CKD progression and cardiovascular events with finerenone versus placebo. These effects of finerenone were applicable across the broad spectrum of patient participants, including those with baseline comorbidities such as a history of heart failure or a history of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease. We also compare finerenone to steroidal MRAs and discuss the relevance of ongoing and recently completed clinical trials of finerenone in other patient groups, which could expand finerenone use further to a broader spectrum of patients.

## 1. Introduction

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is a leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and a risk factor for progression to end-stage kidney disease (ESKD). Persistently high blood glucose levels (hyperglycemia) in T2D can cause kidney and cardiovascular (CV) injury due to the effects of interrelated hemodynamic dysregulation, metabolic disturbances, and inflammation, which are key drivers for the development and progression of CKD in T2D. Due to the interconnectivity between the kidneys, metabolic processes, and the heart in normal state and in disease, both CKD and T2D are linked with CV morbidity and mortality. Risk for CKD progression and subsequent complications is generally captured and tracked by measurements of kidney function via the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) and the extent of kidney damage measured by the degree of albuminuria. Management of CKD associated with T2D generally involves both nonpharmacologic (lifestyle optimization) and pharmacologic approaches to optimize glucose and blood pressure control, as well as preventing or reducing proteinuria. However, despite these management approaches, there is a continuing residual risk for the development and progression of CKD associated with T2D.

Finerenone is a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (ns-MRA) that can be used to treat patients who have CKD associated with T2D. Results from two large placebo-controlled phase 3 clinical trials (total randomized = 13,171) showed that when used in combination with a renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) inhibitor, finerenone reduced the risk of sustained loss of kidney function, ESKD, and CV events in adult patients with CKD associated with T2D. Finerenone is indicated in the US for the treatment of CKD associated with T2D.

In this review, the authors discuss finerenone in the context of these phase 3 clinical trials in CKD associated with T2D (which included a broad spectrum of patient participants), practical considerations for incorporating finerenone into routine clinical practice (such as serum potassium monitoring for hyperkalemia and combination therapies), comparison to steroidal MRAs and RAAS inhibitors, and finerenone's potential application in other patient populations. A plain language summary is included as Supplementary File S1.

## 2. Methods

PubMed and non-PubMed searches were used to find applicable articles. Three separate PubMed searches were completed, limited to article Title only, English, and the past 10 years:
- Search 1. ("Finerenone" OR "Kerendia" OR "BAY94 *" OR "BAY 94 *"); excluded letters, editorials, and case studies.
- Search 2. ("Spironolactone") OR ("Eplerenone"); limited to phase 2 and phase 3 trials, randomized controlled trials, and meta-analyses/systematic reviews.
- Search 3. ("Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist" OR "MRA"); limited to review articles and meta-analyses/systematic reviews.

Results were downloaded into EndNote 9.3.3 and screened (duplicate removal, title screening, full-text screening). Non-PubMed searches included clinical practice guidelines (KDIGO, ADA/diabetesjournals.org/care, accessed 6 January 2025) and FDA drug labels (fda.gov/drugs, accessed 6 January 2025). The clinicaltrials.gov search method is described in Section 11.

## 3. CKD Associated with T2D Represents a Broad Spectrum of Patients

Globally, an estimated 529 million people were living with diabetes in 2021 (6.1% of the world's population); T2D cases made up 96% of all diabetes cases, with an age-standardized prevalence of 5.9% in 2021. T2D prevalence is projected to rise by 61.2%, affecting over 1.2 billion people in 2050. In the US, around 37 million people have diabetes (11.3% of the US population). T2D is a leading cause of CKD and CV disease worldwide, with approximately 40% of people with T2D developing CKD. Around one-third of people with CKD also have a CV disease comorbidity. Of CV comorbidities, heart failure (HF) with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) and HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) occur in ~17% and ~7% of patients with T2D, respectively.

## 4. Defining CKD

CKD is diagnosed if there is a persistent (>3-month) elevation of urinary albumin excretion (albuminuria), low eGFR, or other manifestations of kidney damage. CKD is staged based on cause, eGFR category, and albuminuria category (via urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio [UACR]). There are six CKD stages: 1, 2, 3a, 3b, 4, and 5. Stage 1 is least severe; stage 5 is most severe and represents ESKD. Many people with stage 1 or 2 CKD may not know they have CKD because the condition can be asymptomatic, emphasizing the need for routine screening of all people with T2D.

According to a consensus report from Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) and the American Diabetes Association, patients eligible for treatment of CKD associated with T2D are those with at least moderately increased albuminuria (UACR >= 30 mg/g), including patients categorized as stage 1 or 2 based on eGFR. An increase in UACR and decrease in eGFR together is associated with a 20-fold increased risk of progression to ESKD compared with no changes in either measure.

## 5. Finerenone Is a ns-MRA: Differences vs. Steroidal MRAs

Overactivation of the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) has an important role in the pathogenesis of kidney and CV diseases. Under pathologic conditions, overactivation of the MR "switches" it from a homeostatic regulator to a pathophysiologic mediator by promoting oxidative stress, inflammation, and fibrosis. In preclinical animal models, finerenone has been shown to prevent inflammation and fibrosis (often mediated by oxidative stress) and subsequent disease progression.

There are steroidal MRAs (spironolactone, eplerenone) and ns-MRAs — the latter including finerenone (currently the only ns-MRA approved for use in the US). The nonsteroidal structure/unique binding of finerenone strongly inhibits cofactor recruitment to the MR and modifies proinflammatory and profibrotic gene pathways independently of aldosterone, unlike steroidal MRAs.

Spironolactone is a nonspecific MRA with affinity for progesterone, androgen, and glucocorticoid receptors (reflecting sex-related side effects such as gynecomastia, sexual dysfunction, and abnormal menstruation). Spironolactone and eplerenone both increase cortisol levels and HbA1c. Eplerenone has greater selectivity for MRs and minimal binding to progesterone, androgen, and glucocorticoid receptors; therefore the frequency of sexual side effects is low. Finerenone has high selectivity and high potency for the MR; it is at least as potent as spironolactone but more potent than eplerenone and is more selective than both steroidal MRAs.

MRA therapy can cause hyperkalemia; all three MRAs carry a hyperkalemia warning in their labels. Greater tissue distribution in the kidneys (as observed with the two steroidal MRAs) may suggest a greater potential for hyperkalemia than for finerenone, which has a more balanced distribution across myocardial and renal tissue.

Spironolactone is approved in the US for HFrEF as add-on therapy, for hypertension, for primary hyperaldosteronism, and for edema. Eplerenone is indicated for HFrEF after acute myocardial infarction (MI) and for hypertension. Neither steroidal MRA is approved for use in patients with CKD and T2D (no supporting phase 3 outcomes data); in phase 2 trials they reduced albuminuria in various populations. In both finerenone phase 3 trials, finerenone or placebo was administered in combination with a RAAS inhibitor.

## 6. The RAAS, Its Inhibition, and the Finerenone Phase 3 Clinical Trials

Patients in the phase 3 trials received finerenone (or placebo) in combination with the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) of a RAAS inhibitor. Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEis) and angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) are types of RAAS inhibitors used clinically to reduce blood pressure and albuminuria. In CKD, pathologic narrowing of the efferent arteriole (dysregulated angiotensin II) and vasodilation of the afferent arteriole increase intraglomerular pressure, resulting in hyperfiltration and downstream albuminuria. RAAS inhibitors reduce albuminuria by causing vasodilation of the efferent arteriole. RAAS inhibitors are recommended by KDIGO (2022) as first-line treatment of albuminuria with and without hypertension in people with diabetes (at the MTD).

## 7. Potential Benefits of Finerenone Across the Spectrum of Patients with T2D and CKD

Two large phase 3 placebo-controlled trials tested finerenone plus MTD of a RAAS inhibitor in patients with CKD associated with T2D. A total of 13,171 patients were randomized, and 12,990 (98.6%) completed the trials. Placebo arm: RAAS inhibitor at MTD plus placebo. Finerenone arm: finerenone 10 or 20 mg/day (starting dose depending on eGFR; target 20 mg/day) plus RAAS inhibitor at MTD. Serum potassium had to be <= 4.8 mmol/L at screening to be eligible and was monitored throughout.

- **FIDELIO-DKD** (Finerenone in Reducing Kidney Failure and Disease Progression in Diabetic Kidney Disease): focused on kidney effects (primary composite: kidney failure or death from kidney causes); CV effects were secondary (death from CV causes, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or HHF). Median follow-up 2.6 years; finerenone lowered CKD progression risk and CV events vs placebo.
- **FIGARO-DKD** (Finerenone in Reducing Cardiovascular Mortality and Morbidity in Diabetic Kidney Disease): focused on CV effects (primary composite: death from CV causes, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or HHF); kidney effects were secondary. Median follow-up 3.4 years; finerenone improved CV outcomes vs placebo.

### 7.1. Patient Pretreatment Clinical Characteristics and Patient Subgroups

Baseline characteristics across both trials: mean duration of diabetes 15.4 years; mean HbA1c 7.7% (poor glucose control, HbA1c > 12%, was an exclusion criterion). Majority (>97%) were taking glucose-lowering therapy: insulin most common (58.6%), followed by GLP-1 receptor agonist (7.2%) and/or SGLT2 inhibitor (6.7%). In addition to a RAAS inhibitor, over 50% were also taking a diuretic. Many had ACC/AHA stage 1 hypertension (mean SBP 136.7 mmHg; uncontrolled hypertension was an exclusion criterion). Approximately 45% had a history of CV disease; patients with chronic symptomatic HF were excluded from both trials.

Subanalyses of FIDELIO-DKD: efficacy/safety by baseline glucose control (HbA1c < 7.5% vs >= 7.5%; insulin use yes vs no); with or without history of HF; with or without history of atrial fibrillation/flutter (AFF); by baseline office SBP quartiles. Main conclusion: finerenone improved cardiorenal outcomes irrespective of baseline HbA1c/insulin use, HF history, AFF history, and baseline office SBP.

Subanalyses of FIGARO-DKD: efficacy/safety with or without history of HF; effect by UACR subgroups (moderately vs severely increased at baseline). Main conclusion: finerenone improved cardiorenal outcomes regardless of baseline HF history and may protect against CKD progression and CV events in T2D patients with early or late-stage CKD.

### 7.2. Pooling of Patient Data (FIDELITY analysis)

A prespecified pooled analysis of both phase 3 trials (FIDELITY) was conducted. Kidney outcomes (N = 13,026):
- Primary kidney composite: (1) time to first onset of kidney failure (ESKD [chronic dialysis >= 90 days or kidney transplantation] or sustained decrease in eGFR to < 15 mL/min/1.73 m2), (2) sustained >= 57% decrease in eGFR from baseline over >= 4 weeks, or (3) renal death.
- Secondary composite kidney outcome: (1) time to first kidney failure, (2) sustained >= 40% decrease in eGFR over >= 4 weeks, (3) renal death, (4) all-cause mortality, (5) all-cause hospitalization, (6) change in UACR from baseline to month 4.

A sustained >= 57% eGFR decrease (equivalent to doubling of serum creatinine) is a more robust kidney failure surrogate than >= 40% decrease. For both kidney composites, risk was significantly reduced by **23%** (>= 57% eGFR decrease composite) and **15%** (>= 40% eGFR decrease composite) with finerenone vs placebo.

Main CV outcome (FIDELITY): time to CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or HHF — risk significantly reduced by **14%** with finerenone vs placebo. A deeper mortality analysis showed finerenone significantly reduced the risk of all-cause and CV mortality vs placebo, consistent across KDIGO risk categories. Two populations were analyzed: intention-to-treat (ITT) and on-treatment (events while on treatment and for >= 30 days after last intake). Risk for all-cause mortality and CV mortality was significantly reduced in the on-treatment analysis, whereas the ITT analysis showed no significant difference — highlighting a need for good adherence. Death from renal causes was low (<0.1% in both groups); no significant difference for non-CV/nonrenal mortality.

### 7.3. CKD Risk Stages at Baseline

Eligible patients are distributed across KDIGO risk categories CKD stages 1–4. In FIGARO-DKD, most patients (82%) were at high risk of CKD progression, 17% at moderate risk, and nearly 1% at low risk (based on eGFR and normal to mildly increased albuminuria).

- **FIDELIO-DKD** definition: persistent moderately elevated albuminuria (UACR 30 to <300 mg/g) with eGFR 25 to <60 mL/min/1.73 m2, OR persistent severely elevated albuminuria (UACR 300–5000 mg/g) with eGFR 25 to <75 mL/min/1.73 m2. Mean baseline eGFR 44.3 mL/min/1.73 m2; median baseline UACR 852 mg/g. Corresponds to CKD stages 2 to 4.
- **FIGARO-DKD** definition: eGFR 25 to 90 mL/min/1.73 m2 with UACR 30 to <300 mg/g, OR eGFR >= 60 mL/min/1.73 m2 with UACR 300 to 5000 mg/g. Mean baseline eGFR 67.8 mL/min/1.73 m2; median baseline UACR 308 mg/g. Corresponds to CKD stages 1 to 4.

In a FIGARO-DKD subanalysis, the benefit of finerenone on CKD progression risk favored those with severely increased albuminuria (UACR >= 300 mg/g) vs moderately increased (30 to <300 mg/g); however, an interaction was only observed in the kidney composite that included sustained eGFR decrease >= 40%. CV outcomes stratified by albuminuria were consistent with the overall population. A focused FIDELITY analysis showed a consistent kidney protective effect across the spectrum of CKD severities, including worse baseline kidney function (eGFR < 45 mL/min/1.73 m2 and UACR >= 300 mg/g).

## 8. Cardiovascular Effects of Finerenone in CKD Associated with T2D

### 8.1. Systolic Blood Pressure

Around two-fifths of patients with CKD associated with T2D also have hypertension (SBP >= 140 mmHg). In a FIDELIO-DKD subgroup analysis stratified by SBP quartiles (<= 128.7, >128.7 to <= 138.3, >138.3 to <= 148.0, >148.0 mmHg), finerenone had a small (statistically insignificant vs placebo) effect on office SBP. Finerenone's cardiorenal benefits were consistent irrespective of baseline office SBP and unaffected by arterial hypertension, suggesting mechanisms beyond BP reduction.

### 8.2. Atherosclerotic CV Disease (ASCVD)

In a FIDELITY pooled analysis, efficacy/safety of finerenone in patients with or without ASCVD at baseline was evaluated. ASCVD history was determined by prespecified medical history of carotid endarterectomy, coronary artery disease, MI, ischemic stroke, and peripheral arterial occlusive disease. Close to half (**45.6%**) of patients had a history of ASCVD at baseline. Those with ASCVD were more likely to have longer T2D duration and a history of AF or coronary heart disease, but less likely to have hypertension. Incidence of composite CV outcomes (CV death or HHF) and all-cause mortality was higher in patients with ASCVD history; no difference in composite kidney outcome. Finerenone reduced the risk of CV events and improved kidney outcomes consistently, irrespective of prevalent baseline ASCVD.

### 8.3. Risk of Heart Failure

Approximately 15% of patients with CKD associated with T2D are living with HF. Finerenone was associated with a significant reduction in the composite endpoint of time to CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or HHF (p < 0.05 vs placebo; both phase 3 trials). In a FIGARO-DKD secondary analysis, finerenone significantly reduced HF-associated time-to-event outcomes: a **29% reduction** in risk of first HHF and an **18% reduction** in the composite of time to CV death or first HHF vs placebo (independent of history of HF); the risk of new-onset HF was **32% lower** with finerenone vs placebo. A FIDELIO-DKD subanalysis showed that a history of HF at baseline did not modify the response to finerenone for CV and kidney outcomes. A FIDELITY subgroup analysis showed HF-related outcomes might be reduced within weeks of initiating finerenone, with benefit maintained for at least 4 years.

### 8.4. Atrial Fibrillation

In a FIDELIO-DKD analysis, 461 (**8.1%**) of patients had AFF at baseline. Those with AFF were more likely to be older, have a history of CV disease, and have a lower median UACR. The risk of new-onset AFF was reduced by **29%** with finerenone vs placebo (hazard ratio [HR] 0.71 [95% CI: 0.53–0.94]; p = 0.0164). Consistent cardiorenal benefit with finerenone was observed irrespective of history of AF.

## 9. Finerenone Use in Clinical Practice: Primary Care

Eligible patients are those with CKD and T2D at risk of kidney disease progression and CV disease, assuming no contraindications. Primary care is an important access point. Early-stage CKD is often asymptomatic, so routine (once per year) screening for CKD in people with T2D is important, alongside access to nephrology services. Management may be suboptimal due to cost, limited/no access to nephrology, and clinical inertia; in higher-income countries governments tend to fund only about half of necessary CKD care. Cardiorenoprotective treatments may be underutilized in patients with T2D; limited published data exist on finerenone prescribing patterns.

## 10. Finerenone Use in Clinical Practice: Serum Potassium Monitoring and Combination Therapy

Finerenone is indicated in the US to reduce the risk of sustained eGFR decline, ESKD, CV death, nonfatal MI, and HHF in adult patients with CKD associated with T2D. Hyperkalemia is an adverse event of special interest. Recommended starting dose is based on eGFR and serum potassium: for acceptable serum potassium, initiate at 10 mg once daily if eGFR is >= 25 to < 60 mL/min/1.73 m2, and at 20 mg once daily if eGFR is >= 60 mL/min/1.73 m2; target daily dose is 20 mg.

Finerenone should only be started in patients with serum potassium levels no greater than **5.0 mEq/L**. Strategies to manage hyperkalemia risk include structured monitoring, temporary treatment interruption, and dose adjustment. eGFR and serum potassium should be measured prior to initiation and potassium monitored during treatment. The greatest risk of hyperkalemia is a potassium level > 5 mmol/L and eGFR < 25 mL/min/1.73 m2; adding an SGLT2 inhibitor to finerenone may lower hyperkalemia risk. Ongoing studies (CONFIDENCE [NCT05254002], FLAMINGO [NCT05640180]) investigate adding an SGLT2 inhibitor to finerenone plus RAAS inhibitor.

## 11. Unexplored Patient Populations and Areas for Future Research

An April 2025 clinicaltrials.gov search for "finerenone" returned 52 studies; after discarding completed trials, 28 studies were hand-sorted for relevance. Finerenone is being investigated in additional cardiorenal indications:
- **FINEARTS-HF** (NCT04435626): HF trial completed July 2024.
- Primary aldosteronism: four ongoing studies (NCT05814770; NCT06457074; NCT06381323; NCT06164379).
- IgA nephropathy (NCT06580288), cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (NCT06906081), post-radical-nephrectomy for renal cell carcinoma alone or with empagliflozin (NCT06818305).

Observational/real-world studies: FINE-REAL (NCT05348733), FIRST-2.5 (NCT06608212), FIRST-2 (NCT05703880, completed 2024), FINEGUST (NCT05526157), FINEROD (NCT06278207, Japan), IN-REALITY (NCT06763146, India), and a study in HFrEF without T2DM and CKD (NCT05974566).

## 12. Discussion

The FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials amount to 13,171 randomized patients. Clinicians should consider comorbidities, medications, contraindications, side-effect risk, and patient preference. Current long-term safety data are relatively short (~2.6–3.4 years in the phase 3 trials), emphasizing the need for longer follow-up. A limitation is that only 4.7% (FIDELIO-DKD) and 3.5% (FIGARO-DKD) of participants identified as Black, limiting generalizability by race/ethnicity. Other drugs for CKD associated with T2D in the ADA Standards of Care (alongside finerenone as a ns-MRA) include select SGLT2 inhibitors and the GLP-1 RA semaglutide. Novel biomarkers in the setting of CKD are needed.

## 13. Conclusions

Finerenone is indicated for the treatment of patients with CKD associated with T2D. It is an ns-MRA that has demonstrated significantly reduced kidney disease progression and reduced CV event risk across CKD stages in phase 3 trials. The treatment effect was broadly unaffected by baseline CV comorbidities, demonstrating a cardiorenoprotective effect irrespective of hypertension or history of HF, AF, or ASCVD. These efficacy aspects were tested as sub- or secondary analyses (with limitations due to smaller sizes). Applying FIDELIO-DKD/FIGARO-DKD enrollment criteria to the US population, potentially **2.2 million individuals** in the US could benefit from finerenone. Results from the FINEARTS-HF phase 3 trial showed a significantly lower rate of a composite of total worsening HF events and death from CV causes with finerenone vs placebo in patients with HF and mildly reduced or preserved ejection fraction.
