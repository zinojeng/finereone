## Abstract

**OBJECTIVE.** The CONFIDENCE trial demonstrated additive benefits of simultaneous initiation of finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and a sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor compared with monotherapy in reducing the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). This prespecified analysis evaluated whether safety and efficacy of combination therapy varies by baseline glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonist (GLP-1 RA) use.

**RESEARCH DESIGN AND METHODS.** Adults with chronic kidney disease (UACR ≥100 to <5,000 mg/g; estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] 30–90 mL/min/1.73 m²) and type 2 diabetes (glycated hemoglobin <11% [97 mmol/mol]) were randomized (1:1:1) to once-daily finerenone, empagliflozin, or finerenone plus empagliflozin.

**RESULTS.** Among 800 participants, 182 (23%) used a GLP-1 RA at baseline. At day 180, UACR change from baseline in participants using a GLP-1 RA was −51% (95% CI −59 to −40%) with combination therapy, −34% (−48 to −18%) with finerenone, and −36% (−48 to −21%) with empagliflozin. Corresponding results in those not using a GLP-1 RA at baseline were −56% (−62 to −50%), −37% (−45 to −28%), and −33% (−41 to −23%), respectively. Hyperkalemia incidence rates with combination therapy were 9.0% and 9.5% among individuals with and without baseline GLP-1 RA use. eGFR changes were consistent among individuals with and without baseline GLP-1 RA use. Acute kidney injury was uncommon. Decreases in systolic blood pressure were observed and were more pronounced with combination therapy.

**CONCLUSIONS.** In CONFIDENCE, simultaneous initiation with finerenone and an SGLT2 inhibitor was effective and well tolerated compared with monotherapy, irrespective of background use of a GLP-1 RA.

## Introduction

Diabetes is the most common cause of kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplantation globally, accounting for approximately half of all new cases of kidney failure in the U.S. Among people with diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD) markedly amplifies the risks of kidney failure, atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, and all-cause mortality. For CKD management in people with type 2 diabetes, four classes of drugs are recommended on the basis of outcomes trials: (1) renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors (ACE inhibitors or ARBs), (2) SGLT2 inhibitors, (3) GLP-1 RAs with proven benefit for reducing cardiovascular risk and kidney disease progression, and (4) the nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist finerenone for people with CKD and albuminuria to reduce cardiovascular events and CKD progression (if eGFR ≥25 mL/min/1.73 m²).

The COmbinatioN effect of FInerenone anD EmpaglifloziN in participants with CKD and type 2 diabetes using a UACR Endpoint (CONFIDENCE) randomized trial demonstrated that the combination of finerenone and an SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin) provided additive reductions in albuminuria in participants with CKD with albuminuria and diabetes, supporting the use of these agents together to optimize reduction in kidney disease progression. The trial confirmed the safety of this combination by monitoring blood pressure, serum potassium, and eGFR and highlighted that current stepwise-addition clinical practice can potentially be replaced by simultaneous initiation of these two agents.

A 2025 Cochrane review (42 randomized controlled trials; 48,148 participants) concluded that GLP-1 RAs are likely to provide cardiovascular benefit and reduce all-cause mortality in people with diabetes and CKD. The FLOW trial demonstrated that semaglutide reduced the risk of a composite kidney outcome (kidney failure, ≥50% eGFR decline, kidney or cardiovascular death) by 24% compared with placebo; semaglutide also reduced major cardiovascular events by 18% and all-cause mortality by 20%.

In this prespecified analysis of the CONFIDENCE trial, the authors evaluated the efficacy and safety of simultaneous initiation of finerenone and empagliflozin versus finerenone and empagliflozin monotherapy, with or without GLP-1 RA, on a background of RAS inhibition.

## Research Design and Methods

### Trial Setting
CONFIDENCE was a double-blind, randomized, active-controlled trial of empagliflozin alone, finerenone alone, or the combination, in people with CKD with albuminuria and type 2 diabetes. First registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05254002) on 23 February 2022.

### Participants
Eligible adults had type 2 diabetes with HbA1c <11% (97 mmol/mol), eGFR between 30 and 90 mL/min/1.73 m², and albuminuria (UACR 100 to <5,000 mg/g, confirmed by averaging first morning urine samples over 3 consecutive days). All participants had to be receiving the maximally tolerated dose of an ACE inhibitor or ARB for ≥1 month prior to screening. Exclusion criteria included serum potassium >4.8 mmol/L, type 1 diabetes, and use of an SGLT2 inhibitor or potassium binder within 8 weeks before screening. GLP-1 RA treatments were allowed provided the dosage had remained stable for ≥4 weeks prior to screening. GLP-1 RA dose reductions were allowed during the study; dose increases were not allowed.

### Randomization
Participants randomized 1:1:1 to finerenone (10 or 20 mg target dose once daily, plus placebo-matching empagliflozin), empagliflozin (10 mg once daily, plus placebo-matching finerenone), or both in combination. Initial finerenone dose set by kidney function: eGFR ≥60 started at 20 mg daily; baseline eGFR <60 started at 10 mg daily, with uptitration to 20 mg after 30 days if serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L and eGFR decrease <30% versus previous visit. Empagliflozin fixed dose 10 mg.

### Trial Outcomes
Primary efficacy outcome: change from baseline to 180 days in log-transformed mean UACR. Secondary outcome: proportion of participants achieving UACR reduction of >30%, >40%, and >50% at 180 days. Secondary safety outcomes: acute eGFR change >30% from baseline at 30 days, acute kidney injury, hyperkalemia events, change in serum potassium, symptomatic hypotension, severe hypoglycemia, urosepsis/pyelonephritis, and genital mycotic infections.

### Statistical Analyses
Efficacy analyses used the full-analysis set. Primary analysis: mixed model for repeated measures with log-transformed UACR (geometric mean ratios back-transformed to percentage changes). The prespecified interest was effect modification by baseline GLP-1 RA use. GLP-1 RA use was not randomized; causal interaction was not assessed. Logistic regression (adjusted for eGFR and UACR stratification factors, GLP-1 RA use and its interaction with treatment) analyzed threshold UACR reductions. eGFR calculated using the CKD-EPI equation with a modification for Japanese participants. Subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA use were prespecified prior to database lock; models were not adjusted for multiple comparisons.

## Results

Of 800 participants in the full-analysis set, **182 (22.8%) reported GLP-1 RA use at baseline**, comprising a similar proportion in the combination (68 of 269 [25.3%]), finerenone (52 of 264 [19.7%]), and empagliflozin (62 of 267 [23.2%]) groups.

Of the 182 patients receiving a GLP-1 RA at baseline: 102 (56%) semaglutide, 41 (23%) dulaglutide, 28 (15%) liraglutide, 6 (3%) tirzepatide, 5 (3%) lixisenatide. Median duration of baseline GLP-1 RA use prior to randomization was 358 days (range 1–8,522).

Participants prescribed GLP-1 RAs at baseline were less likely to be Asian or from Asia; had higher mean BMI; had greater use of insulin, β-blockers, diuretics, antiplatelet agents, and statins; and were less likely to be taking DPP-4 inhibitors. Median UACR was lower with baseline GLP-1 RA use (539 [IQR 290–1,000] vs. 592 [292–1,167] mg/g).

### Treatment Effect by GLP-1 RA Use

At day 180, UACR change from baseline in participants using a GLP-1 RA: −51% (95% CI −59 to −40%) with combination therapy, −34% (−48 to −18%) with finerenone alone, and −36% (−48 to −21%) with empagliflozin alone. Similar reductions in those not using a GLP-1 RA. In the GLP-1 RA group, UACR change at day 180 with combination vs. finerenone alone and vs. empagliflozin alone were −25% (−44 to 1%) and −23% (−42 to 3%), respectively — similar to the non-GLP-1 RA subgroup. Attenuation of UACR reduction occurred following treatment discontinuation in the combination group for both subgroups.

A greater proportion achieved UACR reductions of >30%, >40%, or >50% with combination therapy versus either monotherapy, irrespective of background GLP-1 RA use. E.g., ~72.1% (95% CI 65.6–78.7%) of combination-group participants without baseline GLP-1 RA use achieved a >30% UACR reduction, and 63.8% (51.7–75.9%) in the combination group with baseline GLP-1 RA use achieved this.

### Adverse Events
Incidence of abdominal symptoms was similar between those receiving and not receiving a GLP-1 RA at baseline. Hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia events were uncommon, occurring in 10 and 8 participants overall, respectively.

### Serum Potassium and Hyperkalemia
Trends for change in serum potassium over time were similar irrespective of GLP-1 RA use. Combination therapy was associated with a slight increase in mean serum potassium, declining to baseline after cessation; similar in the finerenone group. Empagliflozin was not associated with serum potassium changes.

In participants with baseline GLP-1 RA use, treatment-emergent hyperkalemia adverse events occurred in 9.0%, 11.5%, and 6.5% (combination, finerenone alone, empagliflozin alone). In those without GLP-1 RA use, 9.5%, 11.3%, and 2.9%, respectively.

### Changes in eGFR
Early decline in eGFR in all three groups, then stabilized and reversible on discontinuation. eGFR decline >30% at day 30 occurred in 30 participants overall, distribution across groups similar in both GLP-1 RA subgroups. Acute kidney injury uncommon (8 participants overall).

### Changes in Systolic Blood Pressure
Reduction from baseline in SBP in all three arms in both subgroups, more pronounced with combination therapy. SBP returned to baseline after discontinuation. Symptomatic hypotension in 3 participants randomized to combination (1 receiving a GLP-1 RA, 2 not).

## Conclusions

This prespecified subgroup analysis showed consistent treatment benefits of simultaneous initiation of combination finerenone plus empagliflozin compared with either monotherapy, irrespective of baseline GLP-1 RA use. UACR reduction with either monotherapy was likewise not modified by baseline GLP-1 RA use. The data suggest three-class therapy (ACE inhibitors/ARBs, SGLT2 inhibitors, finerenone) reduces UACR in the presence or absence of GLP-1 RA. The study was neither designed nor powered to test additive effect of GLP-1 RA.

Context from prior evidence:
- **FLOW trial**: semaglutide 1.0 mg weekly; primary composite kidney/CV outcome HR 0.76 (95% CI 0.66–0.88) over median 3.4 years, consistent across subgroups including background SGLT2 inhibitor use (P for interaction = 0.109).
- **2024 meta-analysis** (FLOW + AMPLITUDE-O + Harmony Outcomes): kidney composite effect did not vary by SGLT2 inhibitor use (risk ratio 0.79 [95% CI 0.66–0.95]).
- **FIDELITY**: finerenone reduced UACR regardless of baseline GLP-1 RA use; magnitude greater with (−38%) vs. without (−31%) GLP-1 RA use (P for interaction = 0.03). Finerenone vs. placebo UACR change at month 4: −40% (GLP-1 RA + SGLT2i), −38% (GLP-1 RA only), −36% (SGLT2i only) (P for interaction = 0.11).

Median GLP-1 RA use duration was 13 months, implying UACR lowering largely occurred prior to study entry. Numerically lower rates of severe hyperkalemia in baseline GLP-1 RA users, suggesting a possible mitigating effect on hyperkalemia risk. A numerically greater proportion of GLP-1 RA-group participants (particularly on combination therapy) experienced eGFR decline >30%, suggesting a need for closer monitoring. Overall, simultaneous initiation did not increase risk of poor kidney outcomes even in patients already on GLP-1 RAs.

### Limitations
1. Baseline differences (higher BMI etc.) may confound; weight loss is a determinant of UACR reduction; confounding by indication possible.
2. Subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA use were observational (randomization not stratified by GLP-1 RA use) — potential selection bias; causal interaction inference challenging.
3. Limited statistical power to detect additive/synergistic effects given small number on combination therapy at baseline.
4. Differences in quality of care and socioeconomic disparities may confound.
5. Subgroup analysis by specific GLP-1 RA agent not possible (small samples); detailed dose data not systematically collected.

**Conclusion:** Consistent numerical trends suggest benefits of simultaneous initiation of finerenone plus empagliflozin (vs. either monotherapy) occur irrespective of baseline GLP-1 RA use; finerenone and SGLT2 inhibitors (alone or combined) reduce UACR regardless of baseline GLP-1 RA usage. Future dedicated studies are needed to characterize additive/synergistic effects on clinical outcomes.
