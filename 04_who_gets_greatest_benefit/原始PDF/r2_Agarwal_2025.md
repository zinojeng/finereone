## Abstract (verbatim, PubMed PMID 40470996)

Limited evidence exists to support the simultaneous initiation of sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors and finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, in persons with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes. We randomly assigned participants with chronic kidney disease (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], 30 to 90 ml per minute per 1.73 m2 of body-surface area), albuminuria (a urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio of 100 to ≤5000 [with albumin measured in milligrams and creatinine measured in grams]), and type 2 diabetes, who were already taking a renin-angiotensin system inhibitor, in a 1:1:1 ratio to receive finerenone (with empagliflozin-matching placebo) at a dose of 10 or 20 mg per day, empagliflozin at a dose of 10 mg per day (with finerenone-matching placebo), or a combination of finerenone and empagliflozin. The primary outcome was the relative change in the log-transformed mean urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio from baseline to 180 days. Safety was assessed. At baseline, the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio was similar among the participants in the three groups; the median value was 579 (interquartile range, 292 to 1092) among those with available data (265 in the combination-therapy group, 258 in the finerenone group, and 261 participants in the empagliflozin group). At day 180, the reduction in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio with combination therapy was 29% greater than that with finerenone alone (least-squares mean ratio of the difference in the change from baseline, 0.71; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.61 to 0.82; P<0.001) and 32% greater than that with empagliflozin alone (least-squares mean ratio of the difference in the change from baseline, 0.68; 95% CI, 0.59 to 0.79; P<0.001). Neither agent, alone or in combination, led to unexpected adverse events. Symptomatic hypotension, acute kidney injury, and hyperkalemia leading to drug discontinuation were uncommon. Among persons with both chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes, initial therapy with finerenone plus empagliflozin led to a greater reduction in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio than either treatment alone. (Funded by Bayer; CONFIDENCE ClinicalTrials.gov number, NCT05254002.)

## Design (source: NephJC summary + PubMed baseline-characteristics paper PMID 39916475)

- Parallel-group, double-blind, double-dummy, international, randomized phase 2 trial.
- Objective: efficacy (UACR reduction from baseline) and safety of finerenone + empagliflozin vs either drug alone in CKD + T2D.
- Randomization 1:1:1; stratified by eGFR (<60 vs ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m2) and UACR (≤850 vs >850 mg/g).
- 818 participants randomized across 143 sites in 14 countries, July 2022–August 2024 (per baseline-characteristics paper PMID 39916475). Abstract of primary-results paper reports ~800 with per-group UACR data (265 combination / 258 finerenone / 261 empagliflozin).
- Doses: empagliflozin 10 mg once daily; finerenone 10 or 20 mg once daily depending on baseline eGFR. Finerenone 10 mg could be uptitrated to 20 mg after day 30 if potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L and eGFR decline from prior visit <30% (NephJC).

### Inclusion criteria (source: NephJC)
- Adult.
- CKD with eGFR 30–90 mL/min/1.73 m2 (CKD-EPI 2009) and at least one historical eGFR <60.
- UACR 300–5000 mg/g (34–565 mg/mmol) within 3 months prior. (Note: primary-results abstract states enrollment UACR range 100 to ≤5000; NephJC lists 300–5000 — discrepancy flagged, defer to NEJM/abstract range of 100–≤5000.)
- T2D with HbA1c <11% (97 mmol/mol).
- Stable (≥1 month) max tolerated dose of ACEi OR ARB (not both).

## Primary outcome (source: PubMed abstract + Ovid/NEJM extract + NephJC)

- Primary endpoint: relative change in log-transformed mean UACR from baseline to day 180.
- Baseline median UACR 579 mg/g (IQR 292–1092).
- Day 180: combination therapy reduced UACR 29% more than finerenone alone (LS-mean ratio 0.71; 95% CI 0.61–0.82; P<0.001) and 32% more than empagliflozin alone (LS-mean ratio 0.68; 95% CI 0.59–0.79; P<0.001).
- Interpretation: additive effect of combination.
- After stopping treatment at day 180, UACR rebounded by day 210 across all groups (NephJC).
- Press/summary figure (Applied Clinical Trials, ERA 2025): combination achieved ~52% UACR reduction at day 180 — cross-check only, not from primary-results abstract.

## Secondary / safety outcomes (source: NephJC — structure only; exact numbers NOT in retrieved text)

- Prespecified secondary efficacy: proportion achieving UACR reductions >30%, >40%, >50%.
- Safety endpoints: AKI, hyperkalemia, change from baseline in serum potassium, symptomatic hypotension, ketoacidosis, severe hypoglycemia, genital mycotic infections.
- Post-treatment: UACR and eGFR changes after discontinuation (day 180 → 210).
- Abstract-level safety statement: no unexpected adverse events; symptomatic hypotension, AKI, and hyperkalemia leading to drug discontinuation were uncommon.

## Analytic plan (source: NephJC)

- Primary analysis: mixed-model for repeated measures (MMRM).
- Sample size: conservatively via two-sample t-test assuming equal variance and log-normal UACR distribution.

## Relevance to "who gets greatest benefit" (04_who_gets_greatest_benefit)

The primary-results paper reports an overall additive UACR benefit. Subgroup/heterogeneity questions (baseline eGFR/UACR, GLP-1 RA use, KDIGO risk category) are addressed in companion CONFIDENCE analyses that should be fetched separately:
- Mottl A, et al. Baseline kidney function, albuminuria, UACR reduction — post hoc analyses. J Am Soc Nephrol 2026. doi:10.1681/ASN.0000000928 (PMID 41196655). Key: greater albuminuria lowering with higher baseline eGFR/UACR, older age, female sex, and ASCVD — but treatment effect NOT modified by baseline eGFR/UACR.
- Vaduganathan M, et al. Across the spectrum of kidney risk (KDIGO categories). Nephrol Dial Transplant 2025. doi:10.1093/ndt/gfaf160 (PMID 40886054).
- Agarwal R, et al. Impact of baseline GLP-1 RA use. Diabetes Care 2025. doi:10.2337/dc25-1673 (PMID 40968755).
