# Comparative efficacy and safety of finerenone in diabetic kidney disease: a meta-analysis of Asian and non-Asian populations

Raza et al. BMC Nephrology (2025) 26:676

## Abstract

**Background & objective:** Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) is a major global burden, especially in Asia. This study aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of finerenone in diabetic kidney disease, comparing outcomes between Asian and non-Asian populations through a systematic review and meta-analysis.

**Methods:** A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Cochrane Library, ClinicalTrials.gov, Google Scholar, and the Undermind AI platform from inception through March 2025. Studies included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and subgroup analyses that evaluated finerenone in DKD patients. Primary outcomes included a reduction in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) and a decline in the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of ≥40%. Secondary outcomes included cardiovascular events, mortality due to kidney failure, hyperkalemia (serum potassium >5.0 mmol/L), treatment discontinuation, hospitalization, and adverse event–related mortality. Risk ratios (RRs) and mean differences (MDs) were pooled using a random-effects model, and subgroup analyses were performed by ethnicity.

**Results:** Five eligible studies, comprising 8,763 participants, were included in this analysis. Finerenone significantly reduced UACR compared with placebo (MD = −0.38, 95% CI −0.42 to −0.35; p < 0.001), with consistent effects across Asian and non-Asian populations (subgroup p = 0.28). It also significantly reduced the risk of eGFR decline ≥40% (MD = −0.24 [−0.40, −0.09]; p = 0.002), with a greater benefit in the Asian subgroup (subgroup p = 0.03). Cardiovascular event risk was also reduced (RR = 0.85 [0.77–0.95]; p = 0.004), while mortality due to kidney failure showed a non-significant reduction (RR = 0.83 [0.64–1.07]; p = 0.15). Hyperkalemia risk was higher with finerenone (RR = 1.73, 95% CI 1.39–2.14), whereas adverse event–related mortality was lower (RR = 0.65, 95% CI 0.46–0.91).

**Conclusion:** Finerenone provides robust renoprotective and cardioprotective effects in DKD, with broadly consistent efficacy across Asian and non-Asian populations. A greater renal benefit was observed in Asians for eGFR decline ≥40%, though this requires cautious interpretation. Hyperkalemia risk was increased but largely manageable. These findings support integration into DKD therapy and highlight the need for ethnically inclusive, long-term, real-world trials.

**Clinical trial number:** Not applicable.

**Keywords:** Finerenone, Diabetic kidney disease (DKD), Hyperkalemia, Urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR), Assessing estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), Cardiovascular events, Asian population, Meta-analysis

## Introduction

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is one of the most severe and prevalent complications of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), commonly referred to as diabetic kidney disease (DKD). Globally, approximately 30–40% of individuals with T2DM develop CKD during their lifetime, positioning diabetes as the leading cause of end-stage renal disease (ESRD). Between 1990 and 2017, the incidence of DKD rose by 74%. Asia bears the heaviest burden of diabetes-related CKD, led by China and India. Nearly 31% of individuals with diabetes in China present with CKD, while Japan reports a DKD prevalence of approximately 25% to 27% among diabetic patients. India is projected to exceed 130 million diabetes cases by 2045. Asian populations often develop CKD at younger ages and lower body weights than Western counterparts.

Traditionally, DKD management has emphasized glycemic control and blood pressure management, with RAAS blockade using ACE inhibitors or ARBs as standard of care. Intensification of RAAS blockade has been unsuccessful due to adverse events, particularly hyperkalemia and acute kidney injury. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce albuminuria and slow GFR decline while reducing cardiovascular risk, but many patients continue to experience progressive kidney function loss.

Finerenone, a selective nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA), offers a novel mechanism of reno-cardiovascular protection. Unlike steroidal MRAs such as spironolactone, finerenone exhibits higher selectivity for the mineralocorticoid receptor and reduced hormonal side effects. The pivotal FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials demonstrated finerenone's efficacy in reducing the risk of kidney disease progression and cardiovascular events in patients with T2DM and CKD already receiving optimized RAAS blockade. The FIDELITY meta-analysis confirmed benefits extend across different stages of CKD. KDIGO guidelines now recommend finerenone as adjunctive therapy in T2DM patients with CKD and albuminuria.

Questions remain regarding finerenone's efficacy and safety across ethnically diverse populations, particularly Asians, who have historically been underrepresented in global trials. Asian populations may present unique challenges, such as higher baseline albuminuria, distinct dietary habits (e.g., higher potassium intake), and greater predisposition to hyperkalemia. A subgroup analysis of Asian participants from FIDELIO-DKD revealed consistent renoprotective benefits, but a slightly higher rate of hyperkalemia, particularly among those with a baseline serum potassium level of ≥4.8 mmol/L. Few studies have focused exclusively on South Asian populations (India, Pakistan).

## Methodology

### Study objectives
Evaluate efficacy and safety of finerenone in CKD in patients with T2DM, focusing on Asian versus non-Asian comparisons. Primary objectives: assess effectiveness in reducing UACR; evaluate impact on reducing CKD progression via eGFR decline; determine incidence and severity of hyperkalemia and other adverse events.

### Search strategy
Comprehensive literature search in PubMed, Cochrane Library, ClinicalTrials.gov, and Google Scholar up to March 2025, plus screening of the Undermind AI tool. MeSH and keywords: "finerenone," "mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists," "chronic kidney disease with type 2 diabetes," "diabetic kidney disease." Conducted per PRISMA 2020 guidelines.

### Eligibility criteria
Included: (1) adult patients with DKD; (2) finerenone alongside standard care (e.g., RAS inhibitors); (3) placebo + standard care comparator; (4) reported ≥1 relevant clinical outcome (UACR, eGFR, cardiovascular events, hyperkalemia, discontinuation, or adverse event–related mortality); (5) RCTs or post hoc subgroup analyses of RCTs in peer-reviewed journals. Participants categorized as Asian or non-Asian (non-Asian = White, Black, Hispanic). Non-English articles excluded.

### Study screening and selection
Records imported into Rayyan AI. Two reviewers (A.H. and F.Q.) screened titles/abstracts; disagreements resolved by third reviewer (U.Y.). Five studies included — comprising RCTs and subgroup analyses reporting separate Asian/non-Asian data: broader Asian and non-Asian cohorts, China, Japan, Black population, and Hispanic population.

### Data extraction and quality assessment
Two reviewers (M.M. and S.A.R.) extracted data. Primary outcome: UACR reduction. Risk of bias assessed using Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 tool.

### Statistical analysis
Review Manager (RevMan) v5.4. RRs and MDs with 95% CIs pooled using Mantel–Haenszel method with random-effects model. Heterogeneity assessed via Cochran's Q (p < 0.10) and I². Publication bias assessed visually with funnel plots.

## Results

The systematic search identified 683 records. After removing duplicates and screening, five RCTs and subgroup analyses were included, comprising 8,763 patients with DKD. These represented six distinct subgroups: one each from Japan, China, and a broader Asian cohort, and three from non-Asian populations (Black, Hispanic, and other global regions). Intervention arm (finerenone + standard care): 4,423 patients; control arm (placebo + standard care): 4,340 patients. Median participant age ranged from 60 to 66 years, with follow-up 3 to 34 months. ROB 2.0 indicated some concerns in three studies.

### Efficacy outcomes

**UACR:** Finerenone significantly reduced UACR vs placebo. Pooled MD = −0.38 [95% CI −0.42 to −0.35]; p < 0.001, with no heterogeneity (I² = 0%). Subgroups: Asian MD = −0.36 [−0.41 to −0.31]; non-Asian MD = −0.40 [−0.45 to −0.35]. Test for subgroup differences not significant (p = 0.28; I² = 15.2%). (Fig. 3A)

**eGFR Decline ≥40%:** Finerenone significantly reduced risk. Pooled MD = −0.24 [95% CI −0.40 to −0.09]; p = 0.002. Subgroups: stronger protective effect in Asians (MD = −0.40 [−0.59 to −0.20]; p < 0.001) vs non-Asians (MD = −0.13 [−0.26 to −0.01]; p = 0.04). Subgroup difference statistically significant (p = 0.03), suggesting Asian populations may derive greater renal benefit. (Fig. 4A)

**Mortality and Cardiovascular Outcomes:** Non-significant trend toward reduced mortality due to kidney failure (RR = 0.83 [95% CI 0.64–1.07]; p = 0.15); I² = 0%; subgroup interaction not significant (p = 0.75). (Fig. 5A) For cardiovascular events, finerenone significantly reduced overall risk (RR = 0.85 [95% CI 0.77–0.95]; p = 0.004). Subgroups: no significant effect in Asians (RR = 0.84 [95% CI 0.62–1.13]; p = 0.25) but significant reduction in non-Asians (RR = 0.86 [95% CI 0.76–0.96]; p = 0.008). Heterogeneity absent (I² = 0%); subgroup differences not significant (p = 0.91). (Fig. 6A)

### Safety outcomes

**Overall Hyperkalemia:** Finerenone significantly increased risk vs placebo (RR = 1.73 [95% CI 1.39–2.14]; p < 0.001). Subgroups: Asians RR = 1.53 [1.25–1.86], I² = 0%; non-Asians RR = 2.11 [1.28–3.48], I² = 67%; no significant subgroup difference (p = 0.24). Overall heterogeneity moderate (I² = 40%). Sensitivity analyses confirmed robustness. (Fig. 7A)

**Moderate and Severe Hyperkalemia:** Moderate hyperkalemia (>5.5 mmol/L) RR = 2.20 [1.97–2.46]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%. Severe hyperkalemia (>6.0 mmol/L) RR = 3.00 [2.25–4.00]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%. (Fig. 8)

**Hyperkalemia-Related Treatment Discontinuation and Hospitalization:** Discontinuation due to hyperkalemia RR = 2.61 [1.77–3.85]; p < 0.001; consistent across subgroups (I² = 0%, subgroup p = 0.66). Hospitalization due to hyperkalemia RR = 3.30 [1.49–7.29]; p = 0.003; minimal heterogeneity (I² = 8%). (Fig. 9)

**Adverse events leading to death:** Finerenone significantly reduced adverse event–related mortality (RR = 0.65 [95% CI 0.46–0.91]; p = 0.01); I² = 0%; subgroup interaction not significant (p = 0.46). (Fig. 10A)

## Discussion

Finerenone significantly reduces UACR (pooled MD = −0.38; p < 0.001), aligning with FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD (~30–40% decrease from baseline). It attenuated eGFR decline (pooled MD = −0.24, 95% CI −0.40 to −0.09). Reduction in mortality due to kidney failure did not reach significance (RR = 0.83) but trended favorably, in agreement with FIDELITY. Cardiovascular events reduced by 15% (RR = 0.85), aligning with 13–14% reductions in prior trials.

### Subgroup analyses by ethnicity
- **UACR:** Robust antiproteinuric effect across both groups, no significant subgroup difference (p = 0.28); consistent with ~32% UACR reduction in Hispanic patients [24] and ~40% in Black patients [23].
- **eGFR Decline ≥40%:** Statistically significant subgroup difference (p = 0.03), with Asians experiencing greater protective effect (MD = −0.40 [−0.59, −0.20]) vs non-Asians (−0.13 [−0.26, −0.01]). Supports FIDELIO-DKD post hoc analysis by Koya et al. [19], which reported 30% reduction in renal outcomes in Asians (HR 0.70) vs ~12% in non-Asians (HR 0.88). May partly reflect baseline imbalances (proteinuria burden, RAAS inhibitor dosing, dietary potassium) or biological mechanisms (pharmacogenomic variation — CYP450 or MR polymorphisms, aldosterone sensitivity, sodium handling).
- **Kidney disease–related mortality:** Did not reach significance in either subgroup; direction favored finerenone (RR < 1); I² = 0%, subgroup p = 0.75.
- **Cardiovascular events:** Significant reduction in non-Asians (RR = 0.86, p = 0.008); not significant in Asians (RR = 0.84, p = 0.25); subgroup difference not significant (p = 0.91). Aligns with FIDELIO/FIGARO-DKD HRs 0.87–0.88.
- **Hyperkalemia:** Increased in both groups, slightly higher in non-Asians (RR = 2.11) vs Asians (RR = 1.53); subgroup difference approached but did not reach significance (p = 0.24). Moderate/severe hyperkalemia and hyperkalemia-related discontinuation/hospitalization consistent across subgroups (p > 0.05 for all). Ethnicity unlikely a strong independent modifier of risk.

Note: In the Discussion the authors state an increased hyperkalemia risk of "RR = 1.85" and note finerenone-associated increase particularly at serum potassium >5.5 and >6.0 mmol/L; the Results/Abstract report the overall hyperkalemia pooled RR as 1.73 [1.39–2.14].

### Limitations
1. Most included studies were subgroup/post hoc analyses of FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD, increasing reporting bias risk.
2. Baseline imbalances (proteinuria burden, RAAS inhibitor dosing, dietary potassium) may have influenced eGFR decline subgroup differences; formal meta-regression not feasible due to incomplete data.
3. "Asian" vs "non-Asian" obscures within-category heterogeneity; South Asians (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh) remain underrepresented.
4. Biological mechanisms for stronger renal benefit in Asians remain speculative.
5. Higher hyperkalemia risk in non-Asians may reflect unmeasured confounders.
6. Follow-up durations relatively short (median 2.5–3 years), limiting long-term outcome assessment (ESRD, all-cause mortality).
7. Most studies conducted before widespread SGLT2 inhibitor uptake, restricting applicability to current "triple therapy" (RAAS blockade + SGLT2i + MRA).
8. Small number of included studies limits reliability of publication bias assessment.

### Clinical relevance and future directions
Finerenone offers consistent renoprotective (albuminuria reduction, slower eGFR decline) and cardioprotective (fewer CV events) benefits across Asian and non-Asian populations, with renal benefit more pronounced in Asians (possibly partly explained by baseline differences). Hyperkalemia risk increased but generally manageable with monitoring; severe outcomes rare. Aligns with KDIGO recommendations. Gaps: long-term ESRD/all-cause mortality effects uncertain; role in advanced CKD (eGFR < 25) and ACEi/ARB-intolerant patients needs study; integration into "triple therapy" warrants real-world evaluation; expansion of South Asian inclusion critical.

## Conclusion

Finerenone provides clinically meaningful renoprotective and cardioprotective effects in DKD. Subgroup analyses showed broadly consistent efficacy across Asian and non-Asian populations, with a statistically significant greater renal benefit in Asians for eGFR decline ≥40%. For albuminuria reduction and cardiovascular outcomes, effects were directionally similar across ethnicities with no significant subgroup interaction. Hyperkalemia risk increased in both populations, numerically higher in non-Asians, but subgroup tests did not reach significance and severe adverse outcomes remained infrequent and manageable. Observed ethnic differences should be interpreted cautiously given incomplete baseline data, short follow-up, and reliance on post hoc subgroup analyses. Future ethnically inclusive trials, particularly in South Asian populations, are needed.
