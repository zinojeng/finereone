# Efficacy of finerenone in reducing heart failure outcomes in patients with history of heart failure: A meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials

## Background / Introduction

Finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA), has been shown to lower kidney disease progression and clinical cardiovascular outcomes among patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes mellitus. However, the efficacy of finerenone in reducing heart failure outcomes among patients with a history of heart failure has not been well established. The authors aimed to conduct a study-level meta-analysis of available phase III randomized clinical trials.

## Methods

- Conducted and reported following PRISMA 2020 guidelines.
- Systematic literature search in PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov using predefined MeSH terms with Boolean operators 'AND' and 'OR'. Search terms included: "Finerenone", "CKD", "type 2 diabetes", "heart failure", HFpEF, HFrEF, "randomized controlled trials".
- Included all multicentric, double-blind, randomized controlled trials published till July 2024 comparing finerenone with placebo in patients with a history of heart failure at baseline.
- Baseline continuous variables summarized as mean (SD); dichotomous variables as frequency or percentage.
- Conventional meta-analysis using the DerSimonian and Laird random-effects model.
- Outcomes reported as pooled odds ratio (OR) with 95% confidence interval (CI). Statistical significance if 95% CI did not cross "1" and two-tailed p < 0.05.
- Analyses conducted using STATA version 17.1 (StataCorp, College Station, Texas).
- **Included 2 randomized controlled trials** conducted in patients with CKD, type 2 diabetes mellitus, and a history of heart failure at baseline, comparing finerenone with placebo.

## Baseline characteristics

- **Total 1,007 patients** (485 in finerenone group, 522 in placebo group).
- Mean age: 65.95 ± 0.71 years.
- 62% male.
- 93% had hypertension.
- Mean follow-up duration: 3 years.

## Results

- **First hospitalization for heart failure:** Finerenone significantly reduced risk by 32% — OR 0.68 (95% CI: 0.47–0.98), P = 0.04, I² = 0%.
- **Cardiovascular composite outcomes:** Non-significant reduction — OR 0.79 (95% CI: 0.60–1.04), P = 1.10 [as printed], I² = 0.0%.
- **Hyperkalemia:** Finerenone-treated patients had higher risk — OR 2.16 (95% CI: 1.38–3.38), P < 0.001, I² = 0%.

## Discussion

The meta-analysis demonstrated that finerenone resulted in a 32% reduction in the risk of initial hospitalization for heart failure. Non-significant reductions were observed for the risk of cardiovascular composite outcomes. Finerenone was associated with a significantly higher risk of hyperkalemia compared to placebo.

Preclinical studies indicate that MRAs improve LV diastolic function and attenuate LV hypertrophy and myocardial fibrosis. A meta-analysis of 11 randomized trials demonstrated that MRA therapy enhanced diastolic function (by echocardiography) and reduced circulating biomarkers of fibrosis, although no significant changes were seen in LV dimensions or mass.

According to available evidence, in addition to reducing hospitalization for heart failure, finerenone lowers the risk of composite outcomes such as cardiovascular death or first hospitalization for heart failure by 18% (HR: 0.82) and all-cause mortality by 9% (HR: 0.91) compared to placebo. Finerenone use was not associated with adverse cardiovascular outcomes. Consistent with its MRA class, finerenone was linked to a significant increase in hyperkalemia incidence, though this risk appears lower than that observed with spironolactone. Finerenone has higher selectivity for the mineralocorticoid receptor than eplerenone, which may confer cardiorenal protection advantages, particularly in patients with comorbid CKD and diabetes. Finerenone is also responsible for more than 30% decrease in NT-proBNP levels compared to eplerenone.

### Limitations

- Relied on reported (aggregate) data rather than individual patient data, which may affect bias in assessment.
- Subgroup analyses of RCTs included relatively small heart failure patient populations; limited sample size may reduce statistical power and generalizability, affecting external validity.

## Conclusion

The meta-analysis highlights the therapeutic potential of finerenone in reducing the risk of initial hospitalization for heart failure, with no significant impact on cardiovascular composite outcomes. However, finerenone is associated with an increased risk of hyperkalemia.
