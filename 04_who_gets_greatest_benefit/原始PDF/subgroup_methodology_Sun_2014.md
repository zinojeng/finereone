# How to use a subgroup analysis: users' guide to the medical literature

**Authors:** Xin Sun; John P. A. Ioannidis; Thomas Agoritsas; Ana C. Alba; Gordon Guyatt
**Journal:** JAMA. 2014;311(4):405-411.
**DOI:** 10.1001/jama.2013.285063
**PMID:** 24449319

## Abstract (summary retrieved from PubMed)

Clinicians need guidance to apply the results of randomized trials and
systematic reviews to individual patients. Apparently compelling subgroup
effects (differences in treatment effect across subgroups of patients or
across studies) often prove spurious.

The article proposes five key criteria for assessing the credibility of a
subgroup analysis:

1. Whether chance can explain the apparent subgroup effect.
2. Whether the effect is consistent across studies.
3. Whether the subgroup hypothesis was one of a small number specified
   a priori (before data collection), including the correct direction.
4. Whether there is strong pre-existing biological rationale
   (biological plausibility).
5. Whether the evidence comes from within-study (rather than between-study)
   comparisons.

The first four criteria apply to subgroup analyses from a single study as
well as from systematic reviews; the fifth criterion applies only to
subgroup claims derived from multiple studies. Applying these criteria helps
clinicians decide whether a subgroup effect is credible enough to guide
patient-care decisions, or whether the overall (main) trial result should
instead be applied to all patients.
