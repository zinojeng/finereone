# Development of the Instrument to assess the Credibility of Effect Modification Analyses (ICEMAN) in randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses

**Cite as:** CMAJ 2020 August 10;192:E901-6. doi: 10.1503/cmaj.200077

## Abstract

**Background:** Most randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and meta-analyses of RCTs examine effect modification (also called a subgroup effect or interaction), in which the effect of an intervention varies by another variable (e.g., age or disease severity). Assessing the credibility of an apparent effect modification presents challenges; therefore, the authors developed the Instrument for assessing the Credibility of Effect Modification Analyses (ICEMAN).

**Methods:** To develop ICEMAN, the authors established a detailed concept; identified candidate credibility considerations in a systematic survey of the literature; together with experts, performed a consensus study to identify key considerations and develop them into instrument items; and refined the instrument based on feedback from trial investigators, systematic review authors and journal editors, who applied drafts of ICEMAN to published claims of effect modification.

**Results:** The final instrument consists of a set of preliminary considerations, core questions (5 for RCTs, 8 for meta-analyses) with 4 response options, 1 optional item for additional considerations and a rating of credibility on a visual analogue scale ranging from very low to high. An accompanying manual provides rationales, detailed instructions and examples from the literature. Seventeen potential users tested ICEMAN; their suggestions improved the user-friendliness of the instrument.

**Interpretation:** The Instrument for assessing the Credibility of Effect Modification Analyses offers explicit guidance for investigators, systematic reviewers, journal editors and others considering making a claim of effect modification or interpreting a claim made by others.

## Introduction / Background

Investigators who conduct RCTs and meta-analyses of RCTs often perform analyses of effect modification to assess whether intervention effects might vary by another variable such as age, disease severity or, in a meta-analysis, study setting or year of study. The terminology varies (Box 1). Investigators sometimes make claims that an effect modification is present. **Literature surveys suggest that 14%–26% of RCTs and meta-analyses emphasize at least 1 potential effect modification in their abstract or discussion.**

The interest in effect modification is understandable: if patients with differing characteristics respond differently to the same intervention, the overall effect estimate is misleading for some, if not all, patients. However, mistaken claims of effect modification may compromise optimal patient care, and many claims of effect modification have subsequently proved spurious. Applying a mistaken claim may cause harms through administration of ineffective treatment or lead to patients being denied beneficial therapies, and will likely increase health care costs.

Numerous theoretical analyses and simulation studies show that **the fundamental reason for misleading claims of effect modification is chance:** even if the treatment effect is the same for all patients, examining a sufficiently large number of candidates will inevitably reveal an apparent, but misleading, effect modification. Other reasons contributing to spurious claims include selective reporting, lack of background knowledge and prior evidence, and failure to use a proper statistical analysis. Nevertheless, some claims of effect modification — likely a minority — will be true.

Methodologists first suggested credibility criteria for effect modification in the early 1990s. Since then, 30 groups have suggested sets of 3–21 criteria. Previous sets had suboptimal presentation resulting in ambiguity, some criteria were subjective, and none of the previous sets involved a rigorous development process or user testing before publication.

- **Synonyms for effect modification:** Subgroup effect; Statistical interaction; Moderation; Differential effect; Heterogeneity of treatment effects
- **Synonyms for effect modifier:** Subgrouping variable; Predictor of treatment response; Moderator

## Methods

Development of ICEMAN consisted of 4 steps: clarifying the scope and measurement concept of the instrument; a systematic literature survey to identify existing instruments and candidate credibility criteria; a consensus study among experts to identify key criteria and design the instrument; and formal user testing.

### Concept

Members of the core group (S.S., G.H.G., M.B., X.S., M.W., L.T.) began with the following concept:

- Effect modification means that the effect of an intervention on an outcome varies by levels of another variable.
- The aim of the new instrument is to assist users in assessing the credibility of claims that effect modification is present (rather than claims that effect modification is absent, which would require different criteria).
- An effect modification is credible if it is unlikely to be the result of chance or bias.
- Patient importance is not part of the credibility; an effect modification primarily defines an association between the modifier and the causal effect of the intervention on the outcome (the presence of a causal relation between the modifier and the outcome is not necessary for the claim to be valid); and effect modification can be assessed on any scale (e.g., risk ratio or risk difference).
- Target users include health care providers, trial investigators, systematic review authors, health technology assessment practitioners, journal editors, guideline developers and health policy-makers.
- The instrument addresses individual RCTs and meta-analyses of RCTs (including aggregate data and individual participant data meta-analyses).
- The core instrument consists of no more than 8–12 questions and provides explicit response options for each question.
- Responses to individual criteria should vary when applied to different claims.
- The instrument should conclude with a summary assessment expressing overall credibility.

### Systematic literature survey

The objectives (presented in a separate publication) were to identify existing instruments, candidate credibility criteria and leading experts. Based on a comprehensive search of journal articles and textbooks, the authors identified **150 eligible publications, from which they abstracted 36 candidate criteria, 30 previous sets of criteria (none reflected the concept sufficiently) and 40 experts.**

### Consensus study

The steering committee randomized the order of the 40 experts and invited the first 18, of whom **9 agreed, 6 declined, and 3 did not respond.** The final group included **15 members (the core group and 9 experts: R.V., C.H.S., R.A.H., J.G., M.B., G.J.M.G.V., I.J.D., W.S. and J.P.A.I.).** The consensus study included elements of the Delphi method complemented by interactive video conferences.

Members (excluding S.S.) independently rated the importance of each of the 36 candidate criteria from 1 (not important at all) to 7 (highly important). During the first video conference, the group identified **20 criteria that should be included** (some later combined), **8 that should be excluded** and **8 that were considered optional.**

Where possible, the authors used a format similar to that of other research assessment instruments such as the Cochrane risk-of-bias tool and GRADE. A second video conference reached consensus on structure. Because a single version proved excessively complex, the group decided to create separate versions for RCTs and meta-analyses (of any type). A detailed manual was created providing, for each item, a justification of importance, and for each response option a supporting example from the medical literature.

### User testing

Each user received the full text of an RCT or meta-analysis with an authors' claim of effect modification, plus drafts of ICEMAN and the manual. **17 claims were selected to introduce variation across the range of possible credibility (4 very low, 5 low, 4 moderate and 4 high) and across designs (9 RCTs, 4 aggregate data meta-analyses and 4 meta-analyses of individual participant data).**

**17 potential users were recruited from 3 sources: corresponding authors of Cochrane reviews (n = 7), authors of published RCTs (n = 5) and journal editors from personal networks (n = 5).** Interviews were conducted immediately after application using a semistructured guide; interviews lasted **25–70 (median 37) minutes** and were video-recorded, then analyzed with Dedoose software. The instrument was updated after 7, 12 and 15 interviews.

**Ethics approval:** The Hamilton Integrated Research Ethics Board approved the user-testing study.

## Results

The version of ICEMAN for individual RCTs is presented in Appendix 5 and that for meta-analyses of RCTs in Appendix 6. The material can also be downloaded from https://www.iceman.help.

Both versions start with a set of preliminary considerations that link ICEMAN to a specific study, specify the effect modification claim, and alert users that ICEMAN may not apply to effect modifiers measured after randomization.

**The version for RCTs includes 5 core questions and that for meta-analyses 8 core questions (4 questions overlap).** For each core question, ICEMAN provides 4 response options that differ in wording but have the same order and logic: response options on the left indicate definitely or probably reduced credibility, and those on the right indicate probably or definitely increased credibility. The response option "probably no" is included with "unclear" to cover situations with insufficient information.

One optional question allows additional considerations (e.g., results from sensitivity analyses, a dose–response relation).

The instrument concludes with an overall credibility assessment rated on a **visual analogue scale divided into 4 areas (very low, low, moderate and high credibility). The 4 areas correspond roughly to probabilities of less than 25%, 25%–50%, 51%–75% and greater than 75%, respectively, that the effect modification truly exists.** The final item provides suggestions — rather than an algorithm — for judging overall credibility.

## Interpretation

Using a systematic approach involving both experts and potential users, the authors developed ICEMAN. The instrument provides versions for individual RCTs and meta-analyses, is short (5 core items for RCTs, 8 for meta-analyses), is structured (preliminary considerations, core questions, overall rating) and provides a detailed manual.

One of the benefits of ICEMAN is that it may help reduce overreliance on the p value for interaction when assessing credibility. The p value counts no more than other items. Instead of a single threshold, the response options are based on **3 thresholds** (interaction p value thresholds of **0.05, 0.01 and 0.005**), emphasizing the continuous character of the concept. Claims of effect modification can be reasonably credible despite borderline p values, whereas claims based exclusively on very low p values may have low credibility.

### Limitations

- Formulating 4 response options required specification of arbitrary threshold values for the number of studies in a meta-analysis, p values, and number of candidate effect modifiers. Experts initially disagreed on specific thresholds; interaction p value thresholds were particularly controversial, but the group reached a compromise (thresholds of 0.05, 0.01 and 0.005).
- The core questions may not include all credibility considerations relevant to complex analyses (e.g., modelling of continuous effect modifiers, data-driven algorithms for subgroup discovery, 3-way or 4-way interactions, correction for exaggerated magnitude, correlation structure between multiple modifiers). Even so, ICEMAN provides a useful starting point and its optional item can incorporate additional considerations.
- Some properties remain uncertain. The authors plan to investigate reliability (different raters) and validity of ICEMAN ratings. Independent replication as a reference standard is limited because attempts to replicate effect modification findings in RCTs are extremely rare. Users are invited to share ratings to build a database of more or less credible claims.

### Conclusion

ICEMAN provides a systematically developed and thoroughly user-tested instrument for judging the credibility of apparent effect modification. Investigators and readers of RCTs and meta-analyses, and other groups including journal editors, are expected to find the structured assessment of credibility helpful.
