# Efficacy and Safety of Finerenone in Asian Patients with Type 2 Diabetes and Chronic Kidney Disease: A FIDELITY Analysis

**Authors:** Takashi Wada, Stefan D. Anker, Zhihong Liu, Byung Wan Lee, Chien-Te Lee, Peter Rossing, Luis M. Ruilope, Christiane Ahlers, Meike Brinker, Amaninder Mann, Satoshi Yamashita, Bertram Pitt (FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD Investigators)

**Journal:** Kidney Diseases 2025;11:402-415
**DOI:** 10.1159/000545415
**PMID:** 40551874
Received: July 11, 2024 | Accepted: March 4, 2025 | Published online: May 22, 2025

**Keywords:** Finerenone, Chronic kidney disease, Type 2 diabetes, Asian, Cardiovascular and kidney outcomes

## Abstract

**Introduction:** In FIDELITY, a prespecified pooled analysis of the phase III FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials, finerenone reduced the risk of cardiovascular (CV) and kidney events versus placebo in patients with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease, on optimized renin-angiotensin system blockade. This FIDELITY post hoc subanalysis explores the efficacy and safety of finerenone in Asian patients.

**Methods:** For this subanalysis, efficacy outcomes included a CV composite (time to CV death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure) and kidney composite (kidney failure, sustained ≥57% estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] decrease from baseline over ≥4 weeks or renal death) outcome. A change in urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) from baseline to month 4 and eGFR slopes was also assessed. All outcomes were assessed by baseline eGFR (<60 and ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m2) and UACR (<300 and ≥300 mg/g) subgroups. Safety outcomes were reported as treatment-emergent adverse events, including laboratory evaluations for hyperkalemia.

**Results:** In the Asian subpopulation, 1,412/2,858 (49.4%) received finerenone. Finerenone-treated Asian patients had a lower risk of the composite CV outcome (hazard ratio [HR] = 0.90; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.70–1.15) and nominally significant reductions in the risk of ≥57% and ≥40% eGFR composite kidney outcomes (HR = 0.64; 95% CI, 0.50–0.82 and HR = 0.67; 95% CI, 0.56–0.80, respectively) versus those receiving placebo, irrespective of baseline eGFR and UACR. Data on change of eGFR from baseline over the course of the trials indicated that chronic kidney disease progression in Asian patients was slower with finerenone versus placebo. Overall, safety outcomes were balanced between both populations. Serum potassium values with finerenone were similar between the Asian and non-Asian subpopulations (>5.5 mmol/L: 15.6% versus 17.1%; >6.0 mmol/L: 4.6% versus 2.9%, respectively), while hyperkalemia leading to permanent treatment discontinuation with finerenone was low in both populations (Asian: 1.5%; non-Asian: 1.8%).

**Conclusion:** Finerenone reduced the risk of CV and kidney events and demonstrated a well-tolerated safety profile in the FIDELITY Asian subpopulation.

## Plain Language Summary

Type 2 diabetes, a condition in which there is too much glucose in the blood due to a problem with the hormone insulin, is a well-known cause of chronic kidney disease. People with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes are also more likely to experience a heart event, such as a heart attack or stroke. Adults from Asia are more likely to have heart disease and more severe kidney disease compared with White adults. This study of two recent clinical trials, called the FIDELITY project, looked at how well the drug finerenone worked in about 3,000 participants from Asia. Finerenone was shown to slow down the rate of kidney function decline and reduce protein in the urine, as well as to lower the risk of having or dying from a heart event in Asian adults with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes, as it does in non-Asian adults. A side effect of drugs like finerenone is an increase in potassium levels in the blood (hyperkalemia), which in serious cases can lead to changes in heart rate and rhythm. In this study, hyperkalemia was mostly nonserious and easy for doctors to identify and manage.

## Introduction

Asian patients account for over 50% of the global diabetic population, with numbers expected to surpass 355 million by 2040 [1]. Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is a known cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD), affecting approximately 40% of patients with T2D [2]. Asian patients with T2D were found to have a higher prevalence of CKD and more rapid kidney function deterioration versus Caucasians [3, 4]. Compared with Caucasians, Asian patients present with higher levels of albuminuria at diagnosis and are 1.4 times more likely to develop end-stage kidney disease [5, 6]. Based on registry data, Asian patients with both heart failure (HF) and CKD have a two-fold higher risk of mortality or hospitalization for HF [7]. Asian patients with diabetes, CKD, and obesity have an increased risk for adverse outcomes at a lower body mass index (BMI) threshold versus non-Asian patients (BMI >27.5 kg versus BMI >30 kg/m2) [8, 9]. In addition, Asian patients are predisposed to diabetes at a younger age, characterized by early beta-cell dysfunction in the setting of insulin resistance [10–12].

The pathophysiology of both CKD and cardiovascular (CV) disease is associated with mineralocorticoid receptor overactivation [13–15]. Finerenone is approved in Europe and the USA for the treatment of adult patients with CKD associated with T2D [16, 17]. This indication was extended to the Japanese, Chinese, Indian, Thai, Taiwanese, Korean, and Singaporean markets [18–23]. Finerenone reduced the risk of CV and kidney events, with a manageable safety profile in patients with CKD and T2D in FIDELITY [24–26]. This post hoc subanalysis aims to assess the CV and kidney benefits of finerenone observed in the FIDELITY Asian patient subpopulation in comparison with the non-Asian subpopulation.

## Methods

### Study Design and Patients
This subanalysis combines individual patient-level data from the FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993) and FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049) phase III randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter clinical trials. Briefly, adult patients (aged ≥18 years) with CKD (UACR 30–<300 mg/g and eGFR 25−≤90 mL/min/1.73 m2, or UACR 300–≤5,000 mg/g and eGFR ≥25 mL/min/1.73 m2) and T2D, and on optimized renin-angiotensin system (RAS) blockade were eligible if they had a serum potassium level ≤4.8 mmol/L at screening and were without symptomatic chronic HF with reduced ejection fraction. Patients self-identified their race. Patients permitted to identify as multiple races were included as non-Asian. This analysis included patients who self-identified as Asian randomized from sites in Asian and non-Asian countries and territories.

### Randomization and Masking
Patients were randomized 1:1 to once daily oral finerenone (10 or 20 mg) or matching placebo. Randomization was stratified by study, region, albuminuria at screening, eGFR at screening, and history of CV disease.

### Outcomes
Efficacy outcomes: (1) key composite CV outcome (CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for HF); (2) key ≥57% eGFR decline composite kidney outcome; (3) ≥40% eGFR decline composite kidney outcome; (4) change in UACR from baseline to month 4; (5) eGFR slopes. Safety outcomes (treatment-emergent AEs): AKI, investigator-reported hyperkalemia, laboratory hyperkalemia (>5.5 and >6.0 mmol/L), treatment discontinuation, and hospitalization for AKI and hyperkalemia. All outcomes assessed by baseline eGFR (<60 and ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m2) and UACR (<300 and ≥300 mg/g) subgroups.

### Statistical Analysis
Patient demographics and efficacy on the full analysis set (12,990 randomized patients). Safety on the safety analysis set (12,963 patients who took ≥1 dose). Treatment effects for time-to-event outcomes expressed as HR with 95% CIs from stratified Cox proportional hazards models. Analyses performed with SAS software version 9.4.

## Results

### Patients
Of 12,990 patients, 2,858 (22.0%) self-identified as Asian. Of these, 2,637 (92.3%) were from sites in Asia and 221 (7.7%) from the rest of the world. In the Asian subpopulation, 1,412 (49.4%) received finerenone and 1,446 (50.6%) received placebo. Median follow-up was 3.0 years for the overall FIDELITY population (IQR 2.3–3.8 years).

### Baseline Characteristics (key differences)
Asian patients had more advanced CKD at baseline: 2,056 (71.9%) had severe albuminuric CKD (UACR ≥300 mg/g) versus 6,618 (65.3%) non-Asian, despite similar mean baseline eGFR. Asian patients were more likely current smokers, had diabetic retinopathy, lower mean BMI (27.2 vs 32.5 kg/m2), smaller waist circumference. Lower proportion of history of CV disease (34.9% vs 48.7%), HF (2.7% vs 9.2%), atrial fibrillation (3.1% vs 10.0%). Lower mean hs-CRP (1.0 vs 2.7 mg/L). ARBs more commonly used in Asian patients (82.9% vs 54.6%); ACEis more common in non-Asian. Asian patients less likely on diuretics, potassium supplements, beta blockers; greater use of DPP-4 inhibitors, sulfonylureas, alpha-glucosidase inhibitors. Mean daily finerenone dose 17.1 (Asian) vs 16.3 mg (non-Asian); mean treatment duration 33.2 vs 31.1 months.

### Composite CV Outcomes
In the Asian subpopulation, the composite CV outcome occurred in 121/1,412 (8.6%) finerenone vs 138/1,446 (9.5%) placebo. No statistical heterogeneity in treatment effect: Asian HR = 0.90 (95% CI, 0.70–1.15) vs non-Asian HR = 0.85 (95% CI, 0.77–0.94); p-interaction = 0.8454. Non-Asian event rates: finerenone 13.8%, placebo 15.9%.

### Kidney Outcomes
≥57% eGFR decline composite kidney outcome: Asian 105/1,412 (7.4%) finerenone vs 170/1,446 (11.8%) placebo. Greater relative risk reduction in Asian (HR = 0.64; 95% CI, 0.50–0.82) vs non-Asian (HR = 0.85; 95% CI, 0.71–1.00); p-interaction = 0.0493, irrespective of baseline eGFR and UACR.

≥40% eGFR decline composite kidney outcome: Asian 217/1,412 (15.4%) finerenone vs 318/1,446 (22.0%) placebo. Greater effect in Asian (HR = 0.67; 95% CI, 0.56–0.80) vs non-Asian (HR = 0.93; 95% CI, 0.84–1.04); p-interaction = 0.0009.

**UACR:** Finerenone reduced UACR from baseline to month 4 vs placebo in both subpopulations. Asian: 33% reduction (LS mean treatment ratio 0.67; 95% CI, 0.64–0.70; p < 0.0001). Non-Asian: 32% reduction (LS mean treatment ratio 0.68; 95% CI, 0.66–0.71; p < 0.0001).

**eGFR Decline (chronic slope):** Asian finerenone −2.60 vs placebo −3.67 (LS mean difference 1.08; 95% CI, 0.53–1.63; p = 0.0002). Non-Asian finerenone −2.46 vs placebo −3.71 (LS mean difference 1.25; 95% CI, 0.81–1.69; p < 0.0001).

### Safety Outcomes
**Overall:** Any AE higher in Asian patients regardless of treatment arm. AEs leading to discontinuation low and balanced (Asian: 5.9% finerenone vs 5.7% placebo; non-Asian: 6.5% vs 5.3%). Fewer serious AEs with finerenone in both subpopulations. Finerenone reduced mean SBP vs placebo (Asian: −3.9 vs 0.5 mm Hg; non-Asian: −3.0 vs 0.4 mm Hg).

**Kidney safety (AKI):** Low and similar. AKI leading to permanent discontinuation (finerenone vs placebo): Asian 0.0% vs 0.2%; non-Asian 0.3% vs 0.1%. AKI leading to hospitalization: Asian 1.0% vs 1.2%; non-Asian 1.4% vs 1.4%.

**Hyperkalemia:** Investigator-reported hyperkalemia more frequent in Asian for both treatment arms. Laboratory serum potassium similar between subpopulations. Hyperkalemia AEs leading to permanent discontinuation (finerenone vs placebo): Asian 1.5% vs 0.6%; non-Asian 1.8% vs 0.6%. Hyperkalemia leading to hospitalization: Asian 1.1% vs <0.1%; non-Asian 0.9% vs 0.2%. Serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L (finerenone): Asian 15.6%, non-Asian 17.1%. >6.0 mmol/L (finerenone): Asian 4.6%, non-Asian 2.9%.

## Discussion (key points)

Finerenone reduced CV and kidney outcomes versus placebo in Asian patients with CKD and T2D, irrespective of eGFR and UACR levels at baseline; consistent with the non-Asian subpopulation and the overall FIDELITY population [25], and extends findings from the FIDELIO-DKD Asian post hoc analysis [27]. The wide CI crossing unity for the Asian composite CV outcome (HR = 0.90; 95% CI, 0.70–1.15) reflects lower CV event numbers (fewer Asian patients had history of HF, atrial fibrillation, coronary artery disease). The p-interaction (0.8454) indicates no significant heterogeneity.

Finerenone had greater magnitude of effect on composite kidney outcomes in Asian patients with severely vs moderately increased albuminuria. Higher incidence rates of kidney outcomes in finerenone-treated Asian vs non-Asian patients (≥57%: 7.4% vs 4.9%; ≥40%: 15.4% vs 12.4%). Asian patients appear to have faster CKD progression; genetic factors implicated (ACE insertion/deletion polymorphisms, ALDH2 polymorphisms, rs671 SNP). Higher investigator-reported hyperkalemia in Asians may reflect overreporting; laboratory serum potassium was similar across subpopulations. Diuretic use markedly lower in Asian patients. Finerenone has lower risk of hyperkalemia and worsening kidney function vs spironolactone (ARTS study) [42].

**Limitations:** CKD-EPI equation mainly derived/validated in Western populations; a different coefficient used for Japanese patients; question of ethnicity coefficient (FIND-CKD trial upcoming). Small Asian sample vs >10,000 non-Asian (wider CIs). Primarily Eastern Asian patients — not a true representation of full Asian population. Possible unknown confounders (ethnicity as surrogate for diet/lifestyle/social/environmental factors).

## Conclusion

The effect of finerenone versus placebo on reducing composite CV and kidney outcomes in Asian patients with T2D and different stages of CKD, irrespective of baseline eGFR and UACR, was similar to that observed in the non-Asian subpopulation. The safety profile of finerenone was manageable in both subpopulations, consistent with the overall FIDELITY population.
