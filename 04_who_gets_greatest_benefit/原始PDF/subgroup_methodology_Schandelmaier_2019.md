# A systematic survey identified 36 criteria for assessing effect modification claims in randomized trials or meta-analyses

**Journal of Clinical Epidemiology 113 (2019) 159-167** · DOI: 10.1016/j.jclinepi.2019.05.014 · PMID: 31132471

## Abstract

**Objective:** The objective of the study was to systematically survey the methodological literature and collect suggested criteria for assessing the credibility of effect modification and associated rationales.

**Study Design and Setting:** The authors searched MEDLINE, Embase, and WorldCat up to March 2018 for publications providing guidance for assessing the credibility of effect modification identified in randomized trials or meta-analyses. Teams of two investigators independently identified eligible publications and extracted credibility criteria and authors' rationale, reaching consensus through discussion. They created a taxonomy of criteria that was iteratively refined during data abstraction.

**Results:** They identified **150 eligible publications** that provided **36 criteria** and associated rationales. Frequent criteria included:
- **significant test for interaction** (n = 54)
- **a priori hypothesis** (n = 49)
- **providing a causal explanation** (n = 47)
- **accounting for multiplicity** (n = 45)
- **prespecification of analytic details** (n = 39)
- **testing a small number of effect modifiers** (n = 38)

For some criteria, more than one rationale was found; some criteria were connected through a common rationale. For some criteria, experts disagreed regarding their suitability (e.g., added value of stratified randomization; trustworthiness of biologic rationales).

**Conclusion:** Methodologists have expended substantial intellectual energy providing criteria for critical appraisal of apparent effect modification. This survey highlights popular criteria, expert agreement and disagreement, and where more work is needed, including testing criteria in practice.

**Keywords:** Epidemiologic methods (MeSH); Meta-analysis as topic (MeSH); Clinical trials as topic (MeSH); Health care evaluation mechanisms (MeSH); Precision medicine (MeSH); Subgroup analysis

## Key findings (What is new)

- The authors identified **36 criteria** for assessing the credibility of apparent effect modifiers and associated rationales.
- Key differences from previous systematic surveys include explicit definitions and eligibility criteria, a comprehensive search, rigorous methods for data collection and qualitative synthesis, and inclusion of reported rationales for suggested credibility criteria.
- The survey informs those considering making subgroup claims, or evaluating subgroup claims made by others, of the most important criteria and their rationale. Further work should determine the most useful criteria and how they should be structured and implemented to develop and review subgroup claims.

## Background / Introduction (selected)

Most large randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and many meta-analyses include analysis of effect modification (i.e., investigation of whether the effect of an intervention varies depending on patient characteristics such as age or intervention characteristics such as dose). Identification of true effect modification — often referred to as subgroup effect or interaction — is important for optimizing treatment for individual patients. Apparent effect modification may, however, be spurious, and if acted on may be to the patient's detriment.

Many methodologists have provided criteria for judging the credibility of effect modification, in particular for RCTs and meta-analyses of RCTs. Examples include presence of an a priori hypothesis, use of an interaction test, and adjustment for multiplicity. Previous groups had systematically surveyed the methodological literature; two focused on credibility criteria but had important limitations in their search strategy (identifying 18 or fewer relevant publications) and methods for data abstraction. Prior surveys also failed to systematically collect authors' rationales for their suggested criteria.

## Methods (selected)

**Eligibility criteria:** Publications were eligible if they (1) addressed effect modification (including synonyms: subgroup effect, interaction, treatment-by-covariate interaction, moderator effect, predictive factor), under any definition independent of statistical approach, effect measure, or causality; (2) reported one or more criteria for the credibility of apparent effect modification (a criterion defined as a statement linking a characteristic of an apparent effect modification with an increase or decrease in credibility; credibility defined as the extent to which an apparent effect modification represents an accurate characterization of a true underlying effect modification rather than the result of chance or bias); (3) addressed effect modification observed in RCTs or meta-analyses of RCTs; and (4) reflected the authors' own views (excluding reviews or summaries of other studies).

**Search strategy:** In collaboration with an experienced medical librarian, the team developed a search strategy for MEDLINE and Embase designed to capture 10 key publications and cover a wide range of synonyms. An adapted search of the WorldCat library identified potentially eligible textbook chapters via table-of-contents searching. The last update was performed in March 2018. Teams of two methodologically trained reviewers independently screened abstracts and assessed full texts, resolving disagreements by discussion. One reviewer (St.S.) screened reference lists of all eligible publications and other methodological surveys for additional articles.

**Data abstraction:** Using a pretested online spreadsheet, pairs of reviewers independently abstracted reported credibility criteria and rationales (why a criterion would increase or decrease credibility), copying author statements verbatim. To ensure consistency, St.S. was a member of all reviewer pairs. Additional recorded data included type of publication, focus on a specific study design, whether the article included a supporting simulation, and — where an explicit checklist was provided — number of items, intended audience (users interpreting an apparent effect modification vs. investigators planning an analysis), intended study design (RCT, meta-analysis, or both), presence of explicit response options, provision of an overall judgment, and whether checklist development was informed by a systematic survey, a formal consensus study, user testing, a reliability study, or other formal instrument-development methods.

**Qualitative synthesis:** In parallel with data abstraction, the authors developed a taxonomy of criteria and rationales in their own language, creating new categories as they emerged. For each criterion the taxonomy provided a keyword (e.g., "a priori hypothesis") and a collection of common terms conveying the same idea (e.g., pre hoc, post hoc, exploratory, confirmatory). Reviewers used the taxonomy to organize extracted quotations.

## Conclusion

Methodologists have expended substantial intellectual energy providing criteria for critical appraisal of apparent effect modification. The survey highlights popular criteria, areas of expert agreement and disagreement, and where more work is needed — including testing criteria in practice. It supports downstream work (such as the ICEMAN instrument) to structure and implement credibility criteria for developing and reviewing subgroup claims.
