## ABSTRACT

Chronic kidney disease continues to be a significant burden for people living with type 2 diabetes, despite the available guideline-directed treatment options. Traditionally, a stepwise approach has been implemented for the management of chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes, which involves the linear sequential initiation of one therapy after the other on the basis of an individual's treatment outcomes. However, this approach is not beneficial for all individuals, as it can lead to treatment inertia and subsequent disease progression. Therefore, primary care practitioners should consider implementing a more proactive treatment strategy to optimize care. The pillar risk-based approach is an emerging concept with goals of glucose control and blood pressure control as well as comprising simultaneous or rapid sequential initiation of multiple therapies, such as renin–angiotensin system inhibitors (RASi), sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (finerenone), and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, which target the different hemodynamic, metabolic, and fibrotic/inflammatory pathways involved in chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes. This approach enables earlier chronic kidney disease risk reduction, and the recently published CONFIDENCE trial reported tolerability and efficacy of simultaneous initiation of two of these therapies (finerenone and empagliflozin) in those already receiving RASi. This review article provides primary care practitioners with practical considerations, discussing current guideline-directed treatment options for chronic kidney disease in people with type 2 diabetes in the context of a historical stepwise approach versus the new patient-centric pillar risk-based approach.

**Keywords:** Chronic kidney disease; Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist; Guideline-directed treatment; Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist; Renin–angiotensin system inhibitor; Sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor; Type 2 diabetes

## PLAIN LANGUAGE SUMMARY

Chronic kidney disease is a major complication for people with type 2 diabetes. Treatments for people with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease has traditionally followed a stepwise approach, wherein therapies are introduced sequentially, one by one, on the basis of how the person responds to treatment. This stepwise approach to treatment is slow and may take months or years for a person with chronic kidney disease to be on maximal therapy, thus losing crucial time and nephrons. We discuss a potentially different approach to treatment (the pillar risk-based approach) that offers a more proactive strategy and involves starting multiple therapies early and simultaneously or in a rapid stepwise fashion. This pillar risk-based approach integrates blood glucose control and blood pressure control alongside medications that slow progression of chronic kidney disease. Key treatments include renin–angiotensin system inhibitors, sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists. By addressing multiple disease mechanisms at the same time, the pillar risk-based approach to treatment aims to reduce the risk for kidney failure requiring dialysis or kidney transplantation, as well as cardiovascular events and death.

### Key Summary Points
- Chronic kidney disease is common in people with type 2 diabetes and can lead to kidney failure, cardiovascular events, and death.
- Typically, a stepwise approach to treatment has been used to initiate different therapies sequentially, but this approach can lead to treatment inertia and disease progression.
- A pillar risk-based approach is emerging with the aim of initiating multiple therapies simultaneously to enable earlier chronic kidney disease risk reduction by targeting the different hemodynamic, metabolic, and fibrotic/inflammatory pathways involved in chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes.
- The four pillars of the pillar risk-based approach are renin–angiotensin system inhibitors, sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists.
- This review article discusses current guideline-directed treatment options for chronic kidney disease in people with type 2 diabetes in the context of a historical stepwise approach versus a patient-centric pillar risk-based approach.

## INTRODUCTION

The global prevalence of type 2 diabetes (T2D) is increasing, with chronic kidney disease (CKD) often presenting as a complication [1, 2]. CKD affected over 840 million people in 2017 and is expected to be the fifth leading cause of life-years lost in 2040 [3–5]. Approximately up to 50% of all people with T2D develop CKD, with a greater chance of development in those over 65 years of age [6, 7]. Older individuals with CKD have an elevated risk of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality compared with age-matched individuals without CKD [8]. CKD has been identified as the primary mediating factor of the increased risk for mortality in people with T2D [9]. There is a need for primary care practitioners to efficiently diagnose CKD and implement effective treatment strategies to reduce the risk of kidney failure and cardiovascular complications in individuals with T2D and CKD.

There are two main markers of kidney disease that should be monitored at least annually in people at risk of, or diagnosed with, CKD: estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) [10, 11]. eGFR is calculated from serum creatinine concentration, measured in the laboratory using an enzymatic method in a venous blood sample [10]. UACR is measured in a spot urine sample, preferably collected from the first morning void. UACR (i.e., urine albumin [mg]/urine creatinine [g]) is calculated from laboratory-measured urine albumin and creatinine concentrations (preferred) or can be estimated directly using dipstick-based measurement with automated reading [10].

Once an abnormal eGFR or UACR is identified, repeat testing is recommended 3 months later by Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO), an international clinical practice guidelines working group [10]. eGFR is a marker of kidney function, whereas UACR is a marker of kidney damage, and testing these together allows one to characterize and prognosticate CKD and its risk for progression, as well as cardiovascular events and death, as illustrated in the KDIGO heatmap (Fig. 1) [10]. In CKD, UACR may be elevated before eGFR declines to levels associated with impaired kidney function, and should be recognized as a measure that is as important as blood pressure or low-density lipoprotein cholesterol to trigger treatments for reducing cardiovascular and kidney risks [12, 13]. Despite recommendations, UACR testing is widely underutilized in clinical practice, especially compared with eGFR testing [10, 14, 15]. The lack of UACR testing was also noted by The Center for Kidney Disease Research, Education, and Hope Registry, which showed that of over 2.6 million people with T2D at risk of CKD, only 8.7% of adults were tested for UACR (n/N = 52,511/606,064), likely resulting in a significant underestimation of diagnosed CKD cases and complete lack of prognostication [2].

In addition to the KDIGO heatmap, the Kidney Failure Risk Equation is a validated tool used by clinicians in over 30 countries and recommended by the 2024 KDIGO guideline to provide a 2- and 5-year-probability of kidney failure in individuals with advanced CKD (G stages 3–5) by using the individual's UACR, eGFR, sex, and age [10, 16]. Physicians should be aware of this tool as it has demonstrated excellent discrimination in individuals with advanced CKD who are likely to progress to kidney failure at the 2- and 5-year time points and across CKD etiologies (link: https://kidneyfailurerisk.co.uk/) [17].

This review provides clinical considerations for practitioners regarding the current guideline-directed treatment options for the management of CKD in people with T2D and evaluates the historical stepwise approach versus the new pillar risk-based approach. This review is based on previously conducted studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors.

## TREATMENT STRATEGIES FOR CKD IN PEOPLE WITH T2D

Multiple treatment strategies have been shown to reduce the development and progression of CKD in people living with T2D [10, 11]. The most common goal of intervention is to slow progression of CKD, but sometimes with early intervention in mild CKD, UACR and eGFR may revert to normal levels [18–20].

### Lifestyle

A healthy diet with appropriate nutrition, adequate physical activity, smoking cessation, and body weight management can reduce both kidney and cardiovascular disease outcomes, especially when combined with appropriate drug therapies [10, 11]. The KDIGO guideline recommends that people with CKD should undertake moderate physical activity for a cumulative duration of at least 150 min per week or as tolerated and consume more plant-based foods versus animal-based foods, maintaining a protein intake of 0.8 g/kg body weight per day for adults with CKD stages G3 to G5 [10]. The KDIGO guideline also recommends a sodium intake of < 2 g per day (or < 90 mmol of sodium per day or < 5 g of sodium chloride per day) for people with CKD [10].

### Blood Glucose Control

Glycemic control is one of the most important strategies for kidney disease risk reduction in individuals with diabetes, as the importance of achieving glycemic targets to reduce the development and progression of CKD in people with T2D has been demonstrated in multiple studies [21–26]. The UK Prospective Diabetes Study (N = 1148) and the Veterans Affairs Diabetes trial (N = 1791; no. NCT00032487) in people with T2D showed fewer adverse kidney events in the intensively treated arms that achieved a glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) level of approximately 7% compared with those who had HbA1c levels averaging 8.4% [21, 22]. In the ADVANCE study (no. NCT00145925) of 11,140 people with T2D, those who achieved an HbA1c of < 6.5% had a 21% relative reduction in the progression of nephropathy compared with those who maintained standard glucose control, with an average HbA1c of 7.3% [23]. In addition, in the ACCORD study (no. NCT00000620) of 10,251 people with T2D, the intensive glycemia arm targeted HbA1c levels < 6.0% and demonstrated a reduction in the development of moderately increased albuminuria compared with the standard treatment group (target HbA1c of 7.0–7.9%) [24, 25]. However, it should be noted that an unexpected increase in mortality was observed in the ACCORD study intensive group, which is inconsistent with other studies of intensive glycemic control versus standard therapy [24, 25]. The 2025 American Diabetes Association recommends an HbA1c goal of < 7% for many nonpregnant people without significant hypoglycemia living with T2D, irrespective of CKD status [27].

### Blood Pressure Control

Hypertension is a strong risk factor for the development and progression of CKD [10]. Among all people with diabetes, a blood pressure of < 130/80 mmHg is recommended to reduce cardiovascular disease risk and development/progression of CKD [28]. The 2024 KDIGO guideline suggests that a lower systolic blood pressure target of < 120 mmHg may be targeted in those with hypertension and CKD; however, blood pressure targets should be individualized on the basis of frailty, risk of falls and fractures, and life expectancy [10].

### Renin–Angiotensin System Inhibitors (RASis)

RASis, such as angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEis) or angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs), are recommended to treat people with CKD and moderately to severely increased albuminuria (UACR ≥ 30 mg/g or ≥ 3 mg/mmol) with diabetes irrespective of the presence or absence of hypertension [10]. A recent meta-analysis including 1739 people with moderate-to-severe albuminuria from 18 trials found that ACEis or ARBs reduced the risk of kidney failure requiring renal replacement therapy compared with placebo or other antihypertensive therapies in people with CKD (adjusted hazard ratio [HR] 0.66; 95% confidence interval [CI] 0.55–0.79; P < 0.001) [29]. The overall mean systolic blood pressure was similar for individuals in the RASi and comparator groups [29]. The specific RASis irbesartan and losartan have demonstrated significant kidney benefit compared with placebo in people with T2D, severe albuminuria, and hypertension [30, 31].

### Sodium–Glucose Cotransporter 2 Inhibitors (SGLT2is)

SGLT2is show major kidney protective effects and are recommended to treat people with T2D and/or CKD and/or heart failure [10]. The Nuffield Department of Population Health Renal Studies Group and SMART (SGLT2 Inhibitor Meta-Analysis Cardio–Renal Trialists) Consortium meta-analysis of 13 trials including 90,409 participants (82.7% with diabetes [> 99% T2D]) showed that SGLT2is reduced the risk of kidney disease progression (defined as kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplantation, a ≥ 50% reduction in eGFR, a sustained low eGFR [< 15 mL/min/1.73 m² or < 10 mL/min/1.73 m²], or death from kidney failure) by 37% (relative risk [RR] 0.63; 95% CI 0.58–0.69) compared with placebo [32]. In addition to kidney benefits, SGLT2is reduced the risk of the composite of cardiovascular death or hospitalization for heart failure (RR 0.77; 95% CI 0.74–0.81), as well as cardiovascular death alone (RR 0.86; 95% CI 0.81–0.92) compared with placebo [32]. There was a consistent treatment effect on reducing kidney disease progression across CKD etiologies and the degree of albuminuria [32]. In the setting of diabetes, even people without albuminuria have demonstrated cardiovascular and kidney benefits with SGLT2is [33]; SGLT2is appear to be the only drug class to date with which such benefits are reported in people without albuminuria.

### Nonsteroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRAs)

Finerenone, a nonsteroidal MRA, reduces kidney disease progression in people with CKD and T2D [34]. In the recently published FINE-HEART prespecified participant-level pooled analysis (N = 18,991) of the FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993) [35], FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049) [36], and FINEARTS-HF (NCT04435626) [37] trials in participants with heart failure and CKD with T2D, finerenone significantly reduced all-cause mortality (HR, 0.91; 95% CI 0.84–0.99; P = 0.03), hospitalization for heart failure (HR, 0.83; 95% CI 0.75–0.92; P < 0.001) and the composite kidney outcome (defined as a decrease in eGFR to ≥ 50% from baseline, sustained decline in eGFR to < 15 mL/min/1.73 m², kidney failure, and death due to kidney failure [HR, 0.80; 95% CI 0.72–0.90; P < 0.001]) compared with placebo [38]. Cardiovascular death occurred in 421 (4.4%) individuals receiving finerenone and 471 (5.0%) receiving placebo, but did not reach statistical significance (HR, 0.89; 95% CI 0.78–1.01; P = 0.08) [38].

The 2024 KDIGO guideline recommends the addition of finerenone to RASis and SGLT2is for the treatment of adults with T2D and CKD [10]. The CONFIDENCE study (no. NCT05254002) was a prospective, double-dummy, double-blind, randomized trial to determine the safety and albuminuria-reducing effect of simultaneous initiation of finerenone and an SGLT2i (empagliflozin) compared with either therapy alone in 818 people with T2D and albuminuric CKD [39]. After 180 days of treatment, the reduction from baseline in UACR was 52% with combination therapy compared with 32% and 29% with finerenone or empagliflozin monotherapies, respectively [39]. The reduction from baseline in UACR after 180 days of treatment with combination therapy was 29% greater than with finerenone alone (least-squares [LS] mean ratio, 0.71; 95% CI 0.61–0.82; P < 0.001) and 32% greater than with empagliflozin alone (LS mean ratio, 0.68; 95% CI 0.59–0.79; P < 0.001) [39]. The incidence of hyperkalemia (potassium > 5.5 mmol/L) was lower with combination therapy compared with finerenone alone (15.3% versus 18.6%). Acute kidney injury was very uncommon (< 2%) across all three treatment arms, and symptomatic hypotension was rare (three participants in the combination therapy group) [39]. Given that the change in the UACR ratio over a 6-month period is regarded as a reliable surrogate marker for CKD progression [40], these results suggest enhanced kidney-protective effects with combination therapy [39].

### Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 (GLP-1) Receptor Agonists

The 2024 KDIGO guideline recommends the use of a long-acting GLP-1 receptor agonist in adults with T2D and CKD who have not achieved individualized glycemic targets following treatment with metformin and an SGLT2i or who are unable to use those medications [10]. A meta-analysis demonstrated that in six randomized trials in people with T2D and high cardiovascular risk (N = 47,434), GLP-1 receptor agonists reduced the composite kidney endpoint of development of severely increased albuminuria, doubling of serum creatinine or a ≥ 40% decline in eGFR, kidney replacement therapy, and death due to kidney disease by 21% (HR, 0.79; 95% CI 0.73–0.87; P < 0.001) compared with placebo [41]. After the release of the 2024 KDIGO guideline, the only dedicated kidney outcome study with a GLP-1 receptor agonist in people with CKD and T2D on a background of maximally tolerated RASis was completed (FLOW [no. NCT03819153]). In FLOW, semaglutide reduced the risk of the primary composite kidney endpoint (defined as the initiation of long-term dialysis, kidney transplantation, or a sustained reduction in the eGFR to < 15 mL/min/1.73 m²; HR 0.79; 95% CI 0.66–0.94), cardiovascular events (HR 0.82; 95% CI 0.68–0.98; P = 0.029) [42], and cardiovascular death (HR 0.71; 95% CI 0.56–0.89) compared with placebo, further supporting the therapeutic role of GLP-1 receptor agonists in this population [11, 42].

### The Benefit of Additive Therapies

An actuarial survival analysis using pooled individual participant data from 12 large outcomes trials estimated the lifetime cardiovascular, kidney, and mortality outcomes in people with T2D and albuminuria using combination therapy (SGLT2is, GLP-1 receptor agonists, and finerenone when added to RASis) versus conventional care (RASis and traditional risk-factor control) [43]. Using these simulated data, combination therapy was estimated to reduce major adverse cardiovascular events (HR 0.65; 95% CI 0.55–0.76), with a number needed to treat of 23 (95% CI 18–33) over 3 years [43]. For a 50-year-old individual starting combination therapy instead of conventional care, the estimated major adverse cardiovascular event-free survival was increased by 3.2 years (95% CI 2.1–4.3), CKD progression-free survival was increased by 5.5 years (95% CI 4.0–6.7), and time to all-cause death was attenuated by 2.4 years (95% CI 1.4–3.4) [43]. While these estimates of risk reduction still need to be demonstrated in clinical trials and using real-world data, these results are encouraging for people with CKD and T2D.

Similarly, prespecified secondary analyses of the FLOW study in people with T2D and CKD showed that semaglutide benefits versus placebo on major cardiovascular events, kidney outcomes, and all-cause death were similar regardless of baseline SGLT2i or MRA use [44, 45]. Also, a secondary analysis of the Dapagliflozin and Prevention of Adverse Outcomes in Chronic Kidney Disease (DAPA-CKD) trial demonstrated similar benefit with dapagliflozin versus placebo on the composite primary outcome of sustained ≥ 50% eGFR decline, end-stage kidney disease, or kidney or cardiovascular death, and the kidney-specific secondary outcome of sustained ≥ 50% eGFR decline, end-stage kidney disease, or kidney death, irrespective of baseline MRA use (P-interaction 0.59 and 0.96, respectively) [46].

## GUIDELINE-DIRECTED CARE FOR THE TREATMENT OF PEOPLE WITH CKD AND T2D

### Historical Stepwise Approach

A linear or stepwise approach involving the initiation of one drug after the other on the basis of efficacy and tolerability has been the traditional approach to the pharmacologic management of CKD in people with T2D [10, 47, 48]. Advantages of this approach were simplicity, easy identification of drug-related side effects, and the potential for a lower pill burden [49]. However, this approach may delay introduction of important medications and may not be beneficial for individuals at high risk of CKD progression.

Following a diabetes diagnosis, first-line monotherapy with metformin may be initiated, followed by sequential treatment intensification targeting an HbA1c level < 7.0% [50, 51]. In people with T2D and CKD or severely elevated HbA1c, the American Diabetes Association recommends simultaneous initiation of metformin and an SGLT2i [50]. Individuals with an elevated HbA1c (> 8.0%) may require many months or years to achieve target glycemic levels and will be at risk of ensuing complications because any duration of glycemic control improves long-term clinical outcomes, particularly if sustained control is implemented early [52, 53]. In addition, this approach can encourage a cycle of treatment failure from delayed treatment advancement (therapeutic inertia) [51]. A study of 5239 people with newly diagnosed T2D treated with metformin found that the median time until the next intervention was approximately 14 months, with 38% of people with an HbA1c level > 7%, 31% with an HbA1c level > 7.5%, and 28% with an HbA1c level > 8% not receiving an intervention within 6 months of an elevated HbA1c level [51, 54].

The order in which sequential therapies are added in a linear fashion for treatment of CKD in T2D is based on the order in which beneficial evidence was generated, which may not provide optimal patient care. First, glucose-lowering therapy is sequentially intensified until adequate glycemic control is achieved. A RASi is then initiated in people with T2D with elevated albuminuria, with sequential intensification targeting the maximal tolerated dose. Albuminuria levels are then re-evaluated, and an SGLT2i may be initiated. This can then be followed by the addition of the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone and potentially followed by the addition of a GLP-1 receptor agonist. Therefore, individuals may only receive the recommended guideline-directed standard of care following a prolonged period after their initial diagnosis of CKD in T2D. This "wait-and-see" approach can create uncertainty for clinicians and patients alike when deciding the next steps of an individual's treatment plan [51]. CKD associated with T2D can rapidly progress in some individuals, so the time over which therapies are incorporated should be minimized [49]. In addition, the historical order of adding one treatment after another does not take into consideration the high early cardiovascular risk for people with CKD and T2D [55], which is not an optimal treatment approach and indicates that change is necessary.

### The New Pillar Risk-Based Approach

A new era of detection, diagnosis, and intervention for people with CKD and T2D involves the pillar risk-based approach, which is based on the concept that a single pillar (a single treatment) is not strong enough to support the whole structure (disease management); therefore, multiple pillars are required for an optimal foundation. In other words, multiple strategies and therapies used together, such as glucose control, blood pressure control, RASis, SGLT2is, a nonsteroidal MRA (finerenone), and GLP-1 receptor agonists, can improve kidney disease management, as each treatment has independent effects on hemodynamic, metabolic, inflammatory, and fibrotic drivers of CKD in T2D [55].

The pillars approach has already been proposed for treatment of T2D, with a goal of reducing comorbid complications including CKD [28]. The simultaneous initiation of a GLP-1 receptor agonist with an SGLT2i has a proven benefit in controlling glycemia, blood pressure, and body weight versus either agent alone in individuals with T2D on a background of metformin (DURATION-8 trial [no. NCT02229396]; N = 695) [56]. The CONFIDENCE study has demonstrated the safety and additive benefit of finerenone and an SGLT2i, which can be initiated together to further slow kidney disease progression and provide long-term benefits for people with CKD and T2D on maximally tolerated RASis [39]. Moreover, in CONFIDENCE, 23% of participants received a GLP-1 receptor agonist at baseline [39].

There are six important pillars for guideline-directed and pharmacologic medical care of CKD and T2D, two of which are target-based and four of which are pharmacologic: glucose control, blood pressure control, RASis, SGLT2is, a nonsteroidal MRA (finerenone), and GLP-1 receptor agonists (Fig. 2). The concept is to implement these therapies in a timely fashion, perhaps borrowing from the heart failure goal-directed medical therapy approach to use low doses of the various treatments to allow multiple agents to be simultaneously initiated or, at a minimum, in rapid sequence.

Lifestyle modifications support the pillar foundations for treatment of CKD in T2D [13]. A healthy diet, increased physical activity, body weight management, and smoking cessation should be encouraged to the patient and reassessed every 3–6 months [10, 11].

To help decide how and for whom to implement the pillar risk-based approach, risk-assessment tools incorporating UACR, eGFR, and the KDIGO guideline heatmap can be used to direct a patient-centric, individualized, and accelerated treatment plan. Combination therapy can be initiated in individuals who are most likely to benefit from rapid initiation and intensification of treatment. In addition to results from CONFIDENCE [39], further evidence comes from the STRONG-HF trial (N = 1078; no. NCT03412201), which showed that rapid up-titration of treatments reduced readmission and risk of death for people with heart failure compared with standard care (risk ratio 0.66; 95% CI 0.50–0.86) [57].

Although this pillar risk-based approach has the important potential advantages of improved risk reduction and the avoidance of therapeutic inertia, one must be aware of possible disadvantages, which include greater treatment cost and burden due to simultaneous side effects [49]. ### Practical Considerations / Frequently Asked Questions

Current guidelines on the treatment of CKD in T2D agree on the need for a patient-centric approach and the timely initiation and escalation of therapy to achieve individualized targets [10, 11, 47, 58]. Practical considerations are provided below using a set of scenarios that primary care practitioners may encounter in their practice. Figure 4 also provides a guide for practitioners to consider when initiating the different treatments for optimal management of CKD in T2D.

**Fig. 4** When to initiate treatment for optimal management of CKD in T2D (mapped onto the KDIGO heatmap):
- **When to initiate a RASi:** Initiate in people with CKD and T2D and with moderate-to-severely increased albuminuria (eGFR categories G1 to G4 and UACR categories A2 to A3).
- **When to initiate an SGLT2i:** Initiate in people with T2D, CKD, and an eGFR of ≥ 20 mL/min/1.73 m².
- **When to initiate a nonsteroidal MRA (finerenone):** Initiate in people with T2D, eGFR > 25 mL/min/1.73 m², normal serum potassium concentration, and albuminuria (> 30 mg/g [> 3 mg/mmol]) despite maximum tolerated dose of a RASi.

**What Should I Do if an Individual's eGFR Decreases Shortly After Initiating a Therapy?** An acute decline in eGFR may be observed following treatment with an SGLT2i, RASi, finerenone, or GLP-1 receptor agonist. It is important to note that this drop is not related to kidney injury but rather due to the drug's mechanism of action in altering the ratio of afferent to efferent blood, which decreases intraglomerular pressure, which, in part, provides long-term kidney benefit [35, 42, 59–62]. The CONFIDENCE study also supported this finding by showing an initial decline in eGFR with combination therapy, finerenone alone, or empagliflozin alone with subsequent stabilization in all treatment groups, and the incidence of kidney injury was low [39]. However, the mechanism for the acute decline in eGFR observed with GLP-1 receptor agonists is unclear and requires further study [63]. Treatment should not be stopped as the decline has been shown to become less steep over time compared with the placebo [35, 42, 59–62]. It is noteworthy that the SGLT2is used in the clinical trials were not required to be stopped if kidney function dropped to less than the eGFR inclusion criterion [64–66]. If an acute marked eGFR decline occurs, ensure there is no secondary contributor such as volume depletion, infection, or use of nephrotoxic medications [61]. If a secondary cause is identified, such as volume depletion, it should be corrected to allow the individual to remain on the necessary pillars of care for CKD in T2D [65, 67–72].

**Should I Be Concerned About Serum Potassium Levels Following Initiation of a RASi or Finerenone?** RASis can be associated with hyperkalemia, which can be managed by a reduction in potassium intake, as well as prescribing diuretics, sodium bicarbonate, or potassium binders to reduce serum potassium levels rather than decreasing the RASi dose or withdrawing treatment, which should only occur if mitigation strategies are ineffective [10]. A meta-analysis comprising seven studies demonstrated an increased risk of all-cause mortality and cardiovascular events with RASi discontinuation in individuals with CKD, suggesting that if the clinical situation is appropriate, RASi treatment should be continued for maximum benefit [73]. Those treated with finerenone should have a serum potassium concentration ≤ 4.8 mmol/L at initiation, and monitoring should take place 1 month after starting. If levels rise to > 5.5 mmol/L, finerenone should be stopped, with adjustments made to diet or the addition of diuretics and/or potassium binders. Treatment may resume when potassium levels are ≤ 5.0 mmol/L [10, 69]. Considering the risk of hyperkalemia is increased with MRAs and RASis, implementing the pillar approach would be advantageous over the stepwise approach, as the addition of an SGLT2i to these therapies may decrease the occurrence of hyperkalemia events [10]. Indeed, in CONFIDENCE, the incidence of hyperkalemia was lower with finerenone and empagliflozin combination therapy than finerenone alone, indicating another advantage of simultaneous administration of these drugs [39]. Patient-and-physician-shared decision-making should be at the center of hyperkalemia management.

**Should I Expect a Reduction in an Individual's UACR Following Initiation of Appropriate Treatments?** Early changes (i.e., 6 months) in UACR have been previously accepted as a valid surrogate outcome for kidney endpoints [47, 74]. Moreover, patients may be more likely to adhere to medications if they experience an improvement in UACR with the addition of medications. UACR is highly variable between measurements and is impacted by numerous other factors (for example, glycemic and blood pressure control, diet, and physical activity); therefore, the lack of decline after initiating medication does not necessarily indicate treatment failure and thus treatment should continue. If UACR continues to climb or the eGFR falls significantly, despite using of all the treatment strategies, consider referral to a nephrologist to determine if another etiology may be present or if intensification of therapy is warranted.

**Should Treatment Be Stopped If the Individual Has a Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)?** There has been debate regarding the association between SGLT2is and UTIs for some time [75]. The prescribing information documents for SGLT2is, such as empagliflozin and dapagliflozin, list UTIs as possible adverse reactions [65, 72]. However, the UTI may resolve following temporary discontinuation, and it is the frequency of UTIs that should be estimated prior to initiation versus after initiation of SGLT2is that should be monitored. The Nuffield Department of Population Health Renal Studies Group and SMART Consortium meta-analysis showed a very small but statistically significant increase in UTIs in those receiving an SGLT2i (RR 1.08; 95% CI 1.02–1.15) [32]. Shared decision-making between the healthcare professional and patient should be implemented to decide if the drug should be permanently discontinued if symptoms persist. A large population-based cohort study of over 100,000 patients from two databases in the USA found that the risk of severe and nonsevere UTI events among those starting an SGLT2i was similar to those starting other antihyperglycemic medication [76].

### Future Directions

Although the available treatments for CKD in T2D have expanded significantly over the past decade, several unanswered questions remain for future research. The CONFIDENCE study demonstrated the superiority of finerenone and empagliflozin over either treatment alone for albuminuria reduction. However, the efficacy of other combinations (dual, triple, or quadruple) not just on albuminuria reduction but on CKD outcomes remains to be studied [39]. Comparison of various combinations at different stages of CKD, as well as a better understanding of the best sequence and speed with which to implement the pillars of CKD treatment, would be helpful. Further research in implementation science to understand systems that promote not only the initiation but also the ongoing adherence to multiple treatments would be practice-changing.

---

## CONCLUSIONS

The traditional stepwise approach is not an optimal strategy for all individuals with commonly coexisting diseases such as CKD and T2D, as it can lead to a cycle of treatment inertia and CKD progression in those with T2D. Primary care practitioners should implement newer disease management strategies using a pillar risk-based approach in individuals at risk of CKD progression. Early, combined initiation of guideline-recommended treatment strategies, such as glucose control, blood pressure control, RASis, SGLT2is, a nonsteroidal MRA (finerenone), and GLP-1 receptor agonists, should be considered. Importantly, results from CONFIDENCE provide reassurance that simultaneous initiation of finerenone and empagliflozin in people already on a RASi is well tolerated [39]. Combination therapy can target multiple disease pathways to achieve optimal patient outcomes and allow treatment advancement as required to achieve individualized targets, along with lifestyle modifications and shared decision-making between the individual and practitioner.
