# Combination therapy with mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists and SGLT2 inhibitors versus SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy in chronic kidney disease: an updated meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials

**Authors:** Shaikh Muhammad Daniyal, Hareem Ajaz, Minahil Riaz, Naveen Murad Khatoon, Zunaira Aftab, Habiba Tauqir Gondal, Isbah Gul, Mahwish Sarwar, Fizza Batool, Syeda Laiba Fahim, Amna Noor, Ayan Khalid, Danish Ali Ashraf, Romal Jabarkhil*

## Abstract

**Background** Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects millions worldwide and poses a major healthcare challenge. While both sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) or aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs) have shown individual benefits in CKD, data on their combined use remain limited. This study examined the safety and efficacy of combining an SGLT2 inhibitor with an MRA or ASI compared to SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy.

**Methods** A comprehensive search was conducted on PubMed, Cochrane (CENTRAL), ScienceDirect, and Embase for randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing combination therapy with an MRA or ASI and an SGLT2 inhibitor with SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy in patients with CKD. Continuous and dichotomous outcomes were pooled as Mean Difference (MD) or Risk Ratio (RR), respectively, with 95% Confidence Intervals (CIs) using Review Manager (v5.4.1.), and heterogeneity was assessed using the I² statistic.

**Results** Five RCTs (n = 808 patients) were included. Combination therapy significantly reduced albuminuria (MD: -32.82% [95% CI: -39.16, -26.48] %; p < 0.001), systolic blood pressure (MD: -5.02 mm Hg [95% CI: -6.95, -3.08] mm Hg; p < 0.001), and estimated glomerular filtration rate (MD: -2.59 mL/min/1.73 m² [95% CI: −3.78, − 1.40] mL/min/1.73 m²; p < 0.001). While the risk of hyperkalemia was higher (RR: 1.91 [95% CI: 1.10, 3.33]; p = 0.02) with combination therapy, no significant differences were found in serious adverse events (RR: 0.95 [95% CI: 0.57, 1.59]; p = 0.86).

**Conclusion** The combination therapy provides added renal and cardiovascular benefits in CKD without increasing serious adverse effects. These findings support its superior therapeutic potential and highlight the need for large-scale, prospective studies to assess long-term effects on hard renal and cardiovascular outcomes.

**PROSPERO registration** CRD420251106173.

**Keywords** Chronic kidney disease, Sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitors, Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, Aldosterone synthase inhibitors, Urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio

## Background

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a growing global health concern, impacting over 800 million individuals worldwide [1]. Characterized by persistent kidney structural and functional impairment, CKD is projected to become the fifth leading cause of death by 2040 [2]. Recent pharmacologic advances have significantly transformed the therapeutic landscape of CKD. Notably, sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, originally developed for glycemic control, have emerged as a cornerstone treatment for CKD owing to their ability to reduce intraglomerular pressure and mitigate glomerular hyperfiltration [3, 4]. Additionally, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) have further diversified the therapeutic options in CKD management. Finerenone, a non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (ns-MRA), is particularly useful in its ability to slow kidney disease progression and cardiovascular complications in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes (T2DM) [5]. Major clinical guidelines that endorse the use of SGLT2 inhibitors and MRAs individually, also recognize the potential therapeutic benefits of combining the two regimens. For instance, The Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) diabetes and CKD clinical practice guidelines recommend adding an ns-MRA to ongoing renin-angiotensin system inhibition and SGLT2 inhibitor therapy to reduce cardiorenal risk in patients with CKD and T2DM who have residual albuminuria [6].

While earlier studies have suggested that combining MRAs with SGLT2 inhibitors offers superior efficacy and safety compared to SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy, the available evidence remains limited [7]. To address this gap, our updated meta-analysis incorporates high-quality new evidence from the CONFIDENCE trial [8], enabling a more comprehensive and well-powered assessment of the efficacy and safety of combination therapy with MRAs and SGLT2 inhibitors in patients with CKD.

## Methods

This systematic review and meta-analysis was conducted in adherence to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines [9], and the review protocol was registered with PROSPERO (CRD420251106173).

### Search strategy
An extensive literature search was independently conducted across PubMed, Cochrane (CENTRAL), ScienceDirect, and Embase targeting randomized controlled trials (RCTs). The search strategy included relevant PubMed entry terms and Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) terms, including (heart failure OR chronic kidney disease OR CKD) AND (mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists OR spironolactone OR finerenone) OR (SGLT2 inhibitors OR empagliflozin OR dapagliflozin) OR (albuminuria OR microalbuminuria OR urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio). All databases were searched from their inception until 26 June 2025. To ensure comprehensive coverage, the Embase search was updated on 29 November 2025.

### Selection criteria
All identified citations were imported into Rayyan.ai for deduplication [10]. Two reviewers (M.S. and S.L.F.) independently screened titles and abstracts. Studies were included if they met the following criteria: (i) published RCTs, (ii) enrolled adult patients with CKD with or without diabetes, (iii) compared combination therapy of either an MRA or an aldosterone synthase inhibitor (ASI) with an SGLT2 inhibitor versus SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy, and (iv) reported at least one predefined renal or cardiovascular outcome. Studies were excluded if: (i) not RCTs, (ii) not published in English, (iii) lacked relevant outcomes, and (iv) were non-human or non-original studies.

### Data extraction
Two reviewers (S.M.D. and M.R.) extracted data. Primary outcomes included percentage change in albuminuria, change in systolic blood pressure (SBP), change in estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR) and change in serum potassium. Secondary outcomes included the proportion of patients with at least a 30% reduction in UACR, the proportion with at least a 50% reduction in UACR, hyperkalemia, and any or serious adverse events.

### Risk of bias
Two authors (H.T.G. and F.B.) independently evaluated the included RCTs using the Revised Cochrane Risk-of-Bias tool for randomized trials (RoB 2.0) [11]. A third author (N.M.K.) resolved discrepancies.

### Certainty of evidence
The certainty of evidence was evaluated using the GRADE framework [12] with GRADEpro software (Supplementary Table 2).

### Data analysis
Meta-analysis was conducted using Review Manager (RevMan) version 5.4.1. Risk ratios (RRs) and mean differences (MDs) were calculated for dichotomous and continuous outcomes. A DerSimonian-Laird random-effects model was used [13]. Statistical heterogeneity was assessed using the Chi-square test and Higgins' I² statistic, with I² values interpreted as low (< 25%), moderate (25–75%), or high (> 75%). Subgroup analyses on primary outcomes assessed: (i) study designs (parallel vs. crossover RCT), (ii) follow-up duration (4 weeks vs. 12–14 weeks vs. 6 months), and (iii) type of add-on therapy (ns-MRA vs. steroidal MRA [s-MRA] vs. ASI). Leave-one-out sensitivity analysis was performed. p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

### Study selection
After extensive literature search, 494 articles were retrieved, of which 38 were duplicates. After screening 456 records, 28 studies were potentially eligible. Full-text review led to exclusion of 23 additional studies. Finally, 5 RCTs were included (Fig. 1, PRISMA flow chart).

### Baseline characteristics
A total of 5 RCTs, comprising 808 patients with CKD, were included.

- **ROTATE-3**: open-label crossover, randomized 46 participants to eplerenone + dapagliflozin or dapagliflozin alone, 4-week treatment period, mean age 70 years.
- **ASi-CKD**: double-blind parallel group, 148 participants — vicadrostat 20 mg + empagliflozin 10 mg (n = 74) vs. placebo + empagliflozin 10 mg (n = 74), mean age 63 years, 14-week treatment exposure.
- **DAPAFINE**: open-label, 10 participants allocated to dapagliflozin then dapagliflozin + finerenone, mean age 59 years, 4-week treatment periods without washout.
- **MIRACLE**: double-blind parallel group, 68 participants — balcinrenone 150 mg + dapagliflozin 10 mg (n = 35) vs. placebo + dapagliflozin 10 mg (n = 33), mean age 73 years, 12 weeks of treatment.
- **CONFIDENCE**: double-blind parallel group, 536 participants — finerenone 10 mg or 20 mg + empagliflozin 10 mg (n = 269) vs. empagliflozin 10 mg alone (n = 267), mean age 67 years, 6-month treatment duration.

Most patients were treated with ACEis/ARBs and had T2DM, except DAPAFINE which excluded patients with diabetes.

### Risk of bias
Among the five studies, all three parallel group, double-blinded trials (ASi-CKD, MIRACLE, and CONFIDENCE) were assessed as having low risk of bias. Of the two open-label crossover trials, ROTATE-3 was identified as having "some concerns" due to deviations from intended interventions, while DAPAFINE was assessed as having high risk of bias due to the absence of a washout period (Figs. 2 and 3).

### Primary outcomes

**Percentage change in albuminuria (UACR)** — Reported by 5 studies. Combination therapy vs. SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy resulted in significant reductions in albuminuria (MD: -32.82% [95% CI: -39.16, -26.48]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%; Fig. 4a). Subgroup analysis showed no effect modification by study design (p = 0.84), follow-up duration (p = 0.97), or type of add-on therapy (p = 1.00).

**Change in systolic blood pressure (SBP)** — Reported by 5 studies. Significant SBP reduction (MD: -5.02 mm Hg [95% CI: -6.95, -3.08]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%; Fig. 4b). No effect modification by study design (p = 0.80), follow-up duration (p = 0.57), or add-on therapy type (p = 0.33).

**Change in eGFR** — Reported by 5 studies. Combination therapy associated with a greater reduction in eGFR (MD: -2.59 mL/min/1.73 m² [95% CI: −3.78, − 1.40]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%; Fig. 4c). No effect modification by study design (p = 0.18), follow-up duration (p = 0.31), or add-on therapy type (p = 0.59).

**Change in serum potassium** — Combination therapy associated with a greater increase in serum potassium (MD: 0.23 mmol/L [95% CI: 0.16, 0.30]; p < 0.001; I² = 15%; Fig. 4d). No effect modification by study design (p = 0.47), follow-up duration (p = 0.70), or add-on therapy type (p = 0.91).

### Secondary outcomes

**≥30% reduction in UACR** — 3 studies. Combination therapy associated with a significantly greater proportion achieving ≥30% UACR reduction (RR: 1.81 [95% CI: 1.19, 2.76]; p = 0.006; I² = 76%; Fig. 5a). Sensitivity analysis excluding CONFIDENCE reduced heterogeneity (I² = 0%) without altering effect size significantly (RR: 2.22 [95% CI: 1.59, 3.10]; p < 0.001).

**≥50% reduction in UACR** — 2 studies. Combination therapy (MRA + SGLT2i) significantly increased proportion achieving ≥50% UACR reduction (RR: 1.92 [95% CI: 1.31, 2.81]; p < 0.001; I² = 39%; Fig. 5b).

**Hyperkalemia (combination vs. SGLT2 inhibitor alone)** — 4 studies. Combination therapy associated with significantly greater risk of hyperkalemia (RR: 1.91 [95% CI: 1.10, 3.33]; p = 0.02; I² = 0%; Fig. 5c).

**Hyperkalemia (combination vs. MRA or ASI alone)** — 4 studies. Combination therapy associated with significantly LOWER rates of hyperkalemia vs. MRA/ASI monotherapy (RR: 0.58 [95% CI: 0.35, 0.96]; p = 0.03; I² = 19%; Fig. 5d).

**Any adverse events** — 5 studies. No significant difference (RR: 1.24 [95% CI: 0.87, 1.76]; p = 0.23; I² = 46%; Fig. 5e).

**Serious adverse events** — 5 studies. No significant difference (RR: 0.95 [95% CI: 0.57, 1.59]; p = 0.86; I² = 0%; Fig. 5f).

## Discussion

In this meta-analysis of 808 patients with CKD, combination therapy with an MRA or ASI plus an SGLT2 inhibitor (vs. SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy) was associated with significant reductions in albuminuria and SBP, suggesting synergistic therapeutic effects. The combination also yielded a greater reduction in eGFR, consistent with the well-documented transient "eGFR dip" that precedes long-term renoprotection [14–16]. Combination therapy was associated with a modest, consistent increase in serum potassium, attributable to the anti-aldosterone effects of MRA. While hyperkalemia risk was elevated vs. SGLT2 inhibitor monotherapy, it was notably lower than reported with MRA or ASI monotherapy. Total and serious adverse events did not differ.

The ≥30% UACR reduction outcome was the only outcome with substantial heterogeneity, primarily influenced by the CONFIDENCE trial (longer follow-up, higher baseline UACR, exclusively T2DM population).

Findings are consistent with prior literature suggesting additive benefits of combination therapy [7]. This stands in contrast to a prespecified integrated analysis of ~13,000 patients from FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD, which reported no significant interaction between baseline SGLT2 inhibitor use and finerenone's effect on hard CV and renal endpoints [17]; the discrepancy may be explained by confounding in non-randomized SGLT2 inhibitor use and the distinction between synergistic effects on surrogate markers vs. hard outcomes. A previous meta-analysis by Ferreira et al. [7] was limited by shorter follow-up, smaller samples, and limited outcome range; the present analysis addresses these limitations.

The attenuated hyperkalemia risk relative to MRA/ASI monotherapy supports the 'pillars of care' model [31, 32], which posits that combining evidence-based therapies may enhance efficacy while mitigating adverse effects. This hyperkalemia risk reduction with SGLT2 inhibitors has been reported previously [33].

All included patients had CKD, with over 85% having T2DM; eGFR ranged 30–60 mL/min/1.73 m² and UACR 90–600 mg/g.

### Future implications
Several trials are underway in broader populations including HFpEF and non-diabetic CKD [37–40]. An "accelerated, risk-based approach" to initiation of combination therapy has been proposed for high/very-high-risk CKD categories with T2DM [41].

### Limitations
(1) Findings based primarily on surrogate endpoints; hard clinical outcomes (all-cause mortality, CV mortality, progression to ESKD) were not reported. (2) Pooled population predominantly T2DM, limiting generalizability. (3) Heterogeneity in follow-up durations and add-on therapy types. (4) Lack of washout in DAPAFINE may have caused carryover effects. (5) Publication bias could not be formally assessed due to limited number of studies.

## Conclusion
This meta-analysis offers robust evidence supporting additive clinical benefits of combination therapy with an MRA or ASI plus an SGLT2 inhibitor in CKD, particularly in reducing albuminuria and SBP. Although a modest increase in serum potassium and hyperkalemia risk was noted, the overall safety profile remained favourable, especially vs. MRA or ASI monotherapy. These findings highlight the therapeutic potential of a mechanism-based approach and substantiate the evolving "pillars of care" framework, while underscoring the need for large-scale, prospective studies evaluating long-term hard renal and cardiovascular outcomes.
