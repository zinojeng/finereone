# Diabetic Nephropathy: Update on Pillars of Therapy Slowing Progression

**Sandra C. Naaman and George L. Bakris**
Section of Endocrinology, Diabetes, and Metabolism, Department of Medicine, and American Heart Association Comprehensive Hypertension Center, University of Chicago Medicine, Chicago, IL
Corresponding author: George L. Bakris, gbakris@uchicago.edu

*Diabetes Care 2023;46(9):1574–1586 | https://doi.org/10.2337/dci23-0030*
Received 21 April 2023 and accepted 16 June 2023.

## Article Highlights

- Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) affects 700 million people worldwide and has a threefold higher risk of all-cause mortality than in people without DKD.
- Since the institution of the renin-angiotensin system (RAS) blockade, two additional evidence-based therapies, sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors and finerenone (a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid antagonist) have been demonstrated, when either is combined alone with maximally tolerated RAS blockade, to slow the decline in DKD to about 2.5–3 mL/min/year.
- An additional class, the glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs), is supported by retrospective analyses but awaits confirmation in the ongoing outcome trial, A Research Study to See How Semaglutide Works Compared to Placebo in People With Type 2 Diabetes and Chronic Kidney Disease (FLOW).
- The complex pathophysiology underlying DKD calls for a pillared approach to therapy similar to that for heart failure management.

## Abstract

Management of diabetic kidney disease (DKD) has evolved in parallel with our growing understanding of the multiple interrelated pathophysiological mechanisms that involve hemodynamic, metabolic, and inflammatory pathways. These pathways and others play a vital role in the initiation and progression of DKD. Since its initial discovery, the blockade of the renin-angiotensin system has remained a cornerstone of DKD management, leaving a large component of residual risk to be dealt with. The advent of sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors followed by nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists and, to some extent, glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonists (GLP-1 RAs) has ushered in a resounding paradigm shift that supports a pillared approach in maximizing treatment to reduce outcomes. This pillared approach is like that derived from the approach to heart failure treatment. The approach mandates that all agents that have been shown in clinical trials to reduce cardiovascular outcomes and/or mortality to a greater extent than a single drug class alone should be used in combination. In this way, each drug class focuses on a specific aspect of the disease's pathophysiology. Thus, in heart failure, β-blockers, sacubitril/valsartan, a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and a diuretic are used together. In this article, we review the evolution of the pillar concept of therapy as it applies to DKD and discuss how it should be used based on the outcome evidence. We also discuss the exciting possibility that GLP-1 RAs may be an additional pillar in the quest to further slow kidney disease progression in diabetes.

## Introduction / Epidemiology

Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) is a serious microvascular complication that affects approximately 40% of individuals with diabetes (1). Presently the leading cause of end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) worldwide, DKD affects 700 million people, and it disproportionately affects those who are socially disadvantaged (2). The global percentage of prevalent ESKD patients with diabetes increased from 19.0% in 2000 to 29.7% in 2015, while the percentage of incident ESKD patients due to diabetes increased from 22.1% to 31.3% (3). In 2017, half of all patients in the U.S. with ESKD had diabetes reported as the primary etiologic cause (4). The current global prevalence of diabetes is 564 million and is projected to grow to 600 million by 2035 (5), reaching 783 million by 2045 (6).

The number of renal replacement treatment (RRT) recipients has steadily climbed from 2.819 million in 2010 to a projected estimate of 4.35 million by 2030 (7). Albeit lifesaving, RRT expansion is not economically tenable, given the unhealthy aging trends of growing populations, and is often inaccessible to many low- and middle-income countries.

Characterized by progressive kidney fibrosis resulting in loss of function, chronic kidney disease (CKD) from diabetes or other causes is operationally defined by KDIGO as abnormalities of kidney structure or function present for >3 months, requiring one of two criteria for >3 months: either eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² or markers of kidney damage including albuminuria (>30 mg/g, or persistent albuminuria >300 mg/24 h) irrespective of eGFR (8). A small subgroup progress to ESKD without significant albuminuria (9). Death from cardiovascular (CV) causes is the single most competing risk in CKD patients before reaching stage 4 CKD (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73 m²), with 5.1% of people in stage 3 CKD and only 0.3% in stage 4 (13). Compared with the general population, DKD confers a threefold higher risk of all-cause mortality and a 16-year loss in life expectancy. Reduced eGFR and albuminuria independently predict increased CV morbidity and mortality, and the presence of both exerts multiplicative effects on CV mortality risk (11,14,15).

Nothing beyond glycemic and blood pressure control was available to halt CKD progression until a trial of captopril in 1993 in people with type 1 diabetes (18), which revealed a key role of RAS blockade for slowing DKD by approximately 5–7 mL/min/year. Subsequent work on angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) further solidified RAS blockade in all patients with diabetes (18–20). However, substantial residual risk persisted, as the rate of decline with RAS blockers was estimated between 4 and 6 mL/min/year (normal annual decline is approximately 0.7–0.9 mL/min/year). In 2014, the serendipitous discovery of SGLT2 inhibitors provided hope that DKD progression could be further slowed (21). Around the same time, trials began on the nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (NS-MRAs), specifically finerenone — distinctly different from spironolactone — which also slowed DKD, independent of SGLT2 inhibitor use (22). We now have two evidence-based medications that, when combined with RAS inhibition, slow DKD progression to approximately 2.5–3 mL/min/year, provided blood pressure and glucose are at goal.

## Pathophysiologic Mechanisms and Potential Therapeutic Targets

The mechanisms underlying DKD can be broadly conceptualized as an interplay of three key processes — hemodynamic, metabolic, and inflammatory — with variable contributions depending on individual genetic makeup (Fig. 2).

**Hemodynamic effects** center around the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. Angiotensin II binds AT1 and AT2 receptors. AT1 activation mediates increased efferent arteriolar resistance, raising intraglomerular pressure to maintain filtration rate (27). AT2 activation modulates renal vasodilating prostaglandin release, opposing AT1 action (28). High angiotensin II exerts nonhemodynamic effects contributing to renal injury: increased adrenal aldosterone secretion, induction of fibrogenic chemokines (MCP-1 and TGF-β), and macrophage activation (29–31). Increases in intraglomerular pressure induced by RAS activation are noted in up to 75% and 40% of individuals with type 1 and type 2 diabetes, respectively (32). Podocyte injury leads to foot process effacement and podocyte loss, the unifying mechanism underlying albuminuria in diabetes. RAS blockade improves permselectivity, whereas dihydropyridine calcium blockers like nifedipine used alone worsen it (39).

In diabetes, supraphysiologic glucose delivered to the proximal tubule upregulates SGLT1 and SGLT2 to maximize glucose and sodium reabsorption (41,42). Reduced sodium delivery to the distal nephron produces negative tubuloglomerular feedback. By blocking glucose reabsorption at the proximal tubule and diverting it into urine, SGLT2 inhibitors restore tubuloglomerular balance, lowering intraglomerular pressure and reducing hyperfiltration (44,45). However, this is not feasible when eGFR is below 45 mL/min/1.73 m², when autoregulation is not functional (46); the mechanism of renoprotection in advanced kidney disease is unclear.

**Metabolic pathways:** Hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, and dyslipidemia coexist, setting in motion dysregulated metabolic pathways related to oxidative stress and inflammation. The polyol and protein kinase C (PKC) pathways augment oxidative stress and deplete endothelial nitric oxide synthase. NF-κB–mediated cytokines (TNF-α and IL-6) favor an inflammatory response. Advanced glycation end products (AGEs) engaging their receptors (RAGEs) trigger NF-κB upregulation and proinflammatory cytokines, reduce nitric-oxide bioavailability, and increase reactive oxygen species (49).

**Inflammation and fibrosis:** Mounting evidence implicates interaction between the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), aldosterone, and Rac1 in driving inflammation leading to fibrosis. MR activation occurs in the aldosterone-responsive distal nephron, causing sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion. While critical for survival in low-sodium states, it becomes pathologic with persistently high sodium intake (54). Outside the distal nephron, MRs are expressed on podocytes, fibroblasts, vascular cells, and macrophages, which do not uniformly coexpress the steroid-blunting 11β-HSD2, permitting unbridled MR activation (55). MR is upregulated in hyperglycemia, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and obesity, increasing transcription of profibrotic factors PAI-1, TGF-β1, connective tissue growth factor, and extracellular matrix proteins (56).

Macrophage infiltration is a hallmark of DKD (57). Macrophage-MR activation polarizes differentiation toward the M1 proinflammatory phenotype (59), whereas MR inhibition favors switching toward an M2 anti-inflammatory phenotype (60). The therapeutic effects of MR antagonism in DKD were first reported in 2001 in animal models (61). There are no outcome trials with steroidal MRAs due to tolerability issues, though they reduce albuminuria and blood pressure in early DKD. In phase 2 trials (ARTS-DN and ARTS-DN Japan), finerenone demonstrated up to 38% albuminuria reduction versus placebo in albuminuric DKD (62,63). Other NS-MRAs (esaxerenone, apararenone) also reduced albuminuria (64,65), though esaxerenone is only available in Japan (66); none of these have been tested in outcome trials.

## Mechanism Application to Outcome Data and Guidelines: Pillars of Therapy

Following the 2001 trials showing ARB benefit, nothing further curtailed DKD until SGLT2 inhibitors. Trials of novel targeted therapies between 2002 and 2010 all failed (Fig. 5). Since dapagliflozin's approval in January 2014, other SGLT2 inhibitors were added; approval of the NS-MRA finerenone in July 2021 advanced the field from one to three therapies.

Albuminuria is an established continuous variable: levels exceeding 30 mg/day predict adverse CV outcomes; those exceeding 300 mg/day are typically diagnostic of kidney disease and associated with accelerated DKD decline (67,68). Reduction in albuminuria is associated with slowed DKD progression and reduced CV event rates (69–72). DKD outcome trials included people at high risk (eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m² and UACR >300 mg/day).

### Pillar 1: RAS Blockers

The captopril trial was the first to establish ACE inhibition in delaying DKD in type 1 diabetes: ACEI achieved slower creatinine clearance decline (11% vs. 17% in placebo) and a 50% risk reduction in combined renal end points (dialysis, transplantation, or death) independent of blood pressure (18). Two additional landmark trials — RENAAL (20) and IDNT (19) — preceded formal integration of RAS blockers into standard of care (16). The modest improvement in eGFR decline (to ~4 mL/min/year) implied high residual risk. Subsequent strategies — dual ACEI/ARB blockade (VA NEPHRON-D), renin inhibition (ALTITUDE), PKC-β inhibition, endothelin antagonism, and TGF-β production inhibition — were all disappointing (73–79).

### Pillar 2: SGLT2 Inhibitors

Originally designed to lower glucose via urinary glucose excretion, SGLT2 inhibitors proved to be cardiorenal risk–reducing agents (80). A meta-analysis of six double-blinded randomized trials found consistent reduction in heart failure hospitalization and progression to ESKD (81).

- **CREDENCE** was the first nephropathy outcome trial to specify its primary outcome as ESKD, doubling of creatinine, or death from renal or CV causes. Canagliflozin achieved a 30% lower relative risk of the primary end point and a 32% lower relative risk of ESKD progression; unequivocal renal benefits prompted early termination (82).
- **DAPA-CKD** randomized a mixed cohort (two-thirds with diabetes, one-third without). Dapagliflozin achieved a 44% relative risk reduction in sustained ≥50% eGFR reduction, ESKD, or renal/CV death, and a 29% relative risk reduction in CV death, consistent regardless of diabetes status (83).
- **EMPA-KIDNEY:** Over 6,600 participants with and without diabetes, wide GFR range, normal or high albuminuria. Over ~2 years' median duration, empagliflozin reduced ESKD progression or CV death by 28% (HR 0.72; 95% CI 0.64–0.82; P < 0.001) and reduced hospitalization from any cause by 14% (HR 0.86; 95% CI 0.78–0.95; P = 0.003) (84).

When SGLT2 inhibitor data are evaluated together with ACEI/ARB use, kidney function declines are diminished by nearly 30–40% above ACEI/ARB use alone (85).

### Pillar 3: NS-MRAs

Blocking angiotensin II should partially inhibit aldosterone secretion, but this is not long-standing due to "aldosterone escape/breakthrough." In one study using UACR to indicate trandolapril's renoprotective effects, aldosterone breakthrough was observed in 40% of patients at 40 weeks (86). A sustained UACR reduction of at least 30% is associated with slowing of DKD and decreased mortality (88,89), adopted by the FDA and ADA. In a meta-analysis of 829 patients on dialysis, MRA achieved a 66% reduction in CV mortality but a threefold increase in hyperkalemia risk (90), so MRAs have generally been contraindicated in advanced kidney disease (91).

Finerenone, apararenone, esaxerenone, and ocedurenone are NS-MRAs distinct from steroidal agents. Finerenone is the only one developed and approved for cardiorenal risk reduction; the others are approved only for blood pressure control. Finerenone demonstrates superior anti-inflammatory and antifibrotic outcomes compared with steroidal counterparts in preclinical studies (92–95) and achieves balanced tissue distribution between heart and kidney (22). Finerenone was studied in two complementary phase 3 trials including over 13,000 participants with type 2 diabetes on maximally tolerated RAS blockade (96,97), enabling a pooled analysis (FIDELITY) (98).

- **FIDELIO-DKD:** finerenone reduced the kidney composite (ESKD, sustained ≥40% eGFR reduction, or renal death) by 18% versus placebo, and the prespecified secondary CV end point (CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke, or heart failure hospitalization) by 14% (96). Trial discontinuation related to hyperkalemia was slightly higher with finerenone than placebo (2.3% vs. 0.9%).
- **FIGARO-DKD** (higher CV risk, less advanced DKD): finerenone reduced the primary CV outcome by 13%, largely from lower heart failure hospitalizations. Hyperkalemia-related discontinuation was 1.2% vs. 0.4% (placebo).
- **FIDELITY** pooled 13,026 patients (CKD stages 1–4, high to very high albuminuria): 23% reduction in doubling of creatinine, ESKD, and renal death; significant 20% reduction in ESKD alone; and 14% decreased risk in composite CV outcomes largely driven by reduced heart failure (98). Clinically important hyperkalemia-related discontinuation was 1.7% (finerenone) vs. 0.6% (placebo) in >6,500 patients per arm. The FDA label allows use of finerenone with potassium levels up to 5 mEq/L.

Comparing hyperkalemia risk: in a resistant-hypertension subgroup with eGFR 37 mL/min/1.73 m², the incidence of hyperkalemia in the FIDELITY study was 11.2% with finerenone versus 64.1% with spironolactone (without a potassium binder) in the AMBER trial comparison (101,102).

### Evidence for Potential Addition of Pillar 4: GLP-1 RAs

GLP-1 RAs are recommended for patients with DKD who have not met glycemic targets despite metformin and SGLT2 inhibitors (103). CV outcome trials (CVOT) demonstrated significant reduction in atherosclerotic CV events, leading to 2020 guideline revisions (104–106).

Post hoc analyses suggest possible benefits in delaying DKD progression (107). A pooled analysis of more than 12,500 participants from SUSTAIN-6 (108) and LEADER (109) evaluated albuminuria, annual eGFR change, and time to persistent eGFR declines. Compared with placebo, semaglutide and liraglutide produced a 24% reduction in albuminuria from baseline to 2 years (95% CI 20–27%; P < 0.001) and significantly slowed annual eGFR decline (0.87 and 0.26 mL/min/1.73 m²/year; P < 0.0001 and P < 0.001, respectively), with benefits more pronounced in patients with baseline eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m². They also lowered the risk of persistent eGFR declines to 40% and 50% (HR 0.86 [95% CI 0.75–0.99], P = 0.039, and HR 0.80 [95% CI 0.66–0.97], P = 0.023), respectively (110).

**AMPLITUDE-O** examined efpeglenatide versus placebo on time to first major adverse CV event and secondary composite kidney outcomes in 4,076 patients with type 2 diabetes and current CKD (eGFR 25.0–60 mL/min/1.73 m²) or CVD history plus a CV risk factor (111). Only 80% were on RAS blockade; 15.2% were using SGLT2 inhibitors at entry. Efpeglenatide achieved a 32% relative risk reduction in the composite kidney outcome versus placebo (HR 0.68; 95% CI 0.57–0.79), independent of baseline SGLT2 inhibitor use (112).

Substantial, sustained weight loss (reaching 20% with some GLP-1 RAs) is important (113). Abdominal obesity is independently associated with albuminuria despite normoglycemia and normotension (114). **FLOW** — an ongoing randomized placebo-controlled trial assessing semaglutide in type 2 diabetes and CKD — is the first designed with primary renal end points (116).

## Clinical Application

DKD management should start with maximally dosed RAS blockade, based on doses used in the original trials where dose reduction to avoid hyperkalemia markedly reduced protection. A large analysis (N = 205,108) showed submaximal ACEI/ARB dosing was associated with worse cardiorenal outcomes (117).

Each drug class that improved outcomes was coupled with RAS blockade and independently showed benefits on kidney and heart outcomes, providing impetus to adopt a pillared approach analogous to heart failure management. Notably, there has never been a trial evaluating simultaneous use of all four agents in heart failure or comparing different combinations head-to-head; each trial was assessed on its merits and combined in retrospect. An adequately powered study of three (or soon four) agents together would require well over 100,000 participants; therefore extrapolation using all three agents together is used.

Practice guidelines direct clinicians to titrate to maximally tolerated RAS blockade before introducing SGLT2 inhibitors, NS-MRAs, and GLP-1 RAs (119). This is challenging due to eGFR dipping at initiation (10–30%) (120,121), but this response is reassuring: acute serum creatinine elevations of up to 30% correlate with long-term renal function preservation (120,122,123). Similar initial changes with long-term amelioration are reported with SGLT2 inhibitors (124) and finerenone, albeit to a lesser degree than RAS blockers (96,120,125–128).

## The Additive Value of Drug Combination

An animal model of preclinical hypertension–induced cardiorenal disease using low-dose combination finerenone and empagliflozin revealed additive cardiorenal benefit above respective monotherapy (reductions in blood pressure, proteinuria, cardiac fibrosis, vasculopathy, and mortality) (129).

In a small open-label randomized crossover study, dapagliflozin plus low-dose steroidal MRA eplerenone had an additive effect on albuminuria reduction versus either drug alone, with significantly less hyperkalemia in the combination group than eplerenone alone (130) — consistent with FIDELIO-DKD data showing greater protection from hyperkalemia when an SGLT2 inhibitor was combined with finerenone (131).

The FIDELITY subgroup analysis showed cardiorenal benefits of finerenone were higher irrespective of concomitant GLP-1 RA or SGLT2 inhibitor use, though these users comprised only 6.9% and 4.6% of the population, precluding firm conclusions; there was no sign of increased kidney injury with coadministration (132). When combined with finerenone, SGLT2 inhibitors reduced hyperkalemic events versus nonusers (8.1% vs. 18.7%) (98,133).

**CONFIDENCE** is an ongoing three-arm double-blinded trial comparing combined empagliflozin plus finerenone versus each drug alone on change in UACR over 8 months in type 2 diabetes with CKD stages 2–3 and UACR 300–5,000 mg/g (134).

## Conclusions

Since the institution of RAS blockade in the 1990s, significant strides have been made in addressing residual risk of DKD progression. We now have two additional drug classes to add to RAS blockers — SGLT2 inhibitors and NS-MRAs — bolstered by robust outcome data, and a possible third (GLP-1 RAs), whose efficacy is supported by retrospective analyses but needs proof in the ongoing FLOW trial. The safety and tolerability of these drug classes given together against maximal RAS blockade are encouraging, and the protective role of SGLT2 inhibitors in preventing hyperkalemia with finerenone use is heartening. Endocrinologists, nephrologists, and cardiologists are strongly encouraged to use a pillared approach to DKD, irrespective of degree of kidney impairment, down to an eGFR of 25 mL/min/1.73 m².
