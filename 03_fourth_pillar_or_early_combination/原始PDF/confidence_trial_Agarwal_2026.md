# ABSTRACT

# BACKGROUND

Hyperkalemia is common with renin-angiotensin system inhibitor (RASi) therapy, frequently leading to treatment interruption and potentially curtailing cardiovascular and kidney benefits. Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors might mitigate the risk of hyperkalemia with RASis.

# OBJECTIVES

This prespecified secondary analysis of the CONFIDENCE trial aimed to investigate the impact of empagliflozin, finerenone (a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist), and their combination on hyperkalemia, and whether hyperkalemia mediates albuminuria reduction in high-risk patients with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes with albuminuria.

# METHODS

In this double-blind, randomized, controlled trial, CONFIDENCE, patients with type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease (estimated glomerular filtration rate: 30-90 mL/min/1.73 m2), and albuminuria (urine albumin-to creatinine ratio [UACR]: 100-5,000 mg/g) on stable doses of RASis were randomized 1:1:1 to empagliflozin, finerenone, or both. The primary outcome was change in UACR from baseline to Day 180. Mean changes in potassium were estimated using linear mixed models. Logistic regression models assessed the risk of moderate (serum potassium 5.5 mmol/L) and severe (serum potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L) hyperkalemia. Causal mediation analysis was used to ascertain the impact of hyperkalemia on mean UACR change at Day 180.

# RESULTS

Patients developing hyperkalemia had lower estimated glomerular filtration rate, higher potassium, and more severe albuminuria at baseline. Hyperkalemia events accumulated over the 180 days of the trial in all 3 groups. There were few treatment discontinuations. Randomization to finerenone was associated with increase in serum potassium. No difference was seen between combination treatment and finerenone in mean change in serum potassium (P = 0.91) or the odds of developing hyperkalemia (P = 0.85). Overall, hyperkalemia occurred in 113 patients (14.5%): 40 of 265 (15.1%) with combination therapy, 48 of 255 (18.8%) with finerenone, and 25 of 259 (9.7%) with empagliflozin. Hyperkalemia was not in the causal pathway of UACR reduction at Day 180.

# CONCLUSIONS

Over 180 days, the combination of empagliflozin and finerenone did not significantly mitigate the risk of hyperkalemia. The treatment effect was maintained regardless of development of hyperkalemia. # Hyperkalemia in the CONFIDENCE Trial

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a global health challenge, frequently accompanied by type 2 diabetes (T2D) mellitus.¹ Renin angiotensin system inhibitor (RASi) therapies, including angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEis), angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), and nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (nsMRAs), are fundamental to mitigating the progression of CKD and reducing cardiovascular morbidity and mortality in these patients.² However, hyperkalemia is a common adverse effect of renin angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASis), often leading to dose reduction or permanent discontinuation of these beneficial treatments.³ This interruption can curtail the vascular, cardiac, and kidney benefits of RAASis.

In recent years, novel therapeutic agents have emerged with demonstrated benefits in cardio-kidney protection. Sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2is), such as empagliflozin, have shown important kidney and cardiovascular protective effects in patients with cardiovascular and/or kidney disease.⁴ Similarly, the nsMRA finerenone has shown clinically significant reductions in adverse kidney and cardiovascular outcomes,⁵ with a potentially improved safety profile compared with steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists.⁶ Although nonrandomized observations have suggested that SGLT2is might mitigate the risk of hyperkalemia when RAASi therapy is initiated, this potential interaction, particularly in the context of nsMRA use, warrants rigorous investigation in randomized controlled settings.⁷

The CONFIDENCE (Combination Effect of Finerenone and Empagliflozin in Participants with Chronic Kidney Disease and Type 2 Diabetes Using a Urinary Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio Endpoint; NCT05254002) trial was a 3-arm randomized controlled trial uniquely designed to evaluate the comparative effects of combination SGLT2i+nsMRA therapy vs either therapy alone.⁸ Although the original trial reported hyperkalemia, a detailed analysis of its time course, determinants, and impact on efficacy was omitted. This represents a key knowledge gap because hyperkalemia could be an adverse safety signal or a complex marker of target engagement. Therefore, we sought to determine whether hyperkalemia, as a potential indicator of more significant RAAS inhibition, is associated with greater urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) reduction. This prespecified secondary analysis aimed to investigate the mean changes from baseline in serum potassium, the risk of developing hyperkalemia and its determinants, and the association of hyperkalemia with the safety and efficacy of reno-protective therapies in high-risk patients with CKD and T2D with albuminuria.

# METHODS

# STUDY DESIGN AND PARTICIPANTS

The CONFIDENCE trial was a double-blind, Phase 2, randomized controlled trial conducted across 14 countries.⁹,10 Patients were randomized in a 1:1:1 ratio to receive empagliflozin alone, finerenone alone, or a combination of both agents. To ensure strict blinding, a double-dummy trial design was used. Eligible participants had T2D, CKD with an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) between 30 and 90 mL/min/1.73 m², and persistent albuminuria, defined as UACR between 100 and 5,000 mg/g, determined by mean value of the first morning void.

Exclusion criteria included a diagnosis of type 1 diabetes, a screening potassium level of 4.8 mmol/L, symptomatic heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, and a stroke, myocardial infarction, or hospitalization for worsening heart failure within 90 days before the screening visit. # INTERVENTIONS

Patients received empagliflozin alone, finerenone alone, or the combination of both. Empagliflozin was dosed at 10 mg/d. Finerenone was initiated at 20 mg/d when the eGFR was ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m², and at 10 mg/d when the baseline eGFR was &#x3C;60 mL/min/1.73 m². Randomization was stratified according to eGFR (≥60 and &#x3C;60 mL/min/1.73 m²) and UACR (≤850 and >850 mg/g) measurements at the screening visit. The trial group assignments were concealed from the investigators, the treating physicians, the participants, and the outcome assessors.

# OUTCOME MEASURES AND DEFINITIONS

Hyperkalemia was defined as a serum potassium level >5.5 mmol/L at any visit, provided the baseline central laboratory measurement was >5.5 mmol/L.⁹ Moderate hyperkalemia was defined as potassium between 5.5 and 6.0 mmol/L, and severe hyperkalemia as potassium ≥6.0 mmol/L. In addition, investigator-reported hyperkalemia or increased blood potassium, spontaneously reported as adverse events irrespective of the cutoff, were prospectively collected.

# HYPERKALEMIA MEASUREMENTS AND DOSE ADJUSTMENTS

Serum potassium was measured at each visit using both central and local laboratories. Local samples were prioritized for safety and dose adjustments due to faster results and reduced risk of falsely elevated potassium levels compared with central laboratory samples. Retesting of potassium values was required within 72 h if confirmation was needed. Patients were seen at screening, baseline, and after randomization on Days 14, 30, 90, and 180. They were seen again 30 days after withdrawal of the treatment.

# STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

Linear mixed models were used to estimate determinants of the mean change of serum potassium from baseline. Determinants of changes in serum potassium were a priori selected from a literature review, and included baseline potassium, baseline eGFR, log of baseline UACR, age, sex, geographic region (North America, Europe, and Asia), diuretic use, beta-blocker use, insulin use, and baseline hemoglobin A1c.

To evaluate the specific effects of treatment on mean change in serum potassium we included treatment, visit, and treatment × visit interaction in the model. We further tested treatment heterogeneity by interacting treatment with covariate terms. Random effects included subject and visits. Maximal likelihood estimates were used to derive the estimated means and SEs.

Logistic regression models were used to assess the odds of moderate and severe hyperkalemia. The predictor variables used were similar to the ones used for linear mixed models.

# BASELINE CHARACTERISTICS AND HYPERKALEMIA INCIDENCE

Baseline characteristics by occurrence of hyperkalemia (serum potassium > 5.5 mmol/L) are shown in Table 1. The time course of change in hyperkalemia events (defined as serum potassium > 5.5, 5.5 to &#x3C; 6.0, and ≥ 6.0 mEq/L) is shown in Figure 3 and the Central Illustration. Over the 6 months of study, hyperkalemia events continued to accumulate in all 3 groups.

Overall, hyperkalemia occurred in 113 patients (14.5%): 40 of 265 (15.1%) in the combination group, 48 of 255 (18.8%) in the finerenone group, and 25 of 259 (9.7%) in the empagliflozin group (Central Illustration). Patients who developed hyperkalemia were more often male, living in Asia, and tended to have higher baseline potassium, lower eGFR, higher UACR, and more severe CKD stage and albuminuria than patients who did not develop hyperkalemia.

# DETERMINANTS OF HYPERKALEMIA

Table 2 shows the logistic regression results. Both baseline serum potassium and eGFR were determinants of any hyperkalemia: adjusted OR (aOR): 9.23 (95% CI: 5.07-16.81) per 1 mmol/L increase in potassium and aOR 1.11 (95% CI: 1.03-1.20) per 5 mL/min/1.73 m² decrease in eGFR (Central Illustration). Treatment influenced the occurrence of any hyperkalemia; compared with empagliflozin alone, the aOR for combination was 2.69 (95% CI: 1.46-4.96) and the aOR for finerenone was 2.56 (95% CI: 1.42-4.59). The OR for finerenone was not significantly different from combination therapy (P = 0.85). Similar determinants emerged for the risk of severe hyperkalemia (Table 2).

# Combination Therapy

Less than 5% of the patients had hyperkalemia at Day 14, but in each of the 3 treatment groups hyperkalemia events accumulated over time. Severe hyperkalemia was uncommon.

# DISCUSSION

Our study extends previous reports of hyperkalemia associated with finerenone monotherapy to include cases occurring during combination therapy.¹⁴,15 With combination therapy, we find that the risk of hyperkalemia and the mean change in potassium from baseline are not substantially mitigated compared with finerenone alone. The trial used a robust design for assessing mean changes in serum potassium. Patients were on background therapy with an ACEi or ARB, and there was no evidence to suggest that the mean serum potassium level was reduced with empagliflozin.

The lack of a clinically significant change in mean decrease in serum potassium is consistent with a prior meta-analysis that reported a mean change in serum potassium of 0.04 mEq/L with SGLT2is (95% CI: − 0.08 to 0.0).¹⁶ However, several studies suggest that the combination of SGLT2i therapy with a RAASi mitigates hyperkalemia.⁷,17 A claims-based data analysis to ascertain the risk of hyperkalemia in people using SGLT2is, glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, and dipeptidyl peptidase-4 inhibitors (DPP4is) included 750,000 matched pairs and found the 3-year absolute risk for hyperkalemia was 4.6% with SGLT2is compared with 7.0% with DPP4is, and was 5.7% with glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists compared with 7.5% with DPP4is.¹⁸ The absolute risk for hyperkalemia is modest and the absolute risk reduction for hyperkalemia was greatest with SGLT2is. In a 2025 pharmacoepidemiology study, SGLT2is reduced the risk of hyperkalemia (HR: 0.89; 95% CI: 0.82-0.96), including reduced risk of hospitalization due to hyperkalemia as well as a lower risk of RAASi discontinuation (36% vs 45%; P 0.001).¹⁹

It is physiologically plausible that empagliflozin’s effects on potassium depend on the patient’s baseline potassium status—becoming more apparent when potassium levels are higher or when the kidney is under greater “potassium stress.”

Finerenone produces a relatively modest mean increase in serum potassium (approximately +0.18 mEq/L), roughly one-third less than that seen with spironolactone or aldosterone synthase inhibitors (approximately +0.3 mEq/L). This smaller potassium excursion may have limited the opportunity to detect a mitigating effect of empagliflozin in the present study. Accordingly, the absence of a demonstrable reduction in hyperkalemia risk should be viewed as hypothesis-generating rather than definitive, consistent with the possibility that SGLT2is exert their most visible protective effects when baseline potassium is higher or mineralocorticoid receptor activation is greater.

The disparity between the current and previous studies in the occurrence of hyperkalemia is complex and remains poorly understood. Experts note that dyskalemia is less common with SGLT2i use. This is because although SGLT2is might lower potassium, they also trigger pathways that limit potassium loss. Pathways that trigger potassium loss include the increased distal delivery of sodium and activation of RAS. However, this effect is transient and acute, and provokes a compensatory mechanism to limit sodium and therefore potassium losses by the kidney. NHE3 inhibition that increases urinary pH and increased urinary flow rate both stimulate potassium excretion in the distal nephron. Pathways that mitigate potassium losses are the following: dampening of RAS activation through tubuloglomerular feedback; reduced sympathetic activation; increased thiazide sensitive sodium chloride cotransporter activity via allosteric modification of the calcium sensing receptor; and increased paracrine signaled electroneutral sodium chloride reabsorption via alpha ketoglutarate. Understandably, hypokalemia is commonly seen with SGLT2is. Furthermore, animal studies suggest that the effect of SGLT2is on urinary potassium losses are attenuated in proportion to glucose-lowering effect of SGLT2is. We excluded patients with poorly controlled diabetes and our trial duration was 6 months. Thus, we might have missed an effect on hyperkalemia that might be evident in studies that are longer in duration. The lack of consistent findings across studies highlights a gap in our understanding regarding the ability of SGLT2is to mitigate the effects of hyperkalemia, and should prompt further evaluation of the mechanisms underlying this potential effect.
