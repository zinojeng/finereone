# CONFIDENCE in the safety and efficacy of dual therapy with an SGLT-2 inhibitor and finerenone in patients with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes

## 1 | Introduction

For nearly two decades, the only evidence-based pharmacological approaches to offer cardiorenal protection in high-risk patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes (T2D) were the optimization of glycaemic and blood pressure (BP) control together with the administration of an agent blocking the renin-angiotensin system (RAS) titrated up to the maximum-labelled or maximally tolerated doses. Over the last 6 years, newer therapies have been approved with the indication of cardiorenal protection in patients with diabetic kidney disease (DKD).

A series of three landmark clinical trials with sodium-glucose co-transporter type 2 (SGLT-2) inhibitors conducted specifically in CKD patients were prematurely completed for reasons of efficacy. Relative to placebo, SGLT-2 inhibitors induced a consistent and impressive improvement in cardiovascular and kidney outcomes, similar in magnitude in patients with and without T2D. Similarly, in a large phase 3 clinical-trial programme involving approximately 13,000 patients with T2D across the entire spectrum of CKD, the non-steroidal mineralocorticoid-receptor antagonist (MRA) finerenone provoked a placebo-subtracted reduction of **14% in the composite cardiovascular outcome** and a placebo-subtracted reduction of **23% in the composite kidney outcome**.

This article critically evaluates the main findings of the recently published **CONFIDENCE** (Combination Effect of Finerenone and Empagliflozin in Participants with Chronic Kidney Disease and Type 2 Diabetes Using a Urinary Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio Endpoint) trial — a phase 2 clinical trial aiming to elucidate whether dual therapy with an SGLT-2 inhibitor and finerenone is safe and more effective than either monotherapy in lowering albuminuria in patients with DKD.

## 2 | Preliminary evidence suggesting a synergistic effect of SGLT-2 inhibitors and finerenone

SGLT-2 inhibitors and finerenone have complementary modes of action, generating the hypothesis that their combined use may result in an over-additive cardiorenal protective effect relative to monotherapy.

**Preclinical evidence:** In a preclinical study, rats with hypertension-induced end-organ damage received once-daily oral treatment with placebo, low and high doses of empagliflozin (3 and 10 mg/kg), low and high doses of finerenone (1 and 3 mg/g), or the combination of low doses of empagliflozin and finerenone. Over 7 weeks of follow-up, the survival rate was significantly higher in the low-dose combination group. Monotherapy either with empagliflozin or with finerenone lowered albuminuria in a dose-dependent manner. However, it was the combination therapy that provoked a premature, sustained, and over-additive reduction in albuminuria. As compared with either monotherapy, dual therapy with low doses of empagliflozin and finerenone resulted in greater regression of histopathological lesions of inflammation and fibrosis in both the heart and the kidneys.

**Post-hoc clinical-trial evidence:** Of the 13,026 patients participating in the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials, **877 patients (6.7%)** were being treated with an SGLT-2 inhibitor at baseline. The placebo-subtracted improvement in the composite cardiovascular outcome with finerenone was similar in magnitude in patients receiving (**HR 0.67; 95% CI 0.42–1.07**) and not receiving (**HR 0.87; 95% CI 0.79–0.96**) simultaneous therapy with an SGLT-2 inhibitor (P_interaction = 0.46). Similarly, the benefit of finerenone versus placebo on the composite kidney outcome was comparable between patients using (**HR 0.42; 95% CI 0.16–1.08**) and not using (**HR 0.80; 95% CI 0.69–0.92**) an SGLT-2 inhibitor at baseline (P_interaction = 0.29).

Conversely, of the 4304 patients enrolled in the DAPA-CKD trial, **229 patients (5.3%)** were receiving background treatment with a conventional steroidal MRA (spironolactone or eplerenone). The benefit of dapagliflozin versus placebo on the primary composite cardiorenal outcome was consistent in patients prescribed (**HR 0.86; 95% CI 0.40–1.47**) and not prescribed steroidal MRAs (P_interaction = 0.59). The incidence of serious adverse events leading to permanent discontinuation was similar between treatment groups, regardless of steroidal MRA use at baseline.

These subgroup comparisons are limited by their post-hoc nature and limited statistical power and cannot demonstrate direct cause-and-effect risk associations.

## 3 | Direct evidence for the safety and efficacy of combination therapy — the CONFIDENCE trial

In the CONFIDENCE trial, **800 patients** with albuminuric CKD and T2D, already taking standard-of-care treatment with a RAS-blocker, were randomly assigned in a **1:1:1 ratio** to receive:
- Finerenone (10–20 mg/day) with empagliflozin-matching placebo, or
- Empagliflozin (10 mg/day) with finerenone-matching placebo, or
- Combination therapy with finerenone and empagliflozin

over a follow-up period of **180 days**.

Randomization was stratified per protocol according to eGFR (<60 and ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²) and UACR (≤850 and >850 mg/g) at screening. At baseline, mean eGFR was **54.4 ± 17.1 mL/min/1.73 m²** and median UACR was **579 mg/g**. Approximately **23%** of patients were being treated with GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP1-RAs); patients were eligible only if the GLP1-RA dose was constant over 4 weeks prior to enrollment, and up-titration of GLP1-RAs after randomization was prohibited.

### Primary efficacy outcome (UACR reduction)

- The proportional reduction in UACR over the trial was **29% higher with dual therapy than with finerenone alone** (least-squares mean ratio of the difference in change from baseline: **0.71; 95% CI 0.61–0.82**).
- The reduction was **32% higher than with empagliflozin alone** (least-squares mean ratio: **0.68; 95% CI 0.59–0.79**). *(Table 1 in the article states "31% greater" for the empagliflozin comparison; the main text states "32% higher" — both refer to the ratio 0.68, 95% CI 0.59–0.79.)*

The more pronounced albuminuria-lowering effect of combination therapy occurred early, over the first 30 days of follow-up. Within a 30-day follow-up period after treatment discontinuation (between days 180 and 210), UACR levels increased but still remained lower than baseline values in the combination-treatment and finerenone groups.

### Safety outcomes

- **Blood pressure:** The reduction in office systolic BP with each monotherapy was less than half of the BP-lowering effect of the combination. However, symptomatic hypotension was recorded in only **three patients** in the combination-treatment group.
- **eGFR decline:** A ≥30% decline in eGFR over the first 30 days occurred more commonly with combination therapy (**6.3%**) than with finerenone alone (**3.8%**) or empagliflozin alone (**1.1%**). This early eGFR decline was most likely haemodynamically mediated and fully reversible with treatment discontinuation after trial termination.
- **Potassium/hyperkalaemia:** Small elevations in serum potassium were observed in the combination-treatment and finerenone groups; empagliflozin monotherapy had only minimal impact on potassium homeostasis. Notably, the incidence of investigator-reported or central laboratory-determined hyperkalaemia (serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L, or >5.5 and <6.0 mmol/L) was **numerically lower with combination therapy than with finerenone alone**. This is consistent with a meta-analysis of six randomized trials involving **49,875 patients** with T2D, in which SGLT-2 inhibitors lowered the risk of serious hyperkalaemia by **16%** relative to placebo, an effect consistent across subgroups including patients receiving MRA treatment.

### Why an albuminuria endpoint matters

A phase 3 trial adequately powered to detect between-group differences in "hard" kidney outcomes (composite of kidney failure, doubling of serum creatinine, or renal death) would require randomization of **~41,000 patients**, so a definitive outcome trial is not anticipated in the foreseeable future.

However, an early albuminuria-lowering effect is the principal mediator of the long-term benefits of both SGLT-2 inhibitors and finerenone on hard kidney outcomes. In a **2019 meta-analysis of 41 trials** providing patient-level data for **29,979 participants**, 3935 patients met the composite endpoint (treated kidney failure, sustained eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m², or doubling of serum creatinine) over a median follow-up of **3.4 years**. An early reduction of **≥30% in albuminuria** over the first 6 months was associated with a **27% reduction in long-term risk** for the composite clinical endpoint (**HR 0.73; 95% CI 0.67–0.81**), a risk association that was strengthened when restricted to patients with high or very high baseline albuminuria.

### Clinical implications

International guidelines recommend serial follow-up UACR testing to assess residual cardiorenal risk and guide treatment intensification, but real-world data suggest albuminuria testing is commonly omitted. A step-by-step approach to implementing guideline-directed therapies may prolong time to effective treatment and aggravate therapeutic inertia. Evidence from CONFIDENCE indicates that **simultaneous initiation** of an SGLT-2 inhibitor and finerenone leads to a greater and more rapid albuminuria-lowering effect than either monotherapy.

## 4 | Conclusion

The CONFIDENCE trial answered a crucial question regarding the safety and potential over-additive efficacy of combination therapy with an SGLT-2 inhibitor and the non-steroidal MRA finerenone in patients with albuminuric CKD associated with T2D. Compared with either monotherapy, combined empagliflozin and finerenone provoked a greater reduction in albuminuria, providing evidence for an over-additive kidney-protective effect.

The proposed complementary mechanism: **empagliflozin** reduces intraglomerular pressure and improves tubular-glomerular feedback through natriuresis and osmotic diuresis, while **finerenone** exerts anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects within the kidney — targeting distinct but synergistic pathways involved in CKD progression.

Serious and unexpected adverse events (symptomatic hypotension, acute worsening of kidney function, permanent discontinuation due to hyperkalaemia) occurred rarely, demonstrating the safety of combination therapy. There are now stronger, more conclusive clinical-trial data that early and simultaneous initiation of these two pillars of therapy affords maximal kidney protection in high-risk patients with DKD. **"The time to change our daily clinical practice is now."**
