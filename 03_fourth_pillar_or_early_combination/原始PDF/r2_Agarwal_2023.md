# The foundation and the four pillars of treatment for cardiorenal protection in people with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes

## Introduction

Of the 8 billion people who share this planet, it is estimated that one in eight have chronic kidney disease (CKD). The prevalence of type 2 diabetes (T2D) is also estimated to be one in eight. According to the Centers for Disease Control, one in three people with T2D have CKD. CKD and T2D collaborate to increase the risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, cardiovascular morbidity (myocardial infarction, strokes, peripheral artery disease and hospitalizations for heart failure) and the progression of kidney disease to kidney failure. In the USA alone, 13% of people >18 years of age are estimated to have diabetes and 34.5% meet the criteria for prediabetes [1]. CKD and T2D together constitute a major public health burden that urgently needs strategies to address them.

## Building the foundation

The foundation of management of the cardiovascular and kidney complications of T2D rests on lifestyle modifications:
- cessation of tobacco smoking
- glycemic control (individualized level)
- lowering blood pressure (BP) to <130/80 mmHg
- management of dyslipidemia centered on statins
- a healthy diet with low glycemic index and restricted sodium
- maintenance of a healthy weight and optimizing physical behaviors

Physical behaviors are summarized in the 2022 guidelines by the six S's: limit **sitting**, increase **stepping**, improve **sleep** duration and quality, encourage at least 150 min/week of **sweating**, improve **strength** through resistance exercise and reduce **sarcopenia** [2]. Studies initiated at the Steno Diabetes Center 3 decades ago demonstrate that reducing cardiorenal risk is optimal when the risk factor intervention is multifactorial [3].

### Albuminuria as a cardiorenal risk factor

Studies pioneered by Carl Erik Mogensen at the Steno Diabetes Center demonstrated the association of excess mortality in people with T2D and albuminuria. In a landmark 1982 study, he demonstrated among just 300 people with T2D followed for 10 years that the urinary concentration of albumin is associated with excess mortality [4]. Subsequently, urinary albumin concentration was paired with urinary creatinine to refine the predictive performance of the urinary albumin:creatinine ratio (UACR) for a wide array of outcomes, including all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, kidney disease progression and risk of acute kidney injury [5].

The Framingham Heart Study established that BP and LDL cholesterol are both prognostic cardiovascular risk factors — and clinically indispensable because they are modifiable. Framingham also recognized that any degree of proteinuria (even by dipstick) is a cardiovascular risk factor [6], and several large studies identified that UACR is a cardiorenal risk factor [5]. We now have direct clinical trial evidence that targeting the mineralocorticoid receptor in patients with T2D with elevated UACR (≥30 mg/g creatinine) can mitigate cardiovascular and kidney disease progression [7]. Thus, like BP and LDL cholesterol, UACR is a **modifiable** cardiovascular risk factor.

## Building the pillars of treatment on the foundation

There are three established pillars of therapy and a fourth emerging pillar in the management of CKD in people with T2D.

### The first pillar — ARBs

In people with T2D and very high albuminuria, losartan or irbesartan slows kidney disease progression. First convincingly demonstrated in 2001 by the RENAAL trial [8] and the Irbesartan Diabetic Nephropathy Trial (IDNT) [9]. Primary endpoint in each: time to first doubling of serum creatinine, end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) or death from any cause.
- Relative risk reduction: **16% in RENAAL** [8], **19% in IDNT** [9]
- RENAAL demonstrated a **28% relative risk reduction** for occurrence of ESKD (not statistically significant in IDNT)
- Each study demonstrated reduction in hospitalization for heart failure

Angiotensin receptor blockers became the standard of care to protect from kidney disease progression in people with T2D and albuminuria.

### The second pillar — SGLT2 inhibitors

For the next 18 years there were many unsuccessful attempts to end cardiorenal disease in T2D. In 2019, cardiorenal protection with the SGLT2 inhibitor canagliflozin in people with T2D and very high albuminuria led to approval for this indication [10]. Dapagliflozin-based treatments extended this option to people without diabetes [11], providing strong evidence that glycemic control alone was not the sole mechanism of SGLT2 inhibitor cardiorenal protection.

### The third pillar — finerenone (nonsteroidal MRA)

In 2020, trials with finerenone, a selective nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA), demonstrated both kidney and cardiovascular protection in one trial [12]. The renal endpoint was distinct from the cardiovascular endpoint and the trial was powered to evaluate each independently.
- **18% relative risk reduction** in this trial of **5734 patients**
- Absolute risk reduction ≈3% in the renal endpoint → **29 patients treated for 3 years** would prevent one kidney failure outcome
- CREDENCE had a **30% relative risk reduction**; FIDELIO-DKD had an **18%** reduction, leading to a perception the third pillar was weaker than the second

However, there were important differences in patients recruited and outcomes. When matched for baseline characteristics and outcomes, the cardiorenal benefits of the two molecules were similar [13]. The EMPA-KIDNEY trial was prematurely stopped for efficacy; it includes patients with no albuminuria and those not on an ACE inhibitor or ARB — a group not represented in earlier trials [14].

### The fourth emerging pillar — GLP-1 receptor agonists

GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP1RAs) were developed for glycemic control in T2D. They improve glycemic control by stimulating glucose-dependent insulin release via incretin, and provoke weight loss by delaying gastric emptying and effects on the satiety center. In people with albuminuria and T2D they reduce the albumin excretion rate [15].
- In a meta-analysis of 11 trials, GLP1RAs reduce the risk of myocardial infarction by **11%** [16]
- **No protection from heart failure** is seen with these agents — in sharp contrast to SGLT2 inhibitors and finerenone
- Both SGLT2 inhibitors and finerenone reduce the risk of hospitalization for heart failure by **25–35%** [17, 18]

The antiatherosclerotic cardiovascular protection of GLP1RAs appears **complementary** to SGLT2 inhibitors. Kidney failure outcomes are being tested in the **FLOW trial** (NCT03819153) [19]; the definitive place of this class remains to be established.

The dual GLP1RA/GIP activator **tirzepatide** has salutary effects on glycemic control, weight loss and BP reduction [20]. In post hoc analyses of a randomized trial in people with T2D at high cardiovascular risk, reductions in UACR and slowing of eGFR decline were reported. Tirzepatide was recently approved by the US FDA for T2D; definitive trials are needed to establish kidney and cardiovascular protection.

This class is critically important for glycemic control, especially in more advanced CKD, where SGLT2 inhibitors lose efficacy and metformin may be contraindicated, whereas GLP1RAs retain their efficacy [2].

## Barriers to building the house

Education, adherence and access are the key barriers to implementation. Despite decades-old guidelines, screening of kidney disease is suboptimal. In an analysis of electronic health record data from **513,165 adults with T2D** receiving primary care from 24 healthcare organizations and 1164 clinical practice sites, the 1-year median testing rate across organizations was 51.6% for both UACR and eGFR, 89.5% for eGFR and 52.9% for UACR. The average prevalence of detected elevated albuminuria increased linearly with UACR testing rates, with estimated prevalence of **6%, 15% and 30%** at UACR testing rates of 20%, 50% and 100%, respectively [21]. Thus a large fraction of patients with CKD and T2D are left undiagnosed.

Access to therapies is a complex barrier. In the USA, prior authorization is still required for all accepted first pillars of care for many health plans. In India, dapagliflozin is available as a generic drug at a price similar to some antihypertensives; in Brazil, the healthcare system provides dapagliflozin at no cost. Adherence to treatments for chronic diseases is even more complicated.

Lifestyle modifications are the foundation for all people with T2D and CKD — they cost little but adherence is difficult. Building the pillars of therapy is easy but associated with increasing costs from the first to the fourth pillar. Randomized trials are testing the value of combination therapies. As an example, the **CONFIDENCE trial** will randomize **807 patients** with T2D, eGFR >25 mL/min/1.73 m², UACR >300 mg/g and a maximally tolerated ACE inhibitor or ARB to either empagliflozin alone (10 mg/day), finerenone alone (target 20 mg/day) or the combination, to test whether combination therapy has greater UACR-lowering ability from baseline to 6 months compared with single treatments [22]. Secondary endpoints include changes in systolic BP, potassium and eGFR; episodes of hyperkalemia, acute kidney injury and symptomatic hypotension will be recorded.

## The commissioned articles and their contributions

The editors commissioned a series of five articles:
- **Bonifant and Weir** [23]: BP measurement techniques, treatment goals/targets, knowledge gaps in T2D and proteinuric CKD, and pharmacological choices; advocate individualizing goals — one size does not fit all.
- **Lo et al.** [24]: development of MRAs from steroidal to nonsteroidal for prevention of cardiorenal outcomes in CKD, with emphasis on heart failure.
- **Gregg and Navaneethan** [25]: strategies for potassium control in the setting of MRAs; conservative strategies (increased monitoring, loop or thiazide diuretics) may be more practical than K binders.
- **Zhang et al.** [26]: methodology of guideline development and evidence-based recommendations for screening frequency, lifestyle modifications, and balancing older vs newer therapies.
- **Nee et al.** [27]: getting guideline-approved therapies to the bedside; a multifaceted action plan from patient to policy.

## Conclusions

CKD in T2D is a self-evident risk factor for kidney failure, but more importantly a **modifiable cardiovascular disease risk factor**. To mitigate risk we need to act early. Implementation and adherence to guidelines to screen for UACR at the time of T2D diagnosis and annually thereafter is critical to identify patients at a stage of kidney injury (not yet kidney failure). The pillars of care — ARBs or ACE inhibitors, SGLT2 inhibitors, finerenone and GLP1RAs — are built on the foundation of lifestyle modifications. From a desert of therapeutic options prior to 2019, we are now rich with multiple strategies. Education, adherence and access to these treatments provide a tremendous opportunity. **There is no time for therapeutic inertia — the time to act is now.**
