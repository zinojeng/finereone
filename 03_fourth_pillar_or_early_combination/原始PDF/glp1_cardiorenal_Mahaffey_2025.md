## Abstract

### Background and Aims
In the FLOW trial, semaglutide reduced the risks of kidney and cardiovascular (CV) outcomes and death in participants with type 2 diabetes (T2D) and chronic kidney disease (CKD). These prespecified analyses assessed the effects of semaglutide on CV outcomes and death by CKD severity.

### Methods
Participants were randomized to subcutaneous semaglutide 1 mg or placebo weekly. The main outcome was a composite of CV death, non-fatal myocardial infarction (MI), or non-fatal stroke (CV death/MI/stroke) as well as death due to any cause by baseline CKD severity. CKD was categorized by estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) < or ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m², urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) < or ≥300 mg/g, or Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) risk classification.

### Results
Three thousand, five hundred and thirty-three participants were randomized with a median follow-up of 3.4 years. Low/moderate KDIGO risk was present in 242 (6.8%), while 878 (24.9%) had high and 2412 (68.3%) had very high KDIGO risk. Semaglutide reduced CV death/MI/stroke by 18% [hazard ratio (HR) 0.82 (95% confidence interval 0.68–0.98); P = .03], with consistency across eGFR categories, UACR levels, and KDIGO risk classification (all P-interaction > .13). Death due to any cause was reduced by 20% [HR 0.80 (0.67–0.95); P = .01], with consistency across eGFR categories and KDIGO risk class (P-interaction .21 and .23, respectively). The P-interaction treatment effect for death due to any cause by UACR was .01 [<300 mg/g HR 1.17 (0.83–1.65); ≥300 mg/g HR 0.70 (0.57–0.85)].

### Conclusions
Semaglutide significantly reduced the risk of CV death/MI/stroke regardless of baseline CKD severity in participants with type 2 diabetes.

**Keywords:** Semaglutide • Cardiovascular outcomes • Type 2 diabetes • Chronic kidney disease

## Introduction

People with T2D and CKD are at high risk for CV events, including CV death, MI, and stroke, as well as death due to any cause. The high risk for CV events and mortality is strongly correlated with the severity of CKD, defined by eGFR, UACR, or KDIGO risk classification using both eGFR and UACR.

Renin–angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors, sodium–glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2i), and finerenone provide both kidney protection and reduction of risk of CV events in patients with T2D and high CV risk or CKD. These therapies are an essential part of a foundation of optimal care as recommended by clinical practice guidelines. Despite these evidence-based therapies, there is a clear unmet need for additional effective therapies as a high residual risk is still present. Treatment with GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) has reduced CV risk in several CV outcomes trials.

A dedicated kidney outcomes trial, the FLOW (Evaluate Renal Function with Semaglutide Once Weekly) trial, showed that semaglutide reduced the risk of major kidney events by 24%, major CV events by 18%, and death due to any cause by 20%, when compared with placebo, in participants with T2D and CKD.

In the prespecified analyses reported here, the composite of CV death/MI/stroke, death due to any cause, and safety outcomes were examined by baseline CKD status.

---

## Methods

### Study design
The design and primary results of the FLOW trial have been published previously. The sponsor performed the analyses. Kidney, CV, and death events had been validated by an independent statistical group.

Adults with T2D (HbA1c ≤10%) and CKD were eligible, defined as:
- eGFR 50–75 mL/min/1.73 m² (CKD-EPI 2009 formula) and UACR >300 to <5000 mg/g, **or**
- eGFR 25–<50 mL/min/1.73 m² and UACR >100 to <5000 mg/g,

while receiving a stable maximal labelled or tolerated dose of RAS inhibitors (ACE inhibitor or ARB).

Participants were randomly assigned 1:1 to subcutaneous semaglutide 1.0 mg or matching placebo once weekly. The trial was stopped early for efficacy based on a recommendation from the independent Data Monitoring Committee.

### Study outcomes
The main outcome was a composite of CV death/MI/stroke (a secondary confirmatory endpoint in the testing hierarchy). Other outcomes included death due to any cause and safety events. All major CV events and cause of death were adjudicated by an independent committee blinded to therapy. Safety was assessed as the number and nature of serious adverse events (SAEs) or adverse events (AEs) leading to discontinuation.

### Baseline kidney function
Participants were grouped by baseline CKD status by three methods: (i) eGFR (CKD-EPI 2009) <60 or ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²; (ii) UACR <300 or ≥300 mg/g; and (iii) KDIGO risk classification (low, moderate, high, very high). Baseline data are from central laboratory analysis only. UACR was calculated as the geometric mean of two first-morning urine voids. No imputations were performed for missing data.

### Statistical analysis
Efficacy analyses used the intention-to-treat principle with in-trial data. Time-to-event outcomes were analysed using a stratified Cox proportional hazards model with randomized treatment as a fixed factor and stratified by SGLT2i use at baseline. For the primary outcome, HR, 95% CI, and P-value were adjusted for the group sequential design using likelihood ratio ordering. Interaction P-values evaluated heterogeneity of treatment effect. No adjustment for multiple comparisons; two-sided P < 0.05 considered significant. Absolute risk difference estimated using a generalized linear regression model with identity link on pseudo-observations from the Aalen–Johansen estimate at Week 156. NNT = 1/(cumulative incidence for placebo − cumulative incidence for semaglutide 1.0 mg). Analyses performed with SAS version 9.4 TS1M5.

## Results

In total, 3533 participants were randomly assigned to semaglutide or placebo with a mean follow-up of 3.4 years. Vital status was known in 98.6% of participants; adherence to randomized treatment was 89%. Baseline CKD status was known for 3532 participants (99.97%).

**Baseline CKD distribution:**
- eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m²: 2813 (79.6%); eGFR ≥60: 719 (20.4%)
- UACR <300 mg/g: 1113 (31.5%); UACR ≥300 mg/g: 2419 (68.5%)
- KDIGO low/moderate risk: 242 (6.8%); high risk: 878 (24.9%); very high risk: 2412 (68.3%)

Participants with higher KDIGO risk classes tended to be slightly older, were more likely to be male, and had slightly lower HDL cholesterol.

### CV death/MI/stroke composite
Rates of CV death/MI/stroke by KDIGO class over median (range) follow-up 3.4 years (0–4.5): 19 (7.9%) low/moderate risk, 98 (11.2%) high risk, 349 (14.5%) very high risk.

In the overall population, semaglutide reduced the composite of CV death/MI/stroke vs. placebo [**HR 0.82 (95% CI 0.68–0.98)**]. Consistent treatment effects were observed across all CKD categories defined by eGFR, UACR, or KDIGO risk class (P-interaction > .05). More refined eGFR subgroups (<30, ≥30–<45, ≥45–<60, ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m²) had small numbers and broad CIs, but non-significant P-interaction values.

### Individual components
The number of MI and stroke events was lower than the number of CV deaths. Trends toward fewer MI events and more stroke events were observed with semaglutide. Consistent treatment effects were observed, with all P-interaction values > .05 except non-fatal MI by eGFR (P-interaction .04): benefit appeared less in eGFR <60 vs. ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m² [HR 0.94 (95% CI 0.63–1.39) vs. HR 0.28 (95% CI 0.09–0.87)]. A trend toward fewer MIs (52 vs. 64) but more strokes (63 vs. 51) was observed with semaglutide vs. placebo.

### All-cause mortality
All-cause mortality was reduced by semaglutide overall [**HR 0.80 (95% CI 0.67–0.95)**], with consistent results across CKD categories defined by eGFR (P-interaction .21) and KDIGO risk class (P-interaction .23). For UACR:
- UACR ≥300 mg/g: HR 0.70 (95% CI 0.57–0.85) — favoured semaglutide
- UACR <300 mg/g: HR 1.17 (95% CI 0.83–1.65) — trend toward higher mortality with semaglutide
- **P-interaction .01**

### KDIGO heat map (Figure 5)
Similar reductions with semaglutide across KDIGO risk class for the composite of CV death/MI/stroke, with HRs ranging from 0.67 to 0.84 and non-significant P-interaction (.79).

### Absolute risk reduction and NNT (Week 156)
- CV death/MI/stroke: ARR −0.02 (95% CI −0.04 to −0.002; P = .035); **NNT 45 (95% CI 23–623)**
- Death due to any cause: ARR −0.03 (95% CI −0.05 to −0.004; P = .019); **NNT 39 (95% CI 21–238)**

### Safety (Table 2, by KDIGO risk class)
Participants with higher KDIGO risk class had more SAEs and fatal events. Within each KDIGO category, the proportions of participants with SAEs were numerically lower for semaglutide than placebo.

## Discussion

In FLOW, subcutaneous semaglutide 1.0 mg once weekly slowed the decline in kidney function, improved kidney and CV outcomes, and reduced death from any cause vs. placebo. The present analyses support a clear and generally consistent benefit on CV outcomes and all-cause mortality across groups with varying CKD severity at baseline defined by eGFR, UACR, and KDIGO risk class.

Participants with lower eGFR, higher UACR, or higher KDIGO risk class had higher incidence of CV outcomes and all-cause mortality. In the placebo group, CV death/MI/stroke over median 3.4 years ranged from 9.2% (KDIGO low/moderate) to 15.5% (KDIGO very high); death due to any cause ranged from 7.6% (low/moderate) to 17.4% (very high). Compared with other dedicated kidney trials like CREDENCE, the FLOW population was at much higher risk, with an approximate 50% higher incidence of CV death/MI/stroke and death from any cause (follow-up 3.4 vs. 2.6 years).

FLOW is the first dedicated kidney outcomes trial in participants with T2D and CKD to evaluate a GLP-1RA. Results were observed in a population receiving maximal labelled/tolerated ACE inhibitors or ARBs, intensive lipid-lowering therapy (>80% of participants), and antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy. 15.6% of participants were receiving SGLT2i at baseline; none were receiving finerenone (FLOW began enrolling in 2019, before finerenone trials completed).

A prior systematic review showed CV reductions with GLP-1RAs in participants with eGFR ≥60 but not <60 mL/min/1.73 m²; FLOW now clearly defines benefit in those with lower eGFR. No heterogeneity in treatment effect by CKD status has been observed for SGLT2i or finerenone on CV composite outcomes.

Of 15 interactions tested, two showed significant P-interaction < .05: death due to any cause by UACR (P .01, favouring semaglutide at higher UACR) and non-fatal MI by eGFR (P .04, larger effect at higher eGFR). The authors are not aware of biologically plausible mechanisms and, given the number of interactions tested without correction for multiplicity and opposite directions of effect, believe these are likely due to chance.

### Limitations
FLOW was not designed with adequate events/power to definitively evaluate treatment effects in subgroups defined by CKD severity; CIs are wide though no statistically significant interaction was observed for the primary composite. CKD was not analysed as a continuous variable due to small numbers of events at the extremes. The two significant interactions (opposite directions) are probably due to chance. Results are only applicable to populations reflective of FLOW inclusion criteria.

### Conclusion
In participants with T2D and CKD, semaglutide reduced the risk of CV death/MI/stroke across subgroups defined by different levels of eGFR, albuminuria, or KDIGO risk class at baseline, and reduced the risk of death from any cause consistently in subgroups defined by eGFR and KDIGO risk. Semaglutide should be considered as part of the therapeutic strategy to reduce CV risk in people living with T2D and CKD.
