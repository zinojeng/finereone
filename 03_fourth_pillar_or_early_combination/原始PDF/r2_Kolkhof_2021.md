## Abstract (verbatim, Europe PMC)

**Introduction**: The nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) antagonist finerenone and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors have demonstrated clinical benefits in CKD patients with type 2 diabetes. Clinical data analyzing the potential value of a combination therapy are currently limited. We therefore investigated cardiorenal protection of respective mono- and combination therapy in a preclinical model of hypertension-induced end-organ damage.

**Methods**: Cardiovascular (CV) morbidity and mortality were studied in hypertensive, N(ω)-nitro-L-arginine methyl ester (L-NAME)-treated, renin-transgenic (mRen2)27 rats. Rats (10- to 11-week-old females, n = 13–17/group) were treated once daily orally for up to 7 weeks with placebo, finerenone (1 and 3 mg/kg), empagliflozin (3 and 10 mg/kg), or a combination of the respective low doses. Key outcome parameters included mortality, proteinuria, plasma creatinine and uric acid, blood pressure, and cardiac and renal histology.

**Results**: Placebo-treated rats demonstrated a 50% survival rate over the course of 7 weeks. Drug treatment resulted in variable degrees of survival benefit, most prominently in the low-dose combination group with a survival benefit of 93%. Monotherapies of finerenone or empagliflozin dose-dependently reduced proteinuria, while low-dose combination revealed an early, sustained, and over-additive reduction in proteinuria. Empagliflozin induced a strong and dose-dependent increase in urinary glucose excretion which was not influenced by finerenone coadministration in the combination arm. Low-dose combination but not respective low-dose monotherapies significantly reduced plasma creatinine and plasma uric acid after 6 weeks. Treatment with finerenone and the low-dose combination significantly decreased systolic blood pressure after 5 weeks. There was a dose-dependent protection from cardiac and kidney fibrosis and vasculopathy with both agents, while low-dose combination therapy was more efficient than the respective monotherapy dosages on most cardiorenal histology parameters.

**Discussion/conclusions**: Nonsteroidal MR antagonism by finerenone and SGLT2 inhibition by empagliflozin confer CV protection in preclinical hypertension-induced cardiorenal disease. Combination of these 2 independent modes of action at low dosages revealed efficacious reduction in important functional parameters such as proteinuria and blood pressure, plasma markers including creatinine and uric acid, cardiac and renal lesions as determined by histopathology, and mortality indicating a strong potential for combined clinical use in cardiorenal patient populations.

**Keywords**: Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonism; Anti-fibrotic Effects; Cardiorenal Protection; Sodium-glucose Cotransporter-2 Inhibition

## Methods (summary)

- **Model**: Hypertensive, L-NAME–treated, renin-transgenic (mRen2)27 rats — a nondiabetic model of severe hypertension-induced cardiorenal end-organ damage.
- **Animals**: Female rats, 10–11 weeks old at start; n = 13–17 per group.
- **Treatment**: Once-daily oral dosing for up to 7 weeks.
- **Groups**:
  - Placebo
  - Finerenone 1 mg/kg (low dose)
  - Finerenone 3 mg/kg (high dose)
  - Empagliflozin 3 mg/kg (low dose)
  - Empagliflozin 10 mg/kg (high dose)
  - Low-dose combination = finerenone 1 mg/kg + empagliflozin 3 mg/kg
- **Outcomes**: Mortality/survival, proteinuria, plasma creatinine, plasma uric acid, systolic blood pressure, urinary glucose excretion, cardiac and renal histopathology.

## Key Results (numbers, traceable to PMC full text)

### Survival / mortality (7 weeks)
- Placebo: **50% survival**.
- Low-dose combination: **93% survival** (survival benefit; **p = 0.0064** vs placebo).
- Monotherapies produced variable, dose-dependent survival benefit (combination most prominent).

### Proteinuria (week 6, change vs placebo)
- Finerenone 1 mg/kg: **−27%** (not significant)
- Finerenone 3 mg/kg: **−87%** (p < 0.05)
- Empagliflozin 3 mg/kg: **−38%**
- Empagliflozin 10 mg/kg: **−64%**
- Low-dose combination: **−86%** (p < 0.05) — described as an **early, sustained, and over-additive** reduction (i.e., greater than expected from the two low-dose monotherapies).

### Systolic blood pressure (week 5)
- Placebo: **202.0 ± 6.8 mm Hg**
- Finerenone 1 mg/kg: **170.0 ± 9.0 mm Hg** (p < 0.05)
- Finerenone 3 mg/kg: **164.4 ± 4.7 mm Hg** (p < 0.01)
- Low-dose combination: **173.1 ± 7.7 mm Hg** (p < 0.05)
- (Empagliflozin monotherapy did not significantly lower SBP; BP reduction driven by finerenone.)

### Plasma creatinine (week 6)
- Only the **low-dose combination** significantly reduced plasma creatinine after 6 weeks; the respective low-dose monotherapies did not. (Exact numeric per-group values presented in the article's figures, not restated in the Results text.)

### Plasma uric acid (week 6)
- **Finerenone 3 mg/kg** and the **low-dose combination** both significantly decreased plasma uric acid after 6 weeks. (Figure data.)

### Urinary glucose excretion
- Empagliflozin **strongly and dose-dependently** increased the urinary glucose-to-creatinine ratio (glycosuria).
- Finerenone had **no effect** on urinary glucose excretion, and did **not** influence empagliflozin-induced glycosuria in the combination arm (confirming independent modes of action).

### Metabolic parameters (nondiabetic model)
- **No significant difference in body weight gain** among placebo, finerenone, empagliflozin, and combination groups.
- Both compounds, alone and in combination, had **no significant influence on fasted plasma glucose** in this nondiabetic animal model.

### Histopathology
- Dose-dependent protection from **cardiac and renal fibrosis and vasculopathy** with both agents.
- **Low-dose combination was more efficient than the respective low-dose monotherapies on most cardiorenal histology parameters** (myocardial/renal fibrosis, vasculopathy, glomerulopathy).
- Semiquantitative per-group histology scores are presented in the article's figures; the Results narrative reports relative/dose-dependent comparisons rather than every individual score.

## Discussion (opening, verbatim)

> "This is the first study describing beneficial effects of a combination of the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone with SGLT2i in preclinical nondiabetic cardiorenal disease. The major outcomes of the current preclinical study are that nonsteroidal MR antagonism by finerenone and SGLT2i by empagliflozin confer pronounced CV protection in hypertension-induced cardiorenal disease."

## Conclusions

Combining low-dose finerenone (nonsteroidal MRA) with low-dose empagliflozin (SGLT2i) — two independent mechanisms of action — produced over-additive/complementary benefits on proteinuria, blood pressure, plasma creatinine and uric acid, cardiac and renal histopathology, and mortality in a nondiabetic, hypertension-induced cardiorenal disease rat model. The findings provide preclinical rationale for combined clinical use of nonsteroidal MRA + SGLT2 inhibition in cardiorenal patient populations. This preclinical work predates and supports the clinical CONFIDENCE trial concept of finerenone + empagliflozin combination therapy.
