# Efficacy of combined SGLT2 inhibitors and finerenone in chronic kidney disease: a systematic review and meta-analysis

**Wen C-F, Chuang M-H, Wu V-C, Chen J-Y.** Front. Pharmacol. 2026;17:1803971. doi:10.3389/fphar.2026.1803971. PMID 42212277.

Affiliations: Chi Mei Medical Center, Tainan; National Taiwan University Hospital; Chia Nan University of Pharmacy and Science; National Cheng Kung University, Tainan, Taiwan.

## Abstract

**Background:** SGLT2 inhibitors (SGLT2is) and finerenone have each demonstrated individual efficacy in reducing cardiorenal events among patients with diabetic CKD. The additive benefits and safety profile of combining these agents remain unclear.

**Methods:** Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials and observational studies comparing finerenone plus SGLT2is versus monotherapy. Primary outcomes: all-cause mortality, MACEs, kidney-specific composite outcomes, and hyperkalemia risk. Pooled odds ratios (OR) and 95% CIs calculated using a random-effects model.

**Results:** Eight studies (N = 1,580 publications identified; 8 studies included; 71,567 participants) were included. Compared with finerenone monotherapy, combination therapy significantly reduced:
- All-cause mortality: **OR 0.58; 95% CI 0.36–0.93**
- MACE risk: **OR 0.70; 95% CI 0.51–0.97**
- Major adverse kidney event (MAKE) risk: **OR 0.63; 95% CI 0.44–0.89**
- UACR reduction: greater vs finerenone monotherapy, mean difference 0.10 (equivalent to ~10% greater reduction; 95% CI 0.00–0.19; p = 0.045)

However, the combined group had a **higher risk of hyperkalemia compared to SGLT2i monotherapy (OR 3.00; 95% CI 2.50–3.61)**. No significant benefit was observed in composite kidney outcomes compared with SGLT2 inhibitors alone.

**Conclusion:** Combining finerenone with SGLT2i may improve survival and reduce risks of MACEs and MAKEs compared with finerenone monotherapy in patients with diabetic CKD. Findings support careful consideration of dual therapy, especially in high-risk populations.

**Keywords:** cardiovascular outcome, chronic kidney disease, finerenone, hyperkalemia, meta-analysis, sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitor

**PROSPERO Registration:** CRD420251023918

## Introduction

SGLT2is reduce intraglomerular pressure, promote natriuresis, and exert anti-inflammatory effects, lowering risk of death, MACEs, and MAKEs. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs), particularly finerenone, offer complementary renoprotective benefits through anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic mechanisms. Unlike steroidal MRAs, finerenone provides a targeted reduction in albuminuria with a lower risk of hyperkalemia. Emerging studies suggest combining SGLT2is with finerenone may provide additional benefits in patients with CKD (Agarwal et al., 2025), but discrepancies in study designs and reported outcomes leave the clinical impact unclear. This meta-analysis evaluates the impact of combined SGLT2i and finerenone therapy on mortality, MACEs, MAKEs, and reduction in proteinuria.

## Methods

- Conducted per PRISMA 2020; registered in PROSPERO (CRD420251023918).
- Systematic search of PubMed, EMBASE, and Cochrane Library from database inception to **6 June 2025**, without language/geographic limits.
- **Inclusion criteria:** adults (≥18 yr) with CKD; intervention = combination therapy (finerenone + SGLT2i); comparator = monotherapy with either finerenone or SGLT2i; outcomes = all-cause mortality, MACEs, MAKEs, UACR reduction, hyperkalemia.
- Eligible designs: RCTs and observational studies.
- **MACE** defined as CV death, non-fatal MI, non-fatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure.
- **MAKE** defined as ESKD requiring hemodialysis for >90 days, eGFR <15 mL/min/1.73 m², kidney transplantation, sustained ≥40% eGFR decline for >4 weeks, or death from kidney disease.
- **Hyperkalemia** defined as potassium level over 5 mmol/L.
- Continuity correction of 0.5 applied to arms reporting zero events.
- Quality: Cochrane RoB 2.0 for RCTs; ROBINS-I for observational studies.
- Random-effects model; ORs with 95% CIs; WMD for continuous UACR outcomes. Heterogeneity via chi-square and I². DerSimonian-Laird estimator (meta package in R v4.3.1).
- Trial sequential analysis (TSA) with O'Brien-Fleming alpha-spending; RRR of 20% assumed for mortality/MACE/MAKE; α = 0.05, power 80%.
- GRADE approach for certainty of evidence.

## Study Selection (PRISMA)

- Records identified: 1,580 (PubMed n=278; Embase n=246; Cochrane n=1,056).
- After duplicates removed (n=519): 1,061 screened.
- Excluded by title/abstract: 1,051.
- Full-text assessed: 10.
- Excluded (n=2): one HF study without CKD subgroup (Solomon et al., 2024); one atrial fibrillation only (Filippatos et al., 2021).
- **8 studies included** in analysis; 71,567 participants total.

## Included Studies (Table 1)

Five RCTs and three retrospective cohort studies. Mean age 60.0–70.7 years; female 24.8%–44.1%.

| Study (year) | Design | Total N | CKD definition (summary) |
|---|---|---|---|
| FIGARO-DKD (2021) | RCT, double-blind, multicenter | 3,990 | UACR 30–300 & eGFR 25–90, or UACR 300–5000 & eGFR >60 |
| FIDELIO-DKD (2022) | RCT, double-blind, multicenter | 2,968 | UACR 30–300 & eGFR 25–60 with DM retinopathy, or UACR 300–5000 & eGFR 25–75 |
| FIDELITY (2022) | Individual patient-level pooled data (FIDELIO+FIGARO) | 6,958 | UACR 30–5000 & eGFR ≥25 |
| Mårup et al. (2024) | Single-center prospective randomized open-label | 20 | UACR 150–2000 & eGFR 25–45 (finerenone + dapagliflozin) |
| Chuang et al. (2025) | Retrospective cohort | 47,743 | eGFR <60, proteinuria, UACR ≥30, or UPCR ≥150 |
| Hanouneh et al. (2024) | Retrospective cohort | 98 | UACR ≥30 & eGFR 25–90 |
| CONFIDENCE (2025) | RCT, double-blind | 800 | UACR 100–5000 & eGFR 30–90 (finerenone + empagliflozin) |
| Kovesdy et al. (2025) | Retrospective cohort | 15,948 | eGFR 15–60 & UACR ≥30 |

Five RCTs administered finerenone at 10–20 mg once daily (Chuang et al. did not report dosing). All eight studies excluded patients with CKD stage 5 or ESKD.

## Quality Assessment

- Three RCTs rated low risk of bias.
- Post hoc subgroup analysis (Filippatos et al., 2022) and Mårup et al. (2024): some concerns (no pre-specified protocol).
- Chuang et al. (2025): moderate risk (confounding control, medication adherence).
- Hanouneh et al. (2024): moderate risk (no pre-specified protocol).
- Kovesdy et al. (2025): serious risk (descriptive report, lacked prior registration).

## Primary and Secondary Outcomes

### Combination vs Finerenone monotherapy
- **All-cause mortality:** OR 0.58; 95% CI 0.36–0.93; p = 0.02; I² = 18.6% (Figure 2a). Studies pooled: FIDELITY, Chuang et al., CONFIDENCE.
- **MACE:** OR 0.70; 95% CI 0.51–0.97; p = 0.03; I² = 0% (Figure 2b).
- **MAKE:** OR 0.63; 95% CI 0.44–0.89; p < 0.01; I² = 0% (Figure 2c).
- **UACR reduction:** combined group mean reduction 54.3% ± 12.8% vs 44.4% ± 15.1% (finerenone alone); mean difference 0.10 (~10% greater); 95% CI 0.00–0.19; p = 0.045; I² = 99.9% (extreme heterogeneity) (Figure 3a).

### Combination vs SGLT2i monotherapy
- **All-cause mortality:** OR 0.73; 95% CI 0.47–1.13; p = 0.16 (not significant).
- **MACE:** OR 0.77; 95% CI 0.55–1.08; p = 0.14 (not significant).
- **MAKE:** OR 0.87; 95% CI 0.58–1.31; p = 0.51 (not significant).
- **UACR reduction:** Figure 3b (no significant additional benefit).
- **Hyperkalemia:** **OR 3.00; 95% CI 2.50–3.61; p < 0.01; I² = 0%** (higher risk with combination) (Figure 4). Studies: FIDELITY (OR 4.07), Chuang et al. (OR 2.95), CONFIDENCE (OR 2.63).

### Hyperkalemia, combination vs Finerenone monotherapy
- OR 1.09; 95% CI 0.52–2.28; p = 0.8252; I² = 95.1% (no significant difference).

Funnel plots suggested possible publication bias for several outcomes.

### GRADE
- Moderate certainty: all-cause mortality and UACR reduction.
- Low certainty: MACE and MAKE.
- Very low certainty: hyperkalemia risk.

### Trial Sequential Analysis
For all-cause mortality, MACE, and MAKE, cumulative Z curves did not cross trial sequential monitoring boundaries for benefit nor enter futility zone — further adequately powered RCTs needed.

## Discussion Highlights

- Finerenone reduces oxidative stress, inflammation, and fibrosis via non-hemodynamic pathways; SGLT2is improve glomerular hemodynamics, metabolic profiles, and tubuloglomerular feedback — biologically complementary mechanisms.
- Combination therapy showed no additional benefit vs SGLT2i monotherapy for mortality/MACE/MAKE, possibly because SGLT2is already provide substantial renoprotection; partial overlap in anti-inflammatory/antifibrotic pathways may attenuate additive effects; short follow-up and low event rates may reduce detectability.
- **Hyperkalemia:** SGLT2is reduce hyperkalemia risk via kaliuretic effects (increased distal sodium delivery, urine flow, elevated glucagon), whereas finerenone (MRA) is associated with impaired potassium excretion. From clinical perspective, hyperkalemia risk should be interpreted primarily relative to finerenone monotherapy, as MRA is the dominant driver of potassium retention. SGLT2i's potassium-lowering effect may not fully counterbalance finerenone's aldosterone-mediated reduction in distal potassium secretion; impaired distal tubular function in CKD may attenuate SGLT2i kaliuretic response.
- First meta-analysis following the CONFIDENCE trial (Agarwal et al., 2025) directly comparing combined therapy with SGLT2i and finerenone against monotherapy.

### Limitations
1. FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD combination data derived from subgroup analyses; baseline characteristics extracted from overall study population.
2. Many finerenone trials retrospectively identified SGLT2i use to define combination group (information bias).
3. Multiple SGLT2is with varying doses vs single MRA — clinical heterogeneity.
4. CONFIDENCE short follow-up (180 days) — possible underascertainment of long-term hard outcomes.
5. UACR reduction analysis based on pre-post changes within each group, not direct between-group comparison; may introduce bias (Cuijpers et al., 2017).

## Conclusion

Combined use of finerenone and SGLT2i offers greater cardiorenal protection than finerenone alone (reductions in mortality, MACEs, MAKEs, and UACR). However, the combination did not show additional benefit compared to SGLT2i monotherapy and was associated with an increased risk of hyperkalemia. Findings highlight both potential advantages and safety considerations, supporting individualized treatment decisions and further evaluation.
