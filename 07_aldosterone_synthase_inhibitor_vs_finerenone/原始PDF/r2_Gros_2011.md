# GPR30 Expression Is Required for the Mineralocorticoid Receptor-Independent Rapid Vascular Effects of Aldosterone

**Gros R, Ding Q, Sklar LA, Prossnitz EE, Arterburn JB, Chorazyczewski J, Feldman RD.**
*Hypertension.* 2011;57(3):442-451. doi:10.1161/HYPERTENSIONAHA.110.161653. PMID: 21242460. PMCID: PMC6626623.

> Attribution: Full text retrieved from PubMed Central (PMC6626623) via the PubMed MCP full-text service. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1161/HYPERTENSIONAHA.110.161653

## Abstract

It has been increasingly appreciated that steroids elicit acute vascular effects through rapid, so-called nongenomic signaling pathways. Though aldosterone, for example, has been demonstrated to mediate rapid vascular effects via both mineralocorticoid receptor-dependent and -independent pathways, the mechanism(s) of this mineralocorticoid receptor-independent effect of aldosterone is yet to be determined. For estrogen, its rapid effects have been reported to be, at least in part, mediated via the 7-transmembrane-spanning, G protein-coupled receptor GPR30. Previous studies have demonstrated common response outcomes in response to both aldosterone and estrogen on GPR30 expression, ie, activation of phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase-dependent contraction and extracellular signal-regulated kinase activation in vascular smooth muscle cells. The present studies were undertaken to test the hypothesis that the rapid response to aldosterone in smooth muscle is dependent on the availability of a GPR30-dependent signaling pathway. These findings not only reconcile differences in the literature for aldosterone response in freshly isolated versus cultured aortic smooth muscle cells but also suggest alternative therapeutic strategies for modulating aldosterone actions on the vasculature in vivo.

## Introduction

Aldosterone and other vasoactive steroids are important physiological and pathophysiological regulators of cardiovascular function. The canonical mechanisms for vasoactive steroids, such as aldosterone and estrogen, have focused on regulation of transcription via activation of their "traditional" cytosolic/nuclear mineralocorticoid and estrogen receptors. However, scientists have speculated that these and other vasoactive steroids might have transcriptionally independent effects mediating their rapid changes in smooth muscle contractile reactivity and regulation of cell growth/death, based on confounding findings going back more than half a century.

The mechanism(s) mediating rapid effects of steroids has remained a focus of controversy. Though the rapid effects of aldosterone and estrogen have been attributed to classical mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), estrogen receptor (ER), and ER receptor in some studies, the insensitivity of these rapid steroid actions to "classical receptor" antagonists has implicated additional mechanisms of action for these responses.

Recent studies have implicated GPR30 (also called GPER) as a mediator of the rapid effects of estrogens. GPR30, first characterized as an "orphan G protein-coupled receptor," is expressed on the cell surface and mediates its effects via several members of the G protein family of GTP-binding proteins; this receptor has been shown to contribute to the rapid effects of estrogen in several model systems. GPR30 is widely distributed and has been identified in both endothelial cells and vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs). However, to date, data have suggested that GPR30 is selectively activated by estradiol (E2).

Because recent studies from our laboratory have revealed common activation of extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) and of vascular contraction via phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3)-dependent kinases, we postulated that the rapid vascular actions of aldosterone might be mediated, at least in part, through a GPR30-dependent pathway. Based on this hypothesis, we examined the role of GPR30 in mediating the rapid vascular effects of aldosterone on vascular smooth muscle ERK phosphorylation and apoptosis as well as on myosin light chain (MLC) phosphorylation and contractility. Our findings are consistent with the interpretation that estrogens are not the sole hormones that mediate their rapid effects via GPR30-dependent pathways and that aldosterone can mediate its rapid vascular effects potently via both GPR30- and MR-dependent mechanisms.

## Methods

### VSMC Primary Cultures
Wistar-Kyoto rats (10 to 12 weeks of age; Charles River) were utilized as a source of VSMCs, cared for in accordance with the Canadian Council on Animal Care guidelines. The protocol was approved by the Animal Use Subcommittee, University of Western Ontario. Isolation of rat aortic VSMCs was performed as described previously.

### Aortic Endothelial Cell Primary Cultures
Rat aortic endothelial cells were cultured per the manufacturer's instructions (Cell Applications) and utilized between passages 4 and 8.

### Generation of Adenoviral Vectors for Expression of GPR30 and MR and for GPR30 Knockdown
The adenoviral vectors were generated with the AdMax adenovirus vector creation kit (Microbix BioSystems Inc.).

### Assessment of Endogenous Receptor Expression
Total RNA isolation used TRIzol (Invitrogen). Receptor mRNA expression was determined using SuperScript One Step RT-PCR kits (Invitrogen) with receptor-specific primers.

### Assessment of ERK Content and ERK1/2 Phosphorylation
Effects of aldosterone, E2, corticosterone, or the GPR30 agonist G1 on ERK1/2 phosphorylation were assessed by immunoblotting.

### Assessment of Apoptosis by Annexin Labeling
VSMCs were cultured 24 hours before gene transfer and infected with adenoviral constructs expressing MR, GPR30, or GFP (control) for 16 hours. After 48 hours of serum starvation, cells were treated with aldosterone (10 pM) for 24 hours, in the absence or presence of eplerenone or the GPR30 antagonist G15. Cells were suspended in annexin binding buffer (10 mmol/L HEPES, 140 mmol/L NaCl, 2.5 mmol/L CaCl2, pH 7.4) with FITC-conjugated annexin V (0.25 µg/mL) and propidium iodide (5 µg/mL) and incubated in the dark for 15 minutes. Annexin binding was assessed using a BD FACScalibur flow cytometer. A total of 20,000 events were analyzed for double-stained positive cells per sample with FlowJo software by a blinded observer. Data were normalized relative to control levels of annexin-positive staining (1.7±0.3% of cells, n=20).

### Assessment of MLC Phosphorylation
Phosphorylation of the 20-kDa regulatory subunit of MLC was assessed by Western blotting using antibodies against phospho-MLC (Cell Signaling Technology) and total MLC (Santa Cruz Biotechnology).

### Statistical Analyses
For multiple group comparisons, ANOVA was followed by Dunnett's multiple comparison tests. Between-paired group differences were determined by Student t test for paired data. P<0.05 on a 2-sided test was taken as the minimum level of significance.

### Materials
The PI3 kinase inhibitor LY294002 and the GPR30-selective agonist G1 were purchased from Calbiochem-Novabiochem. The specific p38 inhibitor SB203580 and its p38 MAPK-inactive analog SB202474 were from Calbiochem. Eplerenone was provided by Pfizer Pharmaceuticals. The selective GPR30 antagonist G15 was synthesized as described. Antiphospho-ERK and antiphospho-MLC antibodies were from Cell Signaling Technology; anti-ERK from Upstate Cell Signaling; anti-MLC from Santa Cruz Biotechnology.

## Results

### Aldosterone Stimulates ERK1/2 Phosphorylation in Freshly Isolated Aortic Ring Segments via Both MR- and GPR30-Dependent Mechanisms
Aldosterone caused a concentration-dependent and saturable increase in phospho-ERK in freshly isolated, endothelium-denuded aortic ring segments. The GPR30 agonist G1 (1 µmol/L) also significantly enhanced ERK phosphorylation (151±16% of control, P<0.05, n=3). Aldosterone-mediated ERK stimulation was partially attenuated by both the MR antagonist eplerenone and the GPR30-selective antagonist G15, suggesting effects mediated via both GPR30-dependent and MR-dependent actions.

### GPR30 Expression Diminishes After Culturing of VSMCs
Endogenous GPR30 and MR expression were detected by RT-PCR in freshly isolated, endothelium-denuded aortic tissue. RT-PCR analysis showed GPR30 expression is significantly reduced in cultured VSMCs compared to freshly isolated aorta. In this heterologous background, flag-tagged GPR30 (44 kDa) and MR (107.0 kDa) proteins could be expressed via adenoviral gene transfer. Gene transfer of GPR30 resulted in a reduction in MR expression by RT-PCR, though neither ER expression was altered.

### Aldosterone-Mediated ERK Activation in VSMCs With Varying Levels of GPR30 Versus MR Expression
In GFP (control)-infected VSMCs, aldosterone mediated a concentration-dependent increase in ERK phosphorylation (ERK activation was 153±4% of control). In contrast to freshly isolated aortic rings, aldosterone-mediated ERK activation in cultured GFP-transduced VSMCs was almost completely inhibited by eplerenone and was not affected by G15. The GPR30 agonist G1 (1 µmol/L) was without significant effect in GFP-infected cells (104±8% of control, P>0.1, n=5), affirming agonist specificity.

### Enrichment of Either MR or GPR30 in Cultured VSMC Enhanced Aldosterone-Mediated ERK Activation
In MR-transduced VSMCs, eplerenone completely suppressed aldosterone ERK activation. In GPR30-transduced cells, aldosterone-mediated ERK phosphorylation was almost completely inhibited by G15, but still partially attenuated by eplerenone. Both eplerenone and spironolactone partially inhibited G1-mediated responses, suggesting both can serve as partial antagonists of GPR30.

### Corticosterone-Mediated Regulation of ERK Phosphorylation Is Not GPR30 Dependent
Corticosterone stimulated ERK phosphorylation in both control and GPR30-expressing cells. However, over the concentration range at which aldosterone had maximal GPR30-dependent effects (1 to 1000 pM), corticosterone effects were not enhanced by GPR30 expression, nor inhibited by G15.

### GPR30-Mediated Regulation of ERK Phosphorylation in Vascular Endothelial Cells
Rat aortic vascular endothelial cells showed persistent GPR30 expression by RT-PCR; ER and androgen receptor mRNA were detectable, but no MR mRNA. In these cells, aldosterone increased ERK phosphorylation with a maximal effect in the 10 pM range after 15 minutes. E2 mediated a concentration-dependent inhibition of ERK phosphorylation (maximal in the low nanomolar range). G15 almost completely attenuated the stimulatory effect of aldosterone but had no effect on E2-mediated inhibition, whereas the ER-specific antagonist ICI-182780 almost completely blocked E2 effects. With shGPR30 transduction, aldosterone (and G1) stimulation of ERK phosphorylation was almost completely ablated, angiotensin II-mediated ERK phosphorylation was unaltered, and E2 inhibitory effects were further enhanced.

### Aldosterone-Mediated Regulation of Apoptosis
Aldosterone mediated a concentration-dependent increase in apoptosis. Aldosterone (10 pM)-stimulated apoptosis was inhibited by the selective MEK inhibitor U0126 but not the MEK-inactive analog U0124, nor by the p38-MAPK inhibitor SB203580; it was also blocked by the PI3 kinase inhibitor LY294002. Elevated MR expression enhanced aldosterone-mediated apoptosis, completely blocked by eplerenone; in GPR30-infected cells, aldosterone-mediated apoptosis was only partially inhibited by eplerenone but significantly blunted by G15 (G15 had no effect in GFP-infected cells).

### Rapid Effects of Aldosterone in Mediating MLC Phosphorylation
In freshly isolated endothelium-denuded aortic tissue, both aldosterone (10 nmol/L, 15 min) and G1 (1 µmol/L, 15 min) stimulated MLC phosphorylation comparably to the alpha-adrenergic agonist phenylephrine (10 µmol/L, 15 min). The aldosterone effect was almost completely inhibited by G15.

In cultured VSMCs, aldosterone mediated a dose-dependent increase in MLC phosphorylation in GFP-expressing cells that was insensitive to G15. Gene transfer of either MR or GPR30 enhanced aldosterone-mediated MLC phosphorylation. Aldosterone-mediated MLC phosphorylation was completely abolished by eplerenone in GFP- and MR-expressing VSMCs, but only partially inhibited by eplerenone in GPR30-infected VSMCs; G15 almost completely abolished the effect in GPR30-infected cells. The GPR30 agonist G1 only increased MLC phosphorylation in GPR30-infected cells (125±3% of control, n=6), not in GFP-infected (97±4% of control, n=6) or MR-infected (93±4%, n=6) VSMCs.

## Discussion

The present studies demonstrate that aldosterone exploits both GPR30 and MR to mediate apoptosis and to activate intermediate signaling pathways, including PI3 kinase, ERK, and MLC phosphorylation. The relative contribution of GPR30 to this signaling depends on the relative concentrations of GPR30 in the target cells. Although MR and GPR30 are expressed in freshly isolated VSMCs, GPR30 expression declines in cultured VSMCs in parallel with GPR30-dependent signaling. These findings suggest that studies examining aldosterone pathways in primary cultures of VSMCs will underestimate the role of GPR30 in vivo. In the vascular endothelial cell model with persistent GPR30 but no detectable MR expression, aldosterone effects are completely GPR30-dependent.

For aldosterone, actions are ERK stimulatory under all conditions studied, whereas E2 actions on ERK are bidirectional and dependent on differential expression of GPR30 versus ER. Aldosterone mediates effects on ERK phosphorylation, apoptosis, and MLC phosphorylation/contraction via a PI3 kinase-dependent pathway with or without GPR30 expression.

Regarding relative potency: E2 effects on ERK phosphorylation were only apparent in the high nanomolar range (beyond physiological concentrations), whereas peak aldosterone effects were in the lower picomolar range. At equivalent free concentrations, aldosterone would be the more important physiological GPR30 agonist in VSMCs.

Eplerenone acts as both an MR antagonist and a partial GPR30 antagonist. The authors caution against assuming that an aldosterone-mediated effect antagonized by eplerenone proves the effect was mediated solely through the MR.

## Perspectives

The present studies provide evidence that acute effects of aldosterone on mechanisms regulating VSMC contractility and apoptosis rely on both MR- and GPR30-dependent pathways, and suggest that agents targeting each of these receptors may provide differential levers to modulate aldosterone actions in different pathological states.
