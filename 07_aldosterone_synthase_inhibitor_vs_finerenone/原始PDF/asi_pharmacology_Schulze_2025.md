## Abstract

**Purpose** In chronic kidney disease (CKD), raised plasma aldosterone levels are strongly associated with adverse cardiorenal outcomes. Current standard of care may improve outcomes; however, elevated aldosterone levels often persist. We report safety results for BI 690517 (vicadrostat), a potent, selective aldosterone synthase inhibitor under investigation for CKD.

**Methods** Four phase 1 studies of BI 690517 conducted in healthy European/Chinese/Japanese men: two single rising dose (SRD) and two multiple rising dose (MRD) studies. Primary endpoint: proportion of participants with investigator-defined drug-related adverse events (AEs).

**Results** Single and multiple doses of BI 690517 ≤ 80 mg (0.7–80 mg [European SRD]; 3–80 mg [Chinese/Japanese SRD and MRD]) were well tolerated. Proportions of participants with drug-related AEs: European SRD, 8.3% (4/48); Chinese/Japanese SRD, 21.4% (12/56); European MRD, 13.9% (10/72); Japanese MRD, 2.8% (1/36). No serious AEs, deaths, or AEs leading to treatment discontinuation were reported; one AE of severe orthostatic hypotension occurred (European SRD). Plasma exposure to BI 690517 increased dose dependently; median time to maximum concentration was 0.50–1.75 h and mean half-life was 4.4–6.3 h. Exposure was slightly higher in Asians versus Europeans and may relate to lower body weight in Asian participants. A standardized high-fat/high-calorie meal reduced the rate, but not extent, of BI 690517 absorption. Plasma aldosterone concentrations decreased markedly 1–2 h after BI 690517 administration; decreases were more pronounced with increasing BI 690517 doses.

**Conclusion** BI 690517 was well tolerated and demonstrated dose-dependent inhibition of aldosterone synthesis. Larger studies are warranted to confirm these findings.

**Keywords** Aldosterone signaling · Aldosterone synthase inhibition · Chronic kidney disease · Pharmacodynamics · Pharmacokinetics · Safety

## Introduction

Aldosterone, a mineralocorticoid hormone synthesized in the kidneys in response to hyperkalemia or sodium depletion, exerts its effects via the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) (Epstein 2021). In people with chronic kidney disease (CKD), raised plasma aldosterone levels are strongly associated with reduced estimated glomerular filtration rate, increased proteinuria, inflammation, and fibrosis (Epstein 2021; Namsolleck and Unger 2014; Zhong et al. 2017). Aldosterone also exerts kidney-independent effects: direct effects of aldosterone on the vasculature, myocardium, and central nervous system lead to elevated blood pressure, necrosis, fibrosis, and endothelial dysfunction, which contribute to the pathophysiology and progression of heart failure (Odedra and Ferro 2006; Messaoudi et al. 2012; Epstein 2021). Thus, CKD and heart failure share common risk factors, such as type 2 diabetes and hypertension, and frequently co-exist (Mullens et al. 2020; McDonagh et al. 2021).

Inhibition of aldosterone signaling may help to improve cardiorenal outcomes in people with CKD or heart failure. Currently, the leading therapeutic strategy for limiting CKD progression is blockade of the renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) using angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEis) and angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs) (Epstein 2021; Alicic et al. 2017; KDIGO 2022; Namsolleck and Unger 2014). In people with CKD, with or without type 2 diabetes, guidelines also recommend sodium–glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors, such as empagliflozin, as these agents improve cardiovascular and kidney disease outcomes and reduce the risk of hyperkalemia associated with RAAS inhibitor use (Rossing et al. 2022b; Tuttle et al. 2022; Neuen et al. 2022; KDIGO 2022; KDIGO CKD Work Group 2024). However, in 10–53% of people with CKD, aldosterone levels remain elevated despite treatment with ACEis and ARBs. This might warrant the use of inhibitors of aldosterone signaling, such as mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) and aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs), in addition to the current standard of care, to improve disease outcomes (Bomback and Klemmer 2007; KDIGO 2022; Namsolleck and Unger 2014; KDIGO CKD Work Group 2024). Indeed, finerenone, a nonsteroidal MRA, reduced CKD progression in phase 3 studies (Bakris et al. 2020; Pitt et al. 2021), leading to the approval of finerenone for the treatment of people with CKD and type 2 diabetes (Frampton 2021).

BI 690517 (vicadrostat) is a potent, selective ASI currently under investigation for the treatment of people with CKD, with or without diabetes, and heart failure. This article describes the findings from two single rising dose (SRD) studies (European, NCT02891148; Chinese/Japanese, NCT03206632) and two multiple rising dose (MRD) studies (European, NCT03100916; Japanese, NCT03608072) that investigated the safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics (PK), and pharmacodynamics (PD) of BI 690517 in healthy male volunteers.

## Methods

### Study ethics

The study protocol and informed consent documentation were reviewed and approved by the institutional review boards for all four studies: Seoul National University Hospital Institutional Review Board, Seoul, Republic of Korea (Chinese/Japanese SRD study); Ethik-Kommission der Landesärztekammer Baden-Württemberg, Stuttgart, Germany (European SRD and MRD studies); and SOUSEIKAI Hakata Clinic, Fukuoka, Japan (Japanese MRD study). The studies were conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki, the International Conference on Harmonization–Good Clinical Practice, and applicable regulatory requirements and standard operating procedures of the study sponsor. All participants provided signed and dated informed consent prior to participation.

The European SRD study was conducted at Humanpharmakologisches Zentrum Biberach, Biberach, Germany, and the European MRD study at CRS Clinical Research Services Mannheim GmbH, Mannheim, Germany. The Chinese/Japanese SRD study was conducted at Seoul National University Hospital, Seoul, Republic of Korea, and the Japanese MRD study at Souseikai Sumida Hospital, Tokyo, Japan.

### Study designs and participants

**SRD studies.** Two phase 1 SRD studies with matching endpoints and exclusion criteria were conducted in healthy male participants. The European SRD study was a non-randomized, open-label trial that aimed to investigate eight oral doses of BI 690517 (0.7 to 160 mg). However, administration of the planned 160 mg dose did not occur; dose escalation had to be stopped prematurely after the 80 mg dose, as exposure in the 160 mg dose group was expected to exceed the maximum acceptable human exposure for this study (defined as a peak plasma BI 690517 concentration [Cmax] ≥ 4000 nM). Eligible participants aged 18–50 years with a body mass index (BMI) of ≥ 18.5 and ≤ 29.9 kg/m² were divided into seven dose groups (BI 690517 0.7, 2, 5, 10, 20, 40, and 80 mg). Dose groups were investigated in ascending order with a 6-day period between each dose escalation. The next dose was given only if no investigator-defined safety concerns arose in the preceding dose group.

The Chinese/Japanese SRD study was placebo controlled, double blind, and randomized within dose groups. Eligible participants aged 20–45 years with a BMI of ≥ 18.5 and ≤ 25.0 kg/m² were divided into six dose groups (three comprising Japanese volunteers and three comprising Chinese volunteers) and randomized within dose groups to receive BI 690517 (3, 20, or 80 mg) or placebo. Due to a dosing error, a seventh unplanned dose group of Chinese volunteers receiving BI 690517 60 mg was also included.

**MRD studies.** Two phase 1 MRD studies with similar endpoints and exclusion criteria were conducted in healthy male volunteers. The European MRD study consisted of two parts: an MRD part (single blind, placebo controlled, and randomized within dose groups) and a food effect (FE) single-dose part (randomized, open label, two-way crossover). Eligible participants were aged 18–50 years and had a BMI of ≥ 18.5 and ≤ 29.9 kg/m². Participants in the MRD part were divided into five sequential dose groups (BI 690517 3, 10, 20, 40, and 80 mg; n = 12 per dose group [9 on active drug; 3 on placebo]). For safety reasons, in the BI 690517 20, 40, and 80 mg dose groups, administration of the study treatment was staggered. All dose groups were investigated consecutively in ascending order, with at least 6 days between each dose escalation. In the FE part, participants received BI 690517 in the morning in a fasted state (F) or after a standardized high-fat, high-calorie breakfast (B). The participants were randomly allocated (1:1) to one of the two treatment sequences (B–F or F–B) with a washout period of at least 7 days between the two treatments.

The Japanese MRD study was double blind, placebo controlled, and randomized within dose groups. Eligible participants were aged 20–50 years, had a BMI of ≥ 18.5 and ≤ 25.0 kg/m², were divided into three dose groups (n = 12 per group), and were randomized (3:1) to receive either BI 690517 (3, 40, or 80 mg) or placebo once daily for 14 days. The dose groups were investigated consecutively in ascending order, with at least 13 days between each dose escalation.

### Study procedures

All participants in the two SRD studies received a single dose of BI 690517 administered orally under fasting conditions. In the European SRD study, participants in the two lowest dose groups (BI 690517 0.7 and 2 mg) received BI 690517 as an oral solution, and participants in the other dose groups (BI 690517 ≥ 5 mg) received film-coated tablets of 5 or 20 mg. In the Chinese/Japanese SRD study and the two MRD studies, all participants received BI 690517 or placebo as film-coated tablets. The BI 690517 film-coated tablets were supplied in 1 and 20 mg dose strengths in the Chinese/Japanese SRD and Japanese MRD studies, and in 1, 5, and 20 mg dose strengths in the European MRD study. In the MRD studies, film-coated tablets were administered orally, once daily in the morning; tablets were administered under fasting conditions in both studies, except the FE part of the European MRD study, for which all participants took two 5 mg tablets, either in a fasted state or following a standardized high-fat, high-calorie breakfast.

### Randomization and blinding

Participants in all four studies were allocated to their respective dose groups based on their temporal availability. In the European SRD study, all participants received active treatment, so the treatment was not blinded. In the Chinese/Japanese SRD and Japanese MRD studies, participants were randomized (3:1) within dose groups to receive either BI 690517 (SRD, 3, 20, or 80 mg; MRD, 3, 40, or 80 mg) or placebo; these studies were double blind. In the European MRD study, participants were randomized within dose groups: in the MRD part, participants were randomized (3:1) to receive BI 690517 (3, 10, 20, 40, or 80 mg) or matching placebo; in the separate FE part, participants were randomized (1:1) to one of the two treatment sequences (F–B or B–F). The MRD part of the European MRD study was single blind. Owing to the rising-dose design of all four studies, the dose level was known to participants and investigators.

### Outcomes

For all four studies, the primary endpoint was the proportion (n [%]) of participants with investigator-defined, drug-related adverse events (AEs). In the two SRD studies, the secondary endpoints were Cmax and area under the plasma BI 690517 concentration–time curve over the time interval from 0 extrapolated to infinity (AUC₀–∞). In the European MRD study (MRD part only) and the Japanese MRD study, secondary endpoints were Cmax; AUCτ,1; Cmax,ss; and AUCτ,ss. In the FE part of the European MRD study, the secondary PK endpoints were Cmax, AUC₀–∞, and AUC₀–tz.

PD parameters measured in all four studies included plasma aldosterone, corticosterone, 11-deoxycorticosterone, cortisol, and 11-deoxycortisol; and urine aldosterone, cortisol, sodium, potassium, and creatinine. In addition, plasma renin and plasma adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) were measured in the two SRD studies and the European MRD study, and urine albumin was measured in the Japanese MRD study.

### Sample size calculation

The planned sample sizes in the European SRD study (N = 56; n = 7 per dose group), the Chinese/Japanese SRD study (N = 48 [Chinese and Japanese, n = 24 each]; n = 8 per dose group [active drug, n = 6; placebo, n = 2]), the MRD part of the European MRD study (N = 60; n = 12 per dose group [active drug, n = 9; placebo, n = 3]), and the Japanese MRD study (N = 36; n = 12 per dose group [active drug, n = 9; placebo, n = 3]) were based on sample sizes commonly used in similar studies rather than power calculations (Broom 1990). In the FE part of the European MRD study, a sample size of 12 participants was considered sufficient.

### Statistical analyses

For all four studies, descriptive statistics were calculated for all study endpoints. Statistical analysis of the primary endpoint was based on all participants who received active drug or placebo. The dose relationship of the secondary endpoints was analyzed for the PK set using a linear regression model. In the two MRD studies, the linearity index was estimated using a linear model providing two-sided 95% (European MRD) or 90% (Japanese MRD) confidence intervals (CIs), and attainment of steady state was analyzed using a linear mixed effect model for trough concentrations. The PK analyses were performed using Phoenix WinNonlin (Version 6.3; Pharsight Corporation, Mountain View, CA, USA).

### Trial registration

The studies were registered with ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT02891148 (European SRD, posted 7 September 2016), NCT03206632 (Chinese/Japanese SRD, posted 2 July 2017), NCT03100916 (European MRD, posted 4 April 2017), and NCT03608072 (Japanese MRD, posted 31 July 2018).

## Results

### Baseline demographics

A total of 212 healthy male participants were included in the four studies, comprising 118 White, 1 Black, and 93 Asian participants aged 18–50 years. In the European SRD study, the treated population included 48 healthy participants (BI 690517 2 mg dose group, n = 6; subsequent six dose groups, n = 7 each), as administration of the planned highest dose of 160 mg did not occur, and two participants allocated to the BI 690517 2 mg dose group discontinued the study prior to receiving study drug. Participant age ranged from 20 to 50 years and BMI from 19.1 to 29.6 kg/m².

In the Chinese/Japanese SRD study, the treated set included 56 male participants (Chinese, n = 32; Japanese, n = 24), split into seven dose groups. Participant age ranged from 20 to 43 years and BMI from 18.6 to 24.9 kg/m².

Of the 72 participants enrolled in the European MRD study (treated set; MRD part, n = 60; FE part, n = 12), 59 participants in the MRD part were White and 1 was Asian of Hispanic/Latino origin; all 12 participants in the FE part were White. One participant in the MRD part discontinued for personal reasons; 71 participants completed the trial. Age ranged from 20 to 50 years (MRD part) and 22 to 50 years (FE part); BMI from 19.2 to 29.8 and 19.7 to 29.3 kg/m², respectively.

The Japanese MRD study enrolled 36 male Japanese participants aged 22 to 45 years, with BMI ranging from 18.9 to 24.2 kg/m².

### Safety outcomes

BI 690517 was well tolerated across the tested dose range, with no serious AEs, deaths, AEs leading to discontinuation of trial medication, or AEs of special interest in any of the four studies. Except for one instance of vessel puncture hematoma in the European SRD study (BI 690517 5 mg dose group), all treatment-emergent AEs (TEAEs) resolved by the end of the study periods. In all four studies, the frequency of drug-related AEs did not show any dose-dependent trends. Apart from one instance of severe drug-related orthostatic hypotension in the European SRD trial (BI 690517 2 mg dose group), all drug-related AEs were of mild or moderate intensity.

**European SRD study.** Drug-related AEs were reported by four participants (n/N = 4/48 [8.3%]): one in the BI 690517 2 mg dose group, two in the 20 mg dose group (n = 3 events), and one in the 80 mg dose group. The only system organ class for which more than one drug-related AE was reported was nervous system disorders (n/N = 3/48 [6.3%]): headache (n = 1; 20 mg), dysgeusia (n = 1; 80 mg), and involuntary muscle contractions (two events in one participant; 20 mg). TEAEs were reported by 27.1% of participants (n/N = 13/48).

**Chinese/Japanese SRD study.** Drug-related AEs were reported by 12 participants (n/N = 12/56 [21.4%]), five of whom received placebo and seven BI 690517 (3 mg, n = 1; 20 mg, n = 3; 80 mg, n = 3). The most frequent drug-related AEs by system organ class were nervous system disorders (headache [n = 2] or dizziness [n = 4]) and vascular disorders (orthostatic hypotension [n = 6]), each reported by 10.7% (n/N = 6/56). TEAEs were reported by 25.0% (n/N = 14/56).

**European MRD study.** Drug-related AEs were reported by 10 participants (total, n/N = 10/72 [13.9%]: MRD part, n/N = 7/60 [11.7%]; FE part, n/N = 3/12 [25.0%]), all of whom received BI 690517 (3 mg, n = 2; 10 mg, n = 4; 20 mg, n = 1; 40 mg, n = 1; 80 mg, n = 2). Nervous system disorders were the most frequent drug-related AEs by system organ class (n/N = 7/72 [9.7%]). TEAEs were reported by 43.1% (total, n/N = 31/72: MRD part, 46.7% [28/60]; FE part, 25.0% [3/12]).

**Japanese MRD study.** One participant in the BI 690517 40 mg dose group (n/N = 1/36 [2.8%]) reported a drug-related AE of increased alanine aminotransferase concentration. TEAEs were reported by 5.6% (n/N = 2/36).

### PK outcomes

Following BI 690517 intake, plasma exposure increased in a dose-dependent manner in all four SRD and MRD studies. The PK plasma profiles were characterized by an early, sharp peak indicative of rapid absorption. The median time to reach maximum plasma BI 690517 concentration ranged from 0.5 to 1.75 h. Dose-proportionality for exposure was observed across studies. Urinary excretion of BI 690517 increased with dose; 24 h after dosing, ≤ 9.1% of the BI 690517 dose was excreted unchanged in urine.

### PD outcomes

**Plasma aldosterone.** The number of samples for which the aldosterone concentration was below the limit of quantification generally increased with dose in all four studies, consistent with a dose-dependent inhibition of aldosterone synthesis by BI 690517. In the MRD studies, higher doses of BI 690517 were associated with lower 12-h area under the effect curve (AUEC) of plasma aldosterone values at days 1 and 14, indicating that inhibition of aldosterone synthesis was maintained after multiple dosing for 14 days. In the European MRD study, BI 690517 exposure > 10,000 nmol·h/L was not associated with further reductions in aldosterone AUEC. Plasma aldosterone decreased markedly within 1–2 h of BI 690517 administration. In the Chinese/Japanese SRD study, strong inhibition was maintained in all dose groups 12 h post dose; however, 24 h post dose, the extent of aldosterone inhibition was greater in participants treated with BI 690517 ≥ 60 mg. Likewise, in the European SRD study, strong aldosterone inhibition was maintained 24 h post dose with BI 690517 ≥ 40 mg. In the MRD studies, a rebound effect on plasma aldosterone concentration following the last BI 690517 dose, which grew stronger until the last measurement, was observed for all active doses. In the European MRD study, this rebound effect was deemed to be dose dependent.

**Plasma corticosterone and 11-deoxycorticosterone.** In the European and Chinese/Japanese SRD studies, plasma corticosterone concentrations followed the expected circadian rhythm, with no significant differences observed between dose groups. In the European SRD study, determination of 11-deoxycorticosterone was attempted but could not be differentiated from 17-hydroxyprogesterone. In the Chinese/Japanese SRD study, one-third of the 11-deoxycorticosterone data points were below the lower limit of quantification. In the MRD studies, levels of aldosterone precursors increased on days 14 and 15 relative to day 1 in the highest dose groups: in the European MRD study, plasma corticosterone levels were elevated in the 80 mg dose group and 11-deoxycorticosterone levels were increased in the 40 and 80 mg dose groups; in the Japanese MRD study, plasma corticosterone and 11-deoxycorticosterone levels were increased in the 40 and 80 mg dose groups.

**Plasma cortisol and 11-deoxycortisol.** In all four studies, there was no clear dose-dependent effect of BI 690517 on plasma cortisol, which followed the expected circadian rhythm (peak in the morning, lowest in the evening). In the Chinese/Japanese SRD study, one Japanese participant in the 80 mg dose group had a plasma 11-deoxycortisol concentration 3- to 25-fold higher than other participants in the 80 mg dose group from 1–4 h post dose. In the European MRD and Japanese MRD studies, there was a marked increase in plasma 11-deoxycortisol on days 14 and 15 in the 40 mg dose group (Japanese MRD) and in the 80 mg dose groups (both studies) compared with baseline, suggesting that the two highest doses of BI 690517 may increase plasma 11-deoxycortisol concentration.

**Plasma renin.** The SRD studies did not show any clear dose-dependent effect of BI 690517 on plasma renin. However, in the European MRD study, on day 14, plasma renin concentrations tended to be higher in groups exposed to higher doses of BI 690517. This dose-dependent increase remained apparent 48 h after administration of the last BI 690517 dose.

**Plasma ACTH.** In the two SRD studies and the European MRD study, plasma ACTH concentration over time was similar across all dose groups, suggesting no effect of BI 690517 on plasma ACTH. However, in the European MRD study, the increase in plasma cortisol following ACTH challenge was less pronounced in participants receiving higher BI 690517 doses. Plasma ACTH followed the expected circadian rhythm.

**Urine aldosterone and cortisol.** In all four studies, most measurements for urine aldosterone were below the lower limit of quantification. Single doses of BI 690517 did not affect urinary cortisol excretion. In the European MRD study, following 14 days of treatment, urinary cortisol excretion was reduced in all BI 690517 dose groups except the 10 mg group (slight increase). In the Japanese MRD study, urinary cortisol excretion was slightly increased with placebo or BI 690517 3 mg, and slightly reduced with BI 690517 ≥ 40 mg. Changes in urinary cortisol excretion were not dose dependent.

**Plasma and urine sodium and potassium.** No dose-dependent changes in serum potassium concentration over time or dose-dependent trends in urinary sodium and potassium excretion were observed in any of the four studies. The mean change from baseline in serum potassium was generally within 5–6% and did not exceed 11% in any study. None of the participants in the Chinese/Japanese SRD and the Japanese MRD study had elevated serum potassium levels (≥ 5.0 mmol/L). Elevated serum potassium was reported at least once during the treatment period by 8.3% of participants in the European SRD study (n/N = 4/48) and 28.9% of participants treated with BI 690517 in the European MRD study (n/N = 13/45). None of the placebo recipients in the European MRD study had serum potassium levels ≥ 5.0 mmol/L. The mean sodium/potassium urinary excretion ratio increased from day −1 to day 1 in all treatment groups in the two SRD studies (except the 2 mg dose group in the European SRD study). In the European SRD study, single doses of BI 690517 ≥ 40 mg increased mean 24-h urinary sodium excretion; a clear reduction in urinary potassium excretion was seen from day −1 to day 1 with all tested doses. No dose-dependent changes in urinary sodium and potassium excretion over 24 h were observed following 14 days of multiple dosing in the two MRD studies.

## Discussion

BI 690517 is a novel, potent, selective ASI under investigation for the treatment of CKD with or without concurrent diabetes. In the current SRD and MRD studies, all single and multiple doses of BI 690517 tested were generally well tolerated in healthy male volunteers. No serious AEs, AEs leading to treatment discontinuation, AEs of special interest, or deaths were reported. One severe drug-related AE was reported (orthostatic hypotension in the BI 690517 2 mg group; European SRD study) and one AE that did not resolve prior to study end (vessel blood hematoma in the BI 690517 5 mg dose group; European SRD study). Overall, nervous system disorders (e.g., dizziness, headache) were the most frequently reported drug-related AEs.

Plasma exposure of BI 690517 increased in a dose-dependent manner across studies and in European/Asian participants. Absorption was rapid following oral dosing. The exposure in Chinese and Japanese participants was only slightly higher than that in European participants, with similar PK characteristics. This difference in exposure may be related to the lower body weight of Asian versus European participants (mean weight 64.4 kg vs. 80.2 kg). This is supported by a population PK model using BI 690517 data from the phase 1 study in participants with diabetic nephropathy (NCT03165240; Bornstein et al. 2024) and phase 2 study in participants with CKD (NCT05182840; Tuttle et al. 2024), indicating Asian and non-Asian participants have similar exposure after accounting for lower mean body weight (unpublished data).

Nonsteroidal MRAs directly block the MR-dependent actions of aldosterone and are recommended as second-line therapy for CKD with concurrent type 2 diabetes and for chronic heart failure. In the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD studies, the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone considerably improved cardiorenal outcomes and reduced disease progression in people with CKD and type 2 diabetes (Bakris et al. 2020; Pitt et al. 2021). However, MRAs have been linked with counter-regulatory effects on the RAAS that increase aldosterone production (Ménard et al. 2014).

ASIs, such as BI 690517, reduce the levels of aldosterone, thereby inhibiting its deleterious actions on the kidneys and heart. As aldosterone synthase shares 93% homology with cortisol synthase (Mornet et al. 1989), previously developed ASIs did not achieve full selectivity for aldosterone synthase over cortisol synthase. The current SRD and MRD studies demonstrated that BI 690517 dose dependently inhibits aldosterone synthesis without any clear dose-dependent effects on plasma and urine cortisol, making BI 690517 a selective ASI. This selectivity may reduce the likelihood of AEs due to inhibition of cortisol synthesis.

The use of nonsteroidal RAAS inhibitors and MRAs has been limited by the incidence of hyperkalemia. In the current SRD and MRD studies in healthy male volunteers, the mean change from baseline in serum potassium was generally within 5–6% and did not exceed 11%. There were no reports of hyperkalemia in the current phase 1 studies, and the tolerability of BI 690517 has since been confirmed in a phase 1 study in participants with type 1 or 2 diabetes and albuminuric CKD (NCT03165240; Bornstein et al. 2024) and a phase 2 study in participants with CKD, with or without type 2 diabetes (NCT05182840; Tuttle et al. 2024). Among those with hyperkalemia in the phase 2 study (BI 690517 doses: 3 mg, 10% [14/146]; 10 mg, 15% [22/144]; 20 mg, 18% [26/146]), most participants had a mild event that did not require intervention.

**Limitations.** Day 1 did not represent a true baseline, as there was an acute effect on urinary sodium and potassium excretion within hours of the first dose. Many plasma aldosterone measurements were below the lower limit of quantification; the aldosterone AUEC estimates for doses ≥ 20 mg were biased towards larger values. Other limitations include small sample size and low statistical power. The European SRD study was not placebo controlled and was open label; the potential for bias was considered low. The FE part of the European MRD study was also open label because fasted and fed conditions were distinguishable.

---

## Conclusions

All tested SRD/MRD doses up to BI 690517 80 mg were well tolerated, with no dose-dependent AEs in healthy male adults across the four studies. Administration of BI 690517 following a standardized high-fat, high-calorie breakfast did not affect the bioavailability of BI 690517 10 mg. Food ingestion decreased the rate of BI 690517 absorption and reduced Cmax slightly; however, these changes are not considered to be clinically meaningful. Overall, exposure to BI 690517 increased in a dose-dependent manner and BI 690517 effectively and dose dependently inhibited aldosterone synthesis in healthy male adults. No relevant ethnic differences in BI 690517 safety, PK, and PD were identified across the four studies. In summary, the benefit–risk evaluation demonstrated in phase 1 studies in healthy volunteers was found to be favorable for further clinical development of BI 690517. These data have since been confirmed in a phase 1 study in participants with type 1 or 2 diabetes and albuminuric CKD and a phase 2 study in participants with CKD, with or without type 2 diabetes.
