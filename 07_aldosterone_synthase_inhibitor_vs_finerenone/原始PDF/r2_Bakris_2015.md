# Effect of Finerenone on Albuminuria in Patients With Diabetic Nephropathy: A Randomized Clinical Trial (ARTS-DN)

**Bakris GL, Agarwal R, Chan JC, Cooper ME, Gansevoort RT, Haller H, Remuzzi G, Rossing P, Schmieder RE, Nowack C, Kolkhof P, Joseph A, Pieper A, Kimmeskamp-Kirschbaum N, Ruilope LM; for the ARTS-DN Study Group.** JAMA. 2015;314(9):884-894. doi:10.1001/jama.2015.10081. PMID: 26325557.

## Importance / Objective

Steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs), when added to a renin-angiotensin system (RAS) blocker, further reduce proteinuria in chronic kidney disease (CKD) but may be underused due to a high risk of adverse events (eplerenone/spironolactone increase hyperkalemia risk in stage ≥3 CKD by as much as 3- to 8-fold).

Objective: to evaluate the safety and efficacy of different oral doses of the **nonsteroidal MRA finerenone (BAY 94-8862)**, given for 90 days to patients with diabetes and high or very high albuminuria who are receiving an ACE inhibitor or ARB.

## Design, Setting, Participants

- Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group, **phase 2B** study.
- Conducted at **148 sites in 23 countries**. Recruitment June 2013 to February 2014; study completed August 2014.
- Of **1501 screened** patients, **823 were randomized** and **821 received study drug**; 764 patients (93%) completed treatment; 812 patients had valid data for the primary analysis (full analysis set).
- Trial registration: clinicaltrials.gov NCT01874431 (article body prints "NCT1874431").

## Interventions

Oral, once-daily finerenone or matching placebo for 90 days, added to standard of care with an RAS blocker:

| Group | n (randomized) |
|---|---|
| Placebo | 94 |
| Finerenone 1.25 mg/d | 96 |
| Finerenone 2.5 mg/d | 92 |
| Finerenone 5 mg/d | 100 |
| Finerenone 7.5 mg/d | 97 |
| Finerenone 10 mg/d | 98 |
| Finerenone 15 mg/d | 125 |
| Finerenone 20 mg/d | 119 |

(The 15-mg/d and 20-mg/d groups were added on recommendation of the independent data monitoring committee after review of ongoing safety data; randomization was adapted accordingly. Note: the ACC trial summary and some secondary sources label the top dose "25 mg" — the JAMA article of record states **20 mg/d**.)

## Baseline characteristics

- Mean age **64.2 years**; **78% male**.
- At baseline, **301 patients (36.7%)** had very high albuminuria (UACR ≥300 mg/g); **328 patients (40.0%)** had an eGFR ≤60 mL/min/1.73 m².
- Randomization stratified by region and severity of albuminuria (high [UACR 30 to <300 mg/g] vs very high [≥300 mg/g]).
- ~45% receiving an ACE inhibitor at baseline; ~one-quarter received RAS inhibition below (2.6%) or at (24.8%) the minimal recommended dosage.

## Inclusion criteria (brief)

Type 2 diabetes; albuminuria (UACR ≥30 mg/g); eGFR >30 mL/min/1.73 m²; treated with at least minimum recommended dose of an RAS blocker; serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L at screening. Patients with eGFR 30-45 must have been on a non–potassium-sparing diuretic. Excluded if on eplerenone, spironolactone, a renin inhibitor, or a potassium-sparing diuretic that could not be discontinued.

## Primary outcome

The **ratio of the urinary albumin-creatinine ratio (UACR) at day 90 vs at baseline** (log-transformed, ANCOVA; prespecified 1-sided linear contrast at α=0.05). Safety end points: changes from baseline in serum potassium and eGFR.

## Key efficacy results

Dose-dependent relationship across all doses demonstrated (1-sided F test for linear contrast, **P < .001**).

**Placebo-corrected mean ratio of UACR at day 90 relative to baseline** (values <1.0 = reduction vs placebo):

| Finerenone dose | Placebo-corrected UACR ratio (day 90/baseline) | 90% CI | P value |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7.5 mg/d | 0.79 | 0.68–0.91 | .004 |
| 10 mg/d | 0.76 | 0.65–0.88 | .001 |
| 15 mg/d | 0.67 | 0.58–0.77 | <.001 |
| 20 mg/d | 0.62 | 0.54–0.72 | <.001 |

(The 1.25-, 2.5-, and 5-mg/d groups did not reach the prespecified significant reduction.)

## Key safety results

- **Hyperkalemia leading to study-drug discontinuation** (serum potassium increase ≥5.6 mmol/L): 12 of 821 patients (1.5%), all in finerenone groups.
  - Incidences: 2.1% (1.25 mg), 1.1% (2.5 mg), 1.0% (5 mg), 2.1% (7.5 mg), **0% (10 mg)**, 3.2% (15 mg), 1.7% (20 mg).
  - No cases in placebo group. Overall incidence for the 7.5- to 20-mg/d groups (doses with a significant primary-endpoint change) was **1.8%**.
- No differences between placebo and finerenone in incidence of the prespecified secondary outcome of eGFR decrease ≥30%.
- No difference in overall incidence of adverse events or serious adverse events between finerenone and placebo groups. Drug-related serious adverse events occurred in 1.5% of patients receiving finerenone.

## Conclusions

Among patients with diabetic nephropathy, most receiving an ACE inhibitor or an ARB, the addition of finerenone compared with placebo resulted in improvement in the urinary albumin-creatinine ratio. Further trials are needed to compare finerenone with other active medications.
