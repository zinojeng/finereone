# The mineralocorticoid receptor: insights into its molecular and (patho)physiological biology

**Say Viengchareun, Damien Le Menuet, Laetitia Martinerie, Mathilde Munier, Laurent Pascual-Le Tallec, Marc Lombès**

*Nucl Recept Signal. 2007 Nov 30;5:e012. doi:10.1621/nrs.05012*

## Abstract

The last decade has witnessed tremendous progress in the understanding of the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), its molecular mechanism of action, and its implications for physiology and pathophysiology. After the initial cloning of MR, and identification of its gene structure and promoters, it now appears as a major actor in protein-protein interaction networks. The role of transcriptional coregulators and the determinants of mineralocorticoid selectivity have been elucidated. Targeted oncogenesis and transgenic mouse models have identified unexpected sites of MR expression and novel roles for MR in non-epithelial tissues. These experimental approaches have contributed to the generation of new cell lines for the characterization of aldosterone signaling pathways, and have also facilitated a better understanding of MR physiology in the heart, vasculature, brain and adipose tissues. This review describes the structure, molecular mechanism of action and transcriptional regulation mediated by MR, emphasizing the most recent developments at the cellular and molecular level. Finally, through insights obtained from mouse models and human disease, its role in physiology and pathophysiology will be reviewed. Future investigations of MR biology should lead to new therapeutic strategies, modulating cell-specific actions in the management of cardiovascular disease, neuroprotection, mineralocorticoid resistance, and metabolic disorders.

## A brief history

In the late 1960s, evidence for the presence of specific receptors mediating corticosteroid action in the toad bladder was initially proposed by the group of Edelman (Porter and Edelman, 1964). Subsequently, Type I and Type II corticosteroid receptors were described and identified as mineralocorticoid (MR) and glucocorticoid receptors (GR) (Marver et al., 1974). MR was characterized as a high affinity (Kd ~1 nM), low capacity (20–50 fmol/mg protein) receptor and demonstrated to be a major regulator of sodium reabsorption in the kidney (Funder et al., 1972). Fifteen years later, the human MR (hMR) cDNA was cloned by the Evans laboratory by screening a human kidney cDNA library at low stringency with a probe encompassing the DNA binding domain of the GR (Arriza et al., 1987). MR was subsequently cloned and characterized in many species including Xenopus, fish, bird and mammals. In the late 1990s came the identification of multiple transcription coregulators that mediate MR transcriptional potency at aldosterone-target genes (O'Malley, 2007). MR is now recognized as a crucial transcription factor involved in many physiological processes and pathological disorders.

## Structure

### Structure of the gene

The gene NR3C2 encoding the hMR is located on chromosome 4 in the q31.1 region and spans approximately 450 kb (Morrison et al., 1990; Zennaro et al., 1995). The gene is composed of ten exons; the first two exons, 1α and 1β, are untranslated, and the following eight exons encode the entire MR protein of 984 amino acids (aa). The rat MR gene is located on chromosome 19q11 and differs slightly in having three untranslated exons (1α, 1β and 1γ) and encoding a 981 aa protein (Kwak et al., 1993); a similar genomic structure is found for mouse MR gene, which encodes a 978 aa protein. In addition, the MR gene does not encode only one protein, but gives rise to multiple mRNA isoforms and protein variants (Pascual-Le Tallec and Lombes, 2005).

### Structure of the protein

Like all members of the nuclear receptor superfamily, MR has three major functional domains: a N-terminal domain (NTD), followed by a central DNA-binding domain (DBD), and a hinge region linking them to a C-terminal ligand-binding domain (LBD). Exon 2 encodes most of the NTD, small exons 3 and 4 for each of the two zinc fingers of the DBD, and the last five exons for the LBD.

The MR NTD is the longest among all the steroid receptors (SR) (602 aa). The NTD is highly variable among SR (less than 15% identity), but for a given receptor highly conserved between species (more than 50% homology). Two distinct activation function 1 domains (AF1), referred to as AF1a (residues 1–167) and AF1b (residues 445–602), have been demonstrated in both rat (Fuse et al., 2000) and human MR (Pascual-Le Tallec et al., 2003). A central inhibitory domain (residues 163–437) has also been characterized. These different domains are now considered to be important determinants of mineralocorticoid selectivity (Pascual-Le Tallec and Lombes, 2005).

The DBD recognizes specific target DNA sequences or hormone response elements (HRE). The MR DBD is 94% identical with that of GR, and more than 90% compared with the progesterone receptor (PR) and the androgen receptor (AR). It is a 66 aa domain encoded by exons 3 and 4, containing two perpendicular α helices coordinated by a zinc ion. The first zinc finger contains the "P box" (Gly 621 Ser-Val 625) responsible for tight binding to the minor groove of the DNA double helix. The second zinc finger facilitates receptor dimerization through the "D box" (Ala 640 GlyArgAsnAsp 645). MR is able to heterodimerize with other members of the SR subgroup, most notably GR and AR (Liu et al., 1995).

The MR LBD is a complex and multifunctional domain that spans 251 aa. It is relatively conserved among SR (~55% homology) and highly conserved across species (80–97% homology). The MR LBD crystal structure has been solved (Bledsoe et al., 2005; Fagart et al., 2005; Li et al., 2005), consisting of 11 α helices and four small antiparallel β strands folding into a three layer helical sandwich. Gln 776 of helix H3 and Arg 817 at the end of helix 5 directly contact the 3-ketone group of aldosterone, and Asn 770 of helix H3 stabilizes aldosterone 18-hydroxyl group. A single residue at position 848 in helix H7 switches hormone specificity between MR and GR (Li et al., 2005); amino acids 820–844 are also critical for aldosterone binding and ligand binding selectivity (Rogerson et al., 2007). The role of Met 852 in accommodating the C7 substituents of antimineralocorticoid spirolactones has been described (Huyet et al., 2007). It has been proposed that MR was closer to the primordial ancestral corticosteroid receptor (Hu and Funder, 2006), with high affinity for aldosterone well before the hormone appeared (Bridgham et al., 2006).

The MR LBD possesses a ligand-dependent AF-2 constituted by helices H3, H4, H5 and H12. Upon ligand binding, H12 closes over the ligand pocket, forming a hydrophobic cleft that serves as a docking surface for transcriptional coactivators possessing a NR box defined by the LXXLL motif. MR interacts with a restricted number of coactivator peptides including SRC-1, ASC2, PGC-1α and PGC-1β (Hultman et al., 2005). The NTD appears to account for ~40–50% of total transactivation and represents a key determinant of MR specificity (Pascual-Le Tallec and Lombes, 2005). MR is expressed as at least two different proteins, MRA and MRB (Pascual-Le Tallec et al., 2004), resulting from strong Kozak sequences initiating alternative translation.

## Interactions

In its non-liganded state, MR interacts with a large variety of proteins in the cytoplasmic compartment, forming a hetero-oligomer (Rafestin-Oblin et al., 1989). MR contacts chaperone proteins such as hsp90 (Binart et al., 1995), and indirectly interacts with hsp70, the p23 and p48 proteins and the FKBP-59 immunophilins or CYP40 cyclophillin. These chaperones maintain MR in an appropriate conformation for ligand binding. MR also interacts with actin (Jalaguier et al., 1996). Upon hormone binding, MR dissociates from chaperone proteins, undergoes nuclear translocation and interacts with numerous molecular partners.

The most important coactivators for MR transactivation are the histone acetylase CBP/p300; the helicase RHA; the transcriptional coactivators SRC-1, SRC-1e, and PGC-1; and the Pol II elongation factor ELL, the first example of a selective transcriptional coregulator of MR (Pascual-Le Tallec et al., 2005). Corepressors able to bind MR include SMRT and NCoR; the apoptosis regulator DAXX; and the specific SUMO-ligase PIAS proteins. SUMO-E3 ligase PIAS proteins repress MR, potentially with DAXX (Lin et al., 2006); the SUMO-E2 activating enzyme Ubc9 interacts with the MR NTD/DBD (1–670 aa) to potentiate aldosterone-dependent MR transactivation (Yokota et al., 2007). MR heterodimerizes with other SR, notably GR and AR (Liu et al., 1995; Savory et al., 2001).

## MR expression

Until the late 1970s, MR expression was considered restricted to polarized tight epithelia showing aldosterone-dependent transepithelial sodium transport (Marver et al., 1974). MR was located by immunohistochemistry in the kidney, most notably in the distal convoluted tubules and cortical collecting ducts. MR is also expressed in glomeruli (mesangial cells, podocytes), distal colon, lung airway epithelia, salivary and sweat glands, liver, and inner ear. Importantly, epithelial expression of MR was always associated with expression of the 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase 2 (11β-HSD2), the enzyme that allows aldosterone to selectively activate MR, by converting glucocorticoid hormones to their 11-keto analogs, unable to bind MR (Edwards et al., 1988; Funder et al., 1988).

Subsequently, MR expression was detected in non-epithelial tissues in which 11β-HSD2 was absent or extremely low: mononuclear leucocytes, heart, hippocampus (dentate gyrus and CA1, 2, 3 nuclei) and hypothalamus. In 1992, MR was localized in cardiomyocytes, endothelial cells and large vessels (Lombes et al., 1992). MR is also expressed in skin keratinocytes, white and brown adipocytes, ocular tissues (retina, iris-ciliary body), placenta, and at the messenger level in uterus, ovaries and testis. This widespread expression raises questions regarding the role of glucocorticoid hormones in MR activation in non-epithelial tissues lacking 11β-HSD2. Real-time PCR quantification of MR and GR transcripts reveals distinct anatomical expression patterns; MR expression is higher than GR in the central nervous system and skeleton (Bookout et al., 2006).

## Regulation of MR expression

In non-epithelial tissues such as the hippocampus, MR exerts its action in balance with GR (de Kloet, 2003). Serotonin and progesterone modulate MR mRNA expression in brain. MR expression levels positively correlate with the severity of heart (Yoshida et al., 2005) and kidney failure (Quinkler et al., 2005). Lowering renal MR expression by RNA interference in cold-induced hypertensive rats prevented the progression of hypertension and attenuated renal damage (Wang et al., 2006). The hMR gene has two 5' untranslated exons (1α and 1β) whose 5'-flanking regions are functional promoters P1 and P2 (Zennaro et al., 1996). The P1 (proximal) promoter has stronger basal activity; in vitro both promoters are stimulated by glucocorticoids, but only P2 is activated by aldosterone. The P1 promoter is active in vivo in all MR-expressing tissues, whereas P2 activity is much lower and restricted to development.

## Cellular mechanisms and cellular biology

### Subcellular distribution

In the absence of ligand, MR is located mainly in the cytoplasm associated with chaperone proteins. Upon ligand binding and dissociation of receptor-associated proteins, activated MR translocates into the nucleus via nuclear localization signals (NLS). Three functional NLS have been described (Walther et al., 2005). NLS0 is located at the end of the NTD (aa 590–602), dependent on Ser 601 phosphorylation. NLS2 is in the LBD; only MR agonists induce rapid translocation, with antagonists less effective (Lombes et al., 1994a). NLS1 is in the C-terminal part of the DBD. A nuclear export signal (NES) is located between the two zinc fingers of the DBD. Although a membrane receptor for corticosteroids might mediate rapid nongenomic effects, evidence for a membrane-bound MR is lacking.

### Promoter binding and transcriptional activation

Upon ligand binding, MR moves into the nucleus and binds specific HRE in target genes, potentially located up to 10 kb upstream or downstream from transcriptional start sites. MR recruits chromatin remodeling complexes and components of the transcriptional machinery to activate Pol II transcription.

### Posttranslational modifications

MR is a phosphoprotein (Alnemri et al., 1991). A tyrosine to cysteine substitution (Y 73 C) in the NTD of the Brown Norway rat was associated with increased MR transactivation and partial activation by progesterone (Marissal-Arvy et al., 2004). Phosphorylation sites at Thr 735 and Ser 737 were identified in the hMR LBD (Hirschberg et al., 2004). MR is phosphorylated within minutes after aldosterone exposure on serine and threonine residues by protein kinase C α, providing evidence for cross-talk between early non-genomic and late genomic effects (Le Moellic et al., 2004; Funder, 2005). Sumoylation is generally repressive; four SUMO sites are in the NTD (K 89, K 399, K 428, K 494) and one in the LBD (K 953). Acetylation (KXKK/RXKK motif matching MR NLS1, notably K 677) may affect shuttling and transcriptional regulation. MR can also be poly-ubiquitylated and targeted to the proteasome (Tirard et al., 2007).

## Target genes and their biological functions

### Epithelial tissues

In classical polarized epithelial tissues (kidney, colon), MR regulates salt balance and water homeostasis by directly stimulating expression of ionic transporters: the amiloride-sensitive Epithelial Na Channel (ENaC) at the apical membrane, and the basolateral Na+,K+-ATPase pump. The best studied aldosterone-induced gene is the serine/threonine kinase SGK1, which activates ENaC by phosphorylating the ubiquitin-ligase Nedd4-2, impairing the Nedd4-2/ENaC interaction and allowing channels to remain at the apical membrane. Other aldosterone-regulated genes include the channel-inducing factor (CHIF), K-ras2, GILZ (which inhibits ERK signaling), Grem2, activating transcription factor 3, the ubiquitin-specific protease Usp2-45 (deubiquitylates ENaC), KS-WNK1, NDRG2, Endothelin-1 (ET-1) and Plasminogen Activator Inhibitor-1 (PAI-1).

### Non-epithelial tissues

In aortic endothelial cells, aldosterone increases osteopontin and angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) gene expression, contributing to endothelial dysfunction and vascular injury. Aldosterone impairs vascular reactivity by decreasing endothelial glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD), increasing oxidative stress and decreasing NO (Leopold et al., 2007). In vascular smooth muscle cells, MDM2 was identified as a mineralocorticoid-responsive gene involved in vascular remodeling. Aldosterone stimulates collagen (I, III, IV) gene expression in cardiac and renal fibroblasts via MR-mediated ERK1/2 activation, contributing to myocardial and tubulointerstitial fibrosis. Target genes in a cardiomyocyte cell line include extracellular matrix regulators (tenascin-X, ADAMTS1, PAI-1, UPAR, hyaluronic acid synthase-2), signaling and vascular tone genes (RGS2, adrenomedullin), and inflammation (orosomucoid). In hippocampus, the balance between GR and MR controls limbic neuron fate; MR has anti-apoptotic (protective) effects on GR-induced apoptosis. In brown adipocytes, aldosterone induces a marked decrease in mitochondrial uncoupling proteins UCP1 and UCP3 (Viengchareun et al., 2001), supporting a role for MR in energy expenditure control.

## MR transgenic models

MR knockout mice (MR DBD disrupted) die around day 10 after birth from renal sodium loss (Berger et al., 1998), prevented by exogenous NaCl (Berger et al., 2000). Rescued animals show impaired neurogenesis and hippocampal granule cell degeneration in adulthood. In contrast, the mouse aldosterone synthase gene knockout is not lethal; high corticosterone and angiotensin II partially rescue sodium balance (Makhanova et al., 2006), underscoring the importance of MR over aldosterone. AQP2-Cre-MR mice (MR inactivation in renal principal cells) exhibited normal sodium excretion with elevated aldosterone on standard diet, but rapid sodium loss on low-sodium diet (Ronzaud et al., 2007). Forebrain-specific MR inactivation impaired spatial learning (Berger et al., 2006).

MR overexpression models: human P1 promoter-driven hMR transgene led to enlarged kidneys, renal tubular dilatation, normal blood pressure, and mild dilated cardiomyopathy with arrhythmia (Le Menuet et al., 2001). Conditional cardiac MR overexpression (α-myosin heavy chain promoter) led to life-threatening arrhythmias (Ouvrard-Pascaud et al., 2005). Notably, none of these MR-overexpressing mice developed cardiac fibrosis, questioning the direct role of cardiomyocyte MR in cardiac fibrosis. MR knockdown by conditional antisense mRNA induced cardiac fibrosis and heart failure (Beggah et al., 2002), possibly related to MR/GR imbalance.

## MR and disease

The most important genetic disorder is autosomal dominant pseudohypoaldosteronism type 1 (adPHA1), caused in most cases by heterozygous loss-of-function mutations of MR. First described in 1958 (Cheek and Perry, 1958), it is characterized by renal resistance to aldosterone: dehydration, failure to thrive, salt wasting, hyperkalemia and metabolic acidosis, despite elevated plasma aldosterone and renin. Approximately 50 distinct MR gene mutations have been described (Geller, 2005). Only heterozygous mutations have been reported in humans, suggesting the homozygous state may be lethal in utero; loss of one allele results in haploinsufficiency.

Only one gain-of-function MR mutation is known: S810L in the LBD, causing constitutive MR activation and illicit activation by progesterone and other steroids including cortisone, 11-dehydrocorticosterone and the antagonist spironolactone; it leads to a severe inherited form of early-onset hypertension exacerbated during pregnancy (Geller et al., 2000). Common MR polymorphisms (e.g., I180V) are related to modulation of stress responsiveness.

Large clinical trials (RALES, EPHESUS, 4E) have demonstrated the major benefits of anti-mineralocorticoid therapy, such as spironolactone and eplerenone, in reducing mortality and improving prognosis of heart failure patients (Pitt et al., 1999) or after post-acute myocardial infarction (Pitt et al., 2003). MR activation is also a critical factor in the early pathogenesis of renal disease in both type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus (Guo et al., 2006).

## Ligands

In humans, aldosterone is the physiological ligand of MR, synthesized in the zona glomerulosa of adrenal cortex under regulation of angiotensin II, ACTH and serum potassium. Cortisol (corticosterone in rodents) is another important physiological ligand with the same affinity for MR, though the dynamic binding parameters and functional properties of glucocorticoid-MR complexes differ from aldosterone-MR. The most potent MR-protective mechanism against glucocorticoid activation is 11β-HSD2, the NAD-dependent enzyme converting cortisol into inactive cortisone in epithelial and vascular target tissues. Deoxycorticosterone and other glucocorticosteroid compounds (dexamethasone, fludrocortisone) act as mineralocorticoid agonists in vitro. Progesterone and androgens and their derivatives can bind MR and exert partial agonist/antagonist effects depending on cellular context.

Several mineralocorticoid antagonists, such as spironolactone and the more selective eplerenone (Pitt et al., 2003), have been developed and are used as antihypertensive and cardiovascular protective agents. Drospirenone is a novel progestogen with considerable MR antagonist activity (Oelkers, 2004). Selective mineralocorticoid receptor modulators (steroid-derived or nonsteroidal scaffold ligands) should ideally produce tissue-dependent beneficial effects: neuronal anti-apoptotic effects and protective effects on renal and cardiovascular systems.

## Conclusions

The roles of MR extend far beyond control of fluid and electrolyte homeostasis, being involved in processes as diverse as cardiovascular remodeling, fat storage, energy balance, and mammalian behavior. MR action must be seen in the context of its functional interaction with transcriptional coregulators, whose physiological roles are quite specific rather than redundant. The combinatorial tissue distribution of MR and its coregulators provides cell-dependent tuning of MR target gene regulation and offers new insights into MR selectivity. Recent evidence supports cross-talk between SR and other signaling pathways (MAPK, src, STAT). The aim of continuing research is to improve comprehension of MR-mediated signaling, both at genomic and non-genomic levels, in order to propose new therapeutic strategies for human diseases.
