## Abstract

Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) blockade has been a mainstay of therapy in cardiovascular and kidney disease for nearly 30 years. Despite the development of multiple drug classes designed to intervene along the RAAS pathway, the residual risk for the totality of adverse cardiorenal outcomes (cardiorenal disease) remains substantial. Dysregulation of aldosterone biosynthesis has pathophysiological consequences that are associated with cardiovascular and kidney damage. Aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASi) provide a mechanism to decrease aldosterone production. These agents may help to reduce residual risk in cardiorenal disease. Preliminary evidence from ASi clinical trials is encouraging, with demonstrated efficacy in improving blood pressure control and decreasing albuminuria in chronic kidney disease. Their therapeutic potential in improving cardiorenal outcomes is being investigated further in ongoing phase III trials where ASi agents are given as monotherapy or with an SGLT2 inhibitor, and these data will be available over the coming months and years. ASi agents may be a new evidence-based addition to the cardiorenal drug armamentarium.

**Keywords:** aldosterone, aldosterone synthase inhibitor, cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, clinical trial, mineralocorticoid receptor, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist

## 1 Introduction

The renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) is a critical regulator of blood volume, electrolyte balance, and systemic vascular resistance. Pathological activation or overactivation of the RAAS pathway has long been recognized as a key mediator of cardiovascular (CV) and kidney disease (hereafter referred to as cardiorenal disease), including CV events, heart failure (HF), and chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression. Multiple classes of drugs have been developed to intervene along the RAAS pathway, including angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEi), angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARB), angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitors, direct renin inhibitors, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists [MRAs, comprising steroidal and nonsteroidal (nsMRA) agents]. Other drug classes proven to improve cardiorenal outcomes include sodium glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors (SGLT2i) and glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists.

Despite these various interventions, the residual risk for the totality of adverse CV and kidney outcomes remains substantial (residual risk = the remaining risk of adverse outcomes despite treatment with the standard of care [SoC]). The concept of residual risk is illustrated by data from the phase III **FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993)** trial, which assessed CKD progression and CV outcomes in patients with advanced CKD and type 2 diabetes (T2D). Despite the significant benefits observed with finerenone, **17.8% of trial participants receiving finerenone experienced a primary endpoint after median follow-up of 2.6 years, and 13.0% of those receiving finerenone experienced a continuing risk of CV events** (Figure 1). Aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASi) are a new drug class that provide a mechanism to decrease aldosterone production and may help to reduce residual risk in cardiorenal disease (Figure 2). Their therapeutic potential for this and other endpoints is currently being investigated. The aim of this review is to summarize the present state of development of ASi agents and discuss central aspects and considerations of ASi therapy with respect to their potential impact on the residual risk of adverse CV and kidney outcomes.

## 2 Search strategy and selection criteria

Literature was retrieved from PubMed and Google Scholar databases, with an emphasis placed on articles published from 2010 onwards, using Boolean searches for terms related to: aldosterone, cardiorenal disease, cardiovascular disease, chronic kidney disease, heart failure, residual risk, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, finerenone, aldosterone synthase inhibitor (or inhibition), baxdrostat, dexfadrostat, lorundrostat, and vicadrostat (searches were limited to human data and English-language articles). Reference lists from retrieved articles were also considered. Additional data were obtained from ClinicalTrials.gov and other websites pertaining to individual therapeutic agents. This is a narrative review.

## 3 Aldosterone pathway: its role as a mediator of cardiorenal disease

Aldosterone acts via the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), which is encoded by the gene *NR3C2* (Nuclear Receptor Subfamily 3 Group C Member 2). Aldosterone acts on tubular cells in the distal nephron, where it has a central role in electrolyte and fluid homeostasis. Aldosterone also targets a range of extra-renal sites where the MR is expressed, including cardiac and vascular tissues, the brain, adipose tissue, leukocytes, and erythrocytes. Aldosterone is produced primarily in the zona glomerulosa of the adrenal cortex via the enzyme aldosterone synthase, the protein product of gene **CYP11B2**, a member of the cytochrome P450 family. Adrenal aldosterone synthesis is regulated mainly by angiotensin II and blood potassium levels, with a lesser stimulatory effect from adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) and other factors. Extra-adrenal sites of aldosterone synthesis have been identified, although their physiological/pathophysiological significance is not yet fully understood.

Aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) catalyzes the final 3 rate-limiting steps in aldosterone synthesis (Figure 3). Gene *CYP11B2* is structurally similar to *CYP11B1*, which encodes 11-beta-hydroxylase (a key enzyme in cortisol production, a glucocorticoid synthesized in the zona fasciculata). Genes *CYP11B2* and *CYP11B1* share **95% homology in their coding regions and 93% homology in their protein products**. Historically, this presented a major challenge in ASi drug development as early candidates were hampered by limited specificity for the gene products of CYP11B2 vs. CYP11B1, and resulting side effects from off-target activity (such as reduced glucocorticoid production).

Aldosterone exerts effects via genomic and nongenomic pathways, with evidence of interplay between them. In the **genomic pathway** (effects over hours to days), aldosterone binds to MRs in the cell cytoplasm, is translocated to the nucleus, and acts as a transcription factor by binding to promoter regions of various genes. **Nongenomic pathway** effects are rapid (seconds to minutes), non-transcription-dependent, mediated via MR and other receptor types (non-MR-dependent; e.g., G protein-coupled estrogen receptor 1, various growth factor receptors, angiotensin II receptor type 1), and may enhance genomic effects by functioning as feedback loops.

**Mechanistic differentiation of therapies:**
- **ASi agents** reduce aldosterone synthesis, thereby limiting ligand availability, and could broadly suppress both genomic and nongenomic signalling.
- **MRAs** act downstream to block MR-dependent genomic effects but may incompletely inhibit nongenomic signalling, and often induce compensatory increases in circulating aldosterone.
- **RAS inhibitors** act upstream in the renin–angiotensin system, lowering aldosterone indirectly and transiently, and are associated with incomplete pathway suppression (called "aldosterone breakthrough").

The role of aldosterone in target organ damage to heart and kidneys is well described. Animal models showed excess aldosterone caused inflammatory, oxidative, and fibrotic effects in heart, kidney, and blood vessels. Dysregulation of aldosterone and MR activation adversely affects various CV cell types (cardiomyocytes, vascular smooth muscle cells, endothelial cells, inflammatory cells, fibroblasts) and results in cardiac hypertrophy, cardiac remodeling, arrhythmias, atherosclerosis, inflammation, and cardiac fibrosis. As a mediator of kidney damage, aldosterone acts on kidney vessels, kidney cells, and infiltrating inflammatory cells to promote inflammation, injury, and fibrosis. MR activation stimulates reactive oxygen species (superoxide and hydrogen peroxide) that induce mitochondrial dysfunction and activate pro-inflammatory transcription factors (activator protein, nuclear factor kappa B). Aldosterone also stimulates profibrotic molecules such as transforming growth factor β1, plasminogen activator factor 1, endothelin, placental growth factor, connective tissue growth factor, and osteopontin.

In a review of 200 publications (mechanistic, preclinical, clinical trials, and genetic association studies), data strongly supported an association between HF and dysregulated aldosterone/MR activation; the latter was also associated with atrial fibrillation and myocardial infarction, but less strongly. Patients with primary aldosteronism (PA) had a higher risk of adverse CV outcomes (coronary artery disease, HF, left ventricular hypertrophy, atrial fibrillation, stroke) that were independent of elevated blood pressure (BP). Mitigation of adverse aldosterone effects in PA (adrenalectomy or MRA treatment) significantly reduced risk of CV events, mortality, and atrial fibrillation. Meta-analyses of MRA phase II and III trials showed MRAs lowered CV mortality in patients with HFrEF, HFmrEF, and HFpEF.

Data from the **Chronic Renal Insufficiency Cohort (CRIC)** study showed that higher serum aldosterone was associated with increased risk of CKD progression independent of baseline kidney function or diabetes — **doubling of serum aldosterone concentration was linked to an 11% increased risk**.

Adverse aldosterone effects may also be mediated by "**aldosterone breakthrough**" (aldosterone levels return to or exceed pretreatment levels during RAAS inhibitor therapy). This is distinct from "**aldosterone escape**" (kidneys excrete excess sodium and prevent severe volume expansion despite elevated mineralocorticoid levels). Patients who develop aldosterone breakthrough may have worse prognosis, due to association with faster deterioration in kidney function and more severe proteinuria. Blocking aldosterone production may address both genomic and nongenomic effects and may prevent aldosterone breakthrough.

## 4 Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists: existing agents with anti-aldosterone effects

**Spironolactone**, a steroidal MRA, was the first anti-aldosterone drug approved by the US FDA and marketed in the 1960s as a potassium-sparing diuretic for edema, PA, and hypertension. However, it is a nonselective agent with affinity for androgen and progesterone receptors, contributing to problematic side effects (gynecomastia, erectile dysfunction in males, menstrual irregularities in females).

**Eplerenone** is a second-generation steroidal MRA with increased MR selectivity and a more favorable side effect profile; FDA approval in 2002. Cardiorenal protective effects were confirmed for spironolactone and eplerenone in key HF and kidney disease trials.

**Finerenone**, a third-generation nonsteroidal MRA with greater MR affinity, received initial FDA approval in 2021 for risk reduction of adverse cardiorenal outcomes in adults with CKD associated with T2D, with subsequent pivotal trials demonstrating efficacy in CKD in T2D as well as HFmrEF and HFpEF.

Other nsMRA agents: **esaxerenone** (approved in Japan 2019 for primary hypertension); **ocedurenone** (terminated during phase III in hypertension for failing to meet primary endpoint). More recently, nsMRA/SGLT2i combination was investigated in the phase II **CONFIDENCE (NCT05254002)** trial, in which finerenone plus empagliflozin led to greater reductions in albuminuria and BP in people with CKD and T2D than SGLT2i monotherapy or placebo. **Balcinrenone (AZD9977)**, a novel nonsteroidal selective MR modulator, is in clinical development for HF and CKD as combination therapy with dapagliflozin.

**Hyperkalemia (HK):** Steroidal MRAs, particularly spironolactone, are associated with increased risk of HK, limiting widespread use. HK risk with RAAS inhibitor therapy may lead to dose reduction or discontinuation, negatively impacting outcomes. Comparison of HK rates suggests a lower incidence with finerenone, though head-to-head data are limited.
- In a prespecified analysis of **FINEARTS-HF (NCT04435626)**, risk of HK (potassium >5.5 mmol/L) was ~2-fold higher with finerenone vs. placebo across all ejection fraction groups.
- A post hoc analysis from FIDELIO-DKD reported ~2-fold higher rates of HK with finerenone vs. placebo (after 2.6 years median follow-up: mild HK [>5.5 mmol/L] 21.4% vs. 9.2%; moderate HK [>6.0 mmol/L] 4.5% vs. 1.4%). Impact of HK was diminished by potassium monitoring and HK management strategies.

## 5 Aldosterone synthase inhibitors: new agents that block aldosterone synthesis

**Osilodrostat (LCI699)** was the first ASi developed for human use; however, its selectivity for CYP11B2 vs. CYP11B1 gene products was limited in vitro, and doses >1 mg inhibited cortisol secretion. It was repurposed and developed as a treatment for Cushing's disease.

Next-generation ASis were developed with greater selectivity for CYP11B2 vs. CYP11B1: **baxdrostat (CIN-107), dexfadrostat (DP-13), lorundrostat (MLS-101), and vicadrostat (BI 690517)**.

**Blood pressure / hypertension:** Phase II and III trials demonstrated efficacy and safety of ASi monotherapy in BP reduction in treatment-resistant hypertension (R-HTN) and uncontrolled hypertension.
- Baxdrostat and lorundrostat produced placebo-adjusted systolic BP reductions of approximately **9 to 12 mmHg** (least-squares mean difference) in phase III trials (**BaxHTN, N = 796** and **Launch-HTN, N = 1083**, respectively) when added to background therapy.
- A baxdrostat phase III trial in R-HTN (**Bax24, N = 218**) demonstrated a significant placebo-adjusted reduction in ambulatory 24-hour average SBP of **14.0 mmHg (95% CI −17.2, −10.8; p < 0.0001)** after 12 weeks.

**ASi + SGLT2i combination:** Several trials evaluate ASi given with SGLT2i added to SoC.
- A phase II trial in CKD demonstrated dose-dependent reductions in albuminuria when **vicadrostat** was given as monotherapy and with **empagliflozin**, alongside background RAS inhibition (**N = 587, of whom 298 received vicadrostat with empagliflozin**), suggesting additive efficacy.
- **Vicadrostat + empagliflozin phase III program (EASi):** EASi-KIDNEY (planned enrollment N = 11,000), EASi-HF Preserved (N = 6000), EASi-HF Reduced (N = 4200), EASi-PROTKT (N = 11,800).
- **Baxdrostat + dapagliflozin phase III program (BaxDuo):** BaxDuo-PACIFIC (N = 5000), BaxDuo-ARCTIC (N = 2500), Prevent-HF (N = 11,300).
- In both EASi and BaxDuo programs, ASi plus SGLT2i is tested against active treatment (SGLT2i monotherapy plus SoC) rather than placebo. Overall participant numbers are considerably larger than ASi monotherapy trials, suggesting most clinical data on ASi will come from studies of ASi administered with SGLT2i.

**Rationale for ASi + SGLT2i:** SGLT2i have proven cardiorenal protective efficacy and improve cardiac and kidney outcomes. SGLT2i may mitigate the risk of serious HK (serum potassium ≥6.0 mmol/L) in people with CKD and T2D — important given that ASi agents have the potential to increase serum potassium.

**Hyperkalemia meta-analyses of ASi:**
- Meta-analysis of ASi RCTs (**7 RCT; N = 1440**): increased risk of HK vs. placebo (**relative risk 2.5; 95% CI 1.2 to 5.4; P < .02; I² = 0%**), with HK occurring in **6.2% of ASi-treated patients vs. 1.2% of placebo**; the increase in mean plasma potassium was small (0.3 mmol/L for ASi vs. 0.0 mmol/L for placebo).
- A further ASi meta-analysis (**5 RCT; N = 2456**): no increase in moderate-to-severe HK (≥6.0 mmol/L) for ASi at standard doses vs. placebo (**odds ratio 3.19; 95% CI 0.79 to 12.95; P = .104**), though a slight increase was associated with high-dose ASi regimens (**odds ratio 4.43; 95% CI 1.10 to 17.82; P = .036**).
- An ASi network meta-analysis (**6 RCT; N = 2149**): most adverse events were mild, serious adverse events infrequent, no drug-related mortalities reported. ASi generally showed favorable safety profiles; results from large, long-term phase III RCTs are awaited.

## 6 Conclusions

Preliminary evidence suggests the ASi drug class may offer additional benefits for cardiorenal disease protection on top of those from existing therapies that act along the RAAS pathway. ASi monotherapies show promise for BP control, and ongoing clinical trials of ASi with SGLT2i therapies will elucidate further outcome benefits in CV and renal diseases. Analysis of data from the multiple phase III trials in progress is needed to confirm long-term safety and efficacy in wider patient populations. **Head-to-head trials may be needed to compare ASi with nsMRA.** The differing roles of combination therapy with ASi vs. nsMRA agents for cardiorenal protection in real-world practice also need to be distinguished. Therapy in the cardiorenal arena has changed dramatically in the past decade with trials showing efficacy of SGLT2i, GLP-1 agonists, and nsMRAs; however, questions remain, including which combinations of these agents will reduce risk optimally and in which patient populations. Nevertheless, ASi agents — particularly when given with SGLT2i — appear well on the way to providing an additional drug class to combat the significant burden of cardiorenal disease outcomes.
