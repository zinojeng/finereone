## Abstract

Primary aldosteronism (PA) is an endocrinopathy characterized by dysregulated aldosterone production that occurs despite suppression of renin and angiotensin II, and that is non-suppressible by volume and sodium loading. The effectiveness of surgical adrenalectomy for patients with lateralizing PA is characterized by the attenuation of excess aldosterone production leading to blood pressure reduction, correction of hypokalemia, and increases in renin-biomarkers that collectively indicate a reversal of PA pathophysiology and restoration of normal physiology. Even though the vast majority of patients with PA will ultimately be treated medically rather than surgically, there is a lack of guidance on how to optimize medical therapy and on key metrics of success. Herein, we review the evidence justifying approaches to medical management of PA and biomarkers that reflect endocrine principles of restoring normal physiology. We review the current arsenal of medical therapies, including dietary sodium restriction, steroidal and nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, epithelial sodium channel inhibitors, and aldosterone synthase inhibitors. It is crucial that clinicians recognize that multimodal medical treatment for PA can be highly effective at reducing the risk for adverse cardiovascular and kidney outcomes when titrated with intention. The key biomarkers reflective of optimized medical therapy are unsurprisingly similar to the physiologic expectations following surgical adrenalectomy: control of blood pressure with the fewest number of antihypertensive agents, normalization of serum potassium without supplementation, and a rise in renin. Pragmatic approaches to achieve these objectives while mitigating adverse effects are reviewed.

## Key metrics of optimized medical therapy

The objectives of medical therapy mirror the physiologic outcomes expected after surgical adrenalectomy:
- Control of blood pressure with the fewest number of antihypertensive agents
- Normalization of serum potassium without potassium supplementation
- A rise in renin (reflecting relief of renin/angiotensin II suppression and restoration of normal physiology)

## Medical Therapies for Primary Aldosteronism

MR antagonists represent the primary pharmacologic approach for PA management; they act by blocking aldosterone's interaction with the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR).

### Steroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

**Spironolactone**
- FDA approved in 1960; described as "a nonselective steroidal derivative of progesterone that competitively antagonizes the MR."
- Undergoes hepatic metabolism into active compounds with long half-lives, supporting once-daily dosing despite spironolactone's own short ~1.4-hour half-life.
- Maximal treatment effect after ~3–4 weeks.
- Dosing typically starts at 12.5–25 mg daily, with escalation up to 400 mg daily in severe cases.
- Controls both hypertension and hypokalemia as monotherapy or in combination.
- Limitations: sex-hormone–related adverse effects (gynecomastia, breast tenderness, menstrual irregularities) and hyperkalemia risk, especially in chronic kidney disease (CKD).

**Eplerenone**
- Selective steroidal MRA developed in 1987.
- "In vitro affinity for the MR is about 40-fold lower compared to spironolactone, but its in vivo potency is partially compensated for by a higher bioavailability." In-vivo efficacy ~half that of spironolactone.
- Requires twice-daily administration (half-life ~3 hours).
- Maximum FDA-approved dose 100 mg daily, though higher doses may be needed for severe PA.
- Food does not affect bioavailability.
- Minimal anti-androgenic risk; preferred for men experiencing such effects from spironolactone.

### Nonsteroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists

**Esaxerenone**
- Currently available only in Japan.
- Phase 3 ESAX-HTN trial (1001 hypertensive adults) showed dose-dependent BP reductions: "esaxerenone 5 mg = −16.5 mmHg, esaxerenone 2.5 mg = −13.1 mmHg, eplerenone 50 mg = −11.9 mmHg" (absolute differences modest).

**Finerenone**
- "A similar potency to spironolactone, and an MR selectivity 500-fold higher than eplerenone."
- Balanced cardiac and renal tissue distribution.
- Large randomized trials showed reduction in CKD progression and cardiovascular events in type 2 diabetes at doses of 10–20 mg daily.
- ARTS-HF study (finerenone vs eplerenone): "similar rates of hyperkalemia among both the finerenone and eplerenone groups," contrary to theoretical advantages.

**Ocedurenone** (formerly KBP-5074)
- Highly selective nonsteroidal MRA; "binding affinity for the MR is greater than that of steroidal MR antagonists."
- Extended ~60-hour half-life permits once-daily dosing.
- Phase 2b trial in advanced CKD: BP reductions of 7.0–10.2 mmHg at 2.5–5.0 mg daily; hyperkalemia incidence 8.8–16.7% depending on dose.

## Epithelial Sodium Channel (ENaC) Inhibitors

ENaC inhibitors block sodium reabsorption at the collecting duct, interrupting the sodium-retention cycle characteristic of PA pathophysiology. However, they cannot address mineralocorticoid-mediated tissue injury, limiting them to an adjunctive or alternative role.

**Amiloride**
- Reversibly blocks the ENaC luminal pore.
- Oral bioavailability ~50%, potentially reduced by food.
- Dosing typically starts at 5 mg daily; severe PA frequently requires 10 mg twice daily or higher.
- PATHWAY-2 open-label extension: amiloride BP reduction "proportional to the severity of renin-independent aldosterone production," comparable to spironolactone.

**Triamterene**
- Less antihypertensive efficacy than amiloride.
- Typical dosing 50–300 mg daily.
- Available as monotherapy or combined with hydrochlorothiazide.

## Aldosterone Synthase Inhibitors (ASIs)

These agents target CYP11B2, the terminal enzyme in aldosterone synthesis. Development has been challenging because CYP11B2 shares 93% homology with CYP11B1 (11β-hydroxylase), risking inadvertent inhibition of cortisol synthesis.

**Baxdrostat** (formerly RO6836191)
- "In vitro selectivity >100-fold over CYP11B1."
- Phase 2 BrigHTN trial (248 treatment-resistant hypertension patients): "baxdrostat achieved dose-dependent reductions in systolic blood pressure (−10 mmHg) compared to placebo" at 0.5–2 mg daily.
- Subsequent HALO trial did not meet its primary endpoint in the full intention-to-treat population; a prespecified subgroup of non-Hispanic patients showed a "significant placebo-adjusted reduction in systolic blood pressure of 12.6 mmHg at a baxdrostat dose of 2 mg."

**Lorundrostat** (MLS-101)
- Completed the Target-HTN phase 2 trial, demonstrating safety and tolerability with robust BP reductions in uncontrolled hypertension despite multiple medications (per 2023 American College of Cardiology presentation).

## Mechanistic contrast relevant to MRA vs ASI

- **MR antagonists** (steroidal: spironolactone, eplerenone; nonsteroidal: finerenone, esaxerenone, ocedurenone) act at the receptor, blocking aldosterone-mediated MR activation and its downstream sodium retention and tissue injury.
- **ENaC inhibitors** (amiloride, triamterene) act downstream at the sodium channel; they interrupt sodium retention but do NOT block mineralocorticoid-mediated tissue injury.
- **Aldosterone synthase inhibitors** (baxdrostat, lorundrostat) act upstream, reducing aldosterone production at CYP11B2; the central challenge is achieving selectivity over the highly homologous CYP11B1 to avoid impairing cortisol synthesis.
