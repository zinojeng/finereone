# Sodium intake and MRA susceptibility in primary aldosteronism (Kidoguchi 2026)

> 取得備註：Springer 全文付費牆 (isAccessibleForFree:false)，PMC 無收錄，Sci-Hub 尚無此新文。以下為 PubMed 結構化摘要逐字內容，**無法取得結果表格的精確數值**（各組 PRA/PAC 數值、responder 人數分佈、OR/95%CI、p 值皆未在摘要中給出）。

## Background
Patients with primary aldosteronism (PA) have an increased risk of developing cardiovascular disease. In patients with bilateral adrenal aldosterone hypersecretion, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) are recommended; however, the benefit of these agents is observed only in cases that show a sufficient increase in post-treatment PRA (responders). Because renin secretion can be influenced by sodium intake, the increase in post-treatment PRA may be attributable to sodium restriction.

## Methods
A total of **90 patients** who received **eplerenone or esaxerenone** for PA treatment were included in the study. Patients whose **PRA was ≥ 1.0 ng/mL/h at one year** after the initiation of MRAs treatment were defined as **responders**. The predictors of responders and the effect of sodium intake were investigated.

(Sodium intake estimated from casual/spot urine using the Tanaka et al. 2002 formula — ref 26.)

## Results
- Both **baseline and post-treatment PRA and PAC** levels were **higher in responders**.
- **Baseline** estimated sodium intake **tended to be lower** in responders.
- **Post-treatment** estimated sodium intake was **significantly lower** in responders.
- Post-treatment PRA value was **significantly correlated** with post-treatment estimated sodium intake.
- Post-treatment estimated sodium intake was an **independent predictor of responders** (multivariate) → post-treatment PRA elevation may be partially attributable to adequate sodium restriction.
- **eGFR change over the 3-year follow-up was NOT different** between responders and non-responders.

## Conclusion
Although sodium restriction is suggested to facilitate attainment of post-treatment **PRA ≥ 1.0 ng/mL/h** (a marker of adequate aldosterone blockade), it remains **uncertain whether achieving PRA ≥ 1.0 ng/mL/h is associated with favorable renal outcomes**.

## Relevance to review (07 — ASI vs finerenone / nsMRA in PA)
- PA cohort n=90 treated with MRA, including **nsMRA esaxerenone** (a nonsteroidal MRA), alongside eplerenone.
- Reinforces the **PRA/renin-response concept** (PA "PAMO"-style renin unsuppression as a treatment-adequacy marker), showing renin rise is **confounded by sodium intake** — a caveat for using post-treatment PRA as a surrogate.
- Negative/null renal signal: **no eGFR difference** by responder status over 3 years → does NOT establish a PA hard outcome benefit for renin-guided MRA titration.
