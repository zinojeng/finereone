## Abstract

Excess aldosterone production contributes to the development of hypertension and results in fibrosis with dysfunction of the heart, vasculature and kidneys. Consequently, new agents have been developed to reduce endogenous aldosterone synthesis. The primary objective of this systematic review is to describe the BP-lowering effects of aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs) in hypertensive patients and, secondly, to describe their potential renal protective effects and possible influence on cortisol production and plasma potassium. We searched PubMed, Embase and ClinicalTrials.gov and included randomized controlled and clinical trials according to PICO using the review tool Covidence. Thirteen studies were included and all demonstrated BP reduction through ASI treatment. Among patients with apparent resistant hypertension, the placebo-corrected reductions in seated systolic BP were 11.0 mmHg for baxdrostat and 9.6 mmHg for lorundrostat. A significant suppression of cortisol production was found for LCI699 (osilodrostat) but not for baxdrostat, lorundrostat, BI 690517 (vicadrostat) or dexfadrostat. Studies on BI 690517 showed a reduction in urine–albumin–creatinine ratio, indicating renal protection. ASIs may increase potassium levels. We conclude that ASIs have promising BP-lowering effects with very limited effects on cortisol production and offer reno-protective effects in chronic kidney disease. Studies on hypertensive target organ damage and cardiovascular outcomes are, however, lacking.

## Summary

Poorly controlled hypertension results in cardiovascular diseases. Excess production of the hormone aldosterone worsens hypertension and organ damage. Aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs) constitute a novel group of medications, and five different ASIs have now been tested in clinical trials. This article systematically assesses the effects of ASIs on blood pressure, and in patients with difficult-to-treat hypertension, ASIs can reduce systolic blood pressure by 10 mmHg. Moreover, ASIs may protect kidney function by decreasing protein excretion. In terms of adverse events, ASIs can increase plasma potassium, whereas one ASI was found to decrease cortisol production. ASIs may improve future hypertension treatment.

## 1 | Introduction

### 1.1 | Background

High systolic blood pressure (BP) is globally a major risk factor for mortality. The age-standardized prevalence of hypertension (systolic BP ≥ 140 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥ 90 mmHg) is 33%. Because of population growth and an increasing proportion of elderly individuals, the number of people with hypertension has increased from 650 million in 1990 to 1.3 billion in 2019. Lowering systolic BP by 10 mmHg significantly reduces the risk of major cardiovascular disease events such as coronary heart disease, stroke and heart failure, resulting in a 13% reduction in all-cause mortality.

Unfortunately, only 23% of women and 18% of men with hypertension gain sufficient BP control. This is due mainly to underdiagnosis; however, even among those diagnosed with hypertension, 61% of women and 63% of men are either untreated or receive treatment without achieving BP control. Among the treated patients, 5%–10% suffer from apparent resistant hypertension, defined as uncontrolled BP remaining at ≥ 130-mmHg systolic BP and/or ≥ 80-mmHg diastolic BP despite concurrent prescription of three or four antihypertensive drugs of different classes or controlled BP with the prescription of ≥ 4 antihypertensive drugs, including a thiazide diuretic and all medications at maximally tolerated doses. This suggests that the currently available antihypertensive agents are insufficient for the treatment of certain hypertensive patients.

The primary objective of this systematic review is to update the current knowledge about the BP-lowering effects of aldosterone synthase inhibitors (ASIs) in humans. The secondary objective is to assess the effects of ASIs on cortisol production and their renal protective properties.

### 1.2 | Aldosterone

The renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system (RAAS) plays a central role in BP regulation because of angiotensin II's effect on vascular tone and aldosterone's effect on electrolyte and water homeostasis. Primary aldosteronism is a condition with uncontrolled high aldosterone production and, consequently, suppressed renin. However, excess aldosterone is also seen in other patients, including some with treatment-resistant hypertension, without meeting the diagnostic criteria for primary aldosteronism. Excess aldosterone can lead to the worsening of hypertension and contribute to hypertensive organ damage in terms of fibrosis and dysfunction of the myocardium, vasculature and kidneys. Therefore, treatment of excess aldosterone is not only of interest in hypertension but also in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) or heart failure.

(Figure 1 illustrates the RAAS system and the contribution of aldosterone to BP regulation.) Renin, produced in the kidneys, converts angiotensinogen from the liver to angiotensin I. This intermediate is converted to angiotensin II by angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE). Angiotensin II is the main stimulant of the adrenal production of aldosterone, which is catalysed by aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2). In the kidneys, aldosterone binds to the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR). This leads to tubular uptake of sodium (Na+) and excretion of potassium (K+) and hydrogen (H+). Sodium is the primary determinant of extracellular fluid volume, and water is passively absorbed as sodium is reabsorbed, leading to increased BP.

However, aldosterone affects multiple other cells expressing MR such as cardiomyocytes, cardiac fibroblasts and vascular smooth muscle cells. Furthermore, aldosterone triggers nongenomic effects that occur independently of MR activation. It has been suggested that G protein-coupled oestrogen receptor-1 (GPER-1) may mediate some of the rapid effects of aldosterone, but the MR-independent effects are not fully understood and may be dependent on the degree of vascular oxidative stress. In unstressed vasculature with normal endothelial function, aldosterone causes vasodilation, whereas in vasculature with inflammation, atherosclerosis and high oxidative stress, aldosterone causes vasoconstriction and increased oxidative damage. In another study, aldosterone at high concentrations, as observed in patients with heart failure, was shown to induce a negative inotropic effect. The effects on vascular function were similar, with aldosterone enhancing the vasoconstrictive response to angiotensin II in isolated human coronary arteries and porcine renal arteries. Notably, these effects were not blocked by MR antagonists. In patients with risk factors such as hypertension, heart failure, high oxygen tension and high salt intake, excess aldosterone might induce rapid vasoconstriction and an increase in vascular stiffness, contributing to elevated BP.

In current antihypertensive treatment, the RAAS is directly targeted by ACE inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs), and MR antagonists. However, even long-term treatment with ACE inhibitors and ARB do not sufficiently inhibit aldosterone synthesis, and MR antagonist only inhibits the effects of aldosterone mediated through the MR and not the MR-independent nongenomic effects.

The ASIs constitute a novel therapeutic group that may overcome some of these problems. ASIs specifically block CYP11B2, the enzyme catalysing the final step in the synthesis of aldosterone and therefore reducing plasma aldosterone concentrations by 50%–70%, as opposed to MR antagonists, which increase circulating aldosterone levels. This new principle of RAAS blockade could therefore improve current antihypertensive treatment regimens. A recent meta-analysis by Marzano et al. based on 1,440 patients with primary hypertension or CKD from seven published RCTs demonstrated an overall reduction in office systolic BP of 6.3 mmHg, which was dependent on dose. Our systematic review also reports data from trials in patients with primary hyperaldosteronism and trials in patients with diabetic kidney disease in addition to data from the HALO trial.

### 1.3 | Cortisol Synthesis

Aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) shares 93% homology with cortisol synthase (CYP11B1). The first generation of ASIs had a low specificity towards CYP11B2; consequently, it also suppressed the production of cortisol. These are now being investigated for their potential to decrease cortisol levels in patients with Cushing's disease. The impact of ASIs on cortisol levels is very important as cortisol suppression is unwanted and can cause major adverse effects. However, cortisol levels vary throughout the day, and serum cortisol is consequently an inadequate marker for cortisol synthase inhibition. The adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) stimulation test can better investigate the ability of the adrenal glands to produce cortisol. In this test, the plasma cortisol levels are measured before and after an injection of ACTH and are used to examine if cortisol synthesis is suppressed.

### 1.4 | Kidney Protection

Hypertension is both a risk factor for and consequence of CKD. Up to 90% of CKD patients have hypertension, which contributes to the progression of kidney disease. The prevalence of apparent resistant hypertension is 23% among CKD patients and 56% among renal transplant patients, underscoring the challenge of treating hypertension in these patient categories. Reduction in albuminuria has been linked to a decrease in the risk of end-stage kidney disease across various cohorts and is therefore often used as a surrogate marker of a drug's reno-protective properties.

ACE inhibitors and ARBs are first-line antihypertensive agents in patients with CKD and albuminuria, as they reduce proteinuria partly independent of their BP-lowering effects. Addition of sodium–glucose cotransport Type 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors and the non-steroid MR antagonist finerenone can further reduce the risk of CKD progression. Having the complex effects of aldosterone in mind, the inhibition of aldosterone synthesis could also be beneficial in CKD as an alternative to MR antagonists.

Inhibition of aldosterone synthesis offers a promising therapeutic approach by reducing both MR-dependent and -independent effects of aldosterone. Although the MR-independent mechanisms are not fully understood, they likely involve rapid nongenomic actions that contribute to vascular dysfunction, oxidative stress and increased vasoconstriction, possibly resulting in hypertension. Aldosterone synthase inhibition may improve treatment outcomes beyond those achievable with current RAAS-targeted therapies, including MR antagonists, particularly in patients with resistant hypertension or CKD.

## 2 | Methods

This review is registered with PROSPERO (registration number: CRD42024586848) and conducted in accordance with PRISMA guidelines.

### 2.1 | Criteria for Inclusion of Studies (PICO)

- **Population:** Human adults ≥ 18 years of age.
- **Intervention:** Any ASI.
- **Comparator:** Placebo, baseline or another antihypertensive agent.
- **Outcome:** Change in systolic BP or/and diastolic BP.

### 2.2 | Search Strategy and Eligibility Criteria

On the 30th of August 2024, the authors searched PubMed, Embase, Clinicaltrials.gov and clinicaltrials.eu. On the 15th of January 2025, the search was updated with studies published between 30th of August 2024 and 15th of January 2025. RCTs and clinical trials meeting PICO were eligible. Studies with only abstracts available or clinical trials with only results were included if relevant data were presented. Search terms (all fields, no filters): '((Aldosterone synthase inhibitor) OR (Baxdrostat) OR (Lorundrostat) OR (BI 690517) OR (Dexfadrostat) OR (LCI699)) AND ((Hypertension) OR (Blood pressure))'.

### 2.3 | Study Selection

Two reviewers (A.A.R. and K.L.N.) independently selected studies using Covidence. Duplicates were identified and excluded. Screening proceeded by title/abstract and then full text against PICO criteria; discrepancies resolved by agreement.

### 2.4 | General Characteristics

Key study characteristics (sample size, population, outcome measures, treatment duration) extracted by one reviewer (A.A.R.) and cross-checked by the second (K.L.N.). Numbers under general characteristics are at randomization; numbers in the results table are patients treated with the given dose.

### 2.5 | Primary Outcome

Change in BP. All but one included study present BP data as changes in seated systolic BP and seated diastolic BP. Two studies present results as changes in 24-h ambulatory BP measurements. Seated/consultation/ambulatory BP in mmHg was extracted; if not available, 24-h ambulatory BP in mmHg was used.

### 2.6 | Comparator

Priority was to extract baseline placebo-corrected changes in systolic and diastolic BP. If unavailable, BP changes of both intervention and placebo groups were extracted; if no placebo results existed, only intervention group results were used.

### 2.7 | Dose of Intervention

Doses are not directly comparable. The dose extracted corresponds to the largest reduction in systolic BP, and this dose was used for extraction of systolic and diastolic BP changes.

### 2.8 | Secondary Outcomes

- **2.8.1 Cortisol Synthesis:** ACTH stimulation test results as first priority; change from baseline in serum or urinary cortisol as secondary. Presented as significant or non-significant.
- **2.8.2 Hyperkalaemia:** Prevalence extracted; combined dosage group compared with respective placebo group. Treatment withdrawal due to hyperkalaemia used as a severity indicator.
- **2.8.3 Renal Protection:** Kidney damage parameters extracted, prioritizing albuminuria or proteinuria.
- **2.8.4 Ongoing Trials:** Identified on ClinicalTrials.gov and ClinicalTrials.eu; presented in Table S1.

## 3 | Results

The initial literature search identified 548 records, and the updated search added 33 records to a total of 581 records. After exclusion of 122 duplications, the Covidence automation tool identified 247 ineligible records (all excluded after independent title/abstract screening). Of the remaining 212 records, 136 were excluded on title/abstract. Full text was assessed for 76 articles, leading to exclusion of 64 studies and inclusion of 12 studies. Since the primary search, one ongoing trial has been published and subsequently included, resulting in **a total of 13 studies** in the review.

### 3.1 | General BP-Lowering Effects of ASIs

Common to all studies is a reduction in systolic and diastolic BP in patients treated with ASI. Seven of the included studies show a ≥ 10-mmHg reduction in systolic BP, and the remaining six show a 4- to 10-mmHg reduction. Seven studies showed a reduction of ≥ 5 mmHg in diastolic BP, and four showed a reduction of 0.9–5 mmHg. Two studies did not present data on changes in diastolic BP.

### 3.2 | BP-Lowering Effects of LCI699 (osilodrostat)

Five included studies provide results for LCI699. All show a decrease in systolic and diastolic BP, but only Andersen et al. demonstrates a significant placebo-corrected effect, with reductions in systolic BP of 12.5 mmHg (p = 0.022) and diastolic BP of 9.2 mmHg (p = 0.007). Calhoun et al. (primary hypertension) presented a nonplacebo-corrected significant reduction in systolic BP of 12.6 mmHg and diastolic BP of 7.1 mmHg, whereas the placebo group decreased by 2.6 mmHg (both systolic and diastolic). Karns et al. demonstrated a placebo-corrected non-significant reduction in resistant hypertension. Two trials without placebo groups investigated LCI699 in primary aldosteronism and Cushing's disease, both demonstrating BP-lowering effects.

### 3.3 | BP-Lowering Effects of Dexfadrostat

One study (Wuerzner et al., Phase II) studied patients with primary aldosteronism and hypertension. Only seated systolic BP was available as a combined group (4, 8 or 12 mg once daily); no placebo group. The combined dosage group showed a significant reduction from baseline in systolic BP of 10.7 mmHg (95% CI [7.9, 13.6]) and diastolic BP of 5.7 mmHg (95% CI [3.5, 7.8]).

### 3.4 | BP-Lowering Effects of Lorundrostat

Two studies investigated lorundrostat in the same population of resistant hypertension. Laffin et al. found a significant placebo-corrected reduction in systolic BP of 9.6 mmHg (90% CI [3.4, 15.8]) and diastolic BP of 5.5 mmHg (90% CI [1.5, 9.4]). In a substudy, Rodman et al. reported on patients with BMI > 30 kg/m²: a significant placebo-corrected reduction in systolic BP of 16.7 mmHg (90% CI [7.9, 25.5]); no diastolic BP data in the abstract. More recently, Laffin et al. (2025) reported a significant placebo-adjusted reduction in systolic BP of 7.9 mmHg (97.5% CI [13.3, 2.6]) in patients on two to five antihypertensive agents; diastolic BP not reported.

### 3.5 | BP-Lowering Effects of Baxdrostat

Two studies examined a 2 mg daily dose in resistant hypertension. Freeman et al. found placebo-corrected significant reductions in systolic and diastolic BP of 11.0 mmHg (95% CI [5.5, 16.4]) and 5.2 mmHg (95% CI [1.6, 8.8]). The HALO study described nonplacebo-corrected reductions in systolic and diastolic BP of 19.8 and 5.4 mmHg; the HALO placebo group also showed large reductions of 16.6 mmHg systolic and 5.9 mmHg diastolic.

### 3.6 | BP-Lowering Effects of BI 690517 (vicadrostat)

Two studies investigated BI 690517 in patients with CKD. Tuttle et al. found non-significant placebo-corrected changes in systolic and diastolic BP of −6.03 mmHg (95% CI [−12.44, 0.38]) and −0.94 mmHg (95% CI [−4.31, 2.43]) using a 20 mg daily dose. Bornstein et al. found a significant, but not placebo-corrected, systolic BP change of −6.38 mmHg (95% CI [−11.64, −1.11]) and a non-significant diastolic BP change of −2.95 mmHg (95% CI [−6.33, 0.43]) among patients with CKD and Type 2 diabetes at a 3 mg daily dose.

### 3.7 | Effects of Aldosterone Synthase Inhibition on Cortisol

Three included studies on LCI699 showed a significant impact on cortisol synthesis (ACTH stimulation test or urinary-free cortisol). None of the studies on baxdrostat, lorundrostat, BI 690517 or dexfadrostat could detect any influence on cortisol levels (ACTH stimulation test, serum cortisol, or 24-h urinary cortisol). Four included studies did not present cortisol measures.

### 3.8 | Prevalence of Hyperkalaemia in Patients Treated With ASI

Prevalence ranged from 0% to 13.5%, with the highest reported by Laffin et al. in resistant hypertension treated with lorundrostat. The most recent lorundrostat study (Laffin et al.) reported 10.1%. Hyperkalaemia prevalence was 3.5% in both baxdrostat studies. For BI 690517 in CKD, Bornstein et al. and Tuttle et al. reported 4.4% and 13.3%, respectively; in Tuttle et al. the placebo group prevalence was 6.8% (highest placebo-group value observed). Less hyperkalaemia was observed in LCI699 studies in primary hypertension: Calhoun et al. 3.1% and Andersen et al. 2.0%. Withdrawal due to hyperkalaemia was rare and inconsistently reported: no withdrawals in Calhoun et al. or Wuerzner et al.; single cases in Andersen et al. and Bornstein et al.; three temporary discontinuations in Freeman et al.; 4% discontinuation in Tuttle et al. The most recent Laffin et al. study did not report withdrawals but stratified severity, with 1% having serum potassium > 6.5 mmol/L.

### 3.9 | Kidney Protection by Aldosterone Synthase Inhibition

Two studies present kidney damage parameter results. Tuttle et al. (BI 690517 in CKD) defined responders as patients with a > 30% reduction in urine albumin–creatinine ratio (uACR) from baseline; the study found a placebo-corrected odds ratio of being a responder of 6.09 (95% CI [2.64, 14.08]) with a 10-mg daily dose. Bornstein et al. (BI 690517 in diabetic kidney disease): a 40-mg dose caused a ≥ 20% decrease from baseline in uACR in 80% of patients, versus 37.5% with placebo. In the study by Laffin et al., a reduction in eGFR of 13%–15% was observed among patients treated with lorundrostat, compared with a 3% decline in placebo; after washout, eGFR increased in the lorundrostat group while remaining stable in placebo—indicating well-known effects of aldosterone inhibition on glomerular haemodynamics also seen with MR antagonists.

## 4 | Discussion

This systematic review demonstrates promising BP-lowering effects of several ASIs across various patient categories. The most pronounced placebo-adjusted reductions were reported in patients with apparent resistant or primary hypertension. ASIs also have effects on primary hyperaldosteronism and Cushing's disease. In addition, ASIs have potential reno-protective properties as documented by a reduction in albumin excretion beyond the blockade with ACE inhibitors and ARBs. The new generation of ASIs shows no or minimal impact on cortisol production. These findings establish ASIs as a novel and overall safe treatment for hypertension and CKD.

### 4.1 | Data Extraction and Interpretation of Results

The review includes very diverse studies (resistant/uncontrolled hypertension to CKD with mildly elevated or controlled BP). Hypertensive patients have a larger potential for BP reduction, which may underestimate the effect of BI 690517 (mainly studied in CKD with well-controlled BP). MR antagonists are unlikely confounders, as all included studies excluded MR-antagonist users or implemented washout. Half of the studies do not present placebo-corrected BP changes—a major challenge, because nonplacebo-corrected results consistently overestimate the BP-lowering effect (white coat effect reduction, placebo physiological responses). The most pronounced placebo effect was in HALO (systolic BP reduction 19.8 mmHg active vs 16.6 mmHg placebo). Data extraction used the dosage associated with the largest systolic BP reduction, which may introduce bias (e.g., BI 690517 doses of 3 mg vs 20 mg across studies). Seated systolic BP was preferred over 24-h ambulatory BP for comparability, but office-seated BP is susceptible to white-coat effects. Phase I, II and III trials were included to maximize data.

### 4.2 | BP-Lowering Effects of ASIs

A previous systematic review summarized ASIs only in resistant hypertension (Calhoun, Freeman, Laffin). This review is the first to summarize BP-lowering effects of all available ASIs across different populations. So far, none of the ASIs tested in humans are significantly superior to others. LCI699 has demonstrated significant BP reduction in primary hypertension. Patients with CKD treated with BI 690517 show moderate BP reductions (none statistically significant and placebo-corrected). Dexfadrostat appears to have superior effects vs LCI699 in primary aldosteronism (not statistically significant). Large, significant placebo-corrected reductions in resistant hypertension were seen with baxdrostat and lorundrostat, whereas LCI699 showed only a small, non-significant placebo-corrected reduction.

According to the European Society of Hypertension guidelines, spironolactone is recommended for resistant hypertension, based on a placebo-corrected significant systolic BP reduction of 8.70 mmHg in PATHWAY-2. Comparable effects are observed for lorundrostat (9.6 mmHg placebo-corrected) and baxdrostat (11.0 mmHg placebo-corrected), suggesting ASIs are at least as effective as spironolactone in resistant hypertension. In contrast, Karns et al. comparing LCI699 with eplerenone reported a non-significant placebo-corrected systolic BP reduction of only 4.3 mmHg with LCI699 vs 9.9 mmHg with eplerenone. Two additional studies demonstrated significantly smaller systolic BP reductions with LCI699 vs eplerenone in primary aldosteronism or primary hypertension. No studies have compared baxdrostat, lorundrostat, dexfadrostat or BI 690517 with MR antagonists. Currently, the BP-lowering properties of ASIs and spironolactone must be considered comparable. No studies are testing ASI effects on cardiovascular outcomes (stroke, myocardial ischemic events).

### 4.3 | The Effect of ASIs on CKD

The only ASI with kidney function results is BI 690517 (vicadrostat), showing promising reno-protective effects in CKD via reduction in urine albumin excretion. Tuttle et al. found patients treated with BI 690517 had a significantly greater chance of reaching a ≥ 30% reduction in uACR (odds ratio 6.09 vs placebo). A recent post hoc analysis revealed BI 690517 reduced uACR also in patients already treated with an SGLT2i, demonstrating additional renoprotection. Longer-term studies with hard renal endpoints (reduced GFR loss) are still lacking.

### 4.4 | The Effect of ASIs on Cortisol Synthesis

LCI699 studies consistently demonstrated depression of cortisol production, likely due to inhibition of cortisol synthase—an undesirable side effect that probably renders LCI699 unsuitable for primary hypertension; it is now approved to reduce cortisol levels in Cushing's disease. In contrast, the new-generation ASIs—baxdrostat, lorundrostat, dexfadrostat and BI 690517—show no signs of cortisol depression in any included study, pointing to high specificity for aldosterone synthase.

### 4.5 | Risk of Hyperkalaemia Associated With ASI Treatment

Hyperkalaemia is a well-documented adverse effect of RAAS inhibition. In the included studies, prevalence ranged from 0% to 13.5%; most cases were mild to moderate. Treatment discontinuation due to hyperkalaemia was reported in 0% to 4% of patients. In primary aldosteronism studies, ASI led to normalization of potassium in most patients with baseline hypokalaemia. A Danish population-based study followed 262,375 new users of RAAS inhibitors, with 16% experiencing a first hyperkalaemia event; thus ASIs may carry a similar, or slightly lower, risk of hyperkalaemia compared with older RAAS-targeting drugs.

## 5 | Limitations

First, no systematic bias assessment was performed. Second, no meta-analysis was conducted; results were not weighted by study size. Inclusion of non-RCT studies and studies not presenting placebo-corrected results introduces a risk of bias and may overestimate ASI effects. Finally, the available studies are highly diverse in aim, population, drug, dose and comparator, reducing the ability to determine which ASI achieves the largest BP reduction.

### 5.1 | Ongoing Trials

Eight ongoing clinical trials investigating ASIs with a change in BP as an outcome were identified (all registered on ClinicalTrials.gov). Details in Table S1.

## 6 | Conclusion

The ASIs constitute a novel drug class with promising antihypertensive effects across various patient categories with elevated BP and may also be reno-protective in CKD. Both lorundrostat and baxdrostat demonstrate BP reductions comparable with spironolactone, the current standard treatment for resistant hypertension. Future studies on each ASI in relevant populations will be essential to evaluate their role in future antihypertensive and cardiorenal treatment.
