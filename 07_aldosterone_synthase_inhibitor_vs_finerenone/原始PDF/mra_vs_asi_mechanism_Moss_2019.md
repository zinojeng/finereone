# The Endothelial Mineralocorticoid Receptor: Contributions to Sex Differences in Cardiovascular Disease

**Authors:** M. Elizabeth Moss; Brigett Carvajal; Iris Z. Jaffe
**Affiliations:** Molecular Cardiology Research Institute, Tufts Medical Center, Boston, MA; Sackler School of Graduate Biomedical Sciences, Tufts University School of Medicine, Boston, MA
**Journal:** Pharmacol Ther. 2019 Jul 02;203:107387. doi:10.1016/j.pharmthera.2019.06.009
**Keywords:** endothelial cell, mineralocorticoid receptor, cardiovascular disease, sex differences, aldosterone, estrogen

---

## Abstract

Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of death for both men and women. The observation that premenopausal women are protected from cardiovascular disease relative to age-matched men, and that this protection is lost with menopause, has led to extensive study of the role of sex steroid hormones in the pathogenesis of cardiovascular disease. However, the molecular basis for sex differences in cardiovascular disease is still not fully understood, limiting the ability to tailor therapies to male and female patients. Therefore, there is a growing need to investigate molecular pathways outside of traditional sex hormone signaling to fully understand sex differences in cardiovascular disease. Emerging evidence points to the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), a steroid hormone receptor activated by the adrenal hormone aldosterone, as one such mediator of cardiovascular disease risk, potentially serving as a sex-dependent link between cardiovascular risk factors and disease. Enhanced activation of the MR by aldosterone is associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease. Emerging evidence implicates the MR specifically within the endothelial cells lining the blood vessels in mediating some of the sex differences observed in cardiovascular pathology. This review summarizes the available clinical and preclinical literature concerning the role of the MR in the pathophysiology of endothelial dysfunction, hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure, with a special emphasis on sex differences in the role of endothelial-specific MR in these pathologies. The available data regarding the molecular mechanisms by which endothelial-specific MR may contribute to sex differences in cardiovascular disease is also summarized. A paradigm emerges from synthesis of the literature in which endothelial-specific MR regulates vascular function in a sex-dependent manner in response to cardiovascular risk factors to contribute to disease. Limitations in this field include the relative paucity of women in clinical trials and, until recently, the nearly exclusive use of male animals in preclinical investigations. Enhanced understanding of the sex-specific roles of endothelial MR could lead to novel mechanistic insights underlying sex differences in cardiovascular disease incidence and outcomes and could identify additional therapeutic targets to effectively treat cardiovascular disease in men and women.

## I. INTRODUCTION

### Gaps in Knowledge of the Mechanisms Underlying Sex Differences in Cardiovascular Disease

Cardiovascular pathologies such as hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure lead to substantial morbidity, and heart disease remains the leading cause of death in both men and women (Xu et al. 2018). While premenopausal women are protected from cardiovascular disease relative to age-matched men, this protection is lost with menopause, implicating sex hormones in the pathogenesis of cardiovascular disease. The role of sex hormones, particularly signaling through estrogen receptor (ER) isoforms α and β, has been extensively studied (Arnold et al. 2017). However, due to the complex nature of sex steroid signaling pathways, the molecular basis for sex differences in cardiovascular disease is still not fully understood, limiting the ability to tailor therapies to male and female patients.

Common cardiovascular risk factors such as metabolic syndrome and obesity abolish the protection from cardiovascular disease in women even prior to menopause (Barrett-Connor et al. 1991; Sowers 1998; Wilson et al. 2002), highlighting the need to investigate molecular pathways outside of traditional sex hormone signaling. Patient cohorts in cardiovascular disease clinical trials are heavily weighted towards male patients, with women constituting only a minority of study participants. Further, most preclinical studies focus on male animals, with very few directly comparing the sexes.

### The Mineralocorticoid Receptor: Regulator of Blood Pressure, Mediator of Cardiovascular Disease

The mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) was first described to contribute to blood pressure control by regulating the transcription and expression of sodium transport proteins in the distal nephron (Arriza et al. 1987). Emerging evidence now points to the MR as a broader mediator of cardiovascular disease risk, potentially serving as a sex-dependent link between cardiovascular risk factors and disease (Davel et al. 2018a). The MR is a transcription factor that can be activated either by glucocorticoids such as cortisol (corticosterone in rodents), which circulate at high levels, or by its more specific but less abundant ligand aldosterone (Aldo) (Funder 2010). Individual tissues maintain specificity of the MR for Aldo by expression of the 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase 2 (11βHSD2) enzyme, which converts MR-binding glucocorticoids to metabolites that cannot bind to the MR (Naray-Fejes-Toth et al. 1998).

Independently of the relationship between the MR and blood pressure, elevated serum Aldo levels are associated with substantially increased risk of stroke, myocardial infarction (MI), and sudden cardiac death (Ivanes et al. 2012; Milliez et al. 2005). Conversely, inhibition of the MR in large randomized clinical trials such as RALES, EPHESUS, and EMPHASIS-HF results in significant reductions in mortality in heart failure patients. This decrease in mortality is associated with only modest changes in blood pressure along with trends towards decreased MI risk when secondary endpoints are examined (Zannad et al. 2011; Pitt et al. 1999; Pitt et al. 2003b). Substantial preclinical investigation has focused on understanding the role of MR signaling in non-renal cells in the development of cardiovascular disease.

### Vascular Cell-Specific Mineralocorticoid Receptors Contribute to Cardiovascular Disease

The vascular wall has three parts: an inner layer of endothelial cells (ECs) forming the interface between circulating blood and underlying tissues; a medial layer of smooth muscle cells (SMCs) which contract or relax to control vessel diameter; and an outer layer of adventitial fibroblasts and adipose cells providing structural support. The inner EC layer contributes to vasodilation by activating ion channels and releasing paracrine factors including the anti-inflammatory, antioxidant gas nitric oxide (NO) (Vanhoutte et al. 2016). The endothelium also regulates inflammatory cell recruitment by modulating endothelial-leukocyte adhesion molecules and by generating reactive oxygen species (ROS).

The MR is expressed in vascular SMCs and ECs. In its genomic role, the MR within ECs (EC-MR) regulates genes contributing to critical EC functions, including inflammatory mediators and regulators of endothelial sodium handling and junctional integrity (Kusche-Vihrog et al. 2010; Moss and Jaffe 2015; Kirsch et al. 2013). EC-MR also contributes to NO bioavailability and oxidative stress via rapid, "non-genomic" signaling (Wehling 2018). Multiple studies demonstrate ECs express 11βHSD2 capable of inactivating cortisol (Christy et al. 2003; Caprio et al. 2008; Liu et al. 2009), suggesting Aldo is the relevant EC-MR ligand, though some studies show low/variable 11βHSD2 expression (Gong et al. 2008). Studies in mice with EC-specific MR deletion reveal that EC-MR contributes to cardiovascular pathology developing in the setting of risk factors such as obesity, diabetes, and hyperlipidemia (Davel et al. 2017).

Functional MR is also expressed in human vascular SMCs (Jaffe and Mendelsohn 2005), where it contributes to vasoconstriction and blood pressure regulation (McCurley et al. 2012; DuPont et al. 2016; Galmiche et al. 2014; Amador et al. 2016) and to vascular remodeling in response to injury, aging, and hypertension (Pruthi et al. 2014; Galmiche et al. 2014; Kim et al. 2018) in vivo in males. In vitro, SMC-MR may contribute to SMC calcification (Jaffe et al. 2007) and cytokine production (McGraw et al. 2013), although it was recently shown not to contribute to atherosclerosis pathogenesis in male mice (Moss et al. 2018). MR also contributes to inflammatory phenotypes in leukocytes such as T cells, neutrophils, and monocytes (Bene et al. 2014). In vitro, macrophage MR contributes to ROS and inflammatory cytokine production and promotes pro-inflammatory "M1-like" polarization (Usher et al. 2010; Bene et al. 2014) and plaque development in atherosclerosis (Shen et al. 2017). Recent in vivo studies implicate T cell MR in hypertension pathogenesis (Sun et al. 2017) and pressure overload-induced cardiac dysfunction (Li et al. 2017a).

### Endothelial Cell Mineralocorticoid Receptors in Cardiovascular Disease: Is There Effect Modification by Sex?

Substantial recent exploration reveals a role for EC-specific MR in endothelial dysfunction, hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure. The vast majority of preclinical investigations into EC-MR function have been conducted only in male animals. Rare publications that directly compare males and females reveal striking sex differences in the role of this receptor in the vascular endothelium.

This review covers the role of the MR in mediating sex differences in 1) endothelial dysfunction, 2) hypertension, 3) atherosclerosis, and 4) heart failure, with a focus on the MR in the vascular endothelium — organized into clinical literature, preclinical literature, and molecular mechanisms.

## II. CLINICAL DATA: CONTRIBUTION OF THE MR TO CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE IN MEN AND WOMEN

### Endothelial Dysfunction

**Epidemiology.** Endothelial dysfunction is marked by impaired endothelium-dependent vasodilation, reduced NO biosynthesis, and increased vascular inflammation, and is the earliest measurable defect in vascular disease pathogenesis. A sub-analysis of Framingham Heart Study offspring found female sex significantly correlated with defects in endothelial-dependent dilation, while male sex did not (Hamburg et al. 2011).

**The MR May Contribute to Endothelial Dysfunction in the Setting of Cardiovascular Risk Factors.** Under baseline conditions without cardiovascular risk factors, the MR does not play a substantial role in vascular dysfunction (reviewed in Biwer et al. 2019). Chronic MR antagonism had no beneficial effect on endothelial function in younger (40s) obese subjects without diabetes/cardiac risk factors (Garg et al. 2014), in older (60s) healthy adults given eplerenone (Hwang et al. 2016), or in 8 older adults with metabolic syndrome (Hwang et al. 2015). However, MR inhibition improved endothelial function when multiple/severe risk factors were present: in older adults with obesity and/or impaired glucose tolerance (Hwang et al. 2013b); improved NO bioactivity and brachial artery endothelial function in heart failure (Farquharson and Struthers 2000; Macdonald et al. 2004); improved coronary flow reserve in type 2 diabetics (Garg et al. 2015); improved brachial artery function in hypertension (Fujimura et al. 2012); and improved resistance vessel function in hypertensive African Americans regardless of gender (Mohandas et al. 2015).

A role for the MR in endothelial dysfunction is seen even prior to menopause in women with enhanced risk: young women with polycystic ovarian syndrome have elevated Aldo vs weight-matched controls (Cascella et al. 2006), and spironolactone improved endothelial function in this population (Studen et al. 2011). Note spironolactone also inhibits the androgen receptor (Yang and Young 2016). In rheumatoid arthritis (a predominantly-female cohort), spironolactone improved endothelial function and reduced inflammatory indicators (Syngle et al. 2009).

### Hypertension

**Epidemiology.** High blood pressure affects 30% of American adults (Fryar et al. 2017). MR inhibitors are effective antihypertensives (Pitt et al. 2003a; Williams et al. 2004), but nearly half of hypertensive patients are inadequately controlled. Therapy-resistant hypertension is more likely to be MR-dependent (Yugar-Toledo et al. 2017; Dudenbostel and Calhoun 2017), and spironolactone or eplerenone effectively reduce blood pressure in resistant hypertension (Glicklich and Frishman 2015; Fernet et al. 2018; Rossignol et al. 2018). Prevalence in premenopausal women (~28%) is lower than age-matched men, but after menopause this reverses, with women ≥60 experiencing higher rates and increased therapy-resistant hypertension (Fryar et al. 2017).

**The MR Contributes to Hypertension in Women.** In >1,500 individuals from the HyperPATH consortium, women had a significantly greater rise in blood pressure than men in response to Aldo-stimulating stimuli including salt restriction (Jurgens and Graudal 2004) and angiotensin-II infusion (Shukri et al. 2018). In obese chronic kidney disease patients, females experienced a greater blood pressure decrease with MR antagonism than males (Khosla et al. 2009). An ERβ gene variant associated with salt sensitivity specifically in premenopausal women (Manosroi et al. 2017).

### Atherosclerosis

**Epidemiology.** Downstream consequences of atherosclerosis (MI, ischemic stroke) account for a majority of deaths worldwide (Barquera et al. 2015). Premenopausal women experience significantly fewer ischemic events than age-matched men (Benjamin et al. 2018). Carotid intima-media thickness is only slightly higher in young men than women, while coronary calcium score is substantially higher in men (Benjamin et al. 2018).

**MR Activation Contributes to Atherosclerosis in Men and Women.** Elevated plasma Aldo significantly correlated with plaque progression while sex and age did not (de Rita et al. 2012). In end-stage renal disease patients, spironolactone prevented intima-media thickness increases in both sexes (Vukusich et al. 2010). Eplerenone reduced intima-media thickness in 12 primary aldosteronism patients, 10 of whom were women (Matsuda et al. 2016). Aldo levels correlated with serum inflammatory factors regardless of gender (Tomaschitz et al. 2011). Multiple studies correlate MR activation with increased ischemic event risk (Milliez et al. 2005; Ivanes et al. 2012).

### Heart Failure

**Epidemiology.** HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction) and HFpEF (preserved ejection fraction) each account for about half the heart failure burden (Dunlay et al. 2017). Men are somewhat more likely to develop HFrEF (higher rates of hypertension and MI). HFpEF is more common in women and is the most common heart failure type in the population over 65 (Upadhya et al. 2017b); risk factors include advanced age and obesity (Owan et al. 2006; Flegal et al. 2016; Tsujimoto and Kajio 2017). Women with HFpEF generally have improved survival vs men, but this is lost with diabetes (Martinez-Selles et al. 2012).

**Clear Benefits of MR Antagonism in HFrEF.** MR inhibition prevents mortality and improves outcomes in HFrEF (Pitt et al. 1999; Pitt et al. 2003b; Zannad et al. 2011). A Framingham sub-analysis showed serum Aldo correlates with cardiac remodeling in women but not men (Vasan et al. 2004). The landmark RALES, EPHESUS, and EMPHASIS-HF trials were heavily weighted towards male participants, with women making up only 27% of subjects. A recent meta-analysis enabled sub-analysis by sex: the male sub-group retained the significant mortality benefit of MR antagonism, while the female sub-group tended towards a decline in sudden cardiac death that was not statistically significant (Rossello et al. 2019). Thus it is unclear whether MR antagonism is beneficial in female HFrEF patients as definitively shown for men.

**MR Antagonism May Specifically Benefit Women with HFpEF.** In 11 women with HFpEF given spironolactone (open-label), peak exercise capacity improved and heart failure score was reduced (Daniel et al. 2009). Meta-analyses in mostly-female cohorts largely found spironolactone improves diastolic function (Pandey et al. 2015; Fukuta et al. 2018), with variable effects on exercise tolerance. The Aldo-DHF trial (>400 patients) demonstrated improved LV parameters with spironolactone (Edelmann et al. 2013). The TOPCAT trial (>3,000 patients, 6 countries) found MR antagonism reduced heart failure hospitalization but did not significantly affect mortality (Pitt et al. 2014); subjects qualifying by natriuretic peptide levels (mostly the Americas) had a significant mortality benefit, while those qualifying by clinical criteria (mostly Russia/Georgia) did not (Pfeffer et al. 2015; Bristow et al. 2016). Another sub-analysis (1,767 patients, equally men/women) showed women with HFpEF had significant reduction in cardiovascular and all-cause mortality with spironolactone, while men did not (Merrill et al. 2019).

### Summary of the Clinical Data

Observational studies and clinical trials support that the MR contributes to endothelial dysfunction, hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure. Differences between men and women appear, e.g. female predominance of salt-sensitivity of blood pressure and sex differences in MR therapy efficacy in heart failure. Many trials use spironolactone (with off-target progesterone/androgen receptor effects) while others use the less potent but more selective eplerenone.

## III. PRECLINICAL DATA: SEX-SPECIFIC ROLES FOR THE ENDOTHELIAL MINERALOCORTICOID RECEPTOR

### A Sex-Specific Role for EC-MR in Endothelial Dysfunction

**MR Inhibition Improves Endothelial Function, Particularly in Obesity.** MR antagonist treatment improved aortic endothelial function and peak relaxations and reduced ROS in male hyperlipidemic rabbits (Rajagopalan et al. 2002) and rats with chronic kidney disease (Gonzalez-Blazquez et al. 2018). Obesity may represent enhanced MR activation, as higher BMI correlates with higher Aldo on a high-salt diet (Bentley-Lewis et al. 2007), likely via adipocyte-derived factors increasing Aldo release from fat (Briones et al. 2012) or adrenal gland (Huby et al. 2016). The adipokine leptin (higher in obese females than males; Deng and Scherer 2010) increases adrenal Aldo secretion and induces endothelial dysfunction in females in an MR-dependent manner (Huby et al. 2016; Faulkner and Belin de Chantemele 2019).

**EC-MR Contributes to Endothelial Dysfunction, with Sex- and Vascular Bed-Dependent Effects.** Global MR inhibition (eplerenone) or EC-specific MR deletion improved aortic ring dilation in obese male mice and lean Aldo-infused mice (Schafer et al. 2013). EC-MR deletion improved resistance vessel function in male mice with angiotensin-II hypertension (Mueller et al. 2015). In female mice, EC-MR deletion prevented Western diet-induced aortic endothelial dysfunction (Jia et al. 2016).

Davel et al. (2018b) was the first study to directly compare EC-MR's role in endothelial function between males and females: obese male mice compensated for endothelial dysfunction with no role for EC-MR in mesenteric artery dysfunction; by contrast, diet-induced obesity caused endothelial dysfunction in female mice, and EC-MR deletion restored endothelial-dependent microvessel relaxation by increasing NO bioavailability. There was no role for EC-MR in healthy male or female mice — the EC-MR-dependent dysfunction was observed in females only with added obesity/hyperlipidemia. The role of EC-MR in endothelial function may depend on sex, vascular bed, and clinical context.

### A Nuanced Role for EC-MR in Hypertension

**The MR May Regulate Blood Pressure in Females with Cardiometabolic Risk Factors, As Shown for Males.** MR inhibition with spironolactone substantially reduced blood pressure in female agouti yellow obese mice (Huby et al. 2016). In gonadectomized rats, high-salt diet increased blood pressure in both sexes, but spironolactone reduced blood pressure only in males (Michaelis et al. 2012).

**EC-MR May Not Directly Control Blood Pressure but Modulates the Response to Hypertension.** Genetic deletion of EC-MR in male mice does not affect blood pressure at baseline (Salvador et al. 2017; Mueller et al. 2015) or in experimentally-induced hypertension (Rickard et al. 2014; Dinh et al. 2016; Lother et al. 2016; Laursen et al. 2018). Male mice overexpressing EC-MR have higher systolic blood pressure and exaggerated vasoconstrictor responses (Nguyen Dinh Cat et al. 2010). In females, EC-MR deletion showed no effect on blood pressure under any dietary condition (Davel et al. 2018b; Jia et al. 2015b). Although EC-MR may not regulate blood pressure per se, it may be critical for pathologic arterial and myocardial remodeling: EC-MR deletion completely prevented cerebral arterial remodeling in male angiotensin-II hypertension (Diaz-Otero et al. 2017; 2018) and prevented pathologic cardiac remodeling without modulating blood pressure (Rickard et al. 2014; Lother et al. 2016).

### EC-MR Differentially Contributes to Atherosclerosis in Males and Females

**The MR Promotes Atherosclerosis in Male Animals.** In apolipoprotein-E-knockout mice, Aldo + high fat diet increases plaque size/inflammation in males within 4 weeks (McGraw et al. 2013; Marzolla et al. 2017). Deletion of 11βHSD2 (constitutive MR activation by corticosterone) accelerates plaque formation in male ApoE-KO mice (Deuchar et al. 2011). MR inhibition (eplerenone/spironolactone) decreases plaque size/inflammation in male mice (Raz-Pasteur et al. 2014, 2012; Keidar et al. 2003; Suzuki et al. 2006; Kratz et al. 2016; Moss et al. 2019), rabbits (Rajagopalan et al. 2002), and pigs (Li et al. 2017b).

**EC-MR Contributes to Atherosclerotic Plaque Inflammation, with Sex-Specific Effects.** ICAM-1 was necessary for Aldo to increase plaque formation/inflammation in male ApoE-KO mice (Marzolla et al. 2017). 11βHSD2 ablation increased endothelial VCAM-1 in male mice (Deuchar et al. 2011). In a study directly comparing sexes (Moss et al. 2019): EC-MR deletion in males significantly reduced atherosclerotic plaque inflammation and leukocyte rolling/adhesion in vivo. Gonad-intact female littermates exhibited less plaque inflammation and fewer leukocyte-endothelial interactions even with intact MR, and EC-MR deletion provided no additional protection in females. These data suggest mechanisms for female protection from atherosclerotic plaque rupture.

### EC-MR Contributes to Heart Failure with Both Reduced and Preserved Ejection Fraction

**MR Inhibitors Improve Function and Survival in Animals with Heart Failure.** Eplerenone added to standard therapy (ACE inhibitor + thiazide + β-blocker) prevented LV hypertrophy in male spontaneously hypertensive heart failure rats (Munoz-Pacheco et al. 2013). MR inhibition + ACE blockade attenuated cardiac contraction defects and fibrosis in male rats (Fraccarollo et al. 2003) and mice (Wang et al. 2004) after MI. In the transverse aortic constriction model, MR inhibition by inducible whole-body knockdown (Montes-Cobos et al. 2015) or by Aldo synthase enzyme inhibition (Furuzono et al. 2017) reduced mortality and improved cardiac function in male mice, even without additional therapies. In the only side-by-side sex comparison in experimental heart failure, after MI female rats benefited more from eplerenone than males (improved ejection fraction, infarct size, cardiac fibrosis, contraction anomalies) (Kanashiro-Takeuchi et al. 2009).

**In Animal Models, EC-MR Contributes to HFrEF in Males and HFpEF in Females.** EC-MR deletion reduced ventricle weight and prevented cardiac fibrosis increase in male hypertensive mice (Rickard et al. 2014; Lother et al. 2016) and improved ejection fraction in male TAC mice (Salvador et al. 2017), independent of inflammation (Salvador et al. 2016). For HFpEF, the Sowers group uses female mice fed a Western diet (high fat, high sucrose): only females, not males, develop diastolic dysfunction (Manrique et al. 2013). MR antagonism improved diastolic function and reduced cardiac fibrosis/inflammation (Bostick et al. 2015), and EC-MR deletion in female mice recapitulated most of these benefits (Jia et al. 2015b).

## IV. MOLECULAR MECHANISMS FOR SEX-SPECIFIC ROLES OF ENDOTHELIAL MR

Potential mechanisms: 1) inflammation; 2) vascular stiffness; 3) oxidative stress; and 4) crosstalk between the MR and sex hormone signaling.

### 1. Inflammation

**EC-MR Regulates Inflammatory Endothelial Adhesion Molecules.** The first description of MR gene-regulatory function in ECs was in human coronary artery ECs, where EC-MR transcriptionally regulated ICAM-1 (Caprio et al. 2008). ICAM-1 is necessary for Aldo to enhance atherosclerosis in male mice (Marzolla et al. 2017); MR inhibition decreased renal ICAM-1 (Kobayashi et al. 2005). In vitro, estrogen via ERα inhibits MR transcription of ICAM-1 (Barrett Mueller et al. 2014). Model: EC-MR regulates ICAM-1 in males to promote inflammation, while in females ERα blocks this. The MR also regulates VCAM-1 (Deuchar et al. 2011; Kobayashi et al. 2005; negatively regulated by estrogen — Gourdy et al. 2003; EC-MR deletion prevented cardiac VCAM-1 upregulation — Lother et al. 2016).

**EC-MR Sex-Dependently Regulates the Selectins.** P-selectin mediates leukocyte capture/fast rolling; E-selectin mediates slow-rolling preceding firm adhesion (Sundd et al. 2011). EC-MR regulates E-selectin in vivo in males: TNFα-induced mesenteric venous E-selectin expression was lower in females than males and not further affected by EC-MR deletion, correlating with sex-dependent effects on leukocyte slow rolling and aortic plaque inflammation (Moss et al. 2019). E-selectin is negatively regulated by estrogen (Tyree et al. 2002). P-selectin may also be EC-MR-regulated but data is less certain; leukocyte fast rolling tended to be reduced by EC-MR deletion in both sexes but not significantly (Moss et al. 2019).

**A Role for EC-MR in Endothelial Permeability.** Aldo/MR activation disrupted tight junction protein membrane localization in human ECs, causing monolayer permeability (Kirsch et al. 2013). In female rats, eplerenone blocked tight junction protein degradation, preventing cerebral aneurysm formation (Tada et al. 2010). This may be via the RhoA signaling pathway; Aldo activates RhoA leading to F-actin stress fiber formation and junction disruption (Kirsch et al. 2013; Lavall et al. 2014).

**EC-MR-Mediated Inflammation May Contribute to Cardiovascular Fibrosis.** EC-MR deletion did not alter cardiac fibrosis in male TAC mice (Salvador et al. 2017), but in females attenuated cardiac (Jia et al. 2015b) and aortic (Jia et al. 2016) fibrosis in Western diet models, and prevented renal artery dysfunction/fibrosis (Aroor et al. 2019). Limited data suggest EC-MR may contribute to fibrosis specifically in females via inflammation, but different models/sexes preclude firm conclusions.

### 2. Vascular Stiffness

Vascular stiffening occurs with aging and chronic cardiometabolic risk factors, preceding hypertension and atherosclerosis (Huveneers et al. 2015). MR blockade reduced aortic stiffness in dilated cardiomyopathy patients (Vizzardi et al. 2015) and attenuated aortic stiffening in Western diet-fed female mice (DeMarco et al. 2015). SMC-MR deletion attenuated aortic stiffness in aging male mice (Kim et al. 2018). Critically, EC-MR contributes to arterial stiffness via endothelial ion channels: in female mice, Aldo induced aortic stiffness via endothelial epithelial sodium channel (EnNaC) expression — a classic renal MR gene target (Jia et al. 2018b; Kusche-Vihrog et al. 2010) — and EC-MR deletion attenuated aortic stiffness via EnNaC regulation (Jia et al. 2016). EnNaC activity also promotes endothelial permeability and inflammation (Jia et al. 2018a). In breast cancer-derived ECs, Aldo upregulated the sodium/hydrogen exchanger via both MR and rapid estrogen signaling (Rigiracciolo et al. 2016).

### 3. Oxidative Stress

In male mice, EC-MR deletion prevented Aldo-induced superoxide formation in cerebral arteries (Dinh et al. 2016). In Western diet females, EC-MR deletion increased eNOS activation and reduced nitrogen free radicals in the aorta (Jia et al. 2016). Thus EC-MR contributes to vascular oxidative stress in both sexes, though mechanisms may differ: hyperlipidemic male mice developed endothelial dysfunction not ameliorated by EC-MR deletion, while in female littermates EC-MR deletion produced compensatory increases in NO production and NO-mediated dilation (Davel et al. 2018b). This enhanced role in females may relate to higher Aldo and/or low estrogen levels.

### 4. Crosstalk Between the MR and Sex Hormones

**Sex-Dependent Regulation of Aldo Production.** Women tend to have higher circulating Aldo than men at baseline (Sequeira et al. 1986) and in pathogenic states (Szymanski et al. 2011; Shukri et al. 2018; Bentley-Lewis et al. 2007), recapitulated in rodents. Female rat zona glomerulosa (ZG) cells produced more Aldo at baseline than male cells (Huang et al. 2018), and Aldo synthase expression increased in female mice with leptin sensitization or obesity (Huby et al. 2016). Female rats show higher Aldo clearance rate than males (Morris et al. 1975). Progesterone: women in the luteal phase (highest progesterone) have higher Aldo than the follicular phase (Szmuilowicz et al. 2006); progesterone increased Aldo production in ZG cells but inhibited the Aldo synthase enzyme in transfected cells (Vecchiola et al. 2013). Estrogen likely does NOT influence Aldo production (Szmuilowicz et al. 2006; Yanes and Romero 2009; Vecchiola et al. 2013), though estrogen increased Aldo only when ERβ was inhibited (Caroccia et al. 2014). AR signaling during prenatal development promotes Aldo production in male offspring (Martinez-Arguelles et al. 2011), while in adult males it inhibits Aldo production (Kau et al. 1999; Hofmann et al. 2012; Carsia et al. 2018).

**Interactions Between the MR and Estrogen Receptors.** In human ECs, ERα activation triggered formation of an ERα-MR complex that inhibited MR transcriptional function (required nuclear translocation, not DNA-binding domain nor rapid signaling). Aldo-induced ICAM-1 expression and leukocyte adhesion were blocked by co-administered estrogen (Barrett Mueller et al. 2014). EC-MR regulated ICAM-1 and E-selectin only in males in vivo or absent estrogen in vitro (Moss et al. 2019). In vascular SMCs, both ERα and ERβ attenuated Aldo-induced oxidative stress (Muehlfelder et al. 2012). Gene expression profiling suggests the MR activates vascular oxidative stress/inflammation genes while estrogen inhibits similar pathways (Schnoes et al. 2008; O'Lone et al. 2007; Newfell et al. 2011).

Non-genomic interactions via the scaffolding protein striatin: striatin recruits ERα to the caveolar membrane facilitating estradiol activation and eNOS phosphorylation (Lu et al. 2004); striatin is upregulated by the MR (Pojoga et al. 2012) and facilitates non-genomic ERK1/2 phosphorylation and ROS induction upon MR activation (Coutinho et al. 2014; Grossmann et al. 2005).

G-protein coupled ER (GPER): implicated as a potential mediator of nongenomic Aldo signaling, though whether via direct Aldo binding or downstream of MR is controversial. GPER was first suggested as an Aldo-binding receptor by Gros et al. (2011); Aldo-induced ERK1/2 phosphorylation was blocked by GPER antagonist G15 or GPER knockdown (Gros et al. 2013). However, MR antagonists eplerenone and spironolactone also blocked these rapid effects, and Cheng et al. (2014) suggested Aldo may not directly bind GPER. GPER and MR colocalize in the presence of Aldo (Rigiracciolo et al. 2016).

**Interactions Between the MR and the Progesterone Receptor.** Progesterone can bind and inhibit the wild-type MR (Rupprecht et al. 1993; Mooij et al. 2015). A ligand-binding domain point mutation instead leads to MR activation by progesterone, causing early-onset hypertension and severe pregnancy-associated hypertension (Geller et al. 2000). PR inhibits MR transcriptional activity (McDonnell et al. 1994), while the MR may activate the PR (Leo et al. 2004).

**Interactions Between the MR and the Androgen Receptor.** Prostate cancer cell lines express 11βHSD2 (Page et al. 1994). Aldo sensitized prostate cancer cells to the AR inhibitor enzalutamide, and MR knockdown increased AR expression (Shiota et al. 2018). Testosterone/dihydrotestosterone bind and inhibit the MR (Takeda et al. 2007). The AR coactivator XRCC6 binds the MR, inhibiting target gene transcription in H9c2 cardiac myocytes (Yang et al. 2014).

### Summary of the Molecular Mechanistic Data

EC-MR regulates inflammatory adhesion molecule expression and endothelial permeability (promoting tissue inflammation); regulates EnNaC and other ion channels (promoting vascular stiffness); and promotes oxidative stress while inhibiting NO availability (impairing endothelial function). Where studied, these roles are often sex-specific, especially in models of obesity and hyperlipidemia. In general, sex hormones tend to INHIBIT the harmful effects of the MR in the cardiovascular system — a paradigm that could explain increased cardiovascular risk in postmenopausal women and in men with low testosterone. No evidence suggests sex hormones can directly activate the wild-type MR.

## V. CONCLUSIONS AND PERSPECTIVES

In humans, the MR is involved in endothelial dysfunction, hypertension, atherosclerosis, and heart failure, and MR inhibition is beneficial in each. In male animal models EC-MR contributes to endothelial dysfunction in response to risk factors, tissue inflammation, and adverse cardiac remodeling in heart failure/hypertension, without contributing to blood pressure regulation itself. In women the MR's role becomes evident after menopause (low MR-inhibiting sex hormones, increased Aldo in obese females), but a role can appear even premenopausally if additional risk factors are present. EC-MR's role in females supports involvement in endothelial dysfunction and cardiac diastolic dysfunction but not inflammation or hypertension.

An important limitation is the paucity of women in clinical trials and, until recently, near-exclusive use of male animals preclinically. With the NIH requirement to address biological sex as a variable (McCullough et al. 2014), sex-difference data is emerging. Endothelial-specific MR may mediate sex differences in endothelial dysfunction, atherosclerosis, and heart failure, while it may not contribute to sex differences in hypertension incidence (instead mediating adverse consequences of elevated blood pressure). Understanding these mechanisms could nominate additional therapeutic targets downstream of EC-MR for tailored cardiovascular disease treatment in both sexes.
