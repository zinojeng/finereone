## Abstract

**Aims:** In a phase 2 trial, the efficacy and safety of the highly selective aldosterone synthase inhibitor vicadrostat, alone or with empagliflozin, were investigated in people with chronic kidney disease (CKD) with or without type 2 diabetes (T2D).

**Materials and Methods:** Adults (n = 586) with CKD (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] 30 to < 90 mL/min/1.73 m², urine albumin-creatinine ratio [UACR] 200 to < 5000 mg/g) receiving a maximally tolerated dose of renin-angiotensin system inhibitor were randomised to receive vicadrostat (3, 10 or 20 mg) or matching placebo for 14 weeks, with or without background empagliflozin. The primary outcome, effect on albuminuria at 14 weeks, as well as systolic blood pressure (SBP) and eGFR, was assessed by T2D and obesity subgroups (body mass index [BMI]: ≥ 30 vs. < 30 kg/m²) at baseline.

**Results:** Consistent with overall study results, the largest UACR reductions were observed in 10 and 20 mg dose groups across both subgroup analyses (adjusted mean reduction 30%–51% vs. 37%–46% in the overall study). UACR reductions were consistent in participants with and without T2D (P_INTERACTION = 0.53 and 0.40 with/without empagliflozin, respectively) and irrespective of BMI category at baseline (P_INTERACTION = 0.35 and 0.44 with/without empagliflozin, respectively). Effects of vicadrostat treatment on reductions in eGFR and SBP were also consistent across T2D and BMI subgroups.

**Conclusions:** Effects of vicadrostat with or without empagliflozin on UACR, eGFR, and SBP were consistent irrespective of T2D and obesity status.

## 1 | Introduction

Despite the availability of evidence-based interventions such as angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin II receptor blockers, glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, and sodium-glucose co-transporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, many people with chronic kidney disease (CKD) remain at risk of losing kidney function and kidney failure, major adverse cardiovascular events, and death. Renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors and non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (nsMRAs) improve CKD outcomes but do not fully block the harmful effects of aldosterone and may be associated with aldosterone increase. Thus, direct inhibition of aldosterone synthase may enhance therapeutic benefits by reducing aldosterone production.

Understanding of the interconnected relationships between cardiovascular, kidney, and metabolic conditions has evolved into the concept of 'cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic' (CKM) syndrome to codify these pathophysiologically interrelated disorders. The combined presence of CKM conditions amplifies morbidity and mortality risks compared with the individual conditions. For example, type 2 diabetes (T2D) is itself a leading cause of CKD through mechanisms including glomerular hypertension, inflammation, and fibrosis. In turn, CKD may worsen glycaemic control and increase cardiovascular risk through intertwining mechanisms that form feedforward loops. Augmented aldosterone bioactivity has emerged as a central component to CKM pathogenesis owing to a wide range of signalling pathways that mediate kidney and cardiovascular disease. An excess of aldosterone associated with obesity or T2D makes it a promising therapeutic target for metabolic-related CKM risks.

Vicadrostat is a potent and highly selective aldosterone synthase inhibitor (ASi) in development for the treatment of CKD and heart failure and for the reduction of cardiovascular risk, given with empagliflozin. The efficacy and safety of vicadrostat, alone or with empagliflozin, were examined in a phase 2 multinational, randomised, dose-finding trial investigating people with CKD with or without T2D. The primary findings indicated complementary effects of vicadrostat and empagliflozin on urine albumin-creatinine ratio (UACR) reduction. In the 10 mg dose groups, placebo-corrected UACR change from baseline was 37%–40% after 14 weeks, irrespective of empagliflozin or placebo background treatment. Participants receiving vicadrostat and empagliflozin together showed response rates of up to 70%, defined as a UACR reduction > 30%, a clinically relevant threshold that predicts long-term preservation of kidney function. In the context of the associations between adiposity, T2D, and increased aldosterone bioactivity, the aims of the present analyses were to assess the effects of vicadrostat on albuminuria by metabolic subgroups of T2D and obesity, with and without empagliflozin in the background.

## 2 | Materials and Methods

### 2.1 | Trial Overview

The trial design and methodology have been previously published (Figure S1). Adults with CKD (eGFR 30 to < 90 mL/min/1.73 m², UACR 200 to < 5000 mg/g) receiving a maximally tolerated dose of a RAS inhibitor were randomised (R1) 1:1 to receive background empagliflozin 10 mg (n = 356) or matching placebo (PBO_EMPA, n = 358) during an 8-week run-in. Both arms were then re-randomised (R2) 1:1:1:1 to receive vicadrostat (3, 10 or 20 mg) or matching placebo (PBO_VICA) for 14 weeks, in addition to their assigned empagliflozin (n = 76, n = 74, n = 74 and n = 74, in the vicadrostat 3, 10, 20 mg and PBO_VICA groups, respectively) or PBO_EMPA (n = 71, n = 72, n = 72 and n = 73, respectively).

The primary outcome was the change from R2 baseline in UACR at week 14. During treatment, urine was collected before the first medication intake on two consecutive days at three timepoints (total six first morning voids [FMV]) between week −2 and week 0 and between week 12 and week 14 (total six FMVs). Additional outcomes included UACR response (≥ 30% reduction from R2 baseline in UACR at week 14), changes in systolic blood pressure (SBP), and eGFR from R2 baseline to week 14. eGFR was calculated using serum creatinine measured at each required visit. Serum creatinine was analysed by the central laboratory to ensure consistency. The study used the CKD-EPI (Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration) formula. The CKD-EPI formula used race-specific correction factors, as was standard at the time of the trial. SBP was measured with the patient seated after 5 min of rest, before any blood sampling. SBP could be measured electronically or manually with results recorded to the nearest 1 mmHg. Typically, a single seated BP reading was recorded unless the site repeated it for clinical reasons.

Adverse events (AE) and safety data based on the treated set were also assessed. In the present study, subgroup analyses of the effects of vicadrostat, given alone or with background empagliflozin, were performed by T2D status and obesity defined by body mass index (BMI) category (≥ 30 vs. < 30 kg/m²) at baseline.

### 2.2 | Statistical Analyses

The primary efficacy analysis was based on the full analysis set (all participants randomly assigned at R2 baseline with at least one baseline measurement of UACR at week −2/−1 or 0 and at least one post-baseline measurement). Primary and subgroup analyses of the primary outcome were performed for mixed models for repeated measures (MMRM) estimates of change from R2 baseline in log-transformed UACR at week 14 for vicadrostat alone and with empagliflozin. Consistent with the primary analysis of the parent trial, log-transformed FMV UACR was modelled with an MMRM that included treatment and planned visit as fixed effects; baseline log(UACR) and randomisation stratum as covariates; baseline by visit and treatment by visit interactions; and participant as a random effect with an unstructured covariance matrix. Percent change from baseline of UACR FMV was calculated based on back-transformation of adjusted mean change from baseline in log-transformed UACR from MMRM using the following formula: exp(adjusted mean of change from baseline log[UACR]) − 1, in which 'exp(adjusted mean of change from baseline log[UACR])' corresponds to the adjusted geometric mean of change from baseline in log-transformed UACR.

Subgroup analyses were conducted by extending the primary MMRM model to include the treatment-by-subgroup interaction term to test for heterogeneity of treatment effect across subgroups. The nominal interaction p-values were derived from this model. Descriptive statistics were used for the proportional UACR responses, and an adjusted multiple imputation method was used for missing UACR measurements at week 14. Estimated change from R2 baseline in SBP and eGFR at week 14 was obtained using the same MMRM framework as described for UACR, but without log-transformation. Adjusted mean changes and 95% confidence intervals reported in figures and tables represent model-based adjusted mean differences on the natural measurement scale. Safety analyses were descriptive and included all treated patients.

## 3 | Results

### 3.1 | Study Population

Of 714 participants randomised at R1, 586 were randomised at R2 with three people not treated (treated set, N = 583); 542 participants completed the study. UACR data were not collected for 83 participants (full analysis set, N = 503).

The overall mean age of participants was 63.8 years (standard deviation, 11.3) and 33% (196/586) were female. Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics of the overall population were mostly similar across vicadrostat dose groups; selected parameters by subgroups of T2D or BMI are shown in Table 1. A total of 70.6% (414/586) of the trial participants had a prior diagnosis of T2D; participants with T2D had higher mean BMI, SBP, and eGFR, as well as higher median UACR than those without T2D. When participants were stratified by BMI (≥ 30 or < 30 kg/m²), 42.7% (249/583) of trial participants had a BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² at baseline. Median (interquartile range) BMI was 34.0 (31.9, 36.9) kg/m² versus 26.4 (24.5, 28.0) kg/m² in the ≥ 30 kg/m² and < 30 kg/m² BMI subgroups, respectively; a higher proportion of participants in the BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² group were male, had a prior diagnosis of T2D, and had higher median serum aldosterone and UACR.

### 3.2 | Change in UACR to Week 14

The greatest reductions in UACR occurred in the vicadrostat 10 and 20 mg dose groups irrespective of diabetes status and were numerically similar among participants who received vicadrostat/empagliflozin (VicaEmpa) and those who received vicadrostat alone (P_INTERACTION = 0.53 and 0.40, with and without empagliflozin, respectively) (Figure 1). The greatest proportions of participants who achieved a UACR response (defined as a UACR reduction of ≥ 30% from baseline to end of treatment at week 14) were also observed in the vicadrostat 10 and 20 mg dose groups irrespective of diabetes status (Figure 2). A numerically greater proportion of participants who received VicaEmpa met the threshold for UACR response compared with those who received vicadrostat alone.

Among participants with T2D, UACR response was achieved in 74.3% and 58.1% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of participants who received VicaEmpa, and in 46.5% and 50.0% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of those who received vicadrostat. UACR responder rates in participants without T2D who received VicaEmpa were 64.0% and 57.9% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively), compared with 61.1% and 52.4% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) in the vicadrostat arm (Figure 2).

When stratified by BMI category, vicadrostat consistently reduced UACR in participants with BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² and < 30 kg/m² (P_INTERACTION = 0.35 and 0.44 with and without empagliflozin, respectively). The largest reductions were seen in the 10 and 20 mg dose groups, though UACR reductions were not dose-dependent for either BMI subgroup (Figure 1). In the BMI < 30 kg/m² subgroup, 58.3% and 45.9% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of participants receiving VicaEmpa met the ≥ 30% reduction criteria for UACR response, compared with 54.5% and 47.2% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of those receiving vicadrostat alone (Figure 2). In contrast, differential UACR response rates were observed in the BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² subgroup, with a response seen in 87.5% and 76.0% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of participants who received VicaEmpa, whereas the same outcome was observed in 46.4% and 56.5% (10 and 20 mg groups, respectively) of participants who received vicadrostat (Figure 2).

### 3.3 | SBP Data

Vicadrostat consistently reduced SBP compared with placebo regardless of diabetes status, and related to the SBP difference between VicaEmpa and empagliflozin, or between vicadrostat and placebo (P_INTERACTION = 0.92 and 0.79, respectively). Results seemed to indicate some dose-dependent patterns in SBP reduction between the 3, 10, and 20 mg dose groups in the VicaEmpa arm (Figure 3). In BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² and < 30 kg/m² subgroups, overall reductions in SBP were observed with vicadrostat regardless of BMI category, and there were no discernable trends in SBP change by increasing dosages of vicadrostat (P_INTERACTION = 0.89 and 0.46 with and without empagliflozin, respectively) (Figure 3).

### 3.4 | eGFR Data

A summary of the changes in eGFR from baseline to week 14 is shown in Figure 4. eGFR changes observed in the vicadrostat arm were variable between the diabetes and non-diabetes subgroups (P_INTERACTION = 0.004) but without a dose-dependent trend, possibly owing to the small sample sizes that may have contributed to these inconsistencies. Conversely, effects of VicaEmpa on eGFR were consistent across these subgroups and by dose (P_INTERACTION = 0.71). Changes in eGFR were consistently observed in participants of both BMI categories (P_INTERACTION = 0.93 and 0.15 with and without empagliflozin, respectively). Although the eGFR changes observed were modest in both subgroups of BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² and < 30 kg/m², there appeared to be a dose-dependent pattern of small reduction in participants who received VicaEmpa. Meanwhile, participants with BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² who received vicadrostat alone demonstrated the greatest mean changes in eGFR of −6.6% and −6.4% in the 10 and 20 mg dose groups, respectively (Figure 4).

### 3.5 | Safety Data

Overall safety for vicadrostat with and without empagliflozin has been previously described. A summary of AEs, serious AEs, and AEs leading to discontinuation of vicadrostat are shown in Table S1. People with T2D had a higher incidence of all AEs, serious AEs, and AEs leading to discontinuation than those without T2D, and this finding was consistent across vicadrostat dose groups, with or without empagliflozin. There were no observed patterns or trends across all AE categories when patients were stratified by BMI.

## 4 | Discussion

Vicadrostat treatment, alone or with empagliflozin, demonstrated dose-dependent reductions in UACR in participants with or without T2D or obesity. Notably, across diabetes and obesity subgroups, responder (≥ 30% UACR decrease) rates were directionally similar and greater for vicadrostat 10 or 20 mg with VicaEmpa than in those treated with vicadrostat alone. Reductions in SBP and eGFR were generally consistent across the diabetes and obesity subgroups. Moreover, no unexpected safety signals were observed by either diabetes or obesity status.

The present findings are in agreement with findings described in trials with non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor agonists. By contrast, the TARGET-HTN trial with the ASi lorundrostat demonstrated larger SBP reductions in participants with BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² compared to those with BMI 25–30 kg/m². However, such differential SBP reductions were not observed in the follow-up LAUNCH-HTN trial where the efficacy of lorundrostat did not differ according to BMI category, nor were they seen with baxdrostat in the BaxHTN trial.

In the present study, the average initial eGFR decline with VicaEmpa was approximately 8%–9%. Both vicadrostat and empagliflozin treatment are associated with an initial transient eGFR decrease, known as 'eGFR dip'. Rather than a sign of structural kidney damage, this dip is haemodynamic and transient in nature and reversible following drug discontinuation. In EMPA-REG OUTCOME, participants with larger mean eGFR dips with empagliflozin (> 10%) showed the same pattern of slowing loss of kidney function over time as those with smaller or no initial dips and showed a reversible rebound towards baseline. Similarly, the EMPA-KIDNEY trial demonstrated that empagliflozin initially caused a modest acute dip in eGFR (~6%) but subsequently halved the long-term rate of kidney function decline across a broad spectrum of patients with CKD. Moreover, in trials with RAS inhibitors and in other SGLT2 inhibitor clinical trials, eGFR dipping was associated with preservation of eGFR over time. Accordingly, although the additive dipping effects of ASi and SGLT2 inhibition merit further investigation in larger, long-term trials, existing evidence suggests that together, they generally exert additive clinical benefit without safety concerns.

Among safety assessments, the incidence of AEs with vicadrostat/empagliflozin treatment was mostly similar between subgroups. However, a higher rate of AEs was observed in the subgroup with diabetes compared to those without diabetes, a finding that may be attributed to the higher burden of illness in this population.

The increasing global prevalence of CKD, particularly in populations with obesity and diabetes, represents a growing public health crisis with significant healthcare and economic consequences. Excess and dysfunctional adiposity and persistent hyperglycaemia share metabolic and haemodynamic pathways which result in deleterious pathophysiological effects on the kidney. Within the CKM framework, obesity increases risks of T2D and hypertension, both of which are risk factors for CKD, and obesity is itself an independent CKD risk factor. The kidney becomes susceptible to the convergence of metabolic-related mechanisms associated with obesity and T2D, including pro-inflammatory signals, oxidative stress, insulin resistance, impaired lipid metabolism, and vascular dysfunction. These cumulative metabolic risks bidirectionally worsen CKD as well as cardiovascular disease. Overall, the UACR responses to vicadrostat are supportive of use in patients with CKD and T2D or obesity, particularly when given with an SGLT2 inhibitor to reduce risks of losing kidney function, kidney failure, and cardiovascular events.

An ongoing phase 3 trial programme will test VicaEmpa versus standard-of-care including empagliflozin in patients with CKD (EASi-KIDNEY, NCT06531824), in patients with heart failure (EASi-HF Preserved, NCT06424288; EASi-HF Reduced, NCT06935370), and for cardiovascular risk reduction (EASi-PROTKT, NCT07064473).

These subgroup analyses have some notable limitations. This phase 2 trial was not powered for diabetes and obesity subgroups. Additionally, T2D and obesity are closely associated, as elevated BMI is a major risk factor for developing T2D, and this interdependence should be considered when interpreting these results.

In conclusion, the effects of vicadrostat on albuminuria reduction, whether given alone or with empagliflozin, were consistent overall irrespective of T2D or obesity. Therefore, vicadrostat given with empagliflozin may be beneficial for a broad range of patients with various CKM conditions.
