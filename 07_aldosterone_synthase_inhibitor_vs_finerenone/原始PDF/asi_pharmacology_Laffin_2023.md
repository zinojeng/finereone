# Aldosterone Synthase Inhibition With Lorundrostat for Uncontrolled Hypertension: The Target-HTN Randomized Clinical Trial

**JAMA. 2023;330(12):1140-1150. doi:10.1001/jama.2023.16029. Published online September 10, 2023.**
Trial registration: ClinicalTrials.gov NCT05001945

## Abstract

**Importance.** Excess aldosterone production contributes to hypertension in both classical hyperaldosteronism and obesity-associated hypertension. Therapies that reduce aldosterone synthesis may lower blood pressure.

**Objective.** To compare the safety and efficacy of lorundrostat, an aldosterone synthase inhibitor, with placebo, and characterize dose-dependent safety and efficacy to inform dose selection in future trials.

**Design, Setting, and Participants.** Randomized, placebo-controlled, dose-ranging trial among adults with uncontrolled hypertension taking 2 or more antihypertensive medications. An initial cohort of 163 participants with suppressed plasma renin (plasma renin activity [PRA] ≤1.0 ng/mL/h) and elevated plasma aldosterone (≥1.0 ng/dL) were enrolled, with subsequent enrollment of 37 participants with PRA greater than 1.0 ng/mL/h.

**Interventions.** Participants were randomized to placebo or 1 of 5 dosages of lorundrostat in the initial cohort (12.5 mg, 50 mg, or 100 mg once daily or 12.5 mg or 25 mg twice daily). In the second cohort, participants were randomized in a 1:6 ratio to placebo or lorundrostat, 100 mg once daily.

**Main Outcomes and Measures.** The primary end point was change in automated office systolic blood pressure from baseline to study week 8.

**Results.** Between July 2021 and June 2022, 200 participants were randomized, with final follow-up in September 2022. Following 8 weeks of treatment in participants with suppressed PRA, changes in office systolic blood pressure of −14.1, −13.2, −6.9, and −4.1 mm Hg were observed with 100 mg, 50 mg, and 12.5 mg once daily of lorundrostat and placebo, respectively. Observed reductions in systolic blood pressure in individuals receiving twice-daily doses of 25 mg and 12.5 mg of lorundrostat were −10.1 and −13.8 mm Hg, respectively. The least-squares mean difference between placebo and treatment in systolic blood pressure was −9.6 mm Hg (90% CI, −15.8 to −3.4 mm Hg; P = .01) for the 50-mg once-daily dose and −7.8 mm Hg (90% CI, −14.1 to −1.5 mm Hg; P = .04) for 100 mg daily. Among participants without suppressed PRA, 100 mg once daily of lorundrostat decreased systolic blood pressure by 11.4 mm Hg (SD, 2.5 mm Hg), which was similar to blood pressure reduction among participants with suppressed PRA receiving the same dose. Six participants had increases in serum potassium above 6.0 mmol/L that corrected with dose reduction or drug discontinuation. No instances of cortisol insufficiency occurred.

**Conclusions and Relevance.** Among individuals with uncontrolled hypertension, use of lorundrostat was effective at lowering blood pressure compared with placebo, which will require further confirmatory studies.

---

## Key Points
- **Question.** Among patients with uncontrolled hypertension, does the aldosterone synthase inhibitor lorundrostat safely lower blood pressure?
- **Findings.** In this randomized clinical trial that included 200 participants, lorundrostat decreased blood pressure significantly more than placebo at the 50- and 100-mg once-daily doses, and adverse events, including hyperkalemia, were uncommon.
- **Meaning.** Aldosterone synthase inhibition with lorundrostat showed potential for blood pressure lowering in patients with hypertension that was inadequately controlled despite background antihypertensive treatment.

## Introduction / Background (Pharmacology rationale)

Hypertension is the leading contributor to cardiovascular morbidity and mortality worldwide. Excess aldosterone production contributes to uncontrolled BP in patients with obesity and other associated diseases, such as obstructive sleep apnea and metabolic syndrome.

Contemporary guidance for treatment of resistant hypertension includes use of mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) following a 3-drug regimen (thiazide-type diuretic, dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker, and ACE inhibitor or ARB). However, antiandrogenic- and progestogenic-related adverse effects are common barriers to wider MRA use, particularly spironolactone. MRAs also do not block nongenomic effects of aldosterone, which may result in increased sympathetic activation, negatively affect glucose homeostasis, and stimulate vascular contractility and stiffness.

**Mechanism.** Decreasing aldosterone production via inhibition of aldosterone synthase, rather than blocking the mineralocorticoid receptor, may avoid these adverse effects. Lorundrostat is a selective inhibitor of aldosterone synthase. The enzyme aldosterone synthase is encoded by *CYP11B2*, which has greater than 93% homology to *CYP11B1* (encodes 11-β-hydroxylase, the rate-limiting step in cortisol synthesis). Given this substantial homology, non-selective inhibitors such as osilodrostat also inhibit cortisol synthesis (20% of osilodrostat trial participants demonstrated inadequate cortisol production following ACTH stimulation) and substantially increased production of 11-deoxycorticosterone (a mineralocorticoid receptor agonist). Lorundrostat appears to overcome this issue given its high selectivity for inhibition of human **CYP11B2 vs CYP11B1 (374:1)** in a preclinical cell-based activity assay (Mitsubishi-Tanabe Pharmaceuticals).

The Trial on the Safety and Efficacy of MLS-101 (Lorundrostat) in Patients With Uncontrolled Hypertension (Target-HTN) was designed to examine safety and efficacy of lorundrostat at 5 different doses, with specific aim of BP reduction among participants with obesity or suppressed renin.

## Methods (key details)

- **Design.** Multicenter, prospective, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind, dose-ranging trial at 43 sites in the United States. Protocol designed by Mineralys Therapeutics. Cytel performed statistical analysis; Cleveland Clinic Coordinating Center for Clinical Research (C5Research) independently confirmed primary end-point analysis.
- **Population.** Adults >18 years with systolic automated office BP (AOBP) ≥130 mm Hg while taking 2 or more antihypertensive medications for ≥4 weeks at maximally tolerated doses.
  - **Cohort 1:** suppressed PRA (≤1.0 ng/mL/h) and serum aldosterone ≥1.0 ng/dL.
  - **Cohort 2 (exploratory):** PRA >1.0 ng/mL/h.
- **Key exclusions.** Orthostatic hypotension, type 1 diabetes, concomitant epithelial sodium channel inhibitor or MRA, eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m², morning serum cortisol <3 µg/dL, serum sodium <135 mmol/L, or HbA1c ≥9%. Cohort 1 also excluded systolic AOBP ≥175 mm Hg, diastolic BP ≥100 mm Hg, or serum potassium >5.2 mmol/L.
- **Procedures.** 2–4 weeks prescreening, 2-week placebo run-in, then randomization and 8 weeks treatment. Cohort 1 randomized 1:1:1:1:1:1 to placebo or 1 of 5 lorundrostat doses (12.5 mg, 50 mg, 100 mg once daily; 12.5 mg or 25 mg twice daily). An interim analysis in January 2022 discontinued randomization to the 2 lowest doses (12.5 mg once daily and 12.5 mg twice daily) due to lack of consistent meaningful reduction of BP. Cohort 2 randomized 1:6 to placebo or lorundrostat 100 mg once daily.
- **Primary end point.** Change in systolic AOBP from baseline to end of week 8.
- **Secondary end points.** Changes in diastolic AOBP, 24-hour ambulatory BP, proportion achieving AOBP <130/80 mm Hg at week 7 or 8. Additional safety/pharmacodynamic endpoints: changes in PRA, serum aldosterone, cortisol, potassium, eGFR.
- **Statistics.** Mixed-model repeated-measures approach. All cohort 2 analyses exploratory; no adjustment for multiplicity (phase 2 exploratory intent). SAS version 9.4.

## Results

### Participants
From July 2021 through June 2022, 380 patients were screened (n = 307 cohort 1; n = 73 cohort 2) and 200 randomized (n = 163 cohort 1; n = 37 cohort 2). Among randomized participants: mean age 65.7 (SD, 10.2) years; 60% women; 36% Black or African American; 48% Hispanic or Latino ethnicity. At randomization, 48% had BMI >30 and 40% had type 2 diabetes. Three or more antihypertensive medications were taken by 42%.
- Cohort 1: mean baseline systolic AOBP 142.2 (SD, 12.5) mm Hg; diastolic AOBP 81.5 (SD, 9.7) mm Hg.
- Cohort 2: mean baseline systolic BP 139.1 (SD, 8.7) mm Hg; diastolic BP 79.1 (SD, 9.7) mm Hg.

### Primary End Point (Cohort 1, systolic AOBP change to week 8)
Placebo group demonstrated change of −4.1 (SE, 2.6) mm Hg. Mixed-model repeated-measures systolic AOBP changes:
- 12.5 mg once daily: −5.6 (SE, 3.2) mm Hg
- 50 mg once daily: −13.7 (SE, 2.7) mm Hg
- 100 mg once daily: −11.9 (SE, 2.8) mm Hg
- 12.5 mg twice daily: −11.3 (SE, 3.2) mm Hg
- 25 mg twice daily: −11.1 (SE, 2.7) mm Hg

Placebo-subtracted change in systolic AOBP:
- 12.5 mg once daily: −1.5 mm Hg (90% CI, −8.3 to 5.3; P = .71)
- 50 mg once daily: **−9.6 mm Hg (90% CI, −15.8 to −3.4; P = .01)**
- 100 mg once daily: **−7.8 mm Hg (90% CI, −14.1 to −1.5; P = .04)**

Twice-daily doses showed similar BP reduction to placebo-corrected once-daily.

### Cohort 2 (non-suppressed PRA, exploratory)
100 mg once daily produced systolic AOBP change of −11.4 (SD, 2.5) mm Hg vs those with suppressed renin in cohort 1. Least-squares mean systolic AOBP difference between the 100-mg once-daily cohorts was 0.74 mm Hg (90% CI, −5.4 to 6.9).

### Secondary End Points
- 50-mg once-daily dose decreased diastolic AOBP by 5.5 mm Hg vs placebo (90% CI, −9.4 to −1.5; P = .02). Other doses did not show statistically significant diastolic lowering.

### Prespecified Exploratory / Obesity Subgroups
Participants with obesity demonstrated greater BP lowering. Individuals with BMI >30 randomized to 50 mg once daily: −16.7 mm Hg (90% CI, −25.5 to −7.9) change in systolic AOBP vs placebo; with 100 mg once daily: −12.3 mm Hg (90% CI, −21.6 to −3.1). Among individuals with BMI 25–30, BP changes were 2.2 mm Hg (90% CI, −7.4 to 11.8) and −4.5 mm Hg (90% CI, −14.5 to 5.5) for 50-mg and 100-mg once-daily doses respectively. Thiazide-type diuretic users randomized to 50 or 100 mg once daily: −12.9 mm Hg (90% CI, −21.2 to −4.7) and −10.0 mm Hg (90% CI, −21.2 to −1.6).

### Pharmacodynamic Biomarker End Points
Lorundrostat reduced serum aldosterone at all doses, whereas the cohort 1 placebo group saw an increase in mean serum aldosterone of 0.14 (SD, 3.68) ng/dL.
- 12.5 mg, 50 mg, 100 mg once-daily doses reduced mean serum aldosterone by 1.05 (SD, 4.33), 2.56 (SD, 3.72), and 2.88 (SD, 4.44) ng/dL respectively.
- Twice-daily doses reduced mean serum aldosterone by 2.42 (SD, 2.78) ng/dL (12.5-mg group) and 3.39 (SD, 4.22) ng/dL (25-mg group).
- Cohort 2, 100 mg daily decreased mean serum aldosterone by 1.19 (SD, 5.38) ng/dL.
- Anticipated increases in PRA ranged 0.78 (SD, 3.44) to 4.65 (SD, 11.27) ng/mL/h in cohort 1; small increases in serum cortisol observed.
- Declines in eGFR paralleled reduction in systolic AOBP, consistent with expected lowering of intraglomerular pressures.

### Safety End Points
- No participant deaths. Three serious adverse events; only 1 deemed treatment related (worsening hyponatremia in cohort 2 100-mg once-daily participant necessitating stopping drug).
- Total of 110 participants (55%) experienced an adverse event; most classified mild.
- **No adrenocortical insufficiency** occurred. Cosyntropin stimulation testing in participants randomized to 100 mg once daily (cohort 1 and 2) was normal (stimulated cortisol >18 µg/dL in all individuals).
- Prespecified adverse events of special interest: 3 participants (2%) with hypotension.
- **Serum potassium:** mean increases similar across doses (~0.25 mmol/L in the 50-mg and 100-mg once-daily groups). Six participants (3.6%) had serum potassium >6.0 mmol/L during the trial; **no instances of hyperkalemia required intervention beyond discontinuation or dose reduction** of lorundrostat.

## Discussion (selected points)

Aldosterone synthase inhibition with lorundrostat was well tolerated and reduced AOBP at doses of 50 and 100 mg once daily. The 50-mg once-daily dose lowered office BP to a similar degree as 100-mg once daily but resulted in fewer adverse events, including fewer episodes of hyperkalemia. Lorundrostat has a relatively short half-life, and once-daily dosing resulted in a 25% lower mean change in serum potassium with 50 mg once daily compared with 25 mg twice daily — suggesting an overnight period of escape from aldosterone suppression may have safety benefits vs agents with longer half-lives.

Comparison to other agents: Osilodrostat (first aldosterone synthase inhibitor) lowered aldosterone and BP vs placebo in phase 2 but was not pursued for BP indication due to lack of specificity. Baxdrostat (selectivity ratio 100:1) demonstrated similar placebo-subtracted BP lowering in a dose-ranging phase 2 trial. Lorundrostat and baxdrostat result in similar rates of hyperkalemia and expected modest initial declines in eGFR.

### Limitations
1. Multiple dosing regimens tested; each studied dose had limited sample size.
2. 24-hour ambulatory BP monitoring not used to confirm sustained hypertension before randomization, resulting in disproportionate white-coat effect in the 50-mg once-daily group.
3. Cohort 1 selected participants with suppressed renin at baseline.
4. Two interim analyses performed — would not be appropriate in subsequent confirmatory trials.

### Conclusions
Lorundrostat at doses of 50 mg and 100 mg once daily decreased AOBP significantly more than placebo. BP reduction was particularly evident among participants with hypertension and concomitant obesity. Lorundrostat was well tolerated, with small expected increases in serum potassium and declines in eGFR suggesting a favorable safety profile, particularly with a 50-mg once-daily dose. Results support further study of lorundrostat as a treatment for uncontrolled hypertension.
