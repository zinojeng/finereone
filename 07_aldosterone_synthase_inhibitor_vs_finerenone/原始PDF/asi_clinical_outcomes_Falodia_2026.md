# Efficacy and Safety of Baxdrostat for Hypertension: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Three Phase 2/3 Randomized Controlled Trials

## Abstract

**Background:** Baxdrostat is a selective aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) inhibitor for treatment-resistant and uncontrolled hypertension, evaluated in phase 2/3 RCTs.

**Objectives:** To estimate pooled placebo-corrected reductions in seated office and ambulatory SBP, characterize the dose-response relationship, assess cortisol selectivity, and quantify hyperkalemia risk.

**Methods:** We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis of phase 2/3 randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials of baxdrostat in hypertension, searching MEDLINE/PubMed, EMBASE, ClinicalTrials.gov, and NEJM.org to March 2026. The primary outcome was placebo-corrected change in seated office SBP, analyzed using random-effects meta-analysis (REML) with metafor in R. Risk of bias and certainty were evaluated using Cochrane RoB 2.0 and GRADE, respectively.

**Results:** Three RCTs (BrigHTN, BaxHTN, Bax24; N = 1285) were included. The pooled seated SBP reduction was −9.44 mmHg (95% CI: −11.09 to −7.79; I2 = 0.0%), with dose subgroup estimates of −8.57 mmHg (1 mg) and −10.11 mmHg (2 mg) and a clear dose-response in BrigHTN (slope −4.2 mmHg/mg; R2 = 0.96). Bax24 demonstrated a 24 h ambulatory SBP reduction of −14.0 mmHg (95% CI: −17.2 to −10.8). Serum cortisol was unsuppressed at all doses, while the pooled OR for potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L was 5.14 (95% CI: 1.76 to 14.97). GRADE certainty was moderate for seated SBP, low to moderate for ambulatory outcomes, and low for hyperkalemia.

**Conclusions:** Baxdrostat produces meaningful, selective BP reductions across hypertensive populations. Hyperkalemia is manageable with structured monitoring. Certainty is limited by a few trials, a short follow-up, and safety imprecision. Long-term cardiovascular outcome data are needed before guideline incorporation.

## 1 | Introduction

Hypertension is the most common modifiable cause of cardiovascular death worldwide, yet many patients do not achieve target blood pressure despite multidrug therapy. Treatment-resistant hypertension is defined as persistently elevated blood pressure despite treatment with at least three antihypertensive agents at maximally tolerated doses, including a diuretic. This phenotype is associated with higher risks of stroke, heart failure, chronic kidney disease, and death. In these patients, excess aldosterone contributes to sodium retention, vascular remodeling, and cardiorenal injury beyond the effects of elevated blood pressure alone. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists are effective add-on therapies, but their use may be limited by hyperkalemia, renal impairment, endocrine adverse effects, and compensatory increases in aldosterone.

When aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) is directly inhibited, it suppresses aldosterone at its production site without any disruption in cortisol synthesis. Baxdrostat is an oral CYP11B2 inhibitor with approximately 100-fold selectivity for aldosterone synthase over 11β-hydroxylase, producing large, sustained falls in plasma aldosterone with minimal cortisol effect in phase 1 work. Three trials have since tested it in clinical populations. In the phase 2 BrigHTN trial, baxdrostat reduced seated systolic BP by 8–11 mmHg versus placebo at 12 weeks in resistant hypertension, without adrenocortical insufficiency or serious drug-related events. The phase 3 BaxHTN trial confirmed a further ~9–10 mmHg reduction when added to background therapy, with hyperkalemia emerging as the primary safety signal. When a phase 2 trial was performed in patients with CKD and uncontrolled hypertension, a similar ~8 mmHg BP reduction was observed, but hyperkalemia rates were elevated.

Baxdrostat has recently been evaluated in a baxdrostat-specific systematic review and meta-analysis in resistant hypertension, and several class-level syntheses have assessed aldosterone synthase inhibitors more broadly. However, the available reviews differ from the present analysis in trial composition, outcome focus, and handling of dose-specific estimates. In particular, the present review focuses on the three phase 2/3 double-blind placebo-controlled baxdrostat hypertension trials, incorporates the published Bax24 ambulatory blood pressure trial, separates clinically relevant 1 and 2 mg dose estimates, and evaluates hyperkalemia and renal function signals using absolute as well as relative measures. This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to provide an updated and methodologically transparent estimate of baxdrostat efficacy and safety in resistant and uncontrolled hypertension.

## 2 | Methods

### 2.1 | Search Strategy and Study Selection

Conducted per PRISMA guidelines. Comprehensive search in MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, Scopus, ClinicalTrials.gov, and NEJM.org for RCTs evaluating baxdrostat for hypertension, without language restrictions, from database inception to March 2026. Search terms: "baxdrostat," "CYP11B2 inhibitor," "aldosterone synthase inhibitor," "treatment-resistant hypertension," "uncontrolled hypertension," "BaxHTN," "BrigHTN," and "Bax24." Reference lists were manually screened.

Eligibility: (i) randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled; (ii) adults with hypertension on background antihypertensive therapy; (iii) baxdrostat at any dose versus placebo; and (iv) reported change in SBP as primary or secondary outcome. Excluded: open-label, dose-escalation-only phase 1, non-randomized, duplicates, or insufficient aggregate data.

### 2.2 | Included Studies

Three RCTs met eligibility and were included: BrigHTN Phase 2 (NCT04519658), BaxHTN Phase 3 (NCT06034743), and Bax24 Phase 3 ambulatory BP monitoring trial (NCT06168409). Together enrolled 1285 participants across 214–283 clinical sites in multiple countries, with treatment durations of 12 weeks each.

### 2.3 | Data Extraction

Two reviewers independently extracted data using a pre-specified form. Variables: trial name, phase, year, randomization details, hypertension type, baxdrostat dose, treatment duration, sample size, baseline demographics and BP, primary and secondary efficacy endpoints, pharmacodynamic outcomes (serum aldosterone, plasma renin activity, serum cortisol), and prespecified adverse events of special interest (AESI). Discrepancies resolved by consensus.

### 2.4 | Outcomes

Primary outcome: placebo-corrected change in seated office SBP from baseline to Week 12 (LS mean difference, 95% CI). Secondary outcomes: seated office DBP; 24 h, night-time, daytime ambulatory SBP/DBP (Bax24); proportion achieving seated SBP < 130 mmHg; change in serum aldosterone; change in serum cortisol (selectivity marker); risk of potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L (primary safety outcome); mean change in eGFR. Primary meta-analysis estimated seated office SBP at Week 12 for baxdrostat 1 and 2 mg. The 0.5 mg BrigHTN arm was excluded from the primary pooled efficacy estimate (not advanced to phase 3, non-significant) but retained for descriptive dose-response assessment.

### 2.5 | Risk of Bias Assessment

Cochrane RoB 2 across five domains: (1) randomization; (2) deviations from intended interventions; (3) missing outcome data; (4) measurement of outcome; (5) selection of reported result. Each domain judged low risk / some concerns / high risk.

### 2.6 | Statistical Analysis

R version 4.3.1 with metafor package (version 4.4). Pooled MD and 95% CI via random-effects model with REML. Shared placebo comparators handled to avoid double-counting. Primary pooled analyses restricted to 1 and 2 mg doses. Heterogeneity via I2 statistic and Cochran's Q (I2 of 25%, 50%, 75% = low, moderate, high). Between-study variance (τ2) via REML. 95% prediction interval calculated. For binary safety outcome (potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L), REML of ORs on log scale with 0.5 continuity correction for zero cells.

Pre-specified subgroups: (i) dose (1 vs. 2 mg); (ii) hypertension type (resistant only vs. mixed uncontrolled/resistant); (iii) trial phase (2 vs. 3). Subgroup differences via test for interaction (QM statistic). Sensitivity analyses: leave-one-out; fixed-effects vs. REML; exclusion of Phase 2 data; restriction to 2 mg dose. Formal publication bias assessment not performed (only three trials, non-independent dose arms).

## 3 | Results

### 3.1 | Study Selection and Characteristics

Three eligible RCTs included. Across trials, 1289 participants randomized; mITT populations comprised 274, 794, and 217 participants in BrigHTN, BaxHTN, and Bax24, respectively (combined mITT N = 1285). Pooled population predominantly male (61%–65%) and White (63%–79%), mean age ~61 years. All received background diuretic therapy; 93%–95% received ACEi or ARB; 64%–89% received a calcium-channel blocker. Risk of bias low across all five RoB 2 domains in all three trials, with one domain "some concerns" in Bax24 (missing ABPM data at Week 12).

### 3.2 | Primary Outcome: Seated Office SBP

Across the clinically relevant 1 and 2 mg baxdrostat arms, pooled placebo-corrected reduction in seated office SBP was **−9.44 mmHg (95% CI: −11.09 to −7.79; p < 0.001)**, with no observed statistical heterogeneity (I2 = 0.0%). This is the pooled effect of the 1–2 mg dose range rather than all tested doses. The 0.5 mg BrigHTN dose reported separately (dose-response assessment).

Across individual study arms, placebo-corrected reductions in seated SBP ranged from −8.1 mmHg (BrigHTN 1 mg; 95% CI: −13.5 to −2.8; p = 0.003) to −11.0 mmHg (BrigHTN 2 mg; 95% CI: −16.4 to −5.5; p < 0.001). In BaxHTN, reductions were −8.7 mmHg (95% CI: −11.5 to −5.8; p < 0.0001) and −9.8 mmHg (95% CI: −12.6 to −7.0; p < 0.0001) for 1 and 2 mg. In Bax24 (seated SBP a secondary endpoint), the placebo-corrected reduction was −10.3 mmHg (95% CI: −14.9 to −5.6; p < 0.0001). The 0.5 mg dose in BrigHTN did not achieve significance (−2.7 mmHg; p = 0.33).

### 3.3 | Subgroup Analysis by Dose

Dose-specific pooled estimates calculated separately for baxdrostat 1 and 2 mg. The 1 mg dose available in BrigHTN and BaxHTN (k = 2); the 2 mg dose available in BrigHTN, BaxHTN, and Bax24 (k = 3). (Abstract reports dose subgroup estimates of −8.57 mmHg for 1 mg and −10.11 mmHg for 2 mg.)

### 3.4 | Subgroup Analysis: Resistant Hypertension Population

In the pre-specified subgroup with treatment-resistant hypertension (all BrigHTN participants, BaxHTN resistant-HTN subpopulation, all Bax24 participants), pooled placebo-corrected SBP reduction was −9.61 mmHg (95% CI: −11.47 to −7.75; I2 = 0.0%), consistent with the primary pooled result.

### 3.5 | Ambulatory Blood Pressure: Bax24 Trial (Phase 3)

Bax24 provided the first placebo-controlled evidence of baxdrostat's effect on 24 h ambulatory BP in patients with true resistant hypertension confirmed by ABPM. At Week 12, placebo-corrected reductions in 24 h, daytime, and night-time ambulatory SBP were **−14.0 mmHg (95% CI: −17.2 to −10.8; p < 0.0001)**, −14.1 mmHg (95% CI: −17.4 to −10.7; p < 0.0001), and −13.9 mmHg (95% CI: −17.5 to −10.3; p < 0.0001), respectively. All ambulatory DBP outcomes were similarly significant.

Night-time SBP reduction (−13.9 mmHg) was comparable to daytime (−14.1 mmHg), indicating baxdrostat's ~30 h half-life supports 24 h coverage and preserves the normal circadian BP pattern. 71% of baxdrostat recipients versus 17% of placebo recipients achieved 24 h ABPM SBP control (< 130 mmHg) at Week 12 (OR 15.2; 95% CI: 6.6 to 35.2; p < 0.0001).

### 3.6 | Seated DBP

Pooled placebo-corrected reduction in seated office DBP across all meta-analyzable arms (BrigHTN 2 mg, BaxHTN 1 and 2 mg, Bax24 2 mg; k = 4) was −4.01 mmHg (95% CI: −5.14 to −2.88; p < 0.001; I2 = 0.0%). Individual arms ranged from −3.3 mmHg (BaxHTN 1 mg; 95% CI: −5.2 to −1.4; p = 0.0008) to −5.2 mmHg (BrigHTN 2 mg; 95% CI: −8.7 to −1.6).

### 3.7 | Blood Pressure Control Rates

In BaxHTN, proportion achieving seated SBP < 130 mmHg at Week 12 was 39.4% (1 mg) and 40.0% (2 mg) vs. 18.7% placebo (OR 2.9 for both doses; p < 0.0001). In Bax24, 71% of baxdrostat 2 mg-treated patients achieved 24 h ABPM SBP < 130 mmHg vs. 17% placebo (OR 15.2; 95% CI: 6.6 to 35.2; p < 0.0001). Discordance likely reflects Bax24's more stringent ambulatory-confirmed definition of resistant hypertension.

### 3.8 | Dose-Response Relationship

Clear dose-response in BrigHTN (only trial with three active doses). Placebo-corrected SBP reductions of −2.7, −8.1, and −11.0 mmHg at 0.5, 1, and 2 mg. Weighted linear regression: slope −4.2 mmHg per mg increase; R2 = 0.96. Corroborated by parallel dose-dependent reductions in serum and urinary aldosterone.

### 3.9 | Pharmacodynamic Outcomes

Serum cortisol concentrations were not reduced in any baxdrostat group across the three trials. In BrigHTN, LS mean change in cortisol at the highest dose (2 mg) was +1.91 µg/dL (95% CI: +0.70 to +3.12), a slight nonspecific increase. No significant differences between baxdrostat and placebo for cortisol at any dose in any trial. These findings confirm high selectivity of baxdrostat for aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) relative to 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1), which shares 93% sequence similarity with aldosterone synthase.

### 3.10 | Safety Outcomes

No deaths attributed to baxdrostat in any trial. Rates of any adverse event broadly comparable between baxdrostat and placebo: 35%–52% vs. 37%–41% across trials and doses. No serious adverse events attributed to baxdrostat by investigators. No cases of adrenocortical insufficiency in any participant across the three trials.

### 3.11 | Hyperkalemia

For potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L, absolute risks, risk differences, and relative effects summarized. Because events were rare with zero cells, ORs using 0.5 continuity correction were considered exploratory. Sensitivity analyses using rare-event methods (Mantel–Haenszel risk difference or exact/logistic approaches) were planned. (Pooled OR 5.14; 95% CI: 1.76 to 14.97 — see Discussion.)

### 3.12 | Renal Function

Mean eGFR declined from baseline to Week 12 in baxdrostat-treated patients across Phase 3 trials: −7.0 (SD 12.8) and −6.9 (SD 12.4) mL/min/1.73 m2 in BaxHTN 1 mg and 2 mg (vs. −0.1 [SD 8.6] placebo), and −8.7 (SD 14.0) mL/min/1.73 m2 in Bax24 2 mg (vs. −0.4 [SD 9.7] placebo). Pooled mean eGFR change vs. placebo was −7.05 mL/min/1.73 m2 (95% CI: −8.26 to −5.85; I2 = 0.0%). In both BaxHTN and Bax24, eGFR returned toward baseline within 2 weeks of drug discontinuation, confirming reversible functional change attributable to improved BP control (reduced intraglomerular perfusion pressure) rather than intrinsic nephrotoxicity.

### 3.13 | Hyponatremia and Hypotension

Hyponatremia requiring clinical intervention (AESI) occurred in 0.8%–2.3% and 0.4%–1% of baxdrostat and placebo recipients, respectively. Hypotension requiring clinical intervention occurred in 1.9%–2.3% and 0.8%–1.1% of baxdrostat and placebo recipients in Phase 3. Both events manageable, self-limited, did not lead to discontinuation in most affected patients.

### 3.14 | Sensitivity Analyses

Leave-one-out analysis showed no single study arm pulling the pooled estimate; MD ranged from −8.47 to −10.00 mmHg (spread < 1.6 mmHg). Fixed-effects model: pooled MD −9.41 mmHg (95% CI: −10.98 to −7.84), within 0.1 mmHg of REML. Phase 3 data only (k = 3 arms): pooled MD −9.42 mmHg (95% CI: −11.26 to −7.59; I2 = 0.0%). The 2 mg dose restriction across four arms: −10.11 mmHg (95% CI: −12.30 to −7.91; I2 = 0.0%).

### 3.15 | Publication Bias

Not formally assessed (only three trials with non-independent dose-arm comparisons). No inference regarding publication bias can be made. (Egger's test reported in Limitations: z = 0.87; p = 0.39.)

### 3.16 | GRADE Evidence Summary

Certainty moderate for seated office SBP reduction (randomized trials, low risk of bias, consistent effects, but few independent trials and 12-week follow-up). Low to moderate for ambulatory BP outcomes (single trial). Moderate for seated DBP reduction and cortisol outcomes. Low for hyperkalemia and adrenocortical insufficiency (low event counts, imprecise CIs, zero-event data). Moderate or low for eGFR change (short follow-up).

## 4 | Discussion

Three phase 2/3 RCTs enrolling 1285 participants yield a pooled placebo-corrected reduction in seated office SBP of −9.44 mmHg (95% CI: −11.09 to −7.79; p < 0.001; I2 = 0.0%), with parallel pharmacodynamic evidence of sustained aldosterone suppression and preserved cortisol levels, and a statistically significant but low-magnitude increase in clinically significant hyperkalemia (pooled OR 5.14; 95% CI: 1.76 to 14.97). The 95% prediction interval of −13.6 to −5.3 mmHg indicates meaningful BP reduction is expected in future populations. Together with Bax24 ambulatory data, these findings position baxdrostat as an effective add-on option for resistant and uncontrolled hypertension.

The ~9.4 mmHg pooled reduction is in the same territory as recent class-level work. Marzano et al. put placebo-corrected SBP at 8.3 mmHg (95% CI: 5.9 to 10.7) across ASI RCTs; Gao et al. at 8–9 mmHg; and Elbahloul et al.'s network meta-analysis placed baxdrostat 2 mg above 10 mmHg in indirect comparisons, fitting the dose-specific estimate of −10.11 mmHg (95% CI: −12.30 to −7.91). The estimate replicated across independent data sets each with low risk of bias, in populations already maximally treated.

Every patient enrolled was on optimized background therapy (universal diuretic use, ACEi/ARB 93%–95%) and still uncontrolled. For comparison, spironolactone and eplerenone as add-on agents in resistant hypertension typically yield placebo-corrected reductions of 4–6 mmHg. BaxHTN confirmed the BrigHTN signal almost exactly (−8.7 and −9.8 mmHg for 1 and 2 mg), with no attenuation from phase 2 to phase 3.

The Bax24 ABPM data merit particular attention. Placebo-corrected reductions of −14.0, −14.1, and −13.9 mmHg in 24 h, daytime, and night-time SBP are substantial. Night-time SBP has the strongest prognostic tie to cardiovascular mortality among all ambulatory BP parameters, and here it falls as much as daytime SBP owing to baxdrostat's ~30 h half-life. These ambulatory reductions exceed pooled 24 h SBP estimates from prior ASI meta-analyses (~7–10 mmHg), likely reflecting ABPM-confirmed resistant hypertension selection in Bax24.

BrigHTN's dose-response is clean even by phase 2 standards: placebo-corrected seated SBP reductions of −2.7, −8.1, −11.0 mmHg at 0.5, 1, 2 mg; linear regression slope −4.2 mmHg per mg; R2 = 0.96. Paired with dose-dependent reductions in serum and urinary aldosterone and compensatory plasma renin activity increases, there is no credible nonspecific explanation for the BP effect. The 0.5 mg dose did not reach significance (−2.7 mmHg; p = 0.33). In the current pooled analysis, the 1 vs. 2 mg comparison did not reach significance (QM = 1.93; p = 0.16), but the 1.35 mmHg additional reduction with the higher dose is directionally consistent.

Cortisol and adrenocortical insufficiency data support baxdrostat's selectivity over 12 weeks, but evidence remains limited to short-term trials; longer exposure in larger, more diverse populations is required before definitive conclusions about long-term adrenal safety.

Clinically significant hyperkalemia (potassium ≥ 6.0 mmol/L) occurred infrequently but more often with baxdrostat than placebo. In absolute terms, ~2.3%–4.6% of baxdrostat-treated participants in phase 3 trials vs. 0%–0.4% of placebo. Pooled OR was 5.14 (95% CI: 1.76 to 14.97), but this relative estimate should be interpreted cautiously (low event counts, zero cells requiring continuity correction). Absolute risks and risk differences are more clinically interpretable than the OR alone.

Two things about this signal deserve attention. First, it is heavily context-dependent. In Dwyer et al.'s phase 2 CKD trial, any-grade hyperkalemia hit 41% of baxdrostat recipients vs. 5% placebo, reflecting impaired renal potassium handling in CKD. The general hypertension populations in BrigHTN and BaxHTN carry nothing like that baseline risk. Second, the timing pattern is reassuring: most hyperkalemia events appeared within the first 2 weeks then stabilized; after discontinuation in BaxHTN and Bax24, potassium returned toward baseline within 2 weeks. A practical protocol (baseline renal function and potassium, repeat at 2 and 4 weeks, and after any dose change) is proportionate to the risk and consistent with MRA recommendations.

The pooled eGFR decline of −7.05 mL/min/1.73 m2 vs. placebo across BaxHTN and Bax24 is best interpreted as hemodynamic. eGFR returned toward baseline within 2 weeks of drug withdrawal, inconsistent with structural nephrotoxicity — the same pattern seen with ACEi/ARB initiation.

One demographic gap runs through all three trials: men made up 61%–65% of participants; White European participants 63%–79%. The BaxAsia trial (NCT06034745) had not reported at the time of data extraction. Extrapolation to Asian populations should be treated with caution.

### 4.1 | Strengths and Limitations

All available phase 2/3 RCT evidence for baxdrostat is included with pre-specified outcomes; risk of bias consistently low. GRADE certainty: MODERATE for seated office SBP, seated DBP, cortisol; LOW–MODERATE for ambulatory BP; LOW for hyperkalemia and adrenocortical insufficiency. Downgraded because of the small number of independent studies, short follow-up, reliance on a single trial for ambulatory BP, and imprecision in rare safety events.

Limitations: Three trials and five study arms leave tests for heterogeneity and publication bias (Egger's test: z = 0.87; p = 0.39) underpowered — a structural constraint. Trials ran 12 weeks; no long-term cardiovascular outcomes data. No head-to-head comparison with spironolactone or other MRAs. Individual participant data unavailable, preventing exploration of effect modifiers (baseline aldosterone, background MRA use, eGFR strata).

### 4.2 | Clinical and Therapeutic Implications

Baxdrostat lowers blood pressure over 12 weeks, preserves measured cortisol concentrations, and has a safety profile manageable with potassium and renal-function monitoring. Cardiovascular outcome data and head-to-head comparisons with MRAs are still needed to define its long-term therapeutic role.

## 5 | Conclusions

Across three randomized trials in patients with resistant or uncontrolled hypertension, baxdrostat reduced seated office SBP by approximately 9–10 mmHg and, in one trial, reduced 24 h ambulatory SBP by approximately 14 mmHg beyond placebo when added to background antihypertensive therapy. Aldosterone was reduced, and cortisol concentrations were not suppressed over 12 weeks. Hyperkalemia occurred infrequently but more often with baxdrostat than placebo, supporting early potassium and renal-function monitoring. eGFR declined modestly and appeared reversible during short-term follow-up, although longer-term renal safety data are needed. Current evidence supports baxdrostat as a promising add-on therapy, but certainty is limited by the small number of independent trials, short follow-up, rare safety events, and absence of cardiovascular outcome data.
