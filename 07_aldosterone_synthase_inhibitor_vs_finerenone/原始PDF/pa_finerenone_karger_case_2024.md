# A Trial of Finerenone in a Patient with Primary Aldosteronism

## Structured Abstract (verbatim, PubMed/Europe PMC)

**Introduction.** Primary aldosteronism (PA), a common secondary cause of arterial hypertension, is treated either surgically or pharmacologically with mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs). These drugs, while effective, can cause allergic reactions and have side effects, including menstrual cycle disorders in women. Finerenone is a new, highly selective, nonsteroidal MRA with excellent side-effect profile, primarily intended to slow the progression of diabetic kidney disease and improve cardiovascular outcomes in these patients. No data are available so far on its effect on patients with PA.

**Case presentation.** A case of a female patient with confirmed primary aldosteronism, in whom adrenal vein sampling failed twice is presented. The patient developed a skin allergic reaction to spironolactone and experienced prolonged vaginal bleedings with eplerenone, which was attributed to the drug's affinity for progesterone receptors. A trial of finerenone was initiated, resulting in mild increase in plasma renin activity and serum potassium, and somewhat control of blood pressure, but far from optimal blood pressure control, normokalemia, or unsuppression of plasma renin activity.

**Conclusion.** This case highlights the challenges of managing PA and describes an attempt of treatment with finerenone to which this patient unfortunately did not adequately respond.

## Case details (from publisher full-text)

- Patient: 54-year-old female; PA diagnosed at age 42; obese (BMI 33.4); history of polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS). Adrenal vein sampling failed twice.
- Prior therapy sequence: amlodipine / moxonidine → spironolactone (discontinued — allergic skin rash) → eplerenone 50 mg (caused abnormal/prolonged vaginal bleeding + endometrial hyperplasia, attributed to progesterone-receptor affinity).
- Finerenone trial: **20 mg once daily**, plus **120 mmol/day potassium supplementation**.
- Documented follow-up on finerenone: **1 month**.
- Outcome: far from optimal BP control, not normokalemic, PRA remained suppressed; patient subsequently opted for **adrenalectomy**.

## Clinical relevance (task framing)

Real-world first-hand evidence that finerenone at its current (CKD/HF) dosing — here 20 mg/day — provides
**inadequate MR blockade in overt PA**: PRA rose only 0.5→0.8 µg/L/h (still suppressed), K+ only reached
~3.5 mmol/L (not normokalemic), and BP remained above target. Aldosterone rose (750→1,414 pmol/L),
consistent with partial/insufficient receptor blockade rather than adequate PA control. Supports the argument
that finerenone dosing designed for cardiorenal indications may under-treat the mineralocorticoid excess of PA.
