# Central infusion of aldosterone synthase inhibitor prevents sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension by central Na+ in Wistar rats

## Abstract

In Wistar rats, increasing cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) Na+ concentration ([Na+]) by intracerebroventricular (ICV) infusion of hypertonic saline causes sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension that can be prevented by blockade of brain mineralocorticoid receptors (MR). To assess the role of aldosterone produced locally in the brain in the activation of MR in the central nervous system (CNS), Wistar rats were infused ICV with artificial CSF (aCSF), Na+-rich (800 mmol/l) aCSF, aCSF plus the aldosterone synthase inhibitor FAD286 (100 μg·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹), or Na+-rich aCSF plus FAD286. After 2 wk of infusion, rats treated with Na+-rich aCSF exhibited significant increases in aldosterone and corticosterone content in the hypothalamus but not in the hippocampus, as well as increases in resting blood pressure (BP) and sympathoexcitatory responses to air stress, and impairment of arterial baroreflex function. Concomitant ICV infusion of FAD286 prevented the Na+-induced increase in hypothalamic aldosterone but not corticosterone and prevented most of the increases in resting BP and sympathoexcitatory and pressor responses to air stress and the baroreflex impairment. FAD286 had no effects in rats infused with ICV aCSF. In another set of rats, 24-h BP and heart rate were recorded via telemetry before and during a 14-day ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF with or without FAD286. Na+-rich aCSF without FAD286 caused sustained increases (∼10 mmHg) in resting mean arterial pressure that were absent in the rats treated with FAD286. These data suggest that in Wistar rats, an increase in CSF [Na+] may increase the biosynthesis of corticosterone and aldosterone in the hypothalamus, and mainly aldosterone activates MR in the CNS leading to sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension.

**Keywords:** brain sodium; brain steroids; mineralocorticoid receptor; cytochrome P-450 aldosterone synthase; hypothalamus; sympathoexcitation; diurnal variation

## Introduction

In Dahl salt-sensitive (S) rats, high salt intake causes a persistent increase in cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) Na+ concentration ([Na+]) and increases aldosterone content in the hypothalamus. Central nervous system (CNS) blockade of aldosterone synthesis, or of mineralocorticoid receptors (MR), prevents the high-salt-induced sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension in Dahl S rats. We postulated that in Dahl S rats on high salt enhanced Na+ entry from blood to brain interstitium activates local aldosterone biosynthesis and release, and thereby activates central pathways resulting in sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension.

If so, specifically increasing CSF [Na+] or aldosterone should reproduce this high-salt diet-induced phenotype. Indeed, in normotensive rats chronic intracerebroventricular (ICV) infusion of Na+-rich artificial CSF (aCSF) increases aldosterone content in the hypothalamus and causes sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension, which can be prevented by CNS blockade of MR with ICV spironolactone. Similarly, ICV infusion of aldosterone causes sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension. Enzymes involved in steroid biosynthesis are present in the CNS. However, it is still doubtful whether the brain can produce aldosterone in biologically active amounts. Changes in content may reflect changes in uptake or metabolism rather than local changes in synthesis. Moreover, it is possible that ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF also increases hypothalamic corticosterone. Both corticosterone and aldosterone can act as agonists contributing to MR stimulation in the CNS. Aldosterone and corticosterone bind MR with equal affinity, but the presence of the corticosterone-inactivating enzyme 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 2 (11β-HSD-2) enhances aldosterone selectivity of the MR in brain regions such as the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus. A decrease in 11β-HSD-2 activity also may lead to activation of MR in the CNS and thereby affect cardiovascular regulation.

In the present study, we evaluated the effects of a chronic increase in CSF [Na+] on hypothalamic aldosterone and corticosterone content and the specific role of locally produced aldosterone in sympathoexcitation and hypertension by ICV infusion of the cytochrome P-450 aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) inhibitor FAD286. Wistar rats were treated for 2 wk with an ICV infusion of Na+-rich CSF or aCSF with or without concomitant ICV infusion of FAD286. Blood pressure (BP) and heart rate (HR) were measured either by telemetry throughout or by intra-arterial catheter after the 2-wk infusion, followed by assessment of sympathoexcitatory and pressor responses to air stress and arterial baroreflex control of renal sympathetic nerve activity (RSNA) and HR. Aldosterone and corticosterone content were measured in the hypothalamus and hippocampus as well as in plasma. The results indicate that a small increase in CSF [Na+] increases both corticosterone and aldosterone in the hypothalamus but not in the plasma, suggesting that an increase in CSF [Na+] activates enzymes in the steroid biosynthetic pathway in the CNS. The increase in aldosterone appears to play a functional role in stimulation of MR in the CNS and thereby in sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension.

## Methods

Male Wistar rats weighing 200–250 g (Charles River, Montreal, QC, Canada) were housed on a 12:12-h light-dark cycle and given standard laboratory chow (120 μmol Na+/g) and tap water ad libitum. The study conformed to Canadian Council on Animal Care / NIH guidelines and was approved by the University of Ottawa Animal Care Committee.

### Protocol 1

After a 1-wk acclimatization, under isoflurane inhalation a stainless steel cannula was implanted into the left lateral cerebral ventricle and fixed to the skull with acrylic cement. The cannula was connected to an osmotic minipump (model 2ML2, Alza) for chronic ICV infusion at 5 μl/h for 14 days. The pump was filled with aCSF or aCSF containing 800 mmol/l Na+ (Na+-rich aCSF) alone or combined with FAD286 (100 μg·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹) (4 groups total, n = 7 or 8/group), implanted subcutaneously. ICV infusion of the Na+-rich aCSF at this rate for 2 wk increases CSF [Na+] by 4–5 mmol/l. FAD286 is a single (+)-enantiomer; FAD286 hydrogen tartrate (Novartis) was used because it is soluble in aCSF (each 1.67 mg of tartrate provides 1 mg of FAD286 free base). Composition of aCSF (in mmol/l): 121 NaCl, 3.4 KCl, 1.2 MgCl₂, 0.6 NaH₂PO₄, 29 NaHCO₃, 3.4 glucose; pH 7.4; osmolality 296 mosmol/kg H₂O.

At the end of the 14-day infusion, PE-10 (fused to PE-50) catheters were inserted in the right femoral vein and abdominal aorta. Via a left flank incision, a pair of silver electrodes was attached around a segment of the renal nerve. About 4–5 h later, after recovery, BP and HR were recorded through a polygraph (Grass model 7E) and tachograph; RSNA signals were amplified (Grass P511), rectified/integrated (Grass 7P10), and fed to an online computer (Polyview 2.0). Background noise on RSNA was subtracted after the rat was killed.

After a 30-min rest, RSNA, BP, and HR were recorded for 5 min. A standardized air jet stress was then applied for two 30-s intervals, 10 min apart (air jet 1–1.5 lb/in.², directed to the face). Peak increases from resting values were recorded, and the mean of the two peak responses used for analysis. Twenty minutes later, phenylephrine (5–50 μg·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹) was infused IV to raise BP by 50 mmHg, then sodium nitroprusside (5–100 μg·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹) to lower BP by 50 mmHg. Arterial baroreflex function (ΔRSNA/ΔHR vs. MAP) was analyzed with a logistic model: ΔRSNA/ΔHR = P1 + P2/[1 + e^(P3(MAP − P4))], where P1 is lower plateau, P2 is range, P3 is curvature coefficient, and P4 is MAP50; maximum slope (gain) = −P2 × P3/4.

About 30 min after baroreflex assessment, 3 ml of blood was collected for aldosterone, corticosterone, and ANG II assays. The rat was killed with IV pentobarbital, the whole brain removed and frozen, and the whole hypothalamus and hippocampus dissected (Glowinski and Iversen). Plasma and brain aldosterone were measured by radioimmunoassay (RIA). For hypothalamic aldosterone, sensitivity was 0.5/14 = 0.035 pg/mg; observed levels (>0.25 pg/mg) were much higher. Intra-assay variation 7%; recovery ≥88%. Cross-reactivities: 0.03% (corticosterone) and 0.14% (11-deoxycorticosterone). Plasma/tissue corticosterone measured with an RIA kit (MP Biomedicals). Plasma ANG II measured by RIA after Sep-Pak C18 extraction and HPLC separation.

### Protocol 2

In three groups of Wistar rats on regular salt, a telemetry probe (DSI model TA11PA-C40) was placed into the abdominal cavity with the catheter in the abdominal aorta. After 3 days of control recordings, a stainless steel cannula was implanted into the left lateral cerebral ventricle, connected to an osmotic minipump (model 2ML2) filled with either aCSF (n = 4) or Na+-rich aCSF with (n = 8) or without (n = 8) FAD286 (100 μg·kg⁻¹·day⁻¹). Telemetry recordings continued for an additional 14 days.

### Statistical analysis

Protocol 1: two-way ANOVA (effects of Na+-rich aCSF and FAD286), followed by Duncan multirange test. Protocol 2: one-way repeated-measures ANOVA with Duncan's test. Diurnal MAP/HR differences: paired t-test. Plasma vs. hypothalamic aldosterone: Pearson correlation. Significance defined as P < 0.05.

## Results

### Brain and plasma aldosterone and corticosterone

ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF significantly increased hypothalamic aldosterone by 85% and corticosterone by 65% (Fig. 1). ICV infusion of FAD286 prevented the increase in hypothalamic aldosterone but not corticosterone. FAD286 had no effects on hypothalamic aldosterone and corticosterone in rats infused with ICV aCSF. Infusion of Na+-rich aCSF alone or combined with FAD286 did not change aldosterone and corticosterone content in the hippocampus or plasma (Table 1). There was no correlation between plasma aldosterone and hypothalamic aldosterone levels. Changes in plasma ANG II after ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF were not significant.

### Blood pressure

After 2 wk of ICV infusion, Na+-rich aCSF increased resting MAP by ∼15 mmHg compared with aCSF control (intra-arterial catheter; Table 1). MAP increased significantly less in rats with ICV Na+-rich aCSF plus FAD286 but was still higher than in aCSF rats. FAD286 had no effects on MAP in rats treated with ICV aCSF. HR tended to be higher in rats treated with ICV Na+-rich aCSF alone.

### 24-h Blood pressure

Average 24-h baseline MAP was ∼95 mmHg. In rats with ICV aCSF, MAP did not change. MAP increased significantly by 9 mmHg on day 1 of ICV Na+-rich aCSF (Fig. 2) and remained around this level for the following 14 days. In rats treated with Na+-rich aCSF plus FAD286, MAP similarly increased on day 1 but then decreased on day 2, returning to preinfusion levels on days 3–4 and remaining at control level. Before ICV Na+-rich aCSF, MAP was ∼5 mmHg higher in the night vs. day phase; on day 1 this difference significantly increased in Na+-rich aCSF groups but not aCSF groups (Fig. 3). The larger diurnal difference in MAP persisted with Na+-rich aCSF but normalized in rats treated with Na+-rich aCSF plus FAD286 from day 4.

On day 1, average HR increased significantly by 30–40 beats/min (Na+-rich aCSF) and by ∼20 beats/min (aCSF, P = 0.1). In all groups HR returned to control levels by day 4. HR showed a modest (∼35 beats/min) diurnal variation, not affected by either infusion.

### Sympathetic reactivity

ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF increased peak excitatory responses of RSNA, MAP, and HR to air stress by 85–125% (Fig. 4). ICV FAD286 prevented most of the enhancement in RSNA, BP, and HR responses. ICV FAD286 had no effects on responses to air stress in rats treated with aCSF.

After 2 wk of ICV Na+-rich aCSF, baroreflex curves for RSNA or HR vs. MAP were less steep, with significantly lower maximal slope and ranges (Fig. 5), consistent with impaired baroreflex function. These changes were prevented by ICV FAD286. ICV FAD286 had no effects on baroreflex function in rats treated with ICV aCSF.

## Discussion

There are two major findings. First, in Wistar rats a small increase in CSF [Na+] by ICV infusion of Na+-rich aCSF increases both aldosterone and corticosterone content in the hypothalamus, but not in the hippocampus or blood. Second, ICV infusion of an aldosterone synthase inhibitor prevents the increase in hypothalamic aldosterone, but not corticosterone, induced by ICV Na+-rich aCSF, and it substantially attenuates sympathetic hyperreactivity, impairment of arterial baroreflex function, and hypertension.

Enzymes involved in steroid biosynthesis are present in the CNS, and there is evidence the rat brain has the enzymatic machinery for steroidogenesis and can synthesize both corticosterone and aldosterone. In rats, cytochrome P-450 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1) converts DOC to corticosterone and cytochrome P-450 aldosterone synthase (CYP11B2) catalyzes the steps between DOC and aldosterone. The brain is a major extra-adrenal site of CYP11B1 and CYP11B2, expressed in several brain areas including the hypothalamus. In the hypothalamus of rats, CYP11B2 mRNA levels are ∼1/10th of adrenal and CYP11B1 ∼1/1,000th. ICV Na+-rich aCSF increased hypothalamic corticosterone by 65% and aldosterone by 85% but had no effects on plasma or hippocampal content. These nonparallel changes suggest a small increase of brain [Na+] may increase activities of both CYP11B1 and CYP11B2, or of other rate-limiting enzymes such as cholesterol side chain cleavage enzyme CYP11A.

Actual synthesis was not measured; increases in content could be due to decreased metabolism or increased uptake. Local infusion of an aldosterone synthase inhibitor distinguishes local synthesis from uptake/metabolism. ICV FAD286 prevented the increase in hypothalamic aldosterone, suggesting the Na+-rich aCSF-induced increase is indeed a result of increased local aldosterone synthesis. The Na+-rich aCSF increased aldosterone in the hypothalamus but not hippocampus, indicating a region-specific effect. High salt intake (transient increases in plasma [Na+]) decreases CYP11B2 expression and aldosterone synthesis in the adrenal gland.

ICV FAD286 prevented the Na+-induced increase in hypothalamic aldosterone but did not lower levels below control and had no effects on aldosterone in ICV aCSF rats, suggesting the basal amount of hypothalamic aldosterone mainly reflects uptake of aldosterone of peripheral (adrenal) origin. Plasma aldosterone and corticosterone remained similar among the four groups, suggesting ICV Na+-rich aCSF and FAD286 had minimal peripheral effects.

Similar to previous studies, ICV Na+-rich aCSF enhanced pressor and sympathoexcitatory responses to air stress, impaired baroreflex control of RSNA and HR, and increased resting BP. These effects can be prevented by ICV MR blocker, indicating dependence on CNS MR activation. The present study suggests locally produced aldosterone is mainly responsible for this MR activation. Aldosterone synthase inhibition substantially attenuated the Na+-rich aCSF-induced sympathoexcitatory enhancement and baroreflex impairment. By telemetry, 24-h BP was normalized in rats treated with Na+-rich aCSF plus FAD286; by arterial catheter the increase in BP was only attenuated, possibly reflecting an enhanced stress effect not completely blocked, a need for higher inhibitor rates, or residual hypothalamic corticosterone acting as an MR agonist. Effects of the aldosterone synthase inhibitor were largely similar to those of an MR blocker, indicating corticosterone exhibited only minimal agonistic activity in this paradigm.

By telemetry, on day 1 resting BP elevated rapidly and to the same extent with and without FAD286; BP stayed high in rats without FAD286, but in FAD286-treated rats declined on day 2 and returned to control on day 4. Since this rapid day-1 increase was absent in aCSF rats, Na+-rich aCSF may first increase release of existing aldosterone (unaffected by a synthase inhibitor), with subsequent pressor effects depending on newly synthesized aldosterone; alternatively it may reflect transient vasopressin release. ICV Na+-rich aCSF caused only a transient (3-day) increase in HR by telemetry; return of resting HR to normal suggests sympathoexcitation by increased CSF [Na+] is not associated with persistent cardiac sympathoexcitation.

Diurnal differences (night vs. daytime) in MAP and HR were ∼5 mmHg and ∼35 beats/min during control. This diurnal variation in BP (not HR) increased by 50% within 1 day of ICV Na+-rich aCSF. Aldosterone synthase inhibition did not affect the increase on the first day but subsequently blocked both, suggesting locally produced aldosterone also contributes to CNS regulation of diurnal rhythms.

### Perspectives and Significance

The present study demonstrates that in Wistar rats a small increase in CSF [Na+] appears to increase local synthesis of aldosterone and corticosterone in the hypothalamus. This aldosterone is the main agonist responsible for stimulation of MR in the CNS and therefore activation of central pathways leading to sympathetic hyperactivity and hypertension. It remains to be determined how an increase in CSF [Na+] increases aldosterone synthesis in the brain, and whether in salt-sensitive animal models high salt intake also enhances local brain aldosterone synthesis.
