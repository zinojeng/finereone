# Risk of dementia in primary aldosteronism compared with essential hypertension: a nationwide cohort study

**Namki Hong¹†, Kyoung Jin Kim²†, Min Heui Yu³, Seong Ho Jeong⁴, Seunghyun Lee⁵, Jung Soo Lim⁵*, Yumie Rhee¹***

*Alzheimer's Research & Therapy* (2023) 15:136 · https://doi.org/10.1186/s13195-023-01274-x
† Namki Hong and Kyoung Jin Kim contributed equally (co-first authors). * Jung Soo Lim and Yumie Rhee are co-corresponding authors.
Received: 22 December 2022 · Accepted: 12 July 2023

## Abstract

**Background** Although hypertension is a critical risk factor for dementia, the association between primary aldosteronism (PA) and dementia has been scarcely reported. We aimed to investigate whether the risk of dementia in patients with PA was elevated compared with patients with essential hypertension (EH).

**Methods** From the National Health Insurance Claim database in Korea (2003–2017), 3,687 patients with PA (adrenalectomy [ADX], n = 1,339; mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist [MRA] n = 2,348) with no prior dementia were age- and sex-matched at a 1:4 ratio to patients with EH (n = 14,741). The primary outcomes were all-cause dementia events, including Alzheimer's disease, vascular dementia, or other dementia combined with a prescription of one or more medications for dementia (donepezil, galantamine, memantine, or rivastigmine). Multivariable Cox regression models were used to evaluate the hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals for the outcome incidence rates between patients with PA and their EH matches.

**Results** During a median follow-up of 5.2 years, there were 156 cases of all-cause dementia (4.2%), 140 cases of Alzheimer's disease (3.8%), and 65 cases of vascular dementia (1.8%). Compared with EH, the risk of all-cause dementia was increased in treated PA (unadjusted hazard ratio [HR] 1.26; p < 0.011). Among PA, MRA group had higher risks of all-cause dementia, especially vascular dementia, adjusted for age, sex, income, comorbidities, and concurrent medication (adjusted HR 1.31; p = 0.027 and adjusted HR 1.62; p = 0.020, respectively) compared to EH. ADX group seemed to have a lower dementia risk than the EH group, but there was no statistical significance after full adjustment. This trend became more prominent when the dementia risks were evaluated from the time of hypertension diagnosis rather than treatment initiation for PA.

**Conclusion** The findings of this cohort study suggest that PA, especially the MRA group, is associated with an increased risk of dementia. Monitoring cognitive function in PA patients even after treatment initiation might be warranted to prevent dementia.

**Keywords** Hyperaldosteronism, Hypertension, Dementia, Alzheimer's disease, Vascular dementia, Adrenalectomy, Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists

## Background

Primary aldosteronism (PA) is the most common cause of secondary hypertension that is characterized by excessive autonomous aldosterone production [1]. The prevalence of PA is currently reported as 6 to 8% among consecutive patients with hypertension in various care settings, with a prevalence as high as 20% in resistant hypertension [2]. PA is associated with a higher risk of long-term complications than essential hypertension (EH). These include atherosclerotic cardiovascular events, atrial fibrillation (AF), heart failure, a higher prevalence of diabetes mellitus (DM), and chronic kidney disease compared to EH, which poses substantial health and economic burdens [3–5].

Hypertension is a well-established modifiable risk factor for dementia, a progressive disease characterized by a decline in memory and cognitive function [6]. Several community-based cohort studies further suggested that persistent midlife hypertension is associated with an increased risk of dementia in later life [7]. Potentially modifiable risk factors, including less education, low social contact, hearing impairment, smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and DM, have been reported. Furthermore, recent clinical guidelines for dementia endorse the use of anti-hypertensive treatment as the only known effective preventive medication for dementia [8]. Considering the difficulties in blood pressure control despite the use of multiple anti-hypertensive medications and co-existing conditions such as DM in patients with PA, the risk of dementia may be higher in PA than in EH. Accordingly, a more tailored strategy to prevent later life dementia in patients with hypertension due to PA will be needed.

To date, no longitudinal or cross-sectional studies have been conducted that directly investigate the connection between primary aldosteronism and the risk of dementia. However, a case report on what is referred to as Conn's dementia, coupled with the established hypertensive effects of primary aldosteronism, provides a possible association with Binswanger's dementia—a subtype characterized by white matter lesions [9]. Furthermore, a recent study has elucidated the relationship between elevated plasma aldosterone levels and white matter lesions in patients with hypertension. This study provides supportive evidence regarding aldosterone's role in cerebral changes that are relevant to dementia [10]. In addition, animal studies have highlighted the association between the activation of the Renin–Angiotensin–Aldosterone System (RAAS) and cognitive impairment [11]. Notably, RAAS operates not only systemically but also locally within organs, including the brain. This localized activity of RAAS is critical in understanding its role in organ damage. These insights underscore the significance of further investigating the implications of primary aldosteronism on dementia risk.

This study therefore aimed to investigate the risk of dementia in individuals with PA, grouped by adrenalectomy (ADX) or mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) treatment, compared to age- and sex-matched EH controls using a nationwide cohort. We also sought to explore potentially vulnerable subgroups for the onset of dementia in patients with PA.

## Methods

### Data source

This study used the Korean National Health Insurance Service (NHIS) database, consisting of 51.5 million Korean inhabitants from 2002 to 2017. South Korea has a nationwide health system covering approximately 97% of the population to facilitate reimbursements, with all medical institutions submitting healthcare utilization-related information. The database contains medical and pharmaceutical claim records, including disease diagnosis codes (ICD-10), medical procedure and hospital admission information, prescribed drugs, health examination data such as anthropometric measures, and death records [12].

This study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of Wonju Severance Christian Hospital (IRB number: CR318340). Informed consent was waived because data from the NHIS cohort were fully anonymized and de-identified.

### Study subjects

To identify patients with likely PA, operational definitions based on a combination of diagnostic, procedure, and medication codes were used. These operational definitions were validated using a multicenter cohort of 920 patients in previous research [13]; the validation cohort consisted of patients with at least one diagnosis code for PA, where 385 (41.9%) had a biochemically confirmed diagnosis of PA. From the NHIS database, patients diagnosed with PA using ICD-10 codes E26 (hyperaldosteronism), I15.20 (hyperaldosteronism from adrenal adenoma), or I15.21 (hyperaldosteronism from bilateral adrenal hyperplasia) more than twice between January 2003 and December 2017 were selected. Among them, patients who received adrenalectomy (unilateral or bilateral) or a prescription of MRA for ≥ 180 days were considered PA. This approach maintained high sensitivity (93.5%), AUC 0.84, and positive predictive value 73.0% [13].

Patients younger than 40, with an ICD-10 code of E26.1 (secondary hyperaldosteronism) at least once, or with follow-up < 180 days were excluded. Patients with any previous dementia diagnosis before study entry were excluded. Those who underwent ADX were assigned to the ADX group; those prescribed MRA ≥ 180 days without ADX were assigned to the MRA group. Control (EH) group: patients with ICD-10 code I10 at least twice a year with antihypertensive drug prescriptions > 180 days, after excluding prior dementia. PA patients (n = 3,687) were matched with EH controls (n = 14,741) based on age, sex, and index year.

### Study outcomes

Primary outcomes were all-cause dementia events, including Alzheimer's disease (AD, ICD-10 F00 or G30), vascular dementia (VD, ICD-10 F01), or other dementia (ICD-10 F02 or F03) combined with a prescription of one or more dementia medications (donepezil, galantamine, memantine, or rivastigmine) [14, 15]. Each patient was followed from the index date (date of claim for ADX or MRA) to the earliest of any study outcome, death, or end of observation (December 31, 2017).

### Covariates

Income was categorized into three groups (low 30%, middle 40%, high 30%). Comorbidities (dyslipidemia, chronic kidney disease, AF, non-fatal stroke, non-fatal myocardial infarction) required relevant ICD-10 codes plus relevant prescription (> 30 accumulative days) before the index year. Concurrent medications (antihypertensive agents, statins, antithrombotic agents) were considered confounders.

### Statistical analysis

Multivariable Cox regression models were used for HRs and 95% CIs. Schoenfeld residuals confirmed no violation of proportionality. Model 1: adjusted for age, sex, income; Model 2: + baseline comorbidities; Model 3: + prescribed medications. Kaplan–Meier survival curves were compared using the log-rank test. Subgroup analyses were conducted for age (≥ 65 and < 65 years), sex, pre-existing cardiovascular diseases (AF, non-fatal stroke, non-fatal MI), and DM. Analyses used SAS 9.3 and R 4.0.1. Statistical significance: two-sided p < 0.05.

## Results

### Characteristics of study participants

PA (n = 3,687) and EH (n = 14,741) groups did not differ in mean age (56 ± 10 years) or proportion of men (47%). Compared to EH, PA had a higher prevalence of comorbidities, including DM (69% vs 40%), dyslipidemia (84% vs 61%), AF (11% vs 2%), non-fatal stroke (9% vs 3%), and myocardial infarction (14% vs 4%). Among PA, the MRA group was older (58.8 vs 51.9 years, p < 0.001), had more men (50.3% vs 42.9%, p < 0.001), and higher prevalence of DM, dyslipidemia, AF, non-fatal MI, and stroke than the ADX group.

### Risk of dementia in primary aldosteronism from treatment initiation

Follow-up ranged from 6 months to 15 years, median 5.2 years (IQR 2.7–8.7). All-cause dementia was newly diagnosed in 156 PA individuals (Total: 156/3687, 4.2%; ADX: 18/1339, 1.3%; MRA: 138/2348, 5.9%) and 522 EH individuals (522/14741, 3.5%). Incidence rate of all-cause dementia was higher in PA vs EH (7.45 vs 5.92/1000 person-years; unadjusted HR 1.26, 95% CI 1.05–1.51). Compared with EH:

- **MRA group** (n = 2348): higher risk of all-cause dementia (10.92/1000 person-years, unadjusted HR 1.88, 95% CI 1.56–2.27).
- **ADX group** (n = 1339): 65% lowered risk (2.16/1000 person-years; unadjusted HR 0.35, 95% CI 0.22–0.57).

After adjustment (Model 2): aHR 0.28 for ADX; aHR 1.30 for MRA. In the full model (Model 3): MRA remained associated with higher all-cause dementia (aHR 1.31, 95% CI 1.03–1.67); ADX lost significance (aHR 0.69, 95% CI 0.41–1.15). For Alzheimer's disease, association attenuated in the full model. For vascular dementia, elevated risk persisted in PA vs EH independent of confounders (Model 3; aHR 1.59, 95% CI 1.07–2.36), mainly driven by MRA (MRA aHR 1.62, 95% CI 1.08–2.45; ADX aHR 1.45, 95% CI 0.75–2.81).

### Risk of dementia in primary aldosteronism from hypertension diagnosis

Analyzed from time of initial hypertension diagnosis instead of treatment initiation: MRA group association with higher all-cause dementia (aHR 1.54 full model, p < 0.001) and VD (aHR 2.18, p < 0.001) remained robust. PA overall also associated with higher all-cause dementia (aHR 1.45, p < 0.001) and Alzheimer's disease (aHR 1.47, p < 0.001), primarily attributed to MRA (all-cause dementia aHR 1.54, p < 0.001; Alzheimer's disease aHR 1.55, p < 0.001). ADX group risk did not differ significantly from EH. (Supplementary Table S2)

### Subgroup analysis

The risk of all-cause dementia did not differ between ADX and EH groups across subgroups, with a beneficial effect of ADX in patients aged < 65 years vs ≥ 65 years (aHR 0.50, 95% CI 0.22–1.14 vs aHR 1.34, 95% CI 0.73–2.46, p for interaction = 0.039). For MRA-treated PA, a stronger association with increased all-cause dementia was observed in women (aHR 2.33 vs EH, 95% CI 1.79–3.03) than men (aHR 1.16, 95% CI 0.88–1.53, p for interaction = 0.001). MRA vs EH was associated with higher all-cause dementia in both older (aHR 1.45, 95% CI 1.16–1.82) and younger age groups (aHR 2.20, 95% CI 1.54–3.15; p for interaction = 0.070). Elevated risk in MRA group remained significant in individuals with DM (aHR 1.67, 95% CI 1.33–2.08) vs without DM (aHR 1.02, 95% CI 0.66–1.60), though interaction not statistically significant (p = 0.061).

## Discussion (summary)

Over a median follow-up of 5.2 years, the study suggested a potential association between PA and increased dementia risk vs EH, though not significant in all adjusted models. The ADX group had a lower or similar risk of all-cause dementia vs EH controls, whereas the MRA group had significantly elevated risk of all-cause dementia, particularly VD, even after full adjustment. The association remained robust whether analyzed from PA treatment initiation or from hypertension onset. Among the MRA group, women were more susceptible than men, with DM and age under 65 as additional potential risk factors.

Proposed mechanisms: PA carries higher cardiovascular risk than EH independent of blood pressure via genomic/non-genomic aldosterone effects (LV hypertrophy, myocardial fibrosis, endothelial dysfunction) [16, 17]. Aldosterone crosses the blood–brain barrier as a lipophilic hormone [20]; MR is expressed at various brain sites including the hippocampus and nucleus of the solitary tract [21]. RAAS activation reduces cerebral blood flow and increases oxidative stress [22]; local (brain) RAAS can cause end-organ damage independent of the systemic pathway [23, 24]. MR blockers (e.g., eplerenone) mitigated ischemic brain damage and reduced cerebral infarct size in animal models [28, 29] — so it is unlikely that MRA treatment itself is the direct cause of increased dementia risk. The MRA group has less favorable long-term outcomes than ADX (higher mortality, AF, CKD, higher post-treatment BP) [32–35]; suboptimal MR blockade or non-compliance may partly explain elevated dementia risk. Lower plasma renin activity and higher plasma aldosterone concentration were associated with higher white matter lesion load [10]. Longer hypertension duration relates to worse outcomes and reduced ADX benefit (PASO) [37], highlighting the importance of early PA diagnosis and treatment.

Sex differences: the increased hazard of all-cause dementia in MRA was more prominent in women, possibly related to estrogen's vasoprotective role [37] and stronger hypertension–white matter hyperintensity associations in women [41, 42]. Age/DM: younger ADX patients had lower dementia risk (removal of excess aldosterone source); co-existing hypertension and DM further increase dementia risk; spironolactone associated with poorer glucose metabolism than adrenalectomy [45].

### Limitations

Causal relationship cannot be established (retrospective design); residual confounding possible. Unavailable covariates included years of education, lifestyle factors (smoking, exercise), and APOE-ε4 genotype. Follow-up was relatively short given the younger mean age. Dementia outcomes were defined by claim datasets rather than cognitive assessment; subtype differentiation (AD, VD, Lewy Body) is inherently challenging, and mixed dementia complicates categorization. Accuracy of PA diagnosis was based on operational claim definitions without serum aldosterone or plasma renin activity data. No blood pressure data available, limiting comparison of BP elevation between PA and EH groups. Distinguishing prodromal dementia at baseline was challenging; NHIS-HEALS regulations precluded certain sensitivity analyses.

## Conclusions

The study provides potential evidence that dementia risk may be higher in patients with PA, especially the MRA-treated group, compared to EH. Females, younger age, and DM could be additional risk factors for incident dementia in the MRA group. All-cause dementia risk remained elevated vs EH even after PA treatment initiation, particularly in the MRA group. Long-term prospective studies are warranted.
