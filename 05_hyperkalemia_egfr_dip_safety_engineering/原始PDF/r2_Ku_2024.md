# Association between Acute Declines in eGFR during Renin-Angiotensin System Inhibition and Risk of Adverse Outcomes

**Ku E, Tighiouart H, McCulloch CE, Inker LA, Adingwupu OM, Greene T, Estacio RO, Woodward M, de Zeeuw D, Lewis JB, Hannedouche T, Hou FF, Jafar TH, Imai E, Remuzzi G, Heerspink HJL, Toto RD, Sarnak MJ.**
JASN 35: 1402–1411, October 2024. doi:10.1681/ASN.0000000000000426. PMID: 38889197.
Received: February 1, 2024. Accepted: June 12, 2024. Published Online Ahead of Print: June 18, 2024.

## Key Points

- Renin-angiotensin system inhibition was favorable for risk of kidney failure (compared with 0% decline with use of placebo or other agents) up to declines in eGFR of 13% over a 3-month period.
- Relation between eGFR decline after renin-angiotensin system inhibitor initiation and risk of outcomes was stronger in the first 2 years of follow-up and waned over time.

## Abstract

**Background.** Declines in GFR occur commonly when renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors are started. Our objective was to determine the relation between declines in eGFR during trials of RAS inhibition and kidney outcomes.

**Methods.** We included participants with CKD (eGFR <60 ml/min per 1.73 m²) from 17 trials of RAS inhibition. The exposure was subacute declines in eGFR expressed as % change between randomization and month 3, and in the subset of trials with data available, we also examined % change in eGFR between randomization and month 1. The primary outcome was kidney failure with replacement therapy. Cox proportional hazards models were used to examine the association between subacute declines in eGFR and risk of kidney failure. We used spline models to identify the threshold of change in eGFR below which RAS inhibition was favorable (conservatively comparing a given decline in eGFR with RAS inhibition to no decline in the comparator).

**Results.** A total of 11,800 individuals with mean eGFR 43 (SD 11) ml/min per 1.73 m² and median urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio of 362 mg/g (interquartile range, 50–1367) were included, and 1162 (10%) developed kidney failure. The threshold of decline in eGFR that favored the use of RAS inhibitors for kidney failure was estimated to be up to 13% (95% confidence interval, 8% to 17%) over a 3-month interval and up to 21% (95% confidence interval, 15% to 27%) over a 1-month interval after starting RAS inhibitors.

**Conclusions.** In patients treated with RAS inhibitors, ≤13% decline in eGFR over a 3-month period or ≤21% decline over a 1-month period was associated with lower risk of kidney failure compared with no decline in those assigned to placebo or other agents.

## Introduction

Renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors, such as angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) or angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), have been shown to reduce the risk of kidney disease progression and cardiovascular risk. Yet, the use of these agents is suboptimal in real-world clinical practice, potentially because many patients with CKD exhibit a (sub)acute drop in eGFR with RAS inhibitor initiation, which may alarm clinicians and patients. These declines in eGFR are often of sufficient magnitude that they would meet the criteria for AKI or acute kidney disease; however, because they are thought to be hemodynamic and benign, experts recommend tolerating changes in serum creatinine during the initiation of treatment with RAS inhibitors, although these recommendations are not always followed in real-world clinical practice.

The objective of this study was to pool individual-level data from 17 completed randomized controlled trials of RAS inhibition to understand the relation between short-term changes in eGFR that occurred in these trials and long-term kidney outcomes.

## Methods (summary)

- **Population:** 11,800 individuals with CKD (eGFR <60 ml/min per 1.73 m²) pooled from 17 randomized controlled trials of RAS inhibition versus placebo or other antihypertensive agents.
- **Exposure:** Subacute decline in eGFR expressed as % change between randomization and month 3 (all trials); and % change between randomization and month 1 in the subset with available data (N=3651).
- **Primary outcome:** Kidney failure with replacement therapy (dialysis or transplant). A composite of kidney failure or death was also examined.
- **Analysis:** Cox proportional hazards models; restricted spline models used to identify the threshold of eGFR change below which RAS inhibition was favorable, conservatively comparing a given decline in eGFR with RAS inhibition to 0% decline (no decline) in the comparator arm.

## Results (key figures)

- Baseline: mean eGFR 43 (SD 11) ml/min per 1.73 m²; median UACR 362 mg/g (IQR 50–1367).
- 1162 (10%) developed kidney failure events over follow-up.
- **3-month threshold:** Decline in eGFR favoring RAS inhibitors estimated up to 13% (95% CI, 8% to 17%). At the 13% decline threshold, the adjusted HR for risk of kidney failure over the 3-month interval was 1.02 (95% CI, 0.86 to 1.22) compared with no decline in the comparator arm.
- **1-month threshold:** Decline in eGFR favoring RAS inhibition estimated up to 21% (95% CI, 15% to 27%).
- For the composite outcome (kidney failure or death), the 3-month favorable threshold was estimated at up to 13% (95% CI, 7.5% to 16%).
- Within the comparator group, any decline in eGFR was associated with a higher hazard for kidney failure compared with those with no decline or an eGFR increase.
- The strength of the association between eGFR decline and subsequent kidney failure was attenuated when the interval was reduced from 3 months to 1 month, and was stronger during the first 2 years of follow-up, waning thereafter.

## Discussion (summary)

This is one of the largest pooled analyses of trial participants with CKD randomly assigned to RAS inhibitor versus alternative antihypertensive therapies or placebo, followed for kidney failure and death. Subacute declines in eGFR were more common in patients treated with RAS inhibition than in comparators. Using 0% decline in the comparator arm as the reference, the authors conservatively estimated thresholds up to 13% (3-month) and 21% (1-month) decline as favorable for RAS inhibitor initiation.

Current guidelines (largely opinion-based) suggest stopping RAS inhibitors when serum creatinine increases by >30% (equivalent to an approximately 27% decline in eGFR). The results — particularly the 1-month analyses — support the concept that RAS inhibition would likely remain beneficial to this level of decline, since the analysis presents a conservative scenario.

**Limitations:** Post hoc, observational analysis that does not preserve original randomization; residual confounding cannot be ruled out; results may not generalize to general nephrology clinic patients; cannot distinguish declines due to RAS inhibition versus natural CKD progression; some trials may have allowed ACEI/ARB dose reduction after acute eGFR declines (data unavailable). The study does not inform whether RAS inhibition should be stopped when subacute declines in eGFR occur.

## Conclusion

Up to a 13% decline in eGFR over a 3-month period or up to 21% decline over a 1-month period represents thresholds of decline in eGFR where the risk of kidney failure remains favorable with RAS inhibition. Data focused on 1-month declines in eGFR are more likely to reflect the hemodynamic changes related to the initiation of RAS inhibitors as opposed to a 3-month interval. The association between declines in eGFR after RAS inhibitor initiation and the risk of outcomes was stronger during the first 2 years of follow-up and waned over time.
