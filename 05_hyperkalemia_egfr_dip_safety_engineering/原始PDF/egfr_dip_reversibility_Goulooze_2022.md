# Dose–Exposure–Response Analysis of the Nonsteroidal Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist Finerenone on UACR and eGFR: An Analysis from FIDELIO-DKD

## Abstract

**Background and Objective:** Finerenone reduces the risk of kidney failure in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes. Changes in the urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) are surrogates for kidney failure. The authors performed dose–exposure–response analyses to determine the effects of finerenone on these surrogates in the presence and absence of sodium glucose co-transporter-2 inhibitors (SGLT2is) using individual patient data from the FIDELIO-DKD study.

**Methods:** Non-linear mixed-effects population pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) models were used to quantify disease progression in terms of UACR and eGFR during standard of care and pharmacodynamic effects of finerenone in the presence and absence of SGLT2i use.

**Results:** The population PK/PD models adequately described effects of finerenone exposure in reducing UACR and slowing eGFR decline over time. The reduction in UACR achieved with finerenone during the first year predicted its subsequent effect in slowing progressive eGFR decline. SGLT2i use did not modify the effects of finerenone. The model demonstrated with 97.5% confidence that finerenone was at least 94.1% as efficacious in reducing UACR in patients using an SGLT2i compared with patients not using an SGLT2i (95% CI of SGLT2i–finerenone interaction: 94.1 to 122%). The 95% CI of the SGLT2i–finerenone interaction for the UACR-mediated effect on chronic eGFR decline was 9.5–144%.

**Conclusions:** The authors developed a model that accurately describes the finerenone dose–exposure–response relationship for UACR and eGFR. The model demonstrated that the early UACR effect of finerenone predicted its long-term effect on eGFR decline. These effects were independent of concomitant SGLT2i use.

---

## Key Points

- Finerenone is a novel, selective, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) that demonstrated efficacy in delaying CKD progression and reducing cardiovascular events in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes in the pivotal FIDELIO-DKD study.
- In this secondary model-based analysis, the dose–exposure–response relationship for UACR and eGFR was accurately quantified.
- The early treatment effect of finerenone on UACR predicted its long-term effect on eGFR, supporting UACR as a surrogate.
- The relationship between finerenone exposure and UACR/eGFR effects was not modified by SGLT2i use and demonstrated independent and additive effects.

## 1. Introduction

Blood pressure–lowering treatment with ACE inhibitors and ARBs has been the mainstay of CKD therapy for two decades, but kidney failure risk remains high. SGLT2is reduce the risk of kidney failure and slow eGFR decline (demonstrated in CREDENCE, confirmed/extended by DAPA-CKD). MRAs reduce albuminuria in CKD, but trials establishing effects on major kidney outcomes were lacking until FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993), which demonstrated that finerenone reduces the risk of a composite kidney outcome in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes.

In FIDELIO-DKD, 5734 patients were randomised 1:1 to finerenone (10 or 20 mg once daily [OD]) or placebo, added to standard of care including optimised ACE inhibitors or ARBs. This analysis investigated the relationship of finerenone exposure with UACR and eGFR response, whether reduced UACR during finerenone treatment was associated with changes in rate of eGFR decline, and whether concomitant SGLT2i use modified the exposure–response relationship.

## 2. Methods

**Clinical study:** FIDELIO-DKD was a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase III study of finerenone added to standard of care with maximally tolerated labeled doses of a renin–angiotensin system inhibitor. Starting dose depended on screening eGFR: finerenone 10 mg/day (or placebo) if eGFR 25 to <60 mL/min/1.73 m²; 20 mg/day if eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73 m². Patients could be up-/down-titrated per changes in eGFR and serum potassium. Primary endpoint: composite of (1) time to first kidney failure, (2) sustained eGFR decrease ≥40% from baseline over ≥4 weeks, or (3) renal death. Plasma finerenone concentrations were determined using a sparse sampling approach; post-hoc PK parameters from a published population PK model provided individual exposures.

**Model development:** UACR and eGFR disease progression models were first developed using placebo-arm data (additive, proportional, and for eGFR exponential progression explored; covariates added via stepwise forward inclusion, α = 0.01). Disease progression parameters were then fixed and active-arm data added to develop the PD model relating daily exposure (AUCτ,md) to UACR and eGFR effects. For eGFR, two effect types were explored: an 'acute' eGFR-declining effect and a modification of 'chronic' eGFR decline. Direct/indirect effects were tested with linear, log-linear, power, and Emax characteristics. Backward deletion used α = 0.001.

**Model integration:** To evaluate how far the long-term eGFR-decline effect could be predicted by the UACR effect, individual post-hoc UACR predictions replaced baseline UACR in the eGFR model. Whether finerenone's chronic eGFR-decline effect was fully characterised via UACR was tested by adding non-UACR-mediated finerenone effects on eGFR decline.

**SGLT2i integration:** SGLT2i use was a time-varying binary variable (use/no use in last 5 days), integrated last. Direct effects and effects on rate of biomarker change were explored, including instantaneous onset/offset and estimated time-to-steady-state models. Finally, whether SGLT2i modified the finerenone effect was tested.

**Computation:** NONMEM v7.4.3, first-order conditional estimation (final eGFR model re-run with SAEM plus importance-sampling expectation step for stability).

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Clinical Data
The analysis included **5674 patients** (full analysis set) with **37,296 UACR** and **78,132 eGFR** observations. **528 patients** used SGLT2is at any time during the treatment period.

**Baseline covariates (median, IQR)** across four groups — Placebo no-SGLT2i (N=2553), Placebo SGLT2i (N=288), Finerenone no-SGLT2i (N=2593), Finerenone SGLT2i (N=240):
- Baseline eGFR: 42.1 / 48.2 / 42.3 / 51.3 mL/min/1.73 m²
- Baseline UACR: 886.8 / 713.8 / 858.5 / 661.3 mg/g
- Baseline serum potassium: 4.4 / 4.3 / 4.4 / 4.3 mmol/L
- Age: 67.0 / 63.0 / 66.0 / 65 years

**UACR:** In the placebo arm, median change from baseline UACR increased over time (from −1.4% at month 4 to +17.1% at year 3). In the active arm, a direct sustained finerenone effect on UACR was observed; maximum reduction achieved after 1 year (−35.1% change from baseline), followed by slight increases to −20.4% at 3 years.

**eGFR:** In the finerenone group, an initial median **3.3 mL/min/1.73 m² decline** in eGFR after 4 months (vs median 0.8 mL/min/1.73 m² decline in placebo). By year 2 the placebo median eGFR was slightly below the finerenone median, indicating the curves cross again within 2 years.

### 3.2 UACR Model
Rate of UACR change over time was largely proportional to current UACR; the progression rate was adjusted based on model-predicted UACR and UACR over baseline. The finerenone effect on UACR was characterised by a power function and an effect compartment equilibrating with exposure. Final-model covariates: baseline UACR and eGFR, hepatic impairment, age, and ethnicity. A more pronounced UACR reduction occurred with 20 mg OD vs 10 mg, but the additional reduction was **subproportional** (effect saturates toward the higher dose).

### 3.3 eGFR Model
Rate of eGFR decline was an absolute change over time (constant chronic slope, independent of current eGFR); the model contains an exponential function reducing the decline rate to 0 as eGFR approaches a **stabilisation point estimated at 16.2 mL/min/1.73 m²**.

Two finerenone treatment effects were identified: **(i) an acute eGFR-decreasing effect** (power function + effect compartment equilibrating with exposure) and **(ii) a superimposed flattening of the chronic slope**. Consistent with a prior ARTS-DN phase IIb analysis, a model assuming **full reversibility of the acute eGFR decline** described the data well, including after finerenone discontinuation.

**Rebound after discontinuation:** Model-free analyses correcting for standard-of-care eGFR decline demonstrated an eGFR **increase (rebound) of 6.9% (95% CI +3.9% to +10.0%)** — compared with a simulated model-based acute decline for finerenone 20 mg OD of 5.4% — in subjects discontinuing treatment (median time between treatment start and discontinuation 1.8 years [5th–95th percentiles: 0.4 to 3.6 years]) with an eGFR follow-up assessment within 90 days after discontinuation.

### 3.4 UACR as a Predictor of eGFR
Model-predicted individual UACR over time was directly linked to the chronic eGFR slope and strongly improved the fit (p < 1e−16) vs models using observed baseline UACR. Longitudinal UACR characterised eGFR decline on both placebo and finerenone. No additional UACR-independent finerenone effect on chronic eGFR slope could be identified, suggesting the long-term eGFR benefit is completely characterised by finerenone's immediate UACR effect.

Data-based visualisation (Fig. 4): with baseline UACR as the only predictor, active treatment had significantly reduced eGFR decline vs placebo (p = 3.88e−05); but with UACR at year 1 as predictor, study treatment was not a significant predictor of eGFR change between year 1 and year 3 (p = 0.075). Thus the attenuated chronic eGFR decline on finerenone (year 1–3) is mediated via its UACR reduction during year 1.

Final eGFR-model covariates: baseline eGFR and serum potassium, hepatic impairment, and ethnicity, plus covariates influencing UACR and propagating to eGFR.

### 3.5 Impact of Concomitant SGLT2i Use

**UACR:** SGLT2i effect modelled as a direct proportional effect on UACR. No significant effect of SGLT2is on the finerenone effect (independent effects). The 95% CI of the interaction effect (94.1–122%) suggests with 97.5% confidence that finerenone is at least 94.1% as efficacious in reducing UACR in patients currently using an SGLT2i vs not.

**eGFR:** Acute reversible eGFR decline with SGLT2i modelled as a direct effect. For SGLT2is, an additional UACR-independent treatment effect on the chronic eGFR slope was identified (unlike finerenone). No significant effect of current SGLT2is on the acute eGFR-decreasing effect of finerenone (95% CI 73.2–120%) nor on its UACR-mediated effect on chronic eGFR slope (95% CI 9.5–144%), suggesting independence.

## 4. Discussion

**Acute reversible decline and sustainable chronic improvement of eGFR:** Renal function decline is characterised by a largely constant eGFR decline rate over time (linear chronic slope modifiable with treatment). Finerenone-treated patients show a **biphasic pattern**: an acute decline as a one-time offset within the first weeks, plus sustained attenuation of the chronic decline slope with benefits increasing over time that eventually outweigh acute effects. The acute decline was reversible and is generally considered to represent **hemodynamic effects**, where even an eGFR decline is considered beneficial for the kidney rather than representing disease progression.

**Short-term UACR effects as a predictor of chronic eGFR benefits:** A single mathematical function characterised the UACR–chronic-eGFR-decline relationship regardless of placebo vs finerenone. Adding a UACR-independent drug effect did not improve the eGFR model, suggesting finerenone's eGFR effect is attributable largely to its UACR effect. The authors caution this does not prove a causal association (UACR may simply be the more sensitive parameter), but supports UACR as a surrogate and early response biomarker.

**Impact of patient characteristics:** The simulated effect of finerenone 20 mg OD on the chronic eGFR slope ranged from −22.1% (high baseline UACR 3366 mg/g) to −41.3% (low baseline eGFR 26.7 mL/min/1.73 m²) relative to placebo. Patients with high baseline UACR have the lowest proportional finerenone effect on chronic eGFR decline, but because of their high baseline rate of eGFR decline, it takes less time before the beneficial effect outweighs the acute eGFR-decreasing effect. The opposite holds for low-UACR patients.

**Additive effects of finerenone and SGLT2is:** Both showed strong, highly significant effects on UACR and eGFR. SGLT2i quantitative effect sizes should be interpreted with caution (trial not randomised for SGLT2i use; potential selection bias). Concomitant SGLT2i use did not statistically significantly modify the finerenone treatment effect — indicating independent and additive effects. Supported by preclinical data (finerenone + empagliflozin combination showed early, sustained, over-additive proteinuria reduction). Combined treatment simulations show larger UACR reductions, more pronounced acute eGFR decline, and further attenuated chronic eGFR slope.

## 5. Conclusions

The finerenone dose–exposure–response analyses for UACR and eGFR based on FIDELIO-DKD provide detailed insights into surrogate-quality biomarkers supporting finerenone's benefits in CKD with type 2 diabetes. Treatment effects on UACR are predictive of finerenone effects on chronic eGFR decline. The analyses demonstrate additive benefits of SGLT2i use in addition to finerenone for UACR and eGFR.

## Key relevance to eGFR-dip reversibility / safety engineering

- **Acute eGFR dip is a hemodynamic, fully reversible effect.** A model assuming full reversibility of the acute eGFR decline described the data well, including post-discontinuation data (consistent with ARTS-DN phase IIb).
- **Quantified rebound:** eGFR increase of **6.9% (95% CI +3.9% to +10.0%)** after discontinuation vs a simulated model-based acute decline of 5.4% for finerenone 20 mg OD.
- **Magnitude of acute dip:** median 3.3 mL/min/1.73 m² decline at month 4 (vs 0.8 in placebo); curves cross again within 2 years.
- **Biphasic pattern:** acute one-time offset + sustained chronic-slope attenuation whose benefit grows over time and outweighs the initial dip.
- **eGFR stabilisation point** estimated at 16.2 mL/min/1.73 m².
