# Comparative efficacy and safety of finerenone in diabetic kidney disease: a meta-analysis of Asian and non-Asian populations

*Raza et al. BMC Nephrology (2025) 26:676. https://doi.org/10.1186/s12882-025-04526-0*

## Abstract

**Background & objective**: Diabetic kidney disease (DKD) is a major global burden, especially in Asia. This study aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of finerenone in diabetic kidney disease, comparing outcomes between Asian and non-Asian populations through a systematic review and meta-analysis.

**Methods**: A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Cochrane Library, ClinicalTrials.gov, Google Scholar, and the Undermind AI platform from inception through March 2025. Studies included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and subgroup analyses that evaluated finerenone in DKD patients. Primary outcomes included a reduction in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) and a decline in the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of ≥40%. Secondary outcomes included cardiovascular events, mortality due to kidney failure, hyperkalemia (serum potassium >5.0 mmol/L), treatment discontinuation, hospitalization, and adverse event–related mortality. Risk ratios (RRs) and mean differences (MDs) were pooled using a random-effects model, and subgroup analyses were performed by ethnicity.

**Results**: Five eligible studies, comprising 8,763 participants, were included in this analysis. Finerenone significantly reduced UACR compared with placebo (MD = −0.38, 95% CI −0.42 to −0.35; p < 0.001), with consistent effects across Asian and non-Asian populations (subgroup p = 0.28). It also significantly reduced the risk of eGFR decline ≥40% (MD = −0.24 [−0.40, −0.09]; p = 0.002), with a greater benefit in the Asian subgroup (subgroup p = 0.03). Cardiovascular event risk was also reduced (RR = 0.85 [0.77–0.95]; p = 0.004), while mortality due to kidney failure showed a non-significant reduction (RR = 0.83 [0.64–1.07]; p = 0.15). Hyperkalemia risk was higher with finerenone (RR = 1.73, 95% CI 1.39–2.14), whereas adverse event–related mortality was lower (RR = 0.65, 95% CI 0.46–0.91).

**Conclusion**: Finerenone provides robust renoprotective and cardioprotective effects in DKD, with broadly consistent efficacy across Asian and non-Asian populations. A greater renal benefit was observed in Asians for eGFR decline ≥40%, though this requires cautious interpretation. Hyperkalemia risk was increased but largely manageable. These findings support integration into DKD therapy and highlight the need for ethnically inclusive, long-term, real-world trials.

**Keywords**: Finerenone; Diabetic kidney disease (DKD); Hyperkalemia; Urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR); estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR); Cardiovascular events; Asian population; Meta-analysis

## Introduction

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is one of the most severe and prevalent complications of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM), commonly referred to as diabetic kidney disease (DKD). Globally, approximately 30–40% of individuals with T2DM develop CKD during their lifetime, positioning diabetes as the leading cause of end-stage renal disease (ESRD) in many parts of the world. Between 1990 and 2017, the incidence of DKD rose by 74%. Asia bears the heaviest burden of diabetes-related CKD: nearly 31% of individuals with diabetes in China present with CKD, while Japan reports a DKD prevalence of approximately 25% to 27% among diabetic patients. India is projected to exceed 130 million diabetes cases by 2045. Asian populations often develop CKD at younger ages and lower body weights than their Western counterparts.

Traditionally, DKD management has emphasized glycemic control and blood pressure management, with RAAS blockade using ACE inhibitors or ARBs as the standard of care. Intensification of RAAS blockade has been unsuccessful due to adverse events, particularly hyperkalemia and acute kidney injury. SGLT2 inhibitors reduce albuminuria and slow GFR decline while also reducing cardiovascular risk, but many patients continue to experience progressive kidney function loss. Finerenone, a selective nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA), offers a novel mechanism of reno-cardiovascular protection. Unlike steroidal MRAs such as spironolactone, finerenone exhibits higher selectivity for the mineralocorticoid receptor and reduced hormonal side effects. It inhibits aldosterone-induced pro-inflammatory and pro-fibrotic pathways.

The pivotal FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials demonstrated finerenone's efficacy in reducing the risk of kidney disease progression and cardiovascular events in patients with T2DM and CKD already receiving optimized RAAS blockade. The FIDELITY meta-analysis confirmed that these benefits extend across different stages of CKD. KDIGO guidelines now recommend finerenone as an adjunctive therapy in T2DM patients with CKD and albuminuria.

Despite these advancements, questions remain regarding finerenone's efficacy and safety across ethnically diverse populations, particularly among Asians, who have historically been underrepresented in global trials. These populations may present unique challenges, such as higher baseline albuminuria, distinct dietary habits (e.g., higher potassium intake), and greater predisposition to hyperkalemia. A subgroup analysis of Asian participants from FIDELIO-DKD revealed consistent renoprotective benefits but a slightly higher rate of hyperkalemia, particularly among those with a baseline serum potassium level of ≥4.8 mmol/L. Few studies have focused exclusively on South Asian populations (India and Pakistan).

## Methodology

### Study objectives
This meta-analysis aimed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of finerenone in managing CKD in patients with type 2 diabetes, with a specific focus on comparisons between Asian and non-Asian populations. Primary objectives: assess effectiveness in reducing UACR; evaluate impact on reducing CKD progression by assessing eGFR decline; determine the incidence and severity of hyperkalemia and other adverse events.

### Search strategy
Comprehensive literature search in PubMed, the Cochrane Library, ClinicalTrials.gov, and Google Scholar up to March 2025, plus screening of the Undermind AI tool. Only peer-reviewed articles from standard databases were included in the final analysis. Conducted in accordance with PRISMA 2020 guidelines.

### Eligibility criteria
Included studies: (1) enrolled adult patients diagnosed with DKD; (2) evaluated finerenone administered alongside standard care (e.g., RAS inhibitors); (3) included a comparator group receiving placebo with standard care; (4) reported at least one relevant clinical outcome (UACR, eGFR, cardiovascular events, or safety endpoints such as hyperkalemia, treatment discontinuation, adverse event–related mortality); (5) were RCTs or post hoc subgroup analyses of RCTs published in peer-reviewed journals. Participants were categorized as Asian or non-Asian (non-Asian encompassed White, Black, and Hispanic participants). Non-English articles were excluded.

### Study screening and selection
Records imported into Rayyan AI. Two reviewers (A.H. and F.Q.) independently screened titles and abstracts; disagreements resolved by a third reviewer (U.Y.). Five studies were included—comprising RCTs and subgroup analyses—reporting separate data for Asian and non-Asian subgroups: broader Asian and non-Asian cohorts [Koya 19], China [Zhang 20], Japan [Katayama 21], Black population [Flack 23], and Hispanic population [Rosas 24].

### Data extraction and quality assessment
Two reviewers (M.M. and S.A.R.) independently extracted data. Primary outcome: UACR reduction. Risk of bias assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2.0 tool for RCTs.

### Statistical analysis
Review Manager (RevMan) version 5.4. RRs and MDs with 95% CIs pooled using the Mantel–Haenszel method with a random-effects model. Subgroup analyses compared Asian versus non-Asian populations. Heterogeneity assessed using Cochran's Q (p < 0.10) and quantified with I². Publication bias assessed visually using funnel plots.

## Results

The systematic search identified 683 records. After removing duplicates and screening, five RCTs and subgroup analyses were included, comprising 8,763 patients with DKD, representing six distinct subgroups: one each from Japan, China, and a broader Asian cohort, and three from non-Asian populations (Black, Hispanic, and other global regions). The intervention arm (finerenone plus standard care) included 4,423 patients; the control arm (placebo plus standard care) included 4,340 patients. Median participant age ranged from 60 to 66 years, with follow-up durations between 3 and 34 months. Risk of bias assessment (ROB 2.0) indicated some concerns in three studies, primarily related to missing outcome data and selective reporting. Funnel plots revealed no major asymmetry (low risk of publication bias).

### Efficacy outcomes

**UACR**: Finerenone significantly reduced UACR compared with placebo. Pooled MD = −0.38 [95% CI: −0.42 to −0.35]; p < 0.001, with no heterogeneity (I² = 0%). Subgroup analyses showed consistent benefits in both Asian (MD = −0.36 [−0.41 to −0.31]) and non-Asian populations (MD = −0.40 [−0.45 to −0.35]). Test for subgroup differences not significant (p = 0.28; I² = 15.2%). (Fig. 3A)

**eGFR Decline ≥40%**: Finerenone significantly reduced the risk of eGFR decline ≥40% versus placebo, pooled MD = −0.24 [95% CI: −0.40 to −0.09]; p = 0.002. Subgroup analyses demonstrated a stronger protective effect in Asian patients (MD = −0.40 [−0.59 to −0.20]; p < 0.001) compared to non-Asian patients (MD = −0.13 [−0.26 to −0.01]; p = 0.04). Subgroup difference reached statistical significance (p = 0.03), suggesting Asian populations may derive greater renal benefit. (Fig. 4A)

**Mortality due to kidney failure**: Non-significant trend toward reduced mortality (RR = 0.83 [95% CI: 0.64–1.07]; p = 0.15). Heterogeneity absent (I² = 0%); subgroup interaction not significant (p = 0.75). (Fig. 5A)

**Cardiovascular outcomes**: Finerenone significantly reduced overall risk of cardiovascular events (RR = 0.85 [95% CI: 0.77–0.95]; p = 0.004). Subgroup analyses: no significant effect in Asian patients (RR = 0.84 [95% CI: 0.62–1.13]; p = 0.25) but significant risk reduction in non-Asian patients (RR = 0.86 [95% CI: 0.76–0.96]; p = 0.008). Heterogeneity absent overall and within subgroups (I² = 0%); subgroup difference not significant (p = 0.91). (Fig. 6A)

### Safety outcomes

Safety analyses highlighted an increased risk of hyperkalemia and related adverse outcomes, but severe events were infrequent and generally manageable.

**Overall Hyperkalemia**: Finerenone significantly increased the risk of hyperkalemia versus placebo (RR = 1.73 [95% CI: 1.39–2.14]; p < 0.001). Subgroup analyses: elevated risk in both Asians (RR = 1.53 [1.25–1.86]; I² = 0%) and non-Asians (RR = 2.11 [1.28–3.48]; I² = 67%), with no statistically significant subgroup difference (p = 0.24). Non-Asian populations exhibited numerically higher relative risks, but formal tests for subgroup differences did not reach significance, suggesting ethnicity may not be a strong independent modifier of risk. Overall heterogeneity moderate (I² = 40%). Sensitivity analyses (sequentially excluding individual studies) confirmed robustness. (Fig. 7A)

**Moderate and Severe Hyperkalemia**: Finerenone more than doubled the risk of moderate hyperkalemia (>5.5 mmol/L) (RR = 2.20 [1.97–2.46]; p < 0.001; I² = 0%), with consistent effects across subgroups. Severe hyperkalemia (>6.0 mmol/L) risk was nearly tripled (RR = 3.00 [2.25–4.00]; p < 0.001), also without heterogeneity (I² = 0%). (Fig. 8a/8b)

**Hyperkalemia-Related Treatment Discontinuation and Hospitalization**: Treatment discontinuation due to hyperkalemia was significantly higher with finerenone, pooled RR = 2.61 [1.77 to 3.85] (p < 0.001), consistent across Asian and non-Asian subgroups (I² = 0%, subgroup p = 0.66). Risk of hospitalization due to hyperkalemia was significantly elevated (RR = 3.30 [1.49 to 7.29], p = 0.003), though overall event rates remained low. Minimal heterogeneity (I² = 8%); no evidence of subgroup effect. (Fig. 9a/9b)

**Adverse events leading to death**: Finerenone significantly reduced the risk of adverse event–related mortality (RR = 0.65 [95% CI: 0.46–0.91]; p = 0.01). Heterogeneity absent (I² = 0%); subgroup interaction not significant (p = 0.46). (Fig. 10A)

## Discussion

This meta-analysis reinforces finerenone's role as a dual-action therapy that significantly improves renal and cardiovascular outcomes in DKD patients, while highlighting the importance of vigilant monitoring due to elevated hyperkalemia risk.

The pooled UACR reduction of −0.38 (p < 0.001) aligns with prior FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD findings (~30–40% decrease from baseline). Finerenone attenuated eGFR decline (pooled MD −0.24; 95% CI −0.40 to −0.09). Although reduction in mortality due to kidney failure did not reach significance (RR = 0.83), the trend favored finerenone, in agreement with the FIDELITY pooled analysis.

Cardiovascular benefits: finerenone reduced cardiovascular events by 15% (RR = 0.85), aligning with earlier 13–14% reductions. In terms of safety, pooled data confirmed increased hyperkalemia risk (RR = 1.85 as stated in Discussion), particularly at serum potassium >5.5 mmol/L and >6.0 mmol/L. Severe hyperkalemia events remained rare, and adverse event–related deaths were significantly lower with finerenone (RR = 0.65).

### Subgroup analyses by ethnicity

**UACR**: Robust antiproteinuric effect across both ethnic groups, no significant subgroup difference (p = 0.28); pooled MD −0.38 (95% CI −0.42 to −0.35), aligning with ~32% UACR reduction in Hispanic patients and ~40% reduction in Black patients.

**eGFR Decline ≥40%**: Statistically significant subgroup difference (p = 0.03), with Asians experiencing greater protective effect (MD = −0.40 [95% CI −0.59, −0.20]) versus non-Asians (−0.13 [95% CI −0.26, −0.01]). Supports the FIDELIO-DKD post hoc analysis by Koya et al. (30% reduction in renal outcomes in Asians, HR 0.70, vs ~12% in non-Asians, HR 0.88). May partly reflect baseline imbalances (proteinuria burden, RAAS inhibitor dosing, dietary potassium intake). Biological mechanisms (pharmacogenomic variation e.g. CYP450 or MR polymorphisms, heightened aldosterone sensitivity, differences in sodium handling) may also contribute.

**Kidney disease-related mortality**: Did not reach significance in either subgroup, direction consistently favored finerenone (RR < 1); no heterogeneity (I² = 0%; subgroup p = 0.75).

**Cardiovascular events**: Significant reduction among non-Asians (RR = 0.86, p = 0.008); non-significant in Asians (RR = 0.84, p = 0.25); subgroup difference not significant (p = 0.91). Aligns with FIDELIO/FIGARO-DKD HRs of 0.87–0.88.

**Hyperkalemia**: Increased in both groups, slightly higher in non-Asians (RR = 2.11) vs Asians (RR = 1.53). Subgroup difference approached but did not reach significance (p = 0.24). Risks of moderate/severe hyperkalemia and hyperkalemia-related discontinuation/hospitalization consistent across subgroups (p > 0.05 for all). Lack of statistical interaction suggests ethnicity is unlikely to be a strong independent modifier of risk; apparent differences may reflect unmeasured factors (baseline dietary potassium intake, background RAAS inhibitor use, trial-specific discontinuation thresholds). Severe hyperkalemia events were uncommon and generally manageable.

### Limitations

1. Most included studies were subgroup or post hoc analyses of the pivotal FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials (risk of reporting bias).
2. Baseline imbalances (proteinuria burden, RAAS inhibitor dosing, dietary potassium) may have influenced subgroup differences in eGFR decline; formal meta-regression not feasible due to incomplete/inconsistent data.
3. Classifying participants as "Asian" vs "non-Asian" obscures heterogeneity; South Asians (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh) remain underrepresented.
4. Biological mechanisms for stronger renal benefit in Asians remain speculative.
5. Higher hyperkalemia risk in non-Asians may reflect unmeasured confounders.
6. Follow-up durations were relatively short (median 2.5–3 years), limiting assessment of long-term outcomes (ESRD, all-cause mortality).
7. Most studies conducted before widespread uptake of SGLT2 inhibitors, restricting applicability to current "triple therapy" regimens.
8. Small number of included studies limits reliability of publication bias assessments.

Finerenone should be considered within the broader 'triple therapy' paradigm of RAAS blockade, SGLT2 inhibition, and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonism, with real-world evidence and prospective trials needed to clarify safety and additive efficacy.

### Clinical relevance and future directions

Findings reinforce finerenone as an effective therapy for DKD, offering consistent renoprotective (albuminuria reduction, slower eGFR decline) and cardioprotective (fewer cardiovascular events) benefits across Asian and non-Asian populations. Renal benefit appeared more pronounced in Asians (may be partly explained by baseline differences). Hyperkalemia risk increased in both groups but generally manageable with careful monitoring, severe outcomes rare. Long-term effects on ESRD progression and all-cause mortality remain uncertain. Integration of finerenone into "triple therapy" regimens warrants urgent real-world evaluation. Expanding inclusion of South Asian populations is especially critical.

## Conclusion

This meta-analysis demonstrates that finerenone provides clinically meaningful renoprotective and cardioprotective effects in patients with DKD. Subgroup analyses showed broadly consistent efficacy across Asian and non-Asian populations, with a statistically significant greater renal benefit in Asians for the outcome of eGFR decline ≥40%. For albuminuria reduction and cardiovascular outcomes, effects were directionally similar across ethnicities with no significant subgroup interaction. Hyperkalemia risk was increased in both populations, with numerically higher relative risks in non-Asians; however, subgroup tests did not reach statistical significance, and severe adverse outcomes remained infrequent and manageable. Observed ethnic differences should be interpreted cautiously given incomplete baseline data, relatively short follow-up, and reliance on post hoc subgroup analyses. Future ethnically inclusive trials, particularly in South Asian populations, are needed.
