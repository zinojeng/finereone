# Real-World Impact of Finerenone on Albuminuria in Patients with Diabetes and CKD

**Marina López-Martínez, Juan León-Román, Ehimy Suárez, Sara Nuñez-Delgado, María Antonieta Azancot, Jorge Iván Zamora-Carrillo, Marc Patricio-Liébana, Alexander Sánchez Olaya, Irene Agraz, Sheila Bermejo, Laia Sans, Nestor Toapanta, Natalia Ramos, María José Soler**

*Int. J. Mol. Sci.* 2025, 26, 11584. https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms262311584
Received 4 September 2025; Revised 4 November 2025; Accepted 7 November 2025; Published 29 November 2025. Open access, CC BY 4.0.

## Abstract

Patients with diabetes and chronic kidney disease (CKD-DM) often have residual albuminuria despite pharmacological treatment. Finerenone targets mineralocorticoid overactivation, but real-world evidence remains limited. This study evaluated the impact of finerenone in a cohort of patients with CKD-DM. This was a real-life study including patients with CKD-DM and an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥ 20 mL/min/1.73 m², treated with finerenone, aged ≥ 18 years, followed at the Nephrology Department of Vall d'Hebron Hospital. Clinical and laboratory data were collected at baseline and at 1, 3, and 6 months of treatment. Changes in albuminuria and eGFR were analyzed in patients who completed 6 months of follow-up. A total of 60 patients were included; 39 (65%) were male, median age 79 ± 10.12 years. Finerenone was initiated at 10 mg daily in 57 patients (95%), with 38.3% escalating to 20 mg after 1 month. After 6 months, the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) decreased by 37.1% (p = 0.012, n = 34). Patients with an initial eGFR drop > 20% showed a greater UACR decrease of around 43% at 3 (p = 0.012) and 6 months (p = 0.013). A significant 9.5% decline in eGFR was observed at 1 month, followed by stabilization at 3 and 6 months. Finerenone was discontinued in 10% of patients due to adverse events. Hyperkalemia occurred in 18.3% of patients, but no hospitalizations for adverse events or heart failure were reported. In summary, finerenone induced a significant 37.1% reduction in albuminuria after 6 months; this reduction was more pronounced in patients who experienced an initial eGFR dip ≥ 20%. Finerenone was generally well tolerated and appears to be a promising therapeutic strategy for reducing albuminuria in this population.

**Keywords:** albuminuria; chronic kidney disease; diabetes; finerenone; hyperkalemia

---

## 1. Introduction (key points)

- WHO: 332 million people living with diabetes and CKD in 2022; both diseases cause over 2 million deaths/year. Around 40% of patients with diabetes develop CKD.
- CKD-DM pathophysiology involves hypertension, altered tubuloglomerular feedback, and mineralocorticoid (MR) overactivation, causing inflammation, oxidative stress, hyperfiltration, and CKD.
- Albuminuria is a key determinant of CKD progression and a recognized cardiovascular risk factor; increased mortality risk begins at 10 mg/g albuminuria and rises ~4-fold at 1 g/g.
- Finerenone is the most recently approved drug for CKD-DM; its high MR selectivity makes it more effective at reducing albuminuria than classical MR antagonists (eplerenone, spironolactone). The FIDELITY analysis (pooled FIGARO + FIDELIO) demonstrated significant reduction in composite kidney outcome; finerenone achieved 30-40% albuminuria reduction after 3-4 months.
- Finerenone was approved/commercialized in Spain in May 2024. Real-world data remain scarce.
- Study aim: evaluate tolerability of finerenone after initiation in a real-world CKD-DM cohort and its short-term effects on renal function and albuminuria over 6-month follow-up.

## 2. Results

### 2.1. Baseline Characteristics

A total of 60 patients who initiated finerenone met all inclusion criteria. Follow-up data were available for all patients at 1 month, for 41 (68.3%) at 3 months, and for 34 (56.7%) at 6 months. The declining number across visits reflected timing of finerenone initiation relative to study closure; no patients were lost to follow-up or excluded after baseline.

**Whole cohort (n = 60):** All had diabetes; median HbA1c 7.2% [6.4-7.6]; 39 (65%) male; mean age 79 ± 10.12 years; median eGFR 49.49 [33.5-60.5] mL/min/1.73 m²; mean potassium 4.54 ± 0.49 mmol/L; median albuminuria 479 [189.5-1199.5] mg/g. Main comorbidities: hypertension (98.3%), dyslipidemia (90%), obesity (38.8%); 96.7% met metabolic syndrome criteria.

**6-month subgroup (n = 34):** mean age 69.15 ± 8.16 years; 67.6% male; median eGFR 42 [27.75-58.25] mL/min/1.73 m²; albuminuria 485 [231.75-1118.50] mg/g.

### 2.2. Renoprotective Treatment During the Study / Dosing

At baseline: 55 patients (91.7%) on RASi, 51 (85%) on SGLT2i, 26 (43.3%) on GLP-1RA. Finerenone initiated at 10 mg once daily in 57 patients (95%). Uptitrated to 20 mg in 23 patients (38.3%) at 1-month visit (41.7% total on 20 mg). At 3 months, 42 patients assessed; two more escalated to 20 mg → 47.6% on 20 mg. Among the 34 patients with 6-month follow-up, dose escalated in 3 more → 50% receiving 20 mg by end of follow-up.

**Figure 1 — Finerenone dosing (10 mg / 20 mg / discontinuation):**
- Baseline: 95% on 10 mg, 5% (already) discontinued category shown as 5%
- 1 month: 53.3% (10 mg), 41.7% (20 mg), 5% discontinued
- 3 months: 50.7% (10 mg), 47.6% (20 mg), 1.7% discontinued
- 6 months: 46.7% (10 mg), 50% (20 mg), 3.3% discontinued

**Discontinuation / reintroduction:** Finerenone discontinued in three patients (5%) during the first month due to acute kidney injury (one also associated with hyperkalemia). Discontinued in one patient (1.7%) at 3 months due to palliative status, and in two patients (3.3%) at 6 months due to hyperkalemia. The drug was reintroduced after three months at 10 mg in three patients in whom finerenone had been discontinued. At reintroduction, mean serum creatinine was 2.08 ± 0.34 mg/dL and mean serum potassium 4.41 ± 0.42 mmol/L. All three were followed for an additional three months; renal function remained stable (mean creatinine 2.00 ± 0.53 mg/dL) without hyperkalemia (mean potassium 4.91 ± 0.26 mmol/L).

### 2.3. eGFR and Albuminuria Evolution During Follow-Up (eGFR dip focus)

- **Whole group (n=60), 1 month:** eGFR dropped from 49.49 [33.50-60.50] to 44.80 [28.25-58.00] mL/min/1.73 m² (p < 0.001).
- **6-month subgroup:** initial eGFR drop at 1 month from 42 [27.75-58.25] to 36.50 [23.75-53.00] mL/min/1.73 m² (p < 0.001). No significant differences vs values at 3 months (p = 0.401) or 6 months (p = 0.371), suggesting **stabilization of renal function following the initial eGFR drop**.
- **Overall UACR:** relative decrease 30.1% (p = 0.012) at 3 months and 37.1% (p = 0.012) at 6 months. A reduction in albuminuria >30% was observed in 70.57% of individuals after 6 months.
- **Subgroup with eGFR drop >20% (n = 10):** albuminuria reduction of 43.09% (p = 0.012) at 3 months and 43.71% (p = 0.013) at 6 months.
- **BMI ≥ 30 subgroup (n = 16):** albuminuria decreased from 467.50 [203.75-940.25] to 276.00 [54.00-1606.00] mg/g at 3 months (p = 0.953) and to 186 [67-1843] mg/g at 6 months (p = 0.918).
- **Non-diabetic CKD (concomitant etiology) subgroup (n = 15, 35.71%):** albuminuria decreased from 459 [201-722] mg/g to 227 [55-2167] at 3 months (p = 0.037) and to 212 [67-1900] at 6 months (p = 0.041).

### 2.4. Other Clinical Parameters (potassium)

- Whole cohort (1 month): potassium increased from 4.55 ± 0.48 to 4.78 ± 0.49 mmol/L (p < 0.001).
- 6-month subgroup: potassium increased significantly after 1 (p = 0.003), 3 (p = 0.003), and 6 months (p < 0.001).
- After the first month, no further significant increases: 4.79 ± 0.52 → 4.87 ± 0.45 mmol/L between months 1 and 3 (p = 0.813); 4.87 ± 0.45 → 4.92 ± 0.42 mmol/L between months 3 and 6 (p = 0.061) — **stabilization of potassium after early increase**.
- HbA1c significantly improved from 7.40% to 7.00% (p = 0.019) at 3 months and 7.05% (p = 0.049) at 6 months.

### 2.5. Adverse Events

Hyperkalemia occurred in 13 patients, all classified as mild (<6 mmol/L); only 3 cases resulted in finerenone withdrawal, the rest controlled through dietary adjustments, potassium chelators, and maintenance of finerenone at 10 mg. No gynecomastia. No hospital admissions due to adverse effects of finerenone or heart failure; no deaths during study follow-up.

## 3. Discussion (key points — eGFR dip reversibility)

- Study confirmed positive effect of finerenone on albuminuria in real-life patients with diabetes and CKD. **Renal function remained stable after the initial eGFR dip throughout follow-up**; only three cases of drug withdrawal due to hyperkalemia.
- No dose-escalation data exist in FIDELIO/FIGARO; real-world goal is to continue finerenone and escalate dose where possible rather than discontinuing, while preventing hyperkalemia.
- UACR reduced by 37.4% (as expected), resembling clinical trial albuminuria reductions. In FIDELIO-DKD (>5700 patients), finerenone achieved 31% UACR reduction vs placebo at 4 months, maintained for 36 months; reduced composite kidney outcome (kidney failure, ≥40% eGFR decline, or renal death) by 18% (HR 0.82; 95% CI 0.73-0.93) and cardiovascular events by 14% (HR 0.86; 95% CI 0.75-0.99). Early UACR decrease ≥30% serves as a surrogate marker for long-term renal/cardiovascular health. 70% of the 6-month cohort achieved ≥30% UACR reduction.
- FIDELITY (prespecified pooled FIDELIO-DKD + FIGARO-DKD, >13,000 patients): 23% lower risk of kidney failure or sustained ≥57% eGFR decline; 14% lower risk of major cardiovascular events.
- In non-DM-attributed CKD, albuminuria reduction was 53.81% at 6 months, supporting antifibrotic/anti-inflammatory actions of finerenone beyond CKD-DM. Ongoing FIND-CKD trial (NCT05047263), first phase 3 study in non-diabetic CKD, has completed recruitment.

**On the eGFR dip specifically:**
- A significant **13.3% reduction in eGFR at one month** was observed — an **expected and transient phenomenon**. eGFR remained stable between months 1 and 3 and showed a **tendency towards recovery between months 3 and 6**, consistent with the literature.
- This eGFR dip has been previously observed after initiation of RAS inhibitors and SGLT2is, reflecting improvement in excessive intraglomerular pressure. Acute eGFR decline after finerenone initiation is associated with reduction in cardiovascular and CKD progression in long-term follow-up.
- **Trial data show this decline is reversible upon treatment discontinuation and may be beneficial for long-term kidney health by slowing disease progression.** Recent FINEARTS-HF results suggest an initial eGFR decline with treatment initiation does not negate long-term cardiovascular and renal benefits and may offer protective effects.
- When the eGFR dip was >20% in the first month, albuminuria decreased by 43.09% and 43.71% at 3 and 6 months respectively. Therefore, this **initial eGFR drop should be recognized as a protective effect and a clinical benefit**.

**Adverse events:** Generally well tolerated; only 13 of 60 (21.67%) experienced mild hyperkalemia; finerenone withdrawn in only 6 patients (10%). No hospital admissions or deaths. In FIDELIO, hyperkalemia occurred in 18% of finerenone group but no fatal cases.

**Limitations:** retrospective design, small sample, absence of a control group — restrict generalizability. Strength: 6-month follow-up and one of first real-life evidence pieces on finerenone.

**Clinical caution:** Given the early eGFR decline, finerenone is not recommended for eGFR <25 mL/min/1.73 m². For eGFR 25-59 mL/min/1.73 m², start at lower dose (10 mg) with close monitoring of serum potassium and eGFR one month afterwards.

## 4. Materials and Methods (summary)

- **Design:** Retrospective, observational, longitudinal cohort study, real-world setting; Nephrology Department, Vall d'Hebron Hospital.
- **Inclusion:** Age ≥ 18; albuminuria ≥ 30 mg/g; eGFR (CKD-EPI) > 20 mL/min/1.73 m²; potassium < 5.5 mmol/L; adequate BP control at baseline (SBP 110-130 mmHg, DBP 60-80 mmHg).
- **Exclusion:** <18 years; albuminuria <30 mg/g; eGFR (CKD-EPI) ≤ 20 mL/min/1.73 m²; potassium ≥ 5.5 mmol/L; inadequate BP control; recent AKI; severe comorbidities incompatible with participation; incomplete follow-up or insufficient records.
- **Follow-up:** up to 6 months from finerenone initiation. Data at baseline, 3, and 6 months. Starting dose based on eGFR: all patients with CKD-EPI < 60 initiated at 10 mg daily; for eGFR > 60, starting dose at physician discretion.
- **Definitions:** AKI = increase in creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL or ≥1.5× baseline. Hyperkalemia = serum potassium ≥5.5 mmol/L; categorized as mild (5.5-5.9), moderate (6.0-6.4), severe (≥6.5).
- **Statistics:** Kolmogorov-Smirnov for normality; normal variables as mean ± SD; non-parametric (e.g., albuminuria) as median [IQR]; Wilcoxon signed-rank for paired non-normal, paired t-test for normal; p < 0.05 significant; SPSS v20.
- **Ethics:** Protocol EOM(AG)033/2021(5838), approved 11 June 2021; informed consent not required (retrospective/observational).
