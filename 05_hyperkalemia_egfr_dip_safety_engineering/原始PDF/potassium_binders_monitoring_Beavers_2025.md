# Hyperkalemia in Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction: Implications and Management

## Abstract

Hyperkalemia is a potentially life-threatening electrolyte imbalance that has traditionally posed significant challenges in the management of heart failure (HF). This review explores the complex interplay between hyperkalemia and the use of guideline-directed medical therapies (GDMT), such as renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (sMRAs), including spironolactone and eplerenone, which are currently recommended in guidelines for improving outcomes in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF). While these therapies reduce mortality and hospitalizations in HFrEF, their benefit in patients with heart failure with left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) ≥ 40% remains less conclusive. Nevertheless, their use in clinical practice is often limited by the risk of hyperkalemia, potentially leading to dose reduction or discontinuation of life-saving treatments. The prevalence of hyperkalemia in HF patients is notably higher compared to the general population, particularly in those with comorbid chronic kidney disease (CKD) and diabetes mellitus, further complicating management. This review emphasizes the importance of regular potassium monitoring, the potential benefits of combining therapies such as sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors with RAASi to reduce the risk of hyperkalemia, and the emergence of the newer non-steroidal MRA (nsMRA), finerenone, which may have a lower risk of hyperkalemia. Additionally, potassium binders such as patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC) are highlighted for their role in managing and preventing hyperkalemia, allowing patients to continue optimal RAASi and MRA therapy without interruption. By synthesizing current evidence on the incidence, risks, and management strategies of hyperkalemia in HF, this review aims to provide a comprehensive guide for clinicians to optimize patient outcomes while mitigating the risks associated with hyperkalemia.

### Plain Language Summary

Hyperkalemia, or high potassium levels in the blood, is a common issue in people with heart failure, especially those on certain medications like renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (sMRAs), such as spironolactone and eplerenone. These medications help the heart work better, reduce hospitalizations, and improve survival in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), but they also increase the risk of hyperkalemia, which can be dangerous. High potassium levels can lead to serious heart problems, making it difficult to manage heart failure effectively. Doctors often have to reduce or stop these medications in patients with hyperkalemia, which limits the full benefits of treatment. This review explains the importance of balancing heart failure treatment while preventing high potassium levels. It highlights strategies to manage hyperkalemia, such as regular potassium monitoring and combining treatments like sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, which may reduce the risk of hyperkalemia while simultaneously improving both HF and kidney outcomes. Newer medications like finerenone, a non-steroidal MRA, have shown promise in potentially reducing the risk of hyperkalemia while still protecting the heart. Additionally, medications called potassium binders, such as patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, help lower potassium levels and allow patients to stay on heart failure medications. The review emphasizes that managing hyperkalemia is crucial for heart failure patients. By using these new approaches and medications, doctors can help patients continue their heart failure treatment safely, improving their health outcomes and reducing the risk of serious complications.

## Introduction

Potassium is the most abundant intracellular cation in the human body, essential for various cellular functions, including maintaining resting membrane potential and facilitating action potentials [1]. Hyperkalemia, defined as plasma potassium levels above 5.0 mEq/L, disrupts cellular electrical activity—particularly in the heart—where it can impair conduction and excitability, leading to potentially fatal arrhythmias [1, 2]. The association between hyperkalemia and increased mortality risk underscores the critical nature of this electrolyte imbalance, especially in vulnerable populations [3–6].

Among hospitalized patients, hyperkalemia is relatively common, with an estimated prevalence ranging from 1 to 10% [7]. Patients with heart failure face an even higher risk, with an incidence 2 to 3 times greater than that of the general population [8, 9]. This increased susceptibility is largely driven by frequent comorbidities such as chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes (T2D), which contribute to fluid and electrolyte imbalances [10]. Additionally, the use of guideline-recommended neurohormonal therapies—including angiotensin receptor–neprilysin inhibitors (ARNIs), angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs), and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs), particularly the steroidal MRAs (sMRAs) spironolactone and eplerenone—can further heighten the risk of hyperkalemia [11]. More recently, the non-steroidal MRA (nsMRA) finerenone has emerged as an alternative with a potentially lower risk of hyperkalemia, as shown in clinical studies on CKD associated with T2D and in heart failure [12–16]. Hyperkalemia may limit achievement of target doses in up to 4.6% of patients on ACEIs/ARBs and 15% of those on MRAs, resulting in dose reductions or discontinuation in 14.2% and 33.3% of cases, respectively [17]. Any delayed dose optimization, dose reduction, or therapy interruption compromises the clinical benefits of these foundational treatments [18–24].

Given the significant risks associated with hyperkalemia in heart failure, a comprehensive understanding of its implications and effective management strategies is warranted. This review synthesizes current knowledge on the impact of hyperkalemia in heart failure patients, focusing on its prevalence, associated risks, and management approaches. By examining the existing literature, we aim to provide insights into the optimal management of hyperkalemia to improve outcomes in this high-risk population.

## Pathophysiology of Potassium Homeostasis

### Overview of potassium homeostasis

Potassium homeostasis is essential for maintaining normal cellular function, particularly in excitable tissues such as the heart and muscles. In the human body, potassium is present at approximately 50 mEq/kg, resulting in a total of approximately 3,500 mEq for a 70-kg individual [25]. Around 98% of potassium resides within cells, while the remaining 2% is found in the extracellular fluid, where normal concentrations range from 3.5 to 5.3 mEq/L [25]. Significant deviations from these levels, whether hyperkalemia or hypokalemia, can be life-threatening due to their potential impact on cellular excitability and conduction [26–28].

The kidneys play a central role in maintaining potassium balance, excreting roughly 90% of the daily potassium intake, with the gastrointestinal tract accounting for the remaining 10% [25]. This excretion process is highly adaptive, allowing the kidneys to adjust the rate of potassium elimination in response to dietary intake over several hours, thus maintaining potassium homeostasis [25]. The regulation of potassium between intracellular and extracellular compartments, known as internal potassium balance, is illustrated in Fig. 1. This balance is primarily maintained by the activity of the Na⁺-K⁺ ATPase, which actively transports potassium into cells and sodium out of cells, thereby sustaining the steep concentration gradient across the cell membrane [25].

The kidneys' ability to maintain normal potassium levels is remarkably robust, as studies have demonstrated that normal potassium balance can be preserved even with a dietary intake of up to 15 g/day for 20 days [29, 30]. This adaptability is further supported by recent research identifying an enteric potassium sensing mechanism that triggers kidney potassium excretion upon potassium entry into the gastrointestinal tract [31, 32]. Additionally, the distal convoluted tubule in the kidney plays a crucial role in potassium homeostasis. This segment acts as a sensor that initiates potassium excretion, independently of mineralocorticoid activity, thereby contributing to the fine-tuning of potassium balance [33].

### Kidney handling of potassium

Potassium is freely filtered at the glomerulus, with approximately 70% reabsorbed in the proximal tubule and thick ascending limb of the nephron, leaving only a small fraction to reach the distal segments. In the proximal tubule, reabsorption primarily occurs via passive paracellular pathways through solvent drag and diffusion, paralleling sodium and water transport to maintain electrolyte balance [25].

In the thick ascending limb, around 25% of potassium is reabsorbed through both transcellular and paracellular mechanisms. This is largely driven by the Na⁺-K⁺-2Cl⁻ cotransporter (NKCC2), a multi-ion transporter and the target of loop diuretics. NKCC2-mediated transport is active and essential for maintaining the medullary concentration gradient critical for urine concentration (Fig. 2) [25].

Potassium secretion begins in the early distal convoluted tubule (DCT) and becomes more pronounced toward the cortical collecting duct. This process is primarily mediated by renal outer medullary potassium (ROMK) channels and high-conductance calcium-activated potassium (Maxi-K) channels. ROMK channels operate electrogenically under baseline conditions, while Maxi-K channels are activated under high tubular flow. These mechanisms allow dynamic regulation of potassium excretion in response to physiological demands [34]. High tubular flow enhances potassium secretion by activating Maxi-K channels and promoting ROMK function via luminal potassium dilution and increased sodium uptake through epithelial sodium channels (ENaC) [35].

The DCT also acts as a potassium sensor. The DCT1 segment predominantly uses the thiazide-sensitive NaCl cotransporter (NCC) for sodium transport, while the DCT2 segment integrates both electroneutral and electrogenic pathways for sodium and potassium exchange [36]. This segmental specialization enables fine-tuned regulation of electrolyte homeostasis.

Dietary potassium—particularly from bicarbonate-rich foods like fruits and vegetables—stimulates renal potassium excretion, supporting balance during increased intake [37]. Additionally, potassium excretion follows a circadian rhythm, with lower excretion rates at night and higher rates in the afternoon, reflecting the dynamic regulation of potassium across different physiological states [38].

## Rate and Incidence of Hyperkalemia in Heart Failure

### Hyperkalemia in the general population compared with heart failure

Hyperkalemia is rare in the general population, with a prevalence of approximately 0.035% [39]. However, the condition is significantly more common in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD), diabetes mellitus (DM), and/or heart failure (HF), which predispose patients to potassium imbalances. In heart failure, the prevalence of hyperkalemia has been reported to range from 7 to 23% at baseline (during the six months prior to study inclusion). With continued follow-up extending up to one year, this prevalence increased, with longer-term studies estimating hyperkalemia prevalence to range between 25 and 39% over a 1-to-3-year period, and it increases further to 26% to 48% in patients with CKD and comorbid HF [40–45]. In a study of the US Medicare population, hyperkalemia was prevalent in patients with both CKD and HF with rates ranging from 8.9% to 9.3% over a 12-month period [41].

These findings indicate that hyperkalemia is significantly more prevalent in HF patients compared to the general population or those with less severe comorbidities [46, 47]. Newly diagnosed heart failure patients may exhibit lower hyperkalemia prevalence, likely due to differences in age, comorbidity burden, and guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) status. This lower prevalence may also reflect the fact that many of these patients have not yet initiated GDMT or are in the early stages of treatment, as commonly observed in de novo heart failure compared to worsening chronic HF cohorts [48].

### Hyperkalemia in the context of GDMT with RAASi and MRA

There is a well-established association between hyperkalemia and the use of renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi), which include angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) [49]. These medications are a cornerstone of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) in heart failure, particularly in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), due to their proven benefits in reducing mortality and hospitalizations. However, while clinical trials of RAASi have shown consistent benefits in reducing mortality and heart failure hospitalizations in patients with HFrEF, evidence supporting their use in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) and heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF) is more variable. The benefits of MRAs, such as spironolactone and eplerenone, have been firmly established in HFrEF, with demonstrated reductions in heart failure hospitalizations and cardiovascular death [48, 50–52]. In contrast, the non-steroidal MRA (nsMRA) finerenone has shown benefits in patients with HFmrEF and HFpEF (left ventricular ejection fraction [LVEF] ≥ 40%), as demonstrated in recent clinical trials [12]. A study examining the use of ACEIs/ARBs (74%), beta-blockers (83%), and/or MRAs (55%) in heart failure patients reported an overall hyperkalemia prevalence of 8% at baseline, with an incidence that increased to 12.3% after nine months of follow-up, following the up-titration of ACEIs/ARBs [53].

A population-based analysis in Ontario demonstrated a fivefold increase in spironolactone prescriptions among heart failure patients treated with angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACEIs) following the publication of the RALES trial. This rise in steroidal MRA (sMRA) use was accompanied by a sharp increase in hospitalizations for hyperkalemia (from 2.4 to 11.0 per 1,000 patients) and a significant rise in hyperkalemia-associated deaths (from 0.3 to 2.0 per 1,000) over a seven-year period [54]. These findings emphasize the need for careful patient selection, appropriate dosing, and close monitoring of serum potassium in real-world settings.

Further supporting this concern, a systematic review of observational and interventional studies in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) receiving RAASi reported moderate-to-severe hyperkalemia rates as high as 38.2% in observational cohorts and 19.0% in clinical trials. The addition of an sMRA to background ACEI or angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB) therapy increased the risk of hyperkalemia by fourfold to 13-fold, reinforcing the importance of vigilant monitoring in these patients [55].

Variations in trial design also influence the reported incidence of hyperkalemia. For example, finerenone trials such as FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD required baseline serum potassium ≤ 4.8 mmol/L, whereas the more recent FINEARTS-HF trial allowed inclusion of patients with potassium up to 5.0 mmol/L (Fig. 3).

However, not all real-world studies have shown a parallel increase in hyperkalemia with MRA use. A population-based cohort study of Medicare beneficiaries with heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) found that, although spironolactone initiation increased following the TOPCAT trial, there was no corresponding rise in hyperkalemia-related hospitalizations [56, 57]. However, subgroup analyses from the TOPCAT clinical trial indicated that patients enrolled from the Americas, where adherence to therapy was higher, experienced notably higher rates of hyperkalemia compared to those from Russia and Georgia—supporting the hypothesis that increased exposure to spironolactone may have contributed to the regional differences in adverse event profiles [56].

Taken together, these findings highlight the ongoing challenge of balancing the life-saving benefits of RAASi and MRAs against the risk of hyperkalemia—particularly at higher therapeutic doses. While agents like finerenone may confer a lower hyperkalemia risk in select populations, potassium monitoring remains essential for all patients receiving MRAs, regardless of the specific agent or heart failure phenotype.

## Implications of Hyperkalemia in Heart Failure

Drug-induced hyperkalemia—or even the perceived risk of it—often leads to clinician hesitancy in initiating or titrating renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi). As a result, clinicians may prematurely discontinue or reduce the dose of these life-saving therapies. However, in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), RAASi therapies significantly reduce mortality, and their withdrawal or underdosing may adversely impact outcomes [56].

Hyperkalemia may also reflect a more advanced disease state in some patients with heart failure (HF), which in turn may lead to more conservative treatment approaches. In a study by Tromp et al., hyperkalemia was more prevalent among patients with more severe HF, highlighting that these individuals may be more difficult to treat with guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) due to heightened risk for treatment-related adverse effects [57].

While landmark randomized trials such as RALES [50] and EMPHASIS-HF [51] reported low discontinuation rates of RAASi and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) therapies—even in the setting of hyperkalemia—real-world data suggest otherwise. In clinical practice, especially among new users of steroidal MRAs (sMRAs), discontinuation is often observed within the first year, frequently in response to even mild elevations in serum potassium [49].

Although the primary focus of this review is hyperkalemia, emerging data underscore the importance of maintaining serum potassium within an optimal range. Multiple observational studies have shown a U-shaped or J-shaped association between potassium levels and mortality, with the lowest mortality risk typically observed when serum potassium is maintained between 4.0 and 4.5 mmol/L [3, 58, 59]. Both hypokalemia (< 3.5 mmol/L) and hyperkalemia (≥ 5.0 mmol/L) have been independently associated with increased short- and long-term mortality [3, 58, 60].

Taken together, these findings highlight the importance of optimized potassium management—not merely to prevent hyperkalemia, but to maintain potassium levels within a normal range that supports clinical stability and enables continuation or escalation of GDMT.

## Prevention of Hyperkalemia in HF

### Importance of improved monitoring in preventing hyperkalemia

Effective monitoring of potassium levels is perhaps one of the most effective and critical method in preventing the complications of hyperkalemia in heart failure (HF) patients, particularly those receiving renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs). Despite clear guideline recommendations, a study by Nilsson et al. found that only 24% of patients initiating steroidal MRA therapy underwent complete potassium and creatinine monitoring across all recommended intervals (pre-initiation, early [1–10 days], and extended [11–90 days] post-initiation). While the study did not evaluate whether this gap in follow-up led to delayed detection of hyperkalemia or therapy discontinuation, insufficient laboratory monitoring may hinder timely intervention and contribute to suboptimal treatment in clinical practice [61]. Without consistent monitoring, patients are at increased risk of adverse outcomes due to hyperkalemia, and clinicians may prematurely reduce or discontinue RAASi therapy, depriving patients of its full cardiovascular benefits (Fig. 4).

Regular monitoring at baseline, after therapy initiation or dose escalation, and periodically during treatment is essential, especially in patients with additional risk factors such as kidney impairment or concurrent use of potassium-altering medications [50]. In patients receiving digoxin, maintaining normal potassium levels is particularly important, as both hypokalemia and hyperkalemia increase the risk of digoxin toxicity and associated arrhythmias [62]. The 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA guidelines recommend regular checks of serum potassium levels and kidney function should be performed according to clinical status, approximately 1 week, then 4 weeks, then every 6 months after initiating or intensifying MRA, with more frequent testing for clinical instability [52]. Implementing automated alerts in electronic health records (EHRs) may help bridge this gap, prompting clinicians to adhere to recommended monitoring intervals, which has been shown to improve outcomes in patients at risk for hyperkalemia [63]. Patient education is also crucial to ensure adherence to regular lab tests, as studies indicate that patients who are actively engaged in their care tend to experience better clinical outcomes [64]. Several home-based potassium monitoring platforms are being developed and researched to ease some of the patient burden tied to appropriate monitoring.

Additionally, multidisciplinary care models, particularly those involving pharmacists, have shown promise in optimizing RAASi and MRA therapy while minimizing the risk of hyperkalemia. Pharmacists play a vital role in monitoring potassium levels, adjusting medications, and educating patients about the importance of adherence to monitoring protocols. This collaborative approach has been associated with better adherence to monitoring, reduced rates of therapy discontinuation, and a reduction in hyperkalemia-related hospitalizations [65].

Dietary potassium intake plays a critical role in managing hyperkalemia, particularly in patients with heart failure who are receiving RAAS inhibitors or MRAs. While moderate potassium restriction (2–3 g/day) may be appropriate for patients with recurrent hyperkalemia or reduced kidney function, overly aggressive restriction can be counterproductive, as it may compromise nutritional adequacy and is often difficult to sustain [66, 67]. A targeted approach focusing on high-potassium foods, especially processed or salt-substitute-containing items, is preferred over broad avoidance of fruits and vegetables. Individualized dietary counseling, ideally in collaboration with a dietitian, can help balance potassium control with cardiometabolic nutrition goals in heart failure patients [68].

### Prevention of CKD

Preventing or delaying the progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD) is an important consideration in the efforts to reduce the risk of hyperkalemia in heart failure (HF) patients, who are already at heightened risk due to their condition and the therapies used to manage it. Several risk factors contribute to the development of CKD, including hypertension, type 2 diabetes mellitus, obesity, smoking, and the use of medications like ACE inhibitors (ACEIs), angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs), and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) [69]. Addressing these risk factors through lifestyle modifications—such as dietary changes, weight management, smoking cessation, and blood pressure control—can significantly reduce the incidence of CKD and, subsequently, hyperkalemia.

Ensuring appropriate screening and monitoring is essential in the early detection and management of CKD. Routine measures like estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) are critical for identifying early-stage CKD in at-risk populations, including those with type 2 diabetes, HF, or those on ACEI/ARB or MRA therapy. A best practice should encourage UACR screening for those at risk for HF (Stage A and B) and those with HF (Stage C).

Pharmacological interventions also play a vital role in CKD prevention. One key intervention is the early use of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors. These agents have shown promise in reducing the progression of CKD and lowering the risk of hyperkalemia, especially in patients with heart failure and type 2 diabetes mellitus. While their kidney protective effects—such as reducing proteinuria and preserving kidney function—are well-established, the specific role of SGLT2 inhibitors in managing hyperkalemia will be further discussed in the section on hyperkalemia management [13].

Emerging evidence suggests that glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) may provide renal protection and reduce hyperkalemia risk in patients with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease (CKD) [70]. In the FLOW trial, semaglutide significantly reduced the risk of kidney disease progression, including kidney failure and major cardiovascular or renal events (HR 0.76; 95% CI, 0.66–0.88) [70]. Observational data also indicate a modest reduction in hyperkalemia incidence among GLP-1RA users compared to other antihyperglycemics [71]. GLP-1RAs may confer benefit through natriuresis, reduced inflammation, and improved fluid balance, making them particularly promising in HFpEF patients with obesity and metabolic comorbidity.

Additionally, nsMRA, finerenone, has demonstrated kidney-protective effects in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes, while carrying a manageable risk of hyperkalemia, as demonstrated in the FIDELITY pooled analysis. This prespecified pooled analysis of the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD trials included 13,026 patients with a median follow-up of 3.0 years. Finerenone significantly reduced the risk of the composite cardiovascular outcome (cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, non-fatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure) with a hazard ratio (HR) of 0.86 (95% CI, 0.78–0.95; P = 0.0018). Similarly, finerenone reduced the composite kidney outcome (kidney failure, sustained ≥ 57% decline in eGFR, or kidney death) with an HR of 0.77 (95% CI, 0.67–0.88; P = 0.0002). Although hyperkalemia leading to treatment discontinuation occurred more frequently in the finerenone group (1.7% vs. 0.6%), overall safety outcomes were similar between treatment arms [13].

### Use of SGLT2 inhibitors and ARNIs in hyperkalemia prevention

An analysis of 27 studies (N = 43,589) highlighted that combining sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2is) with mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) and renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) significantly reduces the risk of hyperkalemia by attenuating the potassium-elevating effects of MRAs [72]. In DAPA-HF, dapagliflozin halved the rate of moderate-to-severe hyperkalemia (> 6.0 mmol/L) among MRA-treated patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) (1.0 vs. 1.7 events per 100 patient-years; hazard ratio [HR] 0.50; P = 0.01) [73]. Similarly, the EMPEROR-Reduced trial demonstrated a lower incidence of investigator-reported hyperkalemia with empagliflozin (6.1% vs. 7.2%; HR 0.83; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.71–0.97; P = 0.018) [3]. A pooled analysis from EMPEROR-Reduced and EMPEROR-Preserved further confirmed that empagliflozin consistently lowered the risk of moderate or severe hyperkalemia across heart failure phenotypes [74]. Proposed mechanisms include enhanced natriuresis, reduced inflammation, improved tubular electrolyte handling, and decreased RAAS activation—all of which contribute to better potassium regulation.

Angiotensin receptor–neprilysin inhibitors (ARNis), such as sacubitril/valsartan, also confer a lower risk of hyperkalemia. In the PARADIGM-HF trial, among patients concurrently using MRAs, sacubitril/valsartan significantly reduced the rate of severe hyperkalemia compared with enalapril (2.2 vs. 3.1 events per 100 patient-years; HR 0.72; 95% CI, 0.56–0.93; P = 0.02) [75].

Together, SGLT2is and ARNis not only reduce the risk of hyperkalemia and may facilitate optimal use of MRA and RAASi, but the therapies each offer direct benefits on patient morbidity and mortality.

### Use of non-steroidal versus steroidal MRAs

Newer non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (nsMRA), finerenone, may offer potential benefits in reducing the overall risk of hyperkalemia compared with steroidal MRAs, such as spironolactone and eplerenone, while maintaining efficacy in patients with heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF), heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF), or comorbid CKD and type 2 diabetes. While direct head-to-head trials comparing sMRAs and nsMRA are limited, various trials have reported on the incidence of hyperkalemia in each drug class. However, in one such head-to-head comparison, the ARTS-HF trial compared finerenone with eplerenone among patients with worsening heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and chronic kidney disease (CKD) or type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). The incidence of hyperkalemia (serum potassium ≥ 5.6 mmol/L) was 3.6% to 3.8% in the finerenone group across different dose ranges, compared to 4.7% in the eplerenone group. The study demonstrated a more favorable hyperkalemia profile with finerenone; however, the doses of finerenone used in ARTS-HF were relatively low compared to subsequent trials [14].

The recent FINEARTS-HF trial examined the clinical outcome benefits and safety of finerenone in patients with heart failure with mildly reduced or preserved ejection fraction (HFmrEF and HFpEF). In this international, double-blind trial, patients with a left ventricular ejection fraction of 40% or greater were randomized to receive either finerenone or placebo. Over a median follow-up of 32 months, finerenone demonstrated a 16% reduction in the composite primary outcome of total worsening heart failure events and death from cardiovascular causes compared to placebo (rate ratio, 0.84; 95% confidence interval [CI], 0.74 to 0.95; P = 0.007). The incidence of hyperkalemia was 9.7% in the finerenone group, compared to 4.2% in the placebo group. Adverse events related to hyperkalemia were generally manageable with appropriate potassium monitoring and dose adjustments [14, 76].

Recent potassium-focused analyses from the FINEARTS-HF trial further emphasized that the clinical benefits of finerenone were preserved even in patients who experienced elevated potassium levels (serum potassium > 5.5 mmol/L). These findings highlight that, with protocol-directed potassium monitoring and appropriate dose adjustments, the risks associated with hyperkalemia can be effectively managed. Importantly, this surveillance approach allowed for the continued use of finerenone, ensuring its cardiovascular benefits were maintained while minimizing adverse events related to potassium disturbances. This reinforces the critical role of individualized care and vigilant monitoring in optimizing the safety and efficacy of finerenone therapy for patients with heart failure [15].

Hyperkalemia is categorized according to KDIGO guidelines, with mild cases defined as serum potassium levels above 5.5 mmol/L and moderate cases between 6.0 and 6.5 mmol/L [76]. In the FIDELIO-DKD trial, finerenone doubled the risk of hyperkalemia compared to placebo (14.3% vs. 6.9%) [76, 77]. However, despite this increase, the incidence of clinically significant hyperkalemia-related adverse events remained low, with only 1.7% of patients requiring permanent discontinuation of finerenone due to hyperkalemia, compared to 0.6% in the placebo group [16]. In both the FIDELITY analysis and FINEARTS-HF, potassium management strategies, such as pausing treatment when serum potassium levels exceeded 5.5 mmol/L and resuming therapy once levels dropped below 5.0 mmol/L, were effective in preventing hyperkalemia-related adverse events. These strategies allowed for successful treatment continuity in more than 13,000 patients who were followed for a median of three years [15].

### Use of potassium binders in hyperkalemia prevention and management

Potassium binders, such as patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC), are an effective therapy for preventing and managing hyperkalemia in heart failure (HF) patients, particularly those receiving renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs). These agents bind excess potassium in the gastrointestinal tract, lowering serum potassium levels and preventing treatment discontinuation due to hyperkalemia [78].

Patiromer, a non-absorbed polymer that binds potassium in the colon, has demonstrated its effectiveness in clinical trials, enabling the continuation or up-titration of RAASi and MRA therapies at optimal doses. The DIAMOND trial, a robust study evaluating potassium binders in the HF population with active or prior hyperkalemia, demonstrated that patiromer effectively controlled serum potassium levels, with a mean difference in potassium of approximately 0.10 mmol/L between the patiromer and placebo groups. This reduction helped decrease the rate of RAASi and MRA discontinuation [78].

Similarly, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC), an inorganic cation exchanger, has shown rapid and sustained potassium-lowering effects. Clinical trials, including the HARMONIZE trial, demonstrated reductions in serum potassium levels by 0.3 to 1.9 mmol/L, depending on baseline potassium levels, with larger reductions seen in those with more severe hyperkalemia. These effects were observed as early as 1 h after administration and sustained over 48 h, making SZC effective for both acute and chronic hyperkalemia management. Patients enrolled in these studies typically had conditions such as chronic kidney disease, heart failure, and diabetes mellitus, and were often on RAAS inhibitors or other hyperkalemia-inducing medications [79–81].

The REALIZE-K trial evaluated the efficacy of sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC) in enabling the optimization of mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) therapy, specifically spironolactone, in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) who had hyperkalemia or were at high risk for it. The study found that 71% of patients receiving SZC maintained normokalemia while on spironolactone doses of at least 25 mg daily, compared to 36% in the placebo group. This significant difference (odds ratio: 4.45; 95% CI: 2.89–6.86; P < 0.001) indicates that SZC effectively facilitates the use of optimal MRA therapy by controlling serum potassium levels [82].

The REALIZE-K trial demonstrated that sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC) enabled more patients with HFrEF and hyperkalemia to maintain normokalemia while receiving ≥ 25 mg of spironolactone daily (71% vs. 36%; OR: 4.45; 95% CI: 2.89–6.86; P < 0.001) [82]. However, a higher rate of worsening HF events was observed in the SZC group (12% vs. 4%) as an exploratory endpoint. This signal may relate to the sodium content of SZC—approximately 400 mg per 10 g dose—which could promote fluid retention in susceptible patients. While all events resolved or were resolving by study end, this highlights the importance of monitoring volume status when using SZC in advanced HF [82].

By enabling the continued use of life-saving heart failure therapies at optimal doses, potassium binders like patiromer and SZC play a pivotal role in mitigating hyperkalemia-related risks. While they have not yet been shown to reduce cardiovascular events directly, their use may help support uninterrupted guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) and allow clinicians to maintain or up-titrate RAASi and MRA therapy.

Table 1 provides a summary of key studies evaluating the effectiveness and safety profiles of potassium-binding agents. The introduction of patiromer and SZC into clinical practice represents a significant advancement in the management of hyperkalemia, particularly in HF patients who require ongoing treatment with RAASi and MRAs. These agents not only help manage potassium levels but also enable the use of optimal GDMT, thus improving overall patient outcomes.

## Conclusion

Hyperkalemia remains a significant and prevalent challenge in the management of heart failure (HF), particularly in patients undergoing treatment with renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) and steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (sMRAs). The delicate balance between managing hyperkalemia and maintaining optimal dosages of these life-saving medications is crucial for improving patient outcomes. As hyperkalemia often leads to the down-titration or discontinuation of RAASi and steroidal MRAs (sMRAs), it directly impacts the ability to achieve the full benefits of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) in HF patients.

Recent advancements have introduced the nsMRA, finerenone, which may offer a lower risk of hyperkalemia compared to traditional sMRAs, potentially allowing broader use in patients at risk of hyperkalemia. Effective monitoring strategies, the use of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2i), and the introduction of potassium binders as needed are critical for the prevention and management of hyperkalemia. These approaches help ensure that patients can safely receive target doses of RAASi and MRAs, optimizing outcomes while mitigating the risks associated with hyperkalemia.
