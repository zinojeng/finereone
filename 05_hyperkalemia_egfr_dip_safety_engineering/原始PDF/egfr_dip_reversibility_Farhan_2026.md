# Early In-Hospital Initiation of SGLT2 Inhibitors After STEMI: A Clinical Review of Hemodynamic and Renal Safety

## Abstract

Survivors of ST-elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI) continue to face appreciable risks of hospitalization for heart failure and acute kidney injury (AKI), even in the era of prompt primary percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI). SGLT2 inhibitors deliver proven cardio-renal benefits in chronic heart failure (HF) and chronic kidney disease (CKD); however, the safety profile of these agents when initiating administration during the index STEMI admission remains poorly characterized. This clinical review summarizes contemporary evidence on the hemodynamic and renal safety of initiating SGLT2i therapy 24-72 h after PCI in patients with STEMI and provides a pragmatic, evidence-informed bedside framework, supported by RCTs and mechanistic/observational data from January 2018 to July 2025.

Key quantitative findings:
- Trial endpoints extracted qualitatively; no formal meta-analysis performed.
- Among at least **11,221 participants**, early SGLT2i initiation was well tolerated: rates of hypotension, volume depletion, AKI, and diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) were comparable to placebo.
- The characteristic **3-6 mL/min/1.73 m² decline in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR)** represented a **reversible tubuloglomerular feedback (TGF) adjustment rather than nephrotoxicity**.

Mechanistic studies attribute these findings to mild natriuresis without sympathetic activation and to afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction, which lowers intraglomerular pressure. The authors propose the **START checklist** (Stable hemodynamics, Tubular reserve, Acid-base stability, Risk factors, Timing 24-72 h) as provisional guidance. Outcome trials awaited: EMPACT-MI and extended EMPRESS-MI read-outs.

**Keywords:** ST-elevation myocardial infarction; myocardial infarction; sodium-glucose transporter-2 inhibitors; acute kidney injury; acute coronary syndrome

## 1. Introduction

- ~3.8% of AMI survivors were re-hospitalized for HF within one year; these patients had markedly higher mortality (11.3% vs. 2.5%) [ref 1].
- AKI occurs in roughly 7% of STEMI patients undergoing primary PCI, rising to ~15% in those >=75 years [ref 2].
- AKI associated with in-hospital death rates of ~27% versus ~2% without AKI [ref 2].
- SGLT2 inhibitors lower systolic blood pressure (SBP) by only ~4-5 mmHg on average [ref 3], without excess symptomatic hypotension or need for vasopressors.

## 2. Methodology

Narrative literature review using PubMed and Embase from January 2018 to July 2025. Priority given to RCTs and prospective cohorts reporting hemodynamic or renal safety outcomes. Data extracted qualitatively; no formal meta-analysis.

## 3. Mechanistic Rationale

### 3.1 Rapid Natriuresis and Preload Reduction Without Sympathetic Activation
- SGLT2 inhibitors block sodium and glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule, causing osmotic diuresis, lowering plasma and interstitial fluid volume, reducing cardiac preload [ref 10].
- Empagliflozin reduced blood and plasma volume by about a few percent within two weeks [ref 10].
- Drugs preferentially remove interstitial fluid without sharply depleting intravascular volume [ref 10].
- Volume reduction does NOT trigger sympathetic or renin-angiotensin system activation [refs 10,11]; in animal models even suppress sympathetic nerve activity; lowered BP does not raise heart rate [ref 11].

### 3.2 Na+/H+ Exchanger (NHE1) Inhibition and Ketone "Super-Fuel" Shift
- Empagliflozin suppresses cardiomyocyte NHE-1 activity, lowering cytosolic Na+ and Ca2+ concentrations [ref 12].
- Shift toward ketone bodies: beta-hydroxybutyrate and other ketones double from baseline during sustained SGLT2 therapy [ref 13]; ketones produce more ATP per unit oxygen than free fatty acids ("super-fuel") [ref 13].

### 3.3 Tubuloglomerular Feedback (TGF) and Intraglomerular Pressure Lowering
- SGLT2 inhibitors trigger TGF: more sodium delivered to macula densa signals afferent arteriole to constrict, lowering intraglomerular pressure and reduced single-nephron GFR [refs 7,11].
- **Clinically produces a small but immediate eGFR decline: average eGFR fall of ~3-6 mL/min/1.73 m² within the first 1-4 weeks [ref 7].**
- **This change is hemodynamic, not nephrotoxic; it reflects the afferent vasoconstriction of TGF.**
- **Once patients are on therapy, GFR stabilizes or even declines more slowly than expected.**
- Long-term, SGLT2 inhibitors slow the rate of kidney function loss by protecting glomeruli from chronic hyperfiltration stress [ref 7].
- **The initial eGFR drop is a reversible adjustment. As pressure inside the glomerulus normalizes, filtration equilibrates.** TGF helps distinguish a harmless SGLT2i-mediated dip from true contrast nephropathy. TGF explains why SGLT2 inhibitors cause a predictable eGFR dip that usually recovers or stabilizes [refs 7,11].

### 3.4 Safety Outcomes of SGLT2 Inhibitors in Post-STEMI Randomized Trials

- **DAPA-MI** (n=4017, 2 countries): dapagliflozin in STEMI patients with impaired LV function or Q-wave MI, without diabetes. Excluded eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m² or chronic HF. Median follow-up 11.6 months. Did not increase adverse events vs. placebo, with reassurance regarding hemodynamic stability, renal function, and infections [ref 4].
- **EMPACT-MI** (n=6522, 22 countries): empagliflozin in patients at high risk of HF after MI. Exclusion: eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m² and SBP <100 mmHg; inclusion of eGFR 20-60 mL/min/1.73 m² added as enrichment. Median 17.9-month follow-up. Well tolerated; adverse event rates similar to placebo; no signal for renal or hemodynamic instability [ref 5].
- **EMPRESS-MI** (n=105, Austria): empagliflozin initiated within 72 h of PCI in STEMI patients. Excluded severe renal dysfunction (eGFR <20) or hypotension. Median 5.5-month follow-up. No excess in creatinine doubling, AKI, or hypotension vs. placebo [ref 18].
- **EMMY** (n=476, UK): empagliflozin initiated within 72 h post-PCI; excluded eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73 m² or BP <110/70 mmHg. Median 6-month follow-up. No increase in adverse events; renal and hemodynamic parameters stable relative to placebo [ref 19].
- **EMI-STEMI** (n=101, single-center, Iran): empagliflozin vs placebo in STEMI without diabetes. SBP <100 mmHg or eGFR <30 excluded. 6 months; no significant differences between groups [ref 20].

Taken together, these five randomized trials consistently demonstrate the safety of initiating SGLT2 inhibitors in the acute and subacute STEMI setting. Adverse event rates comparable to placebo even when therapy initiated within 72 h of PCI. Events such as AKI, hypotension, or volume depletion infrequent and not significantly different. Note: exclusion of severely impaired renal function/hemodynamic instability means results should not be generalized to the most vulnerable post-MI populations.

## 4. Safety Considerations

### 4.1 Hemodynamic Thresholds: When Not to Start
- Key eligibility criteria: SBP >= 100 mmHg, Killip class <= II, no ongoing escalation of inotropes/vasopressors.
- Borderline SBP (90-100 mmHg): consider temporary withholding or dose reduction of loop diuretics; reassess after 12-24 h once hemodynamically stable.

### 4.2 Renal Function and eGFR 'Dip': What is Benign
- **Early, well-recognized effect: initial decline in eGFR, typically within 10-20% of baseline, occurring within days of initiation.**
- **Predominantly hemodynamically mediated via TGF (afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction decreases intraglomerular pressure, mitigates hyperfiltration) [refs 12,24].**
- **The initial decline is benign, usually stabilizes within 1-2 weeks, usually unaccompanied by structural renal injury [ref 21], and is followed by long-term preservation of renal function, with evidence that SGLT2 inhibitors slow progression of CKD [refs 21,22].**
- **The initial decline does not predict long-term renal function loss.**
- In patients with suspected/evolving AKI (KDIGO stage 1+), initiation should be deferred until renal function stabilized.
- **Therapy should be temporarily withheld if serum creatinine rises by more than 30% from baseline within the first week, or if ongoing volume depletion (vomiting, diarrhea, excessive diuresis) [ref 23].**
- Initiation in patients with markedly reduced GFRs generally discouraged because of the anticipated early decline in GFR.

### 4.3 Contrast Nephropathy: Procedural Context Matters
- PCI/angiography expose patients to contrast media, contributor to contrast-associated AKI (CA-AKI).
- Chronic SGLT2 inhibitor use associated with significant reduction in incidence of CI-AKI, with reported risk reductions up to ~48-63% compared with non-use in type 2 diabetes cohorts undergoing coronary angiography or PCI [ref 25].
- To reduce CA-AKI risk, SGLT2i initiation should ideally be delayed for at least 24-48 h following PCI, particularly in borderline renal function or multiple contrast exposures.
- Avoid concurrent NSAIDs and nephrotoxic antimicrobials [refs 25,28].

### 4.4 Rare but Serious Events: DKA and Genital Infections
- Euglycemic DKA (euDKA): rare but potentially life-threatening; risk factors include type 1 DM, recent fasting/ketogenic diets, perioperative stress, insulin deficiency/omission.
- Genital mycotic infection risk increases approximately 2- to 6-fold among SGLT2i users; most infections mild-to-moderate, respond to standard antifungal therapy; routine discontinuation not typically required [refs 29,30].

## 5. The START Algorithm

**S — Stable Hemodynamics:** SBP >= 100 mmHg (ideally lying and standing), Killip I-II, no escalation of inotropes/vasopressors in preceding 12-24 h, oxygen saturation >= 92% on room air or stable low-flow oxygen, able to tolerate oral medications.

**T — Tubular Reserve:** eGFR >= 25 mL/min/1.73 m². Creatinine stability over 24-48 h. Defer if creatinine rise >= 0.3 mg/dL, KDIGO stage 1+ AKI, or recent contrast exposure with renal deterioration. CKD stage 3b eligible with close monitoring [refs 22,33].

**A — Acid-Base Status:** No ongoing acidosis, serum bicarbonate >= 22 mmol/L, normal anion gap, no significant urinary/serum ketones. Avoid in ketogenic diets/prolonged fasting or recent DKA/poorly controlled glycemia [ref 34].

**R — Risk Factors/Contraindications:** Contraindications include type 1 DM, recent DKA (within 3 months), perioperative state (e.g., recent CABG), severe dehydration, significant GI losses, hypotensive volume depletion.

**T — Timing:** Optimal window 24-72 h post-PCI, once hemodynamically stable, diuretic and ACE inhibitor/ARB/MRA therapy reconciled, no active renal decline. Coordinate with cardiology, nephrology, diabetes teams.

### Table 2 START checklist thresholds
| Letter | Safety Check | Pragmatic Threshold | Trial Precedent |
|---|---|---|---|
| S | Stable hemodynamics | SBP >= 100 mmHg; Killip <= II; no inotropes/vasopressors within 24 h | DAPA-MI, EMPACT-MI |
| T | Tubular reserve | eGFR >= 25 mL/min/1.73 m² AND <30% creatinine rise over 48 h | EMPRESS-MI (>=20); EMMY (>=45) |
| A | Acid-base stability | Serum HCO3- >= 22 mmol/L, normal anion gap, negative/trace ketones | Expert consensus (DKA case series) |
| R | Risk factors/contraindications | Exclude type 1 DM, recent DKA (<3 mo), cardiogenic shock, peri-operative status | Uniform across all five trials |
| T | Timing window | 24-72 h after successful PCI, once hemodynamics stable | EMMY (<=72 h), EMPRESS-MI (median 3 d) |

**How to use:** Tick all five boxes before the first dose. Re-check vitals q8-12 h for 48 h; repeat creatinine/ketones at 48-72 h. No randomized data yet in Killip III-IV, SBP <100 mmHg, or eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73 m².

### 5.6 Monitoring Plan
- BP and heart rate every 8-12 h during first 48 h; vigilance for postural/symptomatic hypotension.
- Creatinine and eGFR at 48-72 h and again at 1-2 weeks; serum ketones if acidosis symptoms.
- Monitor volume depletion with loop diuretics (dose reduction if orthostatic); avoid concurrent NSAIDs; sick-day rules education prior to discharge.

## 6. Future Directions and Research Gaps
- EMPA-STEMI evaluating early empagliflozin in STEMI post-PCI, focus on myocardial salvage and ventricular remodeling [ref 20].
- EMPRESS-MI investigating in-hospital SGLT2 initiation in post-MI patients with diabetes [ref 38].
- Evidence gaps: cardiogenic shock STEMI excluded from current trials; limited data on advanced CKD stage 4-5 initiation, particularly regarding AKI risk; role of invasive hemodynamic monitoring (e.g., right heart catheterization) undefined.

## 7. Conclusion
In STEMI patients, judicious initiation of SGLT2i is a promising strategy to mitigate cardiorenal complications. When commenced 24-72 h post-PCI in clinically stable patients, existing evidence indicates no consistent safety concerns regarding hypotension, AKI, or DKA. **The early decline in eGFR observed with therapy initiation is typically mild, predictable, and self-limiting.** The START algorithm helps clinicians identify suitable candidates. Routine incorporation into STEMI pathways awaits confirmation from ongoing outcome trials.
