# Hyperkalemia Risk with Finerenone: Results from the FIDELIO-DKD Trial

## Abstract

**Background.** Finerenone reduced risk of cardiorenal outcomes in patients with CKD and type 2 diabetes in the FIDELIO-DKD trial. We report incidences and risk factors for hyperkalemia with finerenone and placebo in FIDELIO-DKD.

**Methods.** This post hoc safety analysis defined hyperkalemia as >=mild or >=moderate based on serum potassium concentrations of >5.5 or >6.0 mmol/L, respectively, assessed at all regular visits. Cumulative incidences of hyperkalemia were based on the Aalen-Johansen estimator using death as competing risk. A multivariate Cox proportional hazards model identified significant independent predictors of hyperkalemia. Restricted cubic splines assessed relationships between short-term post-baseline changes in serum potassium or eGFR and subsequent hyperkalemia risk. During the study, serum potassium levels guided drug dosing. Patients in either group who experienced >=mild hyperkalemia had the study drug withheld until serum potassium was <=5.0 mmol/L; then the drug was restarted at the 10 mg daily dose. Placebo-treated patients underwent sham treatment interruption and downtitration.

**Results.** Over 2.6 years' median follow-up, 597 of 2785 (21.4%) and 256 of 2775 (9.2%) patients treated with finerenone and placebo, respectively, experienced treatment-emergent >=mild hyperkalemia; 126 of 2802 (4.5%) and 38 of 2796 (1.4%) patients, respectively, experienced moderate hyperkalemia. Independent risk factors for >=mild hyperkalemia were higher serum potassium, lower eGFR, increased urine albumin-creatinine ratio, younger age, female sex, beta-blocker use, and finerenone assignment. Diuretic or sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitor use reduced risk. In both groups, short-term increases in serum potassium and decreases in eGFR were associated with subsequent hyperkalemia. At month 4, the magnitude of increased hyperkalemia risk for any change from baseline was smaller with finerenone than with placebo.

**Conclusions.** Finerenone was independently associated with hyperkalemia. However, routine potassium monitoring and hyperkalemia management strategies employed in FIDELIO-DKD minimized the impact of hyperkalemia, providing a basis for clinical use of finerenone.

## Significance Statement

Hyperkalemia is common after treatment with a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA). In the FIDELIO-DKD randomized trial, the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone improved cardiorenal outcomes, but was associated with a twofold higher risk of hyperkalemia versus placebo, consistent across patient subgroups. Short-term increases in serum potassium and decreases in eGFR with finerenone or placebo were associated with subsequent hyperkalemia; at month 4, the magnitude of the increased hyperkalemia risk for any given change from baseline was smaller with finerenone than with placebo. Routine potassium monitoring, with temporary treatment interruption and dose reduction in the event of hyperkalemia, is necessary for the safe use of finerenone to protect the kidneys and cardiovascular system of patients with CKD and T2D.

## Methods (key points)

- FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993): phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter trial of finerenone in advanced CKD + T2D, on top of maximum tolerated dose of a single RAS inhibitor (ACEi or ARB, not both).
- Inclusion required serum K+ <=4.8 mmol/L at both run-in and screening visits. CKD defined as: (1) UACR 30-<300 mg/g, eGFR 25-<60 ml/min/1.73 m2, plus diabetic retinopathy history; or (2) UACR 300-<=5000 mg/g and eGFR 25-<75 ml/min/1.73 m2. Exclusion: treatment with steroidal MRA, renin inhibitor, or potassium-sparing diuretic that could not be discontinued >=4 weeks before screening.
- Randomized 1:1 to oral finerenone or placebo. Initial dose 10 or 20 mg once daily based on eGFR (25-<60 or >=60 ml/min/1.73 m2, respectively).
- **Potassium management algorithm (Figure 1):** K+ measured at month 1, month 4, and every 4 months thereafter (plus at uptitration/interruption visits).
  - If serum K+ <=4.8 mmol/L: uptitrate from 10 to 20 mg od (if eGFR decrease <30% from last value) or maintain 20 mg.
  - If K+ >4.8-<=5.5 mmol/L: continue same dose.
  - If K+ >5.5 mmol/L: study drug temporarily withheld, K+ rechecked within 72 hours; if K+ <=5.0 mmol/L, restarted at 10 mg od; otherwise withheld until <=5.0 mmol/L.
  - Placebo recipients underwent sham up/downtitration and sham interruptions in the same manner (double-blind).
- Hyperkalemia thresholds aligned to KDIGO: >5.5 mmol/L (>=mild), >6.0 mmol/L (>=moderate), by central laboratory at every study visit.
- Multivariate Cox model (model 1) covariates: age, sex, eGFR categories, serum K+ categories, log2-transformed UACR, RAS inhibitor dosing, diuretic use, beta-blocker use, SGLT-2i use, study drug assignment. Significance threshold P<0.05.

## Results (key numbers)

### Serum K+ at run-in, screening, baseline
- 13,911 enrolled; 8177 did not meet eligibility; 5734 randomized.
- At run-in, K+ available for 12,010/13,911 patients; 2181 (18.2%) had K+ >4.8 mmol/L and were excluded.
- At screening, 640/7114 (9.0%) had K+ >4.8 mmol/L and were screen-failed.
- Baseline mean serum K+: 4.37 +/- 0.46 mmol/L (finerenone) and 4.38 +/- 0.46 mmol/L (placebo).
- Baseline K+ <=4.8 mmol/L vs >4.8 mmol/L: 4889 of 5658 (86.4%) vs 769 of 5658 (13.6%).
- 390 (6.9%) had baseline K+ >5.0 mmol/L.

### Changes in serum K+ during study
- Maximum mean difference between groups: 0.23 mmol/L at month 4, consistent across predefined screening eGFR categories.
- Treatment-emergent >=mild hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5): 597/2785 (21.4%) finerenone vs 256/2775 (9.2%) placebo.
- Treatment-emergent >=moderate hyperkalemia (K+ >6.0): 126/2802 (4.5%) finerenone vs 38/2796 (1.4%) placebo.
- At month 1: K+ >5.5 in 86/2742 (3.1%) finerenone vs 34/2730 (1.2%) placebo; K+ >6.0 in 34/2730 (1.2%) finerenone vs 4/2749 (0.1%) placebo.

### Multivariate risk factors for >=mild hyperkalemia (Figure 3, HR [95% CI])
- Age: 18-44 = 1.03 (0.67-1.59); 45-64 = 1.09 (0.94-1.25); 65-74 = Ref; >=75 = 0.81 (0.65-0.99); P=0.0465.
- Sex: male vs female = 0.85 (0.74-0.97); P=0.0200.
- Serum K+ (mmol/L): <=4.1 = 0.44 (0.35-0.55); >4.1-<=4.5 = Ref; >4.5-<=4.8 = 1.53 (1.29-1.82); >4.8-<=5.0 = 2.78 (2.25-3.44); >5.0 = 4.18 (3.45-5.05); P<0.0001.
- eGFR (ml/min/1.73 m2): <25 = 2.04 (1.35-3.07); 25-<45 = 1.51 (1.19-1.91); 45-<60 = 1.02 (0.80-1.32); >=60 = Ref; P<0.0001.
- Log2 of UACR (per 1 unit / doubling) = 1.12 (1.07-1.17); P<0.0001.
- Diuretic use (yes vs no) = 0.76 (0.66-0.87); P<0.0001.
- SGLT-2i use (yes vs no) = 0.45 (0.27-0.75); P=0.0023.
- Label-recommended dose of ACEi or ARB: <=min = 0.96 (0.82-1.12); >min but <max = 0.97 (0.83-1.15); >=max = Ref; P=0.8508.
- Beta-blocker use (yes vs no) = 1.18 (1.04-1.35); P=0.0133.
- **Treatment assignment: finerenone vs placebo = 2.13 (1.86-2.45); P<0.0001.**

Narrative summary of HRs: higher baseline K+ increased >=mild hyperkalemia risk 1.5-, 2.8-, and 4.2-fold at K+ of 4.5-<4.8, 4.8-5.0, and >5.0 mmol/L vs 4.1-4.5. Risk increased 1.5-fold and twofold as eGFR dropped below 45 and 25. Every doubling of UACR = 1.1-fold increased risk. Finerenone assignment doubled the risk vs placebo. Increased hyperkalemia risk with finerenone was consistent across subgroups (sex, baseline CKD severity, baseline medication use). Significant treatment interactions were noted for risk by baseline serum K+ and age.

### Short-term changes and future risk (Figure 4, restricted cubic splines)
- Larger increases in serum K+ from baseline to month 1 (Fig 4A) or month 4 (Fig 4B) associated with higher subsequent >=mild hyperkalemia risk (P<0.001 both time points; spline).
- At month 1, magnitude of increased risk for any given K+ increase was similar between groups (finerenone vs placebo P=0.233).
- At month 4, relationship differed significantly between groups (P=0.011): a placebo recipient with 0.5-mmol/L K+ increase had ~3.4-fold higher risk vs no change, whereas with finerenone a 0.5-mmol/L increase was associated with a twofold higher risk vs no change.
- Greater decrease in eGFR associated with higher subsequent risk. Month 1 similar between groups (P=0.134); month 4 differed significantly (P=0.034). Placebo patient with 5-ml/min/1.73 m2 eGFR decrease had 1.4-fold higher risk; finerenone recipient with same decrease had same risk as no change.

### Management of hyperkalemia (Table 2 - investigator-reported action with highest effect)
- Total investigator-reported hyperkalemia-related AEs: 516/2827 (18.3%) finerenone; 255/2833 (9.0%) placebo.
- Drug withdrawn: finerenone 64 (2.3%) vs placebo 25 (0.9%).
- Drug interrupted: finerenone 310 (11.0%) vs placebo 146 (5.2%).
- Dose reduced: finerenone 9 (0.3%) vs placebo 6 (0.2%).
- Dose not changed: finerenone 127 (4.5%) vs placebo 74 (2.6%).
- Not applicable/unknown: finerenone 6 (0.3%) vs placebo 4 (0.1%).
- At baseline, 136 (2.4%) on potassium binder (mostly calcium/sodium polystyrene sulfonate). During trial, 307 (10.9%) finerenone and 183 (6.5%) placebo started a potassium binder.
- Only one patient (placebo group) received dialysis while hospitalized for hyperkalemia.
- Most frequent highest-effect action reported by investigator was temporary withdrawal of study drug.

### Efficacy in subgroups by hyperkalemia risk
- Effects of finerenone vs placebo on primary composite kidney, key secondary composite CV, and all-cause mortality were similar among patients with low (<4.8 mmol/L), mid (>=4.8-5.0 mmol/L), or high (>5.0 mmol/L) baseline serum K+, and among patients with moderate-to-severe eGFR impairment at baseline.

## Discussion (selected points)
- Finerenone significantly reduced primary composite kidney outcome by 18% (absolute risk reduction 3.3%) and key secondary composite CV outcome by 14% (absolute risk reduction 1.8%) vs placebo (from primary FIDELIO-DKD report).
- Hyperkalemia was about twofold higher with finerenone than placebo and was the only AE/serious AE to demonstrate excess cases with finerenone.
- Number-needed-to-harm context: in FIDELIO-DKD, 71 patients would have needed to be prescribed finerenone before one permanently stopped the drug (vs 18 for dual RAS blockade in VA NEPHRON-D for severe hyperkalemia; 45 and 26 for ALTITUDE and ORIENT dual RAS studies).
- VA NEPHRON-D severe hyperkalemia (K+ >6.0 or requiring ER/hospitalization/dialysis): 4.4% (losartan) vs 9.9% (losartan + lisinopril).
- Finerenone's low hyperkalemia risk attributed to short half-life (2-3 hours in CKD), absence of active metabolites, balanced kidney-heart distribution (rodent models), and novel MR blockade mechanism. Contrasted with spironolactone (long half-life, multiple active metabolites, ~6:1 kidney:heart tissue distribution).
- Potassium monitoring frequency in FIDELIO-DKD similar to 2012 KDIGO recommendations (3-4 times/year for UACR>300 and eGFR<60; 2-3 times/year for UACR 30-300 and eGFR 15-59).

## Conclusion
Finerenone was associated with a low absolute risk of clinically relevant hyperkalemia, with only a small proportion of events having a clinical effect. Elevated serum K+ and lower eGFR at treatment start did not abrogate the kidney/CV benefits of finerenone. Routine potassium monitoring is appropriate to manage hyperkalemia risk.
