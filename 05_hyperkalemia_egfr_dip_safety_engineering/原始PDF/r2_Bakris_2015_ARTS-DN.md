# Effect of Finerenone on Albuminuria in Patients With Diabetic Nephropathy: A Randomized Clinical Trial (ARTS-DN)

> Bakris GL, Agarwal R, Chan JC, Cooper ME, Gansevoort RT, Haller H, Remuzzi G, Rossing P, Schmieder RE, Nowack C, Kolkhof P, Joseph A, Pieper A, Kimmeskamp-Kirschbaum N, Ruilope LM; for the Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonist Tolerability Study–Diabetic Nephropathy (ARTS-DN) Study Group.
> **JAMA. 2015;314(9):884-894.** doi:10.1001/jama.2015.10081. PMID: 26325557. Trial registration: ClinicalTrials.gov NCT01874431.

---

## Abstract (Structured)

**Importance.** Steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs), when added to a renin-angiotensin system (RAS) blocker, further reduce proteinuria in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) but may be underused because of a high risk of adverse events.

**Objective.** To evaluate the safety and efficacy of different oral doses of the nonsteroidal MRA finerenone, given for 90 days to patients with diabetes and high or very high albuminuria who are receiving an ACE inhibitor or an angiotensin receptor blocker (ARB).

**Design, Setting, and Participants.** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group study conducted at **148 sites in 23 countries**. Patients recruited June 2013 to February 2014; study completed August 2014. Of **1501 screened** patients, **823 were randomized** and **821 received study drug**.

**Interventions.** Oral, once-daily finerenone at 1.25 mg/d (n=96), 2.5 mg/d (n=92), 5 mg/d (n=100), 7.5 mg/d (n=97), 10 mg/d (n=98), 15 mg/d (n=125), and top-dose group (n=119) or matching placebo (n=94) for 90 days.

> NOTE on top dose: the printed abstract lists "25 mg/d, n = 119," but the Methods, Figures, and all Tables throughout the article describe the highest-dose group as **20 mg/d (n=119)** ("finerenone, 1.25 to 20 mg once daily"). The 20-mg/d designation is used consistently in the body of the paper and in all results below.

**Main Outcomes and Measures.** Primary outcome = ratio of urinary albumin-creatinine ratio (UACR) at day 90 vs baseline. Safety end points = changes from baseline in serum potassium and eGFR.

**Results.** Mean age 64.2 years; 78% male. At baseline, 36.7% had very high albuminuria (UACR ≥300 mg/g) and 40.0% had eGFR ≤60 mL/min/1.73 m². Finerenone showed a dose-dependent reduction in UACR. The primary outcome — placebo-corrected mean ratio of UACR at day 90 relative to baseline — was reduced in the 7.5-, 10-, 15-, and 20-mg/d groups:
> - 7.5 mg/d: **0.79** (90% CI, 0.68–0.91; P = .004)
> - 10 mg/d: **0.76** (90% CI, 0.65–0.88; P = .001)
> - 15 mg/d: **0.67** (90% CI, 0.58–0.77; P < .001)
> - 20 mg/d: **0.62** (90% CI, 0.54–0.72; P < .001)

The prespecified secondary outcome of hyperkalemia leading to discontinuation was not observed in the placebo and finerenone 10-mg/d groups; incidences in the finerenone 7.5-, 15-, and 20-mg/d groups were **2.1%, 3.2%, and 1.7%**, respectively. There were no differences between placebo and finerenone groups in the incidence of an eGFR decrease ≥30% or in incidences of adverse events and serious adverse events.

**Conclusions and Relevance.** Among patients with diabetic nephropathy, most receiving an ACE inhibitor or ARB, the addition of finerenone compared with placebo resulted in improvement in the UACR. Further trials are needed to compare finerenone with other active medications.

## Background

- Reviews of RCTs show steroidal MRAs added to a RAS blocker further reduce proteinuria in CKD (diabetic and nondiabetic). However, **eplerenone and spironolactone increase the risk of hyperkalemia in stage ≥3 CKD by as much as 3- to 8-fold.**
- Finerenone (BAY 94-8862) is a novel nonsteroidal MRA with greater receptor selectivity than spironolactone and better receptor affinity than eplerenone in vitro. In preclinical studies, equinatriuretic doses of finerenone provided greater reduction in proteinuria and end-organ damage than eplerenone.
- In the earlier ARTS trial, finerenone 2.5–10 mg/d reduced albuminuria from baseline in patients with CKD and heart failure, with a lower incidence of hyperkalemia than spironolactone.
- ARTS-DN was designed to compare efficacy and safety of different once-daily oral doses of finerenone vs placebo in patients with type 2 diabetes and persistent albuminuria (UACR ≥30 mg/g) receiving a RAS blocker.

---

## Methods

### Study Design
- Multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group **phase 2B** study comparing finerenone 1.25 to 20 mg once daily vs placebo, added to standard of care with a RAS blocker.
- Started June 2013; clinically completed August 2014. Conformed to Declaration of Helsinki and GCP guidelines; ethics/IRB approval obtained; written informed consent from all.
- Initial randomization: equal proportions to finerenone 1.25–10 mg/d or placebo + RAS blocker for 90 days. Central randomization via interactive voice/web response system with computer-generated lists; participants, investigators, and sponsor clinical team blinded.
- The **15-mg/d and 20-mg/d groups were added** on the recommendation of an independent data monitoring committee after review of ongoing safety data; randomization adapted for approximately balanced ratios.
- Randomization **stratified by region and severity of albuminuria** (high [UACR 30 to <300 mg/g] or very high [≥300 mg/g]).
- Planned ~75 patients valid for the full analysis set per group (up to 90 per group to increase safety data for very high albuminuria). Intended that ≥35% of patients have very high albuminuria (UACR ≥300 mg/g).

### Patients (key eligibility)
- **Included:** type 2 diabetes; albuminuria (UACR ≥30 mg/g); **eGFR >30 mL/min/1.73 m²**; on at least the minimum recommended dosage of a RAS blocker before screening; **serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L at screening**.
- Patients with eGFR 30–45 mL/min/1.73 m² had to be receiving a non–potassium-sparing diuretic without adjustment for ≥4 weeks beforehand.
- **Excluded:** concomitant eplerenone, spironolactone, any renin inhibitor, or a potassium-sparing diuretic that could not be discontinued for the run-in and treatment periods.

### Serum Potassium Monitoring
- No dietary sodium/potassium restriction advice; patients kept normal diet.
- Starting potassium-lowering agents (e.g., sodium/calcium polystyrene sulfonate, insulin, glucose infusion) not permitted during study-drug treatment (except non–potassium-sparing diuretics). If hyperkalemia occurred, study treatment was discontinued prior to starting a potassium-lowering agent.
- Potassium supplementation stopped prior to randomization if levels normal; continued/restarted if low.

### Procedures
- Urine and blood assessed in central laboratories (Europe, Asia, US). Urinary albumin by immunonephelometry; urine creatinine by Jaffe reaction. eGFR by CKD-EPI equation. HbA1c by HPLC.

### End Points
- **Primary:** ratio of UACR at day 90 vs baseline.
- **Further efficacy/safety:** proportions with adverse and serious adverse events; change in serum potassium; incidence of serum potassium ≥5.6 mmol/L and >6.0 mmol/L; incidence of eGFR decrease ≥30%, ≥40%, and ≥57% (equivalent to doubling of serum creatinine); change in UACR at days 30 and 60 vs baseline.

### Statistical Analysis
- **Safety analysis set:** all randomized patients who took ≥1 dose with posttreatment data. **Full analysis set (modified ITT):** safety population with baseline and ≥1 postbaseline UACR value. **Per-protocol set:** full analysis set with valid day-90 UACR and no major protocol deviation.
- Primary and supportive analyses on full analysis set; safety data on safety analysis set.
- Log of UACR assumed normally distributed; treatment effect evaluated as ratios to baseline. Primary analysis: ANCOVA on log-transformed day-90/baseline UACR ratios (factors: treatment group, region, type of albuminuria; log-baseline UACR as covariate nested within albuminuria type); prespecified linear contrast tested at 1-sided α=.05, followed by hierarchical pairwise comparisons vs placebo.
- **Last-observation-carried-forward (LOCF)** imputation for missing day-90 UACR (higher of premature-discontinuation and follow-up measurements). Sensitivity analyses across multiple imputation methods.
- Sample size: 75 valid patients/group gives ≥83% power to demonstrate dose-dependent effect for 7 groups (doses up to 15 mg/d) using linear contrast, at 1-sided α=.05. With 15- and 20-mg/d groups added, 600 patients required total; ~1500 enrolled (assuming ≤50% screen-failure) and 823 randomized (assuming 10% dropout). Software: SAS v9.2; sample-size calc via Query Advisor 7.0.
- Post hoc: Pearson correlations between day-90/baseline UACR ratio and change in systolic BP and change in eGFR (baseline→day 90); proportion of patients with UACR decrease ≥30%, ≥40%, ≥50%; CKD stage 3 subgroup analyses for serum potassium and eGFR; categorization of baseline ACEi/ARB dosages.

## Results

### Patient Flow / Demographics
- 1501 screened → **823 randomized** (Figure 1); 764 patients (93%) completed treatment. One patient each in 7.5- and 20-mg/d groups took no study medication (→ 821 received drug). Nine patients had no postbaseline UACR, leaving **812 patients in the primary analysis**.
- Mean age 64.2 y; 78% male. At baseline: **301 patients (36.7%) very high albuminuria (UACR ≥300 mg/g)**; **328 (40.0%) eGFR ≤60 mL/min/1.73 m²**.
- Screening exclusions (Figure 1): 635 did not meet inclusion criteria (of these: 412 no diagnosis of diabetic nephropathy; 106 serum potassium >4.8 mmol/L at screening; 42 UACR in first morning sample <30 mg/g; 75 other), 37 declined, 6 other.
- Baseline RAS therapy (post hoc): ~45% receiving an ACE inhibitor; ~one-quarter received RAS inhibition below (2.6%) or at (24.8%) the minimal recommended dosage. Overall 72.7% received a dosage above the minimum recommended (Physicians' Desk Reference).

### Primary End Point — UACR
- Dose-dependent relationship across all doses (1-sided F test for linear contrast, **P < .001**).
- Placebo-corrected UACR day-90/baseline ratios (full analysis set n=812):
  - 7.5 mg/d: **0.79** (90% CI, 0.68–0.91; P = .004)
  - 10 mg/d: **0.76** (90% CI, 0.65–0.88; P = .001)
  - 15 mg/d: **0.67** (90% CI, 0.58–0.77; P < .001)
  - 20 mg/d: **0.62** (90% CI, 0.54–0.72; P < .001)
  - → corresponds to a **significant UACR reduction of 21% to 38%** in the 7.5–20 mg/d groups vs placebo (Discussion).
- Placebo-corrected ratio decreased over time for 7.5-, 15-, and 20-mg/d groups; lowest ratio at day 60 with slight increase at day 90 for the 10-mg group.
- Multiple-imputation post hoc results were not different from LOCF.
- Exploratory interaction tests: region (P = .30) and severity of albuminuria (P = .80) — no interaction with treatment. A smaller treatment effect was observed within the very high albuminuria group vs the high albuminuria group; in very high albuminuria, 90% CIs for placebo-corrected ratios spanned unity for all finerenone doses.
- **UACR decrease ≥50% from baseline to day 90** (post hoc): **13.6% placebo**; **17.2% (7.5 mg/d), 17.2% (10 mg/d), 33.6% (15 mg/d), 40.2% (20 mg/d)**.

### Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR)
- Placebo-corrected least-squares mean differences in eGFR (baseline → day 90):
  - 7.5 mg/d: **−1.8 mL/min/1.73 m²** (95% CI, −4.4 to 0.8)
  - 10 mg/d: **−2.6** (95% CI, −5.1 to −0.04)
  - 15 mg/d: **−2.2** (95% CI, −4.6 to 0.2)
  - 20 mg/d: **−2.4** (95% CI, −4.9 to 0.0)
- **Changes in the finerenone groups were reversible 30 days after completion of treatment (day 120 follow-up).**
- Incidences of an eGFR decrease ≥40% at any time postbaseline were similar between placebo and finerenone 1.25-, 7.5-, 10-, 15-, and 20-mg/d groups (no cases in 2.5- and 5-mg/d groups). **No occurrences of eGFR decrease ≥57%.**

### Adverse Events (Table 3)
- No difference in overall incidence of adverse events or serious adverse events between finerenone and placebo groups; no relevant increase in AEs across finerenone doses.
- **Any adverse event:** 50.0% (placebo) vs 52.7% (all finerenone, total n=821).
- **Any serious adverse event:** 3.2% (placebo) vs 4.6% total.
- **Drug-related serious adverse events occurred in 1.5% of patients receiving finerenone** (12/821 total).
- Discontinuation due to adverse event: 3.2% placebo vs 4.1% total. Discontinuation due to serious AE: 1.1% placebo vs 2.2% total.

### Serum Potassium — Key Safety Signal
- **12 of 821 patients (1.5%), all receiving finerenone, experienced serum potassium increases ≥5.6 mmol/L leading to study-treatment discontinuation.**
  - Incidences: **2.1% (1.25 mg/d), 1.1% (2.5 mg/d), 1.0% (5 mg/d), 2.1% (7.5 mg/d), 3.2% (15 mg/d), 1.7% (20 mg/d)** — **no cases in the finerenone 10-mg/d group**.
  - Overall incidence for the **7.5- to 20-mg/d groups (the efficacious doses) was 1.8%**.
- **Serum potassium >6.0 mmol/L** was observed only in the finerenone 1.25-mg/d group (2.1%; n=2) and 15-mg/d group (0.8%; n=1) — **not in the 7.5-, 10-, or 20-mg/d groups** (3 cases total: 2 in 1.25 mg/d, 1 in 15 mg/d).
- **CKD stage 3 subgroup (post hoc):** incidences of serum potassium ≥5.6 mmol/L were **2.7% (1.25 mg/d), 5.4% (7.5 mg/d), 4.1% (15 mg/d), 6.3% (20 mg/d)**, with no cases in placebo, 2.5-, 5-, and 10-mg groups. No cases of serum potassium >6.0 mmol/L in this subgroup except the 1.25-mg/d group.

### Blood Pressure
- Placebo-corrected least-squares mean differences in systolic BP (baseline → day 90):
  - 7.5 mg/d: **−2.8 mm Hg** (95% CI, −6.5 to 0.8)
  - 10 mg/d: **0.1 mm Hg** (95% CI, −3.5 to 3.8)
  - 15 mg/d: **−5.1 mm Hg** (95% CI, −8.5 to −1.7)
  - 20 mg/d: **−4.7 mm Hg** (95% CI, −8.2 to −1.3)
- Post hoc: no meaningful correlation across treatment groups between the UACR ratio and change in systolic BP or change in eGFR (baseline → day 90) — i.e., UACR reduction was largely independent of BP and eGFR changes.

## Discussion (Key Points)

- First multicenter clinical trial of finerenone combined with a RAS inhibitor in diabetic nephropathy, investigating an optimal dose for an outcome study.
- Finerenone reduced placebo-corrected UACR at day 90 dose-dependently, with a significant reduction of **21% to 38%** in the 7.5–20 mg/d groups.
- Contrast with steroidal MRAs: a systematic review documented increased hyperkalemia with steroidal MRAs + RAS blockers vs RAS blockade alone (dropout due to hyperkalemia of 8% and 17% in 2 of 8 studies). In one spironolactone study, clinically significant hyperkalemia (K⁺ >6.0 mmol/L) occurred in 52% of patients on high-dose ACEi + low-dose spironolactone over 48 weeks. Higher-than-recommended ACEi dosing may have contributed to higher hyperkalemia rates in those studies.
- In ARTS-DN, hyperkalemia and subsequent discontinuation occurred in **1.8%** of patients on finerenone 7.5–20 mg/d vs none on placebo. The **lack of a significant decrease in eGFR may contribute to the low hyperkalemia risk**; also patients with screening K⁺ >4.8 mmol/L (a known hyperkalemia risk marker) were excluded.
- Only a modest BP reduction at the highest finerenone dose (as in ARTS). This differs from steroidal MRAs, possibly because steroidal MRAs cross the blood-brain barrier and act centrally; [¹⁴C]-finerenone was not found in the brain after oral application in preclinical studies.

### Limitations (author-stated)
- Dose-finding study **lacking an active control group**.
- **60% of patients had eGFR >60 mL/min/1.73 m²**, putting them at lower risk of hyperkalemia.
- Albuminuria reductions are highly correlated with slowed CKD progression but are **not a validated surrogate marker** for renal outcomes such as time to dialysis.
- Short study duration precluded assessment of long-term effects on CKD progression or antifibrotic/anti-inflammatory effects.

---

## Conclusions

Among patients with diabetic nephropathy, most receiving an ACE inhibitor or an ARB, the addition of finerenone compared with placebo resulted in improvement in the UACR. Further trials are needed to compare finerenone with other active medications.

## Relevance to Folder Topic (05_hyperkalemia_egfr_dip_safety_engineering)

Pivotal phase 2B dose-finding trial that established the **hyperkalemia and eGFR-dip safety profile** of finerenone motivating the later FIDELIO-DKD / FIGARO-DKD outcome program:

- **Hyperkalemia:** discontinuation for K⁺ ≥5.6 mmol/L in only 1.8% of the efficacious 7.5–20 mg/d groups; K⁺ >6.0 mmol/L in just 3 patients overall (none on placebo). CKD stage 3 subgroup had higher rates (up to 6.3% for ≥5.6 mmol/L at 20 mg/d) — an early signal that baseline renal function modulates hyperkalemia risk.
- **eGFR dip:** small placebo-corrected eGFR declines (−1.8 to −2.6 mL/min/1.73 m²) that were **reversible by day 120 (30 days off treatment)**; no eGFR decrease ≥57% (no creatinine doubling). This reversible acute eGFR-dip pattern is central to the "safety engineering" narrative of nonsteroidal MRAs.
- **Design safeguards for hyperkalemia:** screening K⁺ ≤4.8 mmol/L cutoff, mandatory non–potassium-sparing diuretic for eGFR 30–45, exclusion of eplerenone/spironolactone/renin inhibitors/K-sparing diuretics, and no permitted co-administration of potassium binders during treatment.
