## Commentary

The past decade of chronic kidney disease (CKD) in type 2 diabetes mellitus has ushered in an exciting era of multidrug therapy. Building on the mainstay of renin-angiotensin system inhibitors, the recent emergence of sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 inhibitors (SGLT2i), nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs), and glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists now form the "4 pillars" of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) in CKD.[1] However, the potential additive kidney and cardiovascular benefits must be balanced by compounding adverse effects, especially the risk of hyperkalemia with the combination of the nonsteroidal MRA, finerenone, with background renin–angiotensin system inhibitors.[2]

Select observational data suggested that the risk of hyperkalemia was reduced in SGLT2i users.[3] However, in a prespecified secondary analysis of the CONFIDENCE (Combination Effect of finerenone and Empagliflozin in Participants with CKD the Type 2 Diabetes Using a Urinary Albumin-to Creatinine Ratio Endpoint) trial, randomization to combination therapy with finerenone and empagliflozin resulted in similar potassium increases and hyperkalemia risk as participants randomized to finerenone monotherapy, suggesting that initiating finerenone and SGLT2i simultaneously does not protect against MRA-mediated hyperkalemia.[4]

In a secondary analysis of the FIVE-STAR (randomized trial to evaluate the effects of finerenone on vascular stiffness and cardiorenal biomarkers in type 2 diabetes and CKD) trial,[5] the investigators examined whether the effects of finerenone on albuminuria and serum potassium differed by baseline GDMT use in CKD.[6] Across all background therapies (i.e., renin–angiotensin system inhibitors, SGLT2i, or glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists), finerenone reduced the urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio by 29% more than placebo over 24 weeks, and the background therapy did not influence the reduction in albuminuria. In terms of potassium homeostasis, finerenone increased potassium by 0.22 mEq/l between baseline and week 24 (4.2–4.4 mEq/l) compared with a 0.06 mEq/l decrease overall in the placebo group over the same time period (4.4–4.3 mEq/l). The use of SGLT2i significantly modified the potassium trajectory for finerenone: In those not on SGLT2i at baseline, finerenone increased potassium by 0.37 mEq/l, whereas those on SGLT2i experienced less finerenone-induced potassium increase (0.13 mEq/l; P-value for interaction = 0.02). Thus, initiation of finerenone on a background of SGLT2i appeared to result in less potassium increase than initiation of finerenone above a non-SGLT2i GDMT regimen.

On one level, this observation directly contrasts with the results of the CONFIDENCE trial, in which the simultaneous initiation of finerenone and empagliflozin did not attenuate the increase in potassium compared with finerenone alone. However, important distinctions between these 2 studies may help explain this discrepancy. In CONFIDENCE, finerenone and SGLT2i therapy were initiated concurrently, whereas the FIVE-STAR analysis evaluated the influence of finerenone on established use of other GDMTs, including SGLT2i, at baseline. The authors of the secondary analysis of FIVE-STAR note that the chronicity of SGLT2i administration before finerenone exposure may have influenced the potassium trajectory of finerenone. Although no head-to-head study has evaluated this hypothesis, some preclinical data support a time-dependent renal adaptation to SGLT2i that could alter potassium handling.

SGLT2i do not promote hypokalemia, but preclinical data suggest that increased distal sodium delivery from proximal inhibition of sodium-glucose cotransport leads to urinary potassium loss via epithelial sodium channel–mediated sodium reabsorption.[7] In a model of aldosterone-treated diabetic fatty rats, esaxerenone (a nonsteroidal MRA) induced hyperkalemia in isolation; however, the addition of dapagliflozin reduced hyperkalemia and was associated with preserved proximal tubular TWIK-related potassium channel 1 (encoded by Kcnk1) expression. These data suggest that SGLT2 inhibition may modify MRA-associated potassium handling via tubular mechanisms beyond simple osmotic diuresis, potentially becoming more effective with chronic SGLT2i exposure (Figure 1).[8]

Another open question is whether mild hyperkalemia in the context of CKD GDMT combination and uptitration is clinically relevant. The CONFIDENCE trial permitted drug continuation with serum potassium levels up to 5.5 mEq/l.[4] Observational data from a population with advanced CKD showed a U-shaped relationship between potassium and mortality risk, with an "optimal" potassium level of 4.9 mEq/l.[9] FIVE-STAR excluded patients with a potassium > 4.8 mEq/l, and no combination GDMT group exceeded an average serum potassium level > 4.6 mEq/l. Randomized trials designed to evaluate the safety of different potassium thresholds in this context are urgently needed.

It is also worth noting that because SGLT2i use was not randomized in FIVE-STAR, the potential for the introduction of confounding by indication existed, which was not the case in CONFIDENCE. For example, patients already prescribed SGLT2i have demonstrated tolerance of combination GDMT by definition, which may influence tolerance of additional GDMT, including potassium response. Additional limitations of this FIVE-STAR secondary analysis include the small sample sizes in the subgroups analyzed and the lack of methods to account for the multiplicity of comparisons.

Although plausible and encouraging, these results should be viewed as hypothesis-generating and supportive of a trial specifically designed to test whether the potassium response to finerenone varies with the timing of SGLT2i exposure. The cleanest design would randomize patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus and albuminuric CKD receiving stable renin-angiotensin system inhibitors, but not yet receiving SGLT2i or finerenone, to 1 of the following 3 sequencing strategies: (i) finerenone-first therapy, with SGLT2i added after the primary potassium assessment window; (ii) simultaneous initiation of finerenone and SGLT2i; or (iii) SGLT2i-first therapy, with finerenone added after a prespecified run-in period sufficient to allow tubular adaptation. The primary end point should be the change in serum potassium after finerenone initiation, supported by clinically actionable secondary end points, including incident potassium > 5.5 mEq/l, potassium > 6.0 mEq/l, treatment interruption, successful rechallenge, achieved finerenone dose, and albuminuria reduction. This design would directly test whether chronic SGLT2i exposure modifies the finerenone-associated risk of potassium while preserving the clinical relevance of GDMT sequencing in CKD.

In summary, this study provides further support for evidence-guided strategies for the combination and sequencing of GDMT in CKD to maximize potential kidney and cardiovascular benefits while balancing the risks inherent in select GDMTs, especially hyperkalemia.
