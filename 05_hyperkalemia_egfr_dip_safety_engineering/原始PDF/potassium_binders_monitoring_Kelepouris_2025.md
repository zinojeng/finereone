## Abstract

**Introduction:** The aim of this study was to highlight the benefits and provide a framework for integrated multispecialty team involvement in the management of hyperkalemia in patients with heart failure (HF) and kidney disease and to ensure that patients receive optimal medical therapy to improve their clinical outcomes.

**Case Presentation:** This report highlights a hypothetical "complex" case of a patient with an acute HF decompensation who experiences hyperkalemia following up-titration of renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitor (RAASi) therapy. Two hypothetical scenarios for treatment management are discussed; in the first, providers down-titrate guideline-directed RAASi to avoid hyperkalemia, whereas in the second, providers take a collaborative interdisciplinary approach to manage hyperkalemia directly with the aim of avoiding RAASi down-titration. In the first typical management scenario, down-titration of guideline-directed RAASi to prevent increases in serum potassium (serum K+) levels leads to the reoccurrence of symptoms and rehospitalization of the patient. In the second proposed management scenario, interdisciplinary team discussions around differing tolerances and approaches to raised serum K+ levels lead to maintenance of guideline-directed RAASi doses with the help of close monitoring of the patient, introduction of a low potassium diet, and prescription of potassium binder therapy.

**Conclusion:** Collaborative multispecialty team management of HF patients may enable successful management of hyperkalemia without the need for discontinuation of guideline-directed RAASi therapy. Collaboration could extend to regular virtual or face-to-face cardiorenal clinics, where complex cases can be discussed, and local guidelines and processes can be developed.

## Introduction

The 2019 Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) Controversies Conference report described heart failure (HF) and chronic kidney disease (CKD) as "concurrent chronic disease epidemics" [1]. The risk factors and pathophysiology of HF and CKD are intertwined, with the existence of one disease accelerating the development and progression of the other [1].

In both HF with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) and CKD, renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitor (RAASi) therapy is a cornerstone of guideline-directed medical treatment (GDMT) and is recommended at the highest tolerated dose [1, 2]. Despite evidence of effectiveness from clinical trials, RAASis, particularly mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, are frequently down-titrated or stopped owing to several factors such as hypotension, worsening kidney function, and/or hyperkalemia (HK) in clinical practice [3–6]. While reducing RAASi dosage can reverse HK, down-titration or discontinuation may contribute to worse outcomes in patients with cardiorenal disease [3]. This report highlights a hypothetical "complex" case of a patient with decompensated HF experiencing HK following up-titration of RAASi and discusses how to achieve optimal RAASi treatment and symptomatic relief by managing HK via a collaborative approach.

## Case Presentation

### Presentation and Clinical History

A female patient in her fifties presented to the emergency department with symptoms indicating acute HF decompensation: progressive shortness of breath on exertion, lower extremity edema, and fatigue. The patient had a history of HFrEF (left ventricular ejection fraction, 35%) and CKD (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], 35 mL/min/1.73 m2). She was previously hospitalized with acute HF and prescribed sacubitril-valsartan (49 mg sacubitril and 51 mg valsartan), carvedilol (3.125 mg) twice daily, spironolactone (25 mg), and dapagliflozin (10 mg) once daily by her cardiologist according to American Heart Association (AHA)/American College of Cardiology (ACC)/Heart Failure Society of America (HFSA) guidelines [2].

Initial laboratory tests revealed an eGFR of 18 mL/min/1.73 m2, a serum potassium (K+) of 4.9 mEq/L, and systolic blood pressure of 100 mm Hg. The patient's electrocardiogram was unchanged from the previous record, without evidence of acute ischemia or arrhythmia. The patient revealed that she frequently consumed foods with a high sodium content. She was admitted to hospital for further treatment.

### Typical Scenario: Management by a Subspecialist

Clinicians discontinued the patient's spironolactone and dapagliflozin owing to low eGFR (≤30 mL/min/1.73 m2) on hospital admission. Carvedilol was continued, and sacubitril-valsartan dosages were increased to 97 mg sacubitril and 103 mg valsartan twice daily for additional symptomatic relief. Intravenous loop diuretics were prescribed to reduce fluid overload, and laboratory tests were performed daily. After 3 days, the patient had symptomatic relief, improved edema, functional status, and ambulation without dyspnea on exertion. Kidney function improved (eGFR, 25 mL/min/1.73 m2); however, serum K+ rose to 5.6 mEq/L, indicating HK. To avoid further serum K+ increases, clinicians discontinued sacubitril-valsartan and spironolactone. Normokalemia was achieved, and the patient was discharged; however, she was no longer receiving GDMT RAASi therapy. While the patient's HFrEF initially remained stable, she was hospitalized with acute HF decompensation 2 months later. Clinicians were faced with a cyclical scenario: up-titrate to GDMT RAASi but risk HK or maintain low RAASi dosage to avoid serum K+ changes, with decompensation suboptimally managed.

### Proposed Scenario: Collaborative Management by an Interdisciplinary Team

Decisions could be made to increase sacubitril-valsartan dosages (97 mg sacubitril and 103 mg valsartan twice daily) and introduce intravenous loop diuretics as before but also continue dapagliflozin and spironolactone despite low eGFR (≤30 mL/min/1.73 m2) on admission in line with the STOP-ACEi and STRONG-HF trials [7, 8]. If as before, symptoms and eGFR improved, but serum K+ increased to 5.6 mEq/L, further discussion between cardiologists, nephrologists, clinical pharmacists, and advanced practitioners (e.g., nurse practitioners, physician assistants) could lead to introducing a low K+ diet while closely monitoring the patient rather than immediately reducing RAASi dosage. If serum K+ increased further, interdisciplinary discussions could result in introducing a K+ binder (e.g., sodium zirconium cyclosilicate or patiromer). A reduction in serum K+ would then be expected, enabling patient discharge with ongoing outpatient monitoring. In the long term, introduction of a K+ binder may allow the patient to continue GDMT RAASi medication. Additionally, coordination with primary care providers could improve health outcomes by stabilizing the patient on GDMT and K+ binders if required.

## Discussion

This report emphasizes the potential benefits of adopting an interdisciplinary collaborative approach and providing rational treatments to successfully maintain GDMT RAASi therapy following HK development. The patient presented with acute decompensated HF and was receiving an angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor, β-blocker, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and a sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitor prescribed by their outpatient cardiologist according to AHA/ACC/HFSA guidelines [2], but their medication dosages had not been adjusted to the recommended maximum following initial HF diagnosis. Rapid up-titration of GDMT, including RAASi, has been shown to significantly reduce signs of congestion, as well as the risk of rehospitalization and death, following HF diagnosis [8]. It is possible that suboptimal RAASi therapy may have in part contributed to the decompensated HF observed when the patient presented to the emergency department. Following RAASi up-titration to provide symptomatic relief, the patient's serum K+ increased to 5.6 mEq/L. In this typical scenario, clinicians down-titrated RAASi to avoid further, potentially arrhythmogenic, escalation of HK, but this led to the recurrence of decompensation. Data suggest that this scenario is common in the clinical setting; incidence of HK in patients with CKD receiving RAASi has been estimated to be approximately 10%, with more than half of these patients then receiving down-titrated or no RAASi therapy [4].

An alternative approach would be the management of HK and HF by a multidisciplinary team of nephrologists, cardiologists, and advanced practitioners. A framework for this collaborative care and key questions for discussion are presented in Figure 1, and there is evidence to support the benefits of multispecialty management of HF [9]. In order to deliver comprehensive treatment for patients with comorbid cardiorenal diseases, we propose the use of cardiometabolic outpatient clinics comprising a multidisciplinary team of relevant clinicians and healthcare professionals, academic-community partnerships in HF care, or involvement of pharmacists in care transitions. A structured multidisciplinary approach may help identify and treat patients with HK and enable consistent, effective communication for transfer of care from secondary to primary providers.

In the proposed scenario, following the interactive discussions outlined in Figure 1, the interdisciplinary team could decide to directly manage serum K+ by introducing a low K+ diet and a K+ binder, such as sodium zirconium cyclosilicate or patiromer. While both K+ binders have demonstrated effectiveness to lower serum K+ in clinical trials, which may help optimize RAASi therapy, AHA/ACC/HFSA guidelines indicate that the effectiveness of K+ binders as an option to enable RAASi dose optimization is uncertain [2]. However, recent practical UK guidance for managing HK in patients with HF and/or CKD discussed the utilization of K+ binders in 1 patient with HFrEF and 2 patients with CKD [10].

### Limitations

As the case study discussed is hypothetical, further real-world evidence of the effectiveness of the multidisciplinary management of patients with HFrEF and CKD would be valuable.

## Conclusion

This article proposes a collaborative approach by cardiologists, nephrologists, clinical pharmacists, and advanced practitioners to directly manage HK using diet and K+ binders to enable RAASi treatment optimization for symptomatic relief. Ultimately, coordination and ongoing dialog between nephrologists, cardiologists, and advanced practitioners are required for the optimal management of HF and CKD.
