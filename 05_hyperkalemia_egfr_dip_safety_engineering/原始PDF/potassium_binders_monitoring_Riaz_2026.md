## ABSTRACT

**Objective:** Hyperkalemia is a potentially life-threatening electrolyte imbalance that contributes to increased morbidity and mortality in patients with heart failure (HF) and chronic kidney disease (CKD). Novel potassium binders (NPBs) have been shown to prevent hyperkalemia; however, their efficacy and safety have not been fully established.

**Methods:** We searched various electronic databases, including MEDLINE (via PubMed), Embase, the Cochrane Library, and ClinicalTrials.gov, to retrieve randomized controlled trials comparing NPB to placebo in patients with chronic hyperkalemia due to HF or CKD.

**Results:** Our meta-analysis, including 12 studies involving 2517 patients, showed that NPBs, including patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, were associated with significant mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) optimization (relative risk [RR]: 1.28; 95% confidence interval [CI]: 1.14-1.43) as compared to placebo. The results were consistent for subgroups of sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, patiromer, CKD, and HF. NPBs also significantly reduced the incidence of hyperkalemia (RR: 0.37; 95% CI: 0.22-0.6). Additionally, NPBs reduced the mean serum potassium level from baseline as compared to placebo (mean difference: -0.46; 95% CI: -0.77 to -0.15) and MRA optimization at less than the target dose (RR: 0.62; 95% CI: 0.42-0.85). The risk of adverse events, serious adverse events, and all-cause mortality remained comparable between the two groups.

**Conclusion:** This meta-analysis highlights the potential of NPBs to facilitate MRA optimization, reduce hyperkalemic episodes, and maintain serum potassium levels in patients with hyperkalemia, without a corresponding increase in adverse events. However, further research through large-scale studies is needed to confirm the sustained clinical benefits of NPBs, establish standardized treatment protocols, and evaluate their effectiveness across diverse patient populations with CKD and HF.

**KEYWORDS:** Chronic kidney disease; hyperkalemia; novel potassium binders; patiromer; sodium zirconium cyclosilicate

## INTRODUCTION

Hyperkalemia, defined as a serum potassium concentration >5.0 to 5.5 mmol/L, is a potentially life-threatening condition leading to cardiac arrhythmias. It has a prevalence of 1.5% in the general population and 10.8% among patients taking antihypertensive medications. Hyperkalemia is associated with a graded increase in the risk of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular morbidity, progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD), and hospitalization, especially in individuals with underlying CKD or heart failure (HF). While guidelines for the acute management of hyperkalemia are well established, treatment for chronic hyperkalemia remains limited. Current treatment options, including dietary restriction, diuretics, sodium bicarbonate, insulin, and beta-agonist bronchodilators, are often limited by suboptimal efficacy, short duration of action, or practical inconvenience.

Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) remain one of the key treatment options for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) due to their cardiovascular benefits. However, these agents can impair renal potassium excretion, and resultant hyperkalemia can lead to suboptimal use of these cardioprotective medications in HF. Traditionally, sodium polystyrene sulfonate has been used for hyperkalemia, which binds to potassium in the gastrointestinal tract, promoting its fecal excretion. Despite its effectiveness, it is associated with poor tolerability and an increased risk of volume overload. Moreover, the US Food and Drug Administration has issued a black box warning for sodium polystyrene sulfonate, as its use can lead to intestinal necrosis. In light of these concerns, attention has shifted toward newer potassium binders (NPBs), such as patiromer and sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC).

Orally administered NPBs, such as patiromer and SZC, represent a relatively new class of FDA-approved drugs that have been studied over the past decade for reducing hyperkalemia incidence in patients with HF and CKD. Like sodium polystyrene sulfonate, they function by binding potassium in the gut, thereby enhancing fecal elimination of potassium. While initial studies suggest favorable efficacy and tolerability, long-term safety data for these newer agents remain limited.

While previous meta-analyses have primarily focused on the role of NPBs in facilitating RAASi optimization in HF, none have comprehensively evaluated their use in the management of hyperkalemia. Furthermore, several new randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have been published since those earlier analyses. Therefore, we conducted this meta-analysis to investigate the safety and efficacy of NPBs in chronic hyperkalemia with greater statistical power and certainty.

## METHODS

This systematic review and meta-analysis, registered in Prospero with registration number CRD420251051646, was performed according to the guidelines of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions and reported according to the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analysis (PRISMA) statement (see checklist in Supplemental Table S1).

### Eligibility criteria and search strategy

The inclusion criteria were as follows: (1) Study design: RCTs; (2) Population: adults >= 18 years of age, with chronic hyperkalemia in the context of CKD, HF, or other related comorbidities; (3) Intervention: NPB therapies including patiromer and SZC; (4) Comparator: placebo; (5) Outcomes: any outcome of interest. The exclusion criteria were (1) study designs other than RCTs, such as case series and case reports, conference abstracts, protocols, quasi-randomized trials, and observational studies; (2) studies focusing on the use of potassium binders for acute or emergency hyperkalemia; (3) studies conducted on animals; and (4) studies in languages other than English.

We systematically searched the following databases and trial registries from inception to April 15, 2025: Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL, via The Cochrane Library), MEDLINE (via PubMed), Embase, Google Scholar, and ClinicalTrials.gov. A predefined search strategy utilizing keywords and medical subject heading terms was employed for each source. We also performed a partial grey literature search using Google Scholar and backward citation tracking through the reference lists of relevant articles. The detailed search strategy is presented in Supplemental Table S2.

### Study selection and data extraction

All retrieved literature from our searches was imported into Rayyan, and duplicates were removed. Two authors independently screened all articles, followed by a full-text review. Discrepancies during the selection process were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third author. Data on study characteristics (e.g., authors and study location), patient population, and primary and secondary outcomes were extracted into a prepiloted Excel sheet. Extracted information included study characteristics, participant demographics, intervention and comparator details, and outcomes of interest. Relevant data were extracted, including patient characteristics (number of participants, mean age, mean estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], mean serum potassium, presence of type 2 diabetes mellitus, atrial fibrillation, and left ventricular ejection fraction [LVEF]); intervention details (dosage and duration); study characteristics (trial name, first author, year of publication, country of origin, and follow-up duration); and outcome measures.

Our primary efficacy outcomes were hyperkalemia and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) optimization. Our secondary outcomes included mean serum potassium change from baseline, MRA at less than the target dose, hypokalemia, all-cause mortality, any adverse events, and serious adverse events.

### Risk of bias assessment

To assess the internal validity of included RCTs, two authors independently applied the revised Cochrane "Risk of Bias" tool (RoB 2.0). Any discrepancies were resolved through consultation with a third author. This tool assesses bias across five domains: the randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, measurement of outcomes, and selection of reported results. The studies were assigned ratings of low risk of bias, some concerns, and high risk of bias.

### Statistical analysis

We used Review Manager (RevMan, version 5.4; The Cochrane Collaboration, Copenhagen, Denmark) for statistical analysis. A random-effects model was employed to account for potential heterogeneity among studies. For dichotomous and continuous outcomes, risk ratios (RRs) and mean difference (MD), respectively, with corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were used. A two-tailed P value < 0.05 was taken to be significant. Heterogeneity among studies was assessed using the I2 statistic and the chi-square test. A P value < 0.10 was considered indicative of significant heterogeneity. Predefined subgroup analyses based on the type of NPBs or the underlying condition (CKD or HF) were performed to compare the effects of patiromer and SZC across primary outcomes. A sensitivity analysis was conducted for primary outcomes by leaving out the DIAMOND and PRIORITIZE HF trials, both of which were terminated prematurely. Finally, in syntheses with at least 10 studies, publication bias was checked using a funnel plot and Egger's test for funnel plot asymmetry in Stata 17.0 (StataCorp LLC, College Station, TX, USA).

## RESULTS

We included 12 RCTs reporting data from 2517 patients in our meta-analysis. The detailed study selection process is illustrated in Figure 1 and Supplemental Table S2. Nine RCTs were multinational, while three were conducted in one country each. Patiromer was used in seven RCTs, while SZC was used in five. CKD was the cause of hyperkalemia in six studies, HF in four studies, and multiple in two studies. The detailed study characteristics of each trial are presented in Table 1.

### Risk of bias in included studies

Of the included studies, six were assessed as having a low risk of bias. Five studies raised some concerns, primarily related to the randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, and selective outcome reporting. One study was judged to have a high risk of bias, mainly due to issues with the randomization process and deviations from the intended treatment. A summary of the risk of bias assessment is shown in Figure 2.

### Synthesis of results: Primary outcomes

**MRA optimization.** In our analysis, NPB was associated with successful MRA optimization in patients with hyperkalemia (RR: 1.28; 95% CI: 1.14-1.43; I2 = 52%; Figure 3). A subgroup analysis based on the type of NPB used (SZC vs patiromer) or on the underlying condition (CKD or HF) found no significant subgroup differences (P heterogeneity = 0.39 and 0.79, respectively; Supplemental Figures S1, S2).

**Hyperkalemia.** Seven studies comprising 1763 patients documented the incidence of hyperkalemia (serum potassium >5.5 mEq/L). Pooled analysis showed that the risk of hyperkalemia was statistically significantly lower in the NPB group compared to placebo (RR: 0.37; 95% CI: 0.22-0.60; Figure 4). The heterogeneity was found to be substantial (I2 = 69%). Upon sensitivity analysis by leave-one-out method, it was observed that by excluding Butler et al, the heterogeneity became minimal (I2 = 26%), while the results remained consistent (RR: 0.31; 95% CI: 0.21-0.47; Supplemental Figure S3). A subgroup analysis based on the type of NPB used (SZC vs patiromer) or on the underlying condition (CKD or HF) found no significant subgroup differences (P heterogeneity = 0.07 and 0.98, respectively; Supplemental Figure S4).

### Synthesis of results: Secondary outcomes

**MRA at less than the target dose.** NPBs significantly decreased the number of patients with MRA at less than the target dose (RR: 0.62; 95% CI: 0.42-0.85; Supplemental Figure S5). The heterogeneity was found to be moderate (I2 = 50%).

**Hypokalemia.** NPBs were significantly associated with the incidence of hypokalemia (RR: 1.51; 95% CI: 1.08-2.10; Supplemental Figure S6). The heterogeneity was minimal (I2 = 0%).

**Mean serum potassium change from baseline.** The pooled analysis of five included studies comprising 1189 patients showed that usage of NPB was associated with a statistically significant reduction in serum potassium level from baseline compared to placebo (MD: -0.46; 95% CI: -0.77 to -0.15; Supplemental Figure S7). There was substantial heterogeneity (I2 = 93%).

**Any adverse events.** Our pooled results showed no statistically significant difference between NPB and placebo regarding the incidence of any adverse events (RR: 1.02; 95% CI: 0.94-1.11; Supplemental Figures S8, S9). There was minimal heterogeneity (I2 = 16%). There was no evidence of publication bias in the outcome of adverse events (Egger's P value = 0.28).

**Serious adverse event.** We found the risk of serious adverse events to be comparable between NPB and placebo (RR: 0.98; 95% CI: 0.76-1.26; Supplemental Figures S10, S11). There was minimal heterogeneity (I2 = 0%). There was no evidence of publication bias in the outcome of serious adverse events (Egger's P value = 0.91).

**All-cause mortality.** The meta-analysis of eight RCTs comparing NPB and placebo revealed no significant difference between the two groups regarding all-cause mortality (RR: 1.07; 95% CI: 0.61-1.87; Supplemental Figure S12). The heterogeneity was minimal (I2 = 0%).

## DISCUSSION

This systematic review and meta-analysis of 12 RCTs offers a comprehensive evaluation of the efficacy and safety of NPBs, including patiromer and SZC, in the management of chronic hyperkalemia by incorporating data from recent trials and investigating diverse clinical outcomes. NPBs were associated with better MRA optimization and reduced the incidence of hyperkalemia episodes without compromising safety. The results were consistent for both patiromer and SZC. However, NPBs were associated with a higher risk of hypokalemia. NPBs reduced the number of patients receiving suboptimal RAASi therapy and mean serum potassium concentration without an increase in adverse events or serious adverse events. NPBs had no impact on the risk of all-cause mortality.

MRAs are known to improve clinical outcomes in patients with CKD and HF. However, the risk of inducing hyperkalemia has been identified as a significant factor limiting the use of MRAs. This electrolyte imbalance presents a serious threat to a patient's health and is linked to poorer outcomes, especially in individuals with HF. In efficacy analysis, it was found that NPBs were associated with increased MRA optimization, resulting in a 28% increase in MRA optimization compared to placebo. This result was consistent with two previous meta-analyses showing a 13% and 24% increase in MRA optimization, respectively. The increase in MRA optimization compared to previous analysis can be attributed to a greater sample size, due to the emergence of new trials like REALIZE-K and Kashihara et al 2025, showing greater MRA optimization than the previous RCTs. Both SZC and patiromer showed consistent efficacy, with patiromer contributing to a greater effect size across studies in our analysis.

Notably, in our analysis, NPBs were associated with a lower incidence of hyperkalemia, in addition to a reduction in mean serum potassium concentration as compared to baseline. Our findings build on the existing meta-analyses and are consistent with recent American College of Cardiology and Kidney Disease-Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) guidelines. Consistent with the previous meta-analyses, NPBs reduced the risk of hyperkalemia, with better MRA optimization. Unlike the meta-analysis by Abuelazm et al, our study demonstrated a reduced mean serum potassium concentration in the NPB group as compared to placebo. This meaningful reduction in serum potassium was not observed in previous meta-analyses. AMBER trials reported the lowest hyperkalemic incidence (6% only) by use of NPBs.

Our safety analysis revealed that NPBs are not associated with any significant increase in adverse events or serious adverse events, consistent with the results of previous meta-analyses. However, NPBs were associated with a heightened risk of hypokalemia, which may be attributed to the concurrent use of loop or thiazide diuretics in patients with CKD and HF, potentially compounding potassium loss. However, despite this finding, we did not observe an increase in serious adverse events attributable to hypokalemia. Studies have shown that hypokalemia is associated with increased risk of arrhythmia in patients with cardiovascular disease, as well as increased all-cause mortality, cardiovascular mortality, and heart failure mortality by up to 10-fold. Fan et al reported a 13% increase in cardiovascular mortality. Our results suggest that while hypokalemia remains a potential side effect of potassium binders in high-risk patients, this did not translate into excess serious adverse events within the available trial data. Although the all-cause mortality rate was comparable between the two groups in our analysis, the results of individual RCTs have presented conflicting evidence. The REALIZE-K trial reported an increase in cardiac failure with the use of SZC. The DIAMOND trial found no significant increase in cardiovascular hospitalization and HF exacerbation, but reported that patiromer significantly increased all-cause mortality. These contradictory findings show disparities among the trials and the need for long-term trials.

The ability of NPBs to lower serum potassium while maintaining a favorable safety profile is largely attributable to their structural and pharmacological properties. Patiromer's low molecular weight and acidic nature enable it to function effectively within the colon without systemic absorption. In contrast, SZC, a nonabsorbable crystalline compound, acts locally in the gastrointestinal tract and is excreted unchanged in the feces, minimizing systemic toxicity. Additionally, SZC maintains its potassium-binding capacity across a wide pH range, allowing for effective potassium exchange throughout the gastrointestinal tract.

While previous meta-analyses have examined the safety and efficacy of NPBs, our meta-analysis offers novel insights by including a larger number of participants through the incorporation of recent trials like REALIZE-K and Kashihara et al 2025, showing a 28% increase in MRA optimization, surpassing previous results (13%-24%). The increased sample size provides a more robust and reliable dataset, strengthening the validity of our findings. Unlike earlier reviews, we focused on both NPBs and data from a larger and more varied group of patients, including HF and CKD. Thus, our study provides a clearer and more reliable picture of the real-world benefits and risks of these treatments.

### Limitations

It is imperative to consider the limitations of our analysis. The RCTs suffered from significant variabilities in protocols, timing of administration, dosages of drugs, and follow-up durations, which resulted in heterogeneity in some of our clinical outcomes. We addressed the issue through subgroup and sensitivity analyses, which led to a significant reduction in heterogeneity. However, this finding should be interpreted cautiously, as subgroup comparisons are observational and prone to bias. Moreover, the DIAMOND trial and PRIORITIZE HF trial were terminated prematurely, which may have contributed to bias in our estimates. This was evident in sensitivity analyses, which showed that these trials were the main contributors to heterogeneity. There was limited data available to conduct analysis on the adverse effects associated with these medications, e.g., hypercalcemia with patiromer and sodium and water retention with SZC. Despite pooling a large number of studies, our meta-analysis was still underpowered for some of the clinical outcomes assessed, as indicated in the results. Lastly, the absence of individual patient data constrained our analysis to aggregate study-level outcomes. Further investigation through long-term, large-scale studies is needed to confirm the broader clinical benefits of NPBs, establish standardized treatment strategies, compare them with conventional therapies, and evaluate their efficacy across diverse CKD and HF populations.

### Clinical implications

Given the favorable safety and efficacy profile consistent with the current clinical guidelines, NPBs can be considered to help maintain normokalaemia, especially in patients with prior hyperkalemia or those requiring higher MRA doses. However, the risk of hypokalemia, particularly with concurrent diuretic use, warrants careful serum potassium monitoring. Further long-term, large-scale RCTs are needed to confirm clinical benefits.
