## Abstract

**Background** Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists reduce mortality in patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction and have become a standard of care in those with resistant hypertension (rHTN). Yet, their use is limited among patients with CKD, primarily due to hyperkalemia.

**Methods** AMBER was a multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group study that reported that the use of the potassium-binding drug patiromer allowed a more persistent use of spironolactone in patients with CKD and rHTN. In this report, we compare the safety and efficacy of patiromer in advanced CKD as a prespecified analysis.

**Results** Of the 295 patients randomized, 66 fell into the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup. In this subgroup, persistent use of spironolactone was seen in 19 of 34 (56%) in the placebo group and 27 of 32 (84%) in the patiromer group (absolute difference 29%; P<0.02). In the eGFR 30–45 subgroup, persistent use of spironolactone was seen in 79 of 114 (69%) in the placebo group and 99 of 115 (86%) in the patiromer group (absolute difference 17%; P=0.003). There was no significant interaction between eGFR subgroups (P=0.46). Systolic BP reduction with spironolactone in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup was 6–7 mm Hg; in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup, it was 12–13 mm Hg. There was no significant interaction between eGFR subgroups on BP reduction (P=0.79). Similar proportions of patients reported adverse events (59% in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup; 53% in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup).

**Conclusions** Patiromer facilitates the use of spironolactone among patients with rHTN, and its efficacy and safety are comparable in those with eGFR 25 to <30 and 30–45 ml/min per 1.73 m².

### Key Points
- In the AMBER trial, patiromer enabled more persistent spironolactone use in patients with rHTN and advanced CKD.
- Efficacy of patiromer was comparable in prespecified subgroups with eGFR 25 to <30 and 30–45 ml/min per 1.73 m².
- Safety of patiromer was consistent between eGFR subgroups and prior reports, with no new safety signals even in the 25 to <30 eGFR subgroup.

## Introduction

The prevalence of apparent resistant hypertension (rHTN) is approximately 10%–15% in the treated hypertensive population. The prevalence of rHTN is substantially higher in patients with lower eGFR and higher degrees of albuminuria. The PATHWAY-2 study demonstrated that among patients with rHTN optimized on a three-drug regimen (an ACE inhibitor, a calcium channel blocker, and a diuretic), compared with adding an α- or β-blocker, spironolactone elicited a greater BP reduction. However, in that trial, patients with eGFR <45 ml/min per 1.73 m² were excluded, and the average eGFR of randomized patients was 91 ml/min per 1.73 m².

The United States guidelines recommend adding a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) for patients with rHTN, but caution is advised for patients with eGFR <30 ml/min per 1.73 m². The European guidelines recommend adding an MRA for rHTN as long as eGFR is >45 ml/min per 1.73 m² and plasma potassium (K⁺) is ≤4.5 mEq/L.

In addition to controlling BP, MRA therapy reduces mortality and hospitalization in patients with heart failure (HF) and comorbid CKD. US HF guidelines recommend MRA use in patients with comorbid HF down to eGFR of 30 ml/min per 1.73 m². The ESC HF guidelines recommend using MRAs with caution or seeking specialist advice for eGFR <30 ml/min per 1.73 m², whereas the ACC/AHA/HFSA guidelines note that MRAs may be harmful in patients with eGFR <30 ml/min per 1.73 m². A 2017 KDIGO Controversies Conference noted that data were insufficient to recommend the use of MRA to treat rHTN in patients with CKD (held prior to publication of AMBER). Among patients with CKD, compared with placebo, a 2019 meta-analysis showed that the risk for hyperkalemia was increased by approximately 2.6-fold. Two consensus statements from the ESC suggest the use of novel K⁺ binders to facilitate MRA use in patients with HF and concomitant CKD. Yet, utilization of K⁺ binders in this population remains low. In HF with reduced ejection fraction, only 33% of eligible patients received an MRA in a large US registry. In a study across the Americas, Europe, Australia, and Asia (1555 patients from 76 practices), 95% of patients with treatment rHTN were prescribed a diuretic, whereas only 36% received MRA. Use of MRA in high-risk patients with CKD, diabetes, and HF also remains low (6.6%), and <1% of patients with hypertension and CKD are prescribed an MRA.

The AMBER trial showed that among patients with rHTN and eGFR between 25 and 45 ml/min per 1.73 m², patiromer, compared with placebo, enabled a more persistent use of spironolactone. Patiromer enabled a greater dose of spironolactone and reduced the risk of hyperkalemia by nearly 50%. Here, we report the prespecified subgroup analysis of AMBER primary and secondary end points by eGFR subgroups (eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup and 30–45 subgroup).

## Materials and Methods

### Study Design and Participants
AMBER was a multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group study to evaluate whether patiromer would allow more persistent use of spironolactone in patients with CKD and rHTN. The study conforms to the Declaration of Helsinki; the protocol was approved by the IRB/independent ethics committee for each institution. All patients provided written informed consent.

Eligible patients were age ≥18 years with an eGFR between 25 and 45 ml/min per 1.73 m² and serum potassium (sK⁺) between 4.3 and 5.1 mEq/L. All patients had rHTN during screening, defined as unattended systolic automated office BP (AOBP) of 135–160 mm Hg despite taking three or more antihypertensives, including a diuretic and an ACE inhibitor or ARB (unless not tolerated/contraindicated). Exclusion criteria included untreated secondary causes of hypertension (other than CKD), recent cardiovascular event, and clinically significant ventricular arrhythmia or atrial fibrillation with heart rate >100 bpm.

AMBER had a screening/run-in period (up to 4 weeks), a double-blind treatment period (12 weeks), and a follow-up visit 2 weeks after the week 12 visit or early termination. Eligible patients were randomly assigned (1:1) via an interactive web response system to receive patiromer or matching placebo in addition to open-label spironolactone once daily and their baseline antihypertensive medications. Visits during the double-blind treatment period were weekly (weeks 1–4) then biweekly (weeks 6–12).

### Treatments
Open-label oral spironolactone was started at 25 mg once daily and increased to 50 mg once daily at week 3 in patients with sK⁺ ≤5.1 mEq/L if systolic AOBP remained ≥120 mm Hg. The starting dose of patiromer was 8.4 g once daily, taken with food at least 3 hours before or after other medications. Study drug dosing adjustments were made at intervals of ≥1 week: upward adjustment to 16.8 g once daily, then 25.2 g once daily for local laboratory sK⁺ >5.1 mEq/L, and downward adjustment for sK⁺ <4.0 mEq/L. A titration algorithm reduced or discontinued spironolactone if decreases in eGFR or hypotension were observed.

### End Points and Statistical Analyses
This prespecified analysis evaluated AMBER primary and secondary end points by eGFR subgroups. The **primary end point** was the difference between treatment groups in the proportion of patients remaining on spironolactone at week 12. The **secondary efficacy end point** was the difference in change in systolic AOBP from baseline to week 12. Post hoc analyses by baseline eGFR included cumulative spironolactone dose, Kaplan–Meier estimated time to discontinuation of spironolactone, percentage receiving spironolactone 50 mg once daily, daily dose of patiromer, rate of spironolactone discontinuation due to hyperkalemia, Kaplan–Meier estimate of time to sK⁺ ≥5.5 mEq/L, and sK⁺ over time.

Safety was assessed by vital signs, AEs, change in eGFR from baseline, and laboratory parameters (including NT-proBNP and urine albumin-creatinine ratio). Laboratory assessments included serum calcium (normal 8.5–10.5 mg/dl) and magnesium (normal 1.8–2.4 mg/dl), and prespecified thresholds (sK⁺ <3.8 and <3.5 mEq/L, serum calcium >10.5 mg/dl, serum magnesium <1.4 and <1.2 mg/dl).

The primary end point used the Cochran–Mantel–Haenszel test stratified by baseline K⁺ category (4.3 to <4.7 versus 4.7–5.1 mEq/L) and presence/absence of diabetes mellitus. The secondary end point used analysis of covariance with baseline systolic AOBP as covariate. Time to discontinuation and time to hyperkalemia (sK⁺ ≥5.5 mEq/L) were analyzed using Kaplan–Meier methods. Statistical analyses were performed on SAS software, version 9.4.

## Results

### Patient Disposition and Baseline Characteristics
In AMBER, 295 patients were randomized to placebo plus spironolactone (n=148) or patiromer plus spironolactone (n=147). Of these, 66 (22%) were in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup (34 placebo, 32 patiromer), and 229 (78%) were in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup (114 placebo, 115 patiromer).

In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, 32 (94%) placebo and 32 (100%) patiromer patients completed the study (109 [96%] and 112 [97%] in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup). The most common reason for study drug discontinuation was meeting a protocol-specified withdrawal criterion for high sK⁺. In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, study drug discontinuation due to hyperkalemia occurred in nine (26%) on placebo and two (6%) on patiromer; rates were 25 (22%) on placebo and eight (7%) on patiromer in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup. In the eGFR 25 to <30 and 30–45 subgroups, 5% and 1% discontinued due to protocol-defined symptomatic hypotension, and 3% and 2% discontinued due to protocol-defined decline in eGFR, respectively.

Baseline demographics were generally similar. In the eGFR 25 to <30 and 30–45 subgroups: 100% and 97.8% were White; 39% and 52% had diabetes; 44% and 45% had a history of HF; mean baseline sK⁺ values were 4.78 and 4.70 mEq/L, respectively.

### Efficacy
There was **no significant interaction** between eGFR subgroups (P=0.46) for the primary end point.

- **eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup:** 55.9% (19/34) receiving placebo remained on spironolactone at week 12 vs 84.4% (27/32) receiving patiromer (between-group difference = 28.5%; 95% CI, 7.6 to 49.4; P<0.02, reported P=0.016).
- **eGFR 30–45 subgroup:** 69.3% (79/114) receiving placebo remained on spironolactone at week 12 vs 86.1% (99/115) receiving patiromer (between-group difference = 16.8%; 95% CI, 6.2 to 27.4; P=0.003).

In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, treatment group separation in time to discontinuation of spironolactone began as early as 2 weeks, whereas it was not until 6 weeks in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup.

The LS mean (SEM) difference between treatment groups (patiromer minus placebo) in **cumulative spironolactone dose** was 732.4 (274.3) mg in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup and 273.8 (139.7) mg in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup. In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, 41% (placebo) and 72% (patiromer) were receiving the 50 mg once daily dose at week 12 (54% and 69% in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup). LS mean (SEM) differences in **average daily dose of spironolactone** (patiromer minus placebo) were 4.3 (1.9) mg in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup and 1.7 (1.1) mg in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup. Median (Q1, Q3) **daily doses of patiromer** were 11.2 (8.4, 16.1) g/d and 9.8 (8.4, 15.9) g/d in the eGFR 25 to <30 and 30–45 subgroups, respectively.

**Hyperkalemia (sK⁺ ≥5.5 mEq/L):** In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, occurred in 18 (53%) receiving placebo and 11 (34%) receiving patiromer. In the eGFR 30–45 subgroup, occurred in 77 (68%) receiving placebo and 41 (36%) receiving patiromer. Separation between treatment groups in first sK⁺ ≥5.5 mEq/L occurred earlier in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup (evident after week 1) than the eGFR 30–45 subgroup (evident after week 3).

**Blood pressure (systolic AOBP change from baseline to week 12):**
- eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup: −6.2 mm Hg (placebo, P=0.014) and −6.5 mm Hg (patiromer, P=0.011); LS mean difference between groups −0.3 (95% CI −7.2, 6.6), P=0.94.
- eGFR 30–45 subgroup: −11.9 mm Hg (placebo, P<0.0001) and −13.0 mm Hg (patiromer, P<0.0001); LS mean difference between groups −1.0 (95% CI −4.9, 2.9), P=0.60.
- No significant interaction between eGFR subgroups (P=0.79).

Additions to antihypertensive medications before week 12 occurred in four patients on placebo (all four in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup) and no patients on patiromer. No additions were reported to be due to new edematous states.

### Safety
Overall similar numbers of patients reported AEs: 19 (56%) placebo and 20 (63%) patiromer in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup (60 [53%] and 62 [54%] in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup). AEs were generally mild to moderate. The most frequent class of AEs in both subgroups was gastrointestinal disorders, occurring in five (15%) placebo and nine (28%) patiromer patients in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup (19 [17%] and 15 [13%] in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup). Diarrhea was the only individual GI AE occurring in ≥4 patients in the eGFR 25 to <30 combined subgroup — reported in three (9%) placebo and five (16%) patiromer patients (five [4.4%] and four [3.5%] in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup).

Incidence of baseline or postbaseline sK⁺ <3.8 mEq/L: one patient in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup receiving patiromer had a postbaseline sK⁺ between 3.0 and <3.5 mEq/L. In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, none had sK⁺ <3.5 mEq/L. No patients in either subgroup had sK⁺ <3.0 mEq/L.

**Serious AEs:** In the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, one SAE occurred in a placebo patient (hypersensitivity; unrelated). In the eGFR 30–45 subgroup, one SAE occurred in each of four patients: three receiving placebo (renal failure, renal colic, and aortic rupture [the AE leading to death]) and one receiving patiromer (humerus fracture).

Mean serum calcium and magnesium levels remained within normal range in both treatment groups; results were not modified by eGFR subgroup. Serum magnesium 1.2–1.4 mg/dl occurred in one placebo and three patiromer patients in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup; no patients had serum magnesium <1.2 mg/dl.

## Discussion

Spironolactone reduces morbidity and mortality in patients with HF with reduced ejection fraction and is a standard of care among patients with rHTN. However, use of spironolactone is low in those with congestive HF and treatment rHTN with CKD and/or diabetes, and it is rarely prescribed to patients with CKD and hypertension. In part, low spironolactone utilization may be because of fear of provoking hyperkalemia. Data from the Swedish Heart Failure Registry indicate that RAAS inhibitors are beneficial in reducing mortality even in patients with stage 4 CKD. However, after hyperkalemia occurs, RAAS inhibitors are promptly discontinued, potentially removing any protective effect. The AMBER study demonstrated that the K⁺-binding drug patiromer enables the persistent use of spironolactone in patients with CKD and rHTN.

In this analysis, results were similar across CKD subgroups with respect to efficacy. However, in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, separation between treatment group curves for discontinuation of spironolactone began as early as 2 weeks, whereas it was not until 6 weeks in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup. Spironolactone, regardless of placebo or patiromer, provoked a statistically significant reduction from baseline in systolic BP. The reduction in the eGFR 30–45 subgroup was 12–13 mm Hg, whereas in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup it was about 6 mm Hg. There was no evidence of interaction between baseline eGFR subgroups for either primary or secondary end point. Patiromer's safety profile was generally consistent between CKD subgroups and previous reports, showing no obvious signal of harm even among patients with eGFR <30 ml/min per 1.73 m².

As patiromer exchanges K⁺ for calcium, this may be an important consideration in advanced CKD. Prior studies in healthy adults suggested only a small fraction of released calcium is available for absorption (73 mg/d at the highest approved dose of patiromer [25.2 g]). Some released calcium may bind to phosphate and reduce serum phosphate levels. In advanced CKD, physicians should consider the risks of a potential small increase in calcium absorption compared with the benefits of continuing spironolactone.

Inclusion of patients with CKD and eGFR 25 to <30 is a strength, as patients with stage 4 CKD are systematically excluded from RCTs; the retention rate was very high. Even in the eGFR 25 to <30 subgroup, spironolactone reduced systolic AOBP by 6 mm Hg, similar to renal denervation trials in ovine models of CKD.

**Limitations:** Power calculations were performed for the primary analysis; most trials, including AMBER, are not powered to detect interaction effects for subgroups. The number of patients with stage 4 CKD is small, so only large differences in safety and efficacy can be appropriately commented on. Because none were found in this prespecified subgroup analysis, the authors conclude the safety and efficacy of patiromer are comparable for early stage 4 CKD and late stage 3 CKD. Enabling spironolactone use with patiromer requires managing drug-drug interactions and adds another therapy. Enabling steroidal MRA use with patiromer may improve heart and kidney risk, but this will need separate studies (e.g., DIAMOND for cardiovascular outcomes, NCT03888066). The nonsteroidal MRA finerenone has not been tested in rHTN, and its BP-lowering potential is minimal.

**Conclusion:** Analyses from AMBER show that patiromer enables the use of spironolactone independent of eGFR within the range studied, and the safety and efficacy of patiromer are comparable in patients within this range of advanced CKD.
