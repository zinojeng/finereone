# Impact of Baseline GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Use on Albuminuria Reduction and Safety With Simultaneous Initiation of Finerenone and Empagliflozin in Type 2 Diabetes and Chronic Kidney Disease (CONFIDENCE Trial)

## Abstract

**Objective.** The CONFIDENCE trial demonstrated additive benefits of simultaneous initiation of finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and a sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor compared with monotherapy in reducing the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). This prespecified analysis evaluated whether safety and efficacy of combination therapy varies by baseline glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonist (GLP-1 RA) use.

**Research design and methods.** Adults with chronic kidney disease (UACR ≥100 to <5,000 mg/g; estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] 30–90 mL/min/1.73 m²) and type 2 diabetes (glycated hemoglobin <11% [97 mmol/mol]) were randomized (1:1:1) to once-daily finerenone, empagliflozin, or finerenone plus empagliflozin.

**Results.** Among 800 participants, 182 (23%) used a GLP-1 RA at baseline. At day 180, UACR change from baseline in participants using a GLP-1 RA was −51% (95% CI −59 to −40%) with combination therapy, −34% (−48 to −18%) with finerenone, and −36% (−48 to −21%) with empagliflozin. Corresponding results in those not using a GLP-1 RA at baseline were −56% (−62 to −50%), −37% (−45 to −28%), and −33% (−41 to −23%), respectively. Hyperkalemia incidence rates with combination therapy were 9.0% and 9.5% among individuals with and without baseline GLP-1 RA use. eGFR changes were consistent among individuals with and without baseline GLP-1 RA use. Acute kidney injury was uncommon. Decreases in systolic blood pressure were observed and were more pronounced with combination therapy.

**Conclusions.** In CONFIDENCE, simultaneous initiation with finerenone and an SGLT2 inhibitor was effective and well tolerated compared with monotherapy, irrespective of background use of a GLP-1 RA.

## Article Highlights

- **Why did we undertake this study?** We investigated whether using GLP-1 RAs alongside coinitiated finerenone and SGLT2 inhibitors impacts safety and effectiveness in people with chronic kidney disease and type 2 diabetes.
- **What is the specific question we wanted to answer?** We wanted to know whether background GLP-1 RA use altered the effects of simultaneously starting finerenone and empagliflozin on top of existing renin-angiotensin system inhibition in the CONFIDENCE trial.
- **What did we find?** GLP-1 RA use at baseline did not change the efficacy or safety of finerenone, empagliflozin, or their combination.
- **What are the implications of our findings?** Our results support the benefits of combination therapy with finerenone and SGLT2 inhibitors, regardless of baseline GLP-1 RA use, supporting the implementation of all four pillars of care.

## Introduction

Diabetes is the most common cause of kidney failure requiring dialysis or transplantation globally, accounting for approximately half of all new cases of kidney failure in the U.S. Among people with diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD) markedly amplifies the risks of kidney failure, atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, and all-cause mortality.

For CKD management in people with type 2 diabetes, four classes of drugs are recommended on the basis of outcomes trials: (1) renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors (ACE inhibitors or ARBs), (2) SGLT2 inhibitors, (3) GLP-1 RAs with proven benefit for reducing cardiovascular risk and kidney disease progression, and (4) the nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist finerenone for people with CKD and albuminuria (if eGFR ≥25 mL/min/1.73 m²).

The COmbinatioN effect of FInerenone anD EmpaglifloziN in participants with CKD and type 2 diabetes using a UACR Endpoint (CONFIDENCE) randomized trial demonstrated that the combination of finerenone and an SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin) provided additive reductions in albuminuria, supporting the use of these agents together and highlighting that stepwise addition can potentially be replaced by simultaneous initiation.

A 2025 Cochrane review (42 RCTs, 48,148 participants) concluded that GLP-1 RAs are likely to provide cardiovascular benefit and reduce all-cause mortality in people with diabetes and CKD. The FLOW trial demonstrated that semaglutide reduced the risk of a composite kidney outcome by 24%, major cardiovascular events by 18%, and all-cause mortality by 20% versus placebo.

In this prespecified analysis of the CONFIDENCE trial, the authors evaluated the efficacy and safety of simultaneous initiation of finerenone and empagliflozin versus monotherapy, with or without GLP-1 RA, on a background of RAS inhibition.

## Research Design and Methods

**Trial setting.** CONFIDENCE was a double-blind, randomized, active-controlled trial of empagliflozin alone, finerenone alone, or the combination in people with CKD with albuminuria and type 2 diabetes. First registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05254002) 23 February 2022.

**Participants.** Eligible adults had type 2 diabetes with HbA1c <11% (97 mmol/mol), eGFR 30–90 mL/min/1.73 m², and albuminuria (UACR 100 to <5,000 mg/g, confirmed by averaging first morning urine samples over 3 consecutive days). All required maximally tolerated dose of ACE inhibitor or ARB for ≥1 month prior to screening. Exclusion criteria: serum potassium >4.8 mmol/L, type 1 diabetes, and use of an SGLT2 inhibitor or potassium binder within 8 weeks before screening. GLP-1 RA treatments were allowed provided dosage was stable ≥4 weeks prior to screening; dose reductions were allowed during the study, but dose increases were not.

**Randomization.** 1:1:1 to finerenone (10 or 20 mg target dose once daily, plus placebo empagliflozin), empagliflozin (10 mg once daily, plus placebo finerenone), or both. Finerenone initial dose determined by kidney function: eGFR ≥60 started at 20 mg daily; eGFR <60 started at 10 mg daily, uptitrated to 20 mg after 30 days if serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L and eGFR decrease <30%. Empagliflozin fixed at 10 mg.

**Trial outcomes.** Primary efficacy outcome: change from baseline to 180 days in log-transformed mean UACR. Secondary outcome: proportion achieving UACR reduction >30%, >40%, and >50% at 180 days. Secondary safety outcomes: acute eGFR change >30% at 30 days, incidence of acute kidney injury, hyperkalemia events, change in serum potassium, symptomatic hypotension, severe hypoglycemia, urosepsis or pyelonephritis, and genital mycotic infections.

**Statistical analyses.** Efficacy analyses used the full-analysis set. Primary analysis used a mixed model for repeated measures with log-transformed UACR. Prespecified interest was effect modification by baseline GLP-1 RA use. GLP-1 RA use was not randomized; the analysis did not assess causal interaction. Subgroup analyses by GLP-1 RA use were prespecified prior to database lock; given exploratory nature, models were not adjusted for multiple comparisons.

## Results

### Participants

Of 800 participants (full-analysis set), 182 (22.8%) reported baseline GLP-1 RA use, similarly distributed across combination (68 of 269 [25.3%]), finerenone (52 of 264 [19.7%]), and empagliflozin (62 of 267 [23.2%]) groups.

Of the 182 baseline GLP-1 RA users: 102 (56%) semaglutide, 41 (23%) dulaglutide, 28 (15%) liraglutide, 6 (3%) tirzepatide, 5 (3%) lixisenatide. Median duration of baseline GLP-1 RA use prior to randomization was 358 days (range 1–8,522).

**Baseline differences by GLP-1 RA use.** Participants prescribed GLP-1 RAs were less likely to be Asian or from Asia; had higher mean BMI; had greater use of insulin, β-blockers, diuretics, antiplatelet agents, and statins; and were less likely to be taking DPP-4 inhibitors. Median UACR was lower in baseline GLP-1 RA users (539 [IQR 290–1,000] mg/g) than non-users (592 [292–1,167] mg/g).

Selected differences reported in the graphical abstract (GLP-1 RA+ vs GLP-1 RA−): White 64% vs 38%; Asian 23% vs 53%; BMI 32.3 vs 27.4 kg/m²; insulin 59% vs 34%; antiplatelets 48% vs 38%; statins 87% vs 71%; DPP-4i 4% vs 41%; diuretics 48% vs 33%; mean eGFR 55.8 vs 53.7 mL/min/1.73 m²; median UACR 539 vs 597 mg/g.

**Table 1 (selected baseline characteristics, GLP-1 RA− [n=618] vs GLP-1 RA+ [n=182]):**
- Age: 66.2 ± 10.6 vs 67.3 ± 9.0 years
- Female: 149 (24.1%) vs 49 (26.9%)
- Body weight: 78.7 ± 18.4 vs 92.8 ± 23.5 kg
- BMI: 28.4 ± 5.4 vs 32.3 ± 7.1 kg/m²
- eGFR: 55.8 ± 17.4 vs 53.7 ± 17.0 mL/min/1.73 m²

- UACR median (IQR): 592 (292–1,167) vs 539 (290–1,000) mg/g

### Treatment Effect by GLP-1 RA Use

At day 180, UACR change from baseline in participants using a GLP-1 RA:
- Combination: −50.6% (95% CI −59.5 to −39.7%)
- Finerenone: −34.5% (−47.7 to −18.1%)
- Empagliflozin: −36.1% (−48.3 to −21.0%)

In participants not using a GLP-1 RA at baseline:
- Combination: −56.5% (−61.9 to −50.3%)
- Finerenone: −36.8% (−44.5 to −28.0%)
- Empagliflozin: −32.7% (−40.9 to −23.2%)

Differences between combination and monotherapies were similar across GLP-1 RA subgroups. In the GLP-1 RA group, combination vs finerenone alone was −24.6% (−43.8 to 1.2%) and combination vs empagliflozin alone was −22.7% (−42.0 to 2.9%). **P for interaction (change in UACR at day 180) = 0.93.**

Attenuation of UACR reduction was observed following treatment discontinuation in the combination group in both subgroups.

**Threshold reductions (P for interaction):**
- >30% reduction: P for interaction = 0.60
- >40% reduction: P for interaction = 0.79
- >50% reduction: P for interaction = 0.84

Approximately 72.1% (95% CI 65.6–78.7%) of combination-group participants without baseline GLP-1 RA use achieved >30% UACR reduction vs 63.8% (51.7–75.9%) with baseline GLP-1 RA use.

### Adverse Events

Incidence of abdominal symptoms was similar between those receiving and not receiving a GLP-1 RA at baseline. Hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia events were uncommon (10 and 8 participants overall, respectively).

### Serum Potassium and Hyperkalemia

Trends for serum potassium over time were similar irrespective of GLP-1 RA use. Combination therapy was associated with a slight increase in mean serum potassium, declining to baseline after treatment cessation; similar trend in the finerenone group. Empagliflozin was not associated with serum potassium changes.

**Treatment-emergent hyperkalemia adverse events:**
- With baseline GLP-1 RA use: combination 9.0%, finerenone 11.5%, empagliflozin 6.5%
- Without baseline GLP-1 RA use: combination 9.5%, finerenone 11.3%, empagliflozin 2.9%

- >5.5 to ≤6.0 mmol/L: 27/199 (13.6%) / 36/208 (17.3%) / (empagliflozin low)

- Total >5.5 mmol/L (n=617): 92/607 (15.2%) combination-inclusive summary; finerenone 78/607 (12.9%); empagliflozin 32/616 (2.5%)

With GLP-1 RA use at baseline — combination (n=67) / finerenone (n=52) / empagliflozin (n=62):
- >5.5 mmol/L: 9/67 (13.4%) / 7/52 (13.5%) / 6/61 (9.8%)
- >6.0 mmol/L: 2/67 (3.0%) / 0/52 (0.0%) / 0/62 (0.0%)

Numerically lower rates of severe hyperkalemia were observed in patients with baseline GLP-1 RA use, aligning with previous literature suggesting a possible protective/mitigating effect of GLP-1 RAs on hyperkalemia risk.

### Changes in eGFR and ≥30% eGFR Change

An early decline in eGFR was observed in all three groups, which stabilized and was reversible upon drug discontinuation. Incidence of eGFR decline >30% at day 30 occurred in 30 participants overall, with similar distribution across treatment groups in both GLP-1 RA subgroups.

Decline in eGFR >30% from baseline to day 30 (Table 3):
- Without GLP-1 RA: combination 12/201 (6.0%), finerenone 8/212 (3.8%), empagliflozin 3/204 (1.5%); total 23/617 (3.7%)
- With GLP-1 RA: combination 5/67 (7.5%), finerenone 2/52 (3.8%), empagliflozin 0/62 (0.0%)

There was a numerically greater proportion of participants in the GLP-1 RA group, particularly those receiving combination therapy, who experienced an eGFR decline >30%, suggesting a need for closer monitoring in this subgroup.

Acute kidney injury was uncommon (eight participants overall). Acute kidney injury (Table 3): without GLP-1 RA total 6/617 (1.0%); with GLP-1 RA — combination 1/67 (1.5%), finerenone 1/52 (1.9%), empagliflozin 0/62 (0.0%).

### Systolic Blood Pressure and Symptomatic Hypotension

In both subgroups, reduction from baseline in SBP was observed in all three arms, more pronounced with combination therapy. SBP returned to baseline following discontinuation. Symptomatic hypotension was reported in three participants randomized to combination therapy (one receiving a GLP-1 RA at baseline, two not).

## Conclusions

This prespecified subgroup analysis of CONFIDENCE showed consistent treatment benefits of simultaneous initiation of combination treatment with finerenone and empagliflozin compared with either monotherapy, irrespective of baseline GLP-1 RA use. UACR reduction with empagliflozin or finerenone monotherapy was also not modified by baseline GLP-1 RA use.

The authors note the study was neither designed nor powered to test the additive treatment effect on UACR reduction with GLP-1 RA, but the complementary-mechanism rationale supports the paradigm that all four classes coadministered might have additive cardiovascular and kidney benefits. Median GLP-1 RA use duration was 13 months, implying that any UACR lowering would largely have occurred prior to study entry; the novel aspect was to evaluate outcomes in patients receiving the four pillars of CKD care.

### Comparison with other trials (from Discussion)
- **FLOW:** semaglutide 1.0 mg weekly; primary composite kidney/CV outcome HR 0.76 (95% CI 0.66–0.88) over median 3.4 years, consistent across subgroups including background SGLT2 inhibitor therapy (P for interaction = 0.109).
- **FIDELITY:** finerenone significantly reduced UACR, persistent regardless of baseline GLP-1 RA use; magnitude greater with (−38%) versus without GLP-1 RA use (−31%) at baseline (P for interaction = 0.03). UACR change was −40% in those on both GLP-1 RA and SGLT2 inhibitor, −38% GLP-1 RA only, −36% SGLT2 inhibitor only (P for interaction = 0.11).
- A 2024 meta-analysis (FLOW, AMPLITUDE-O, Harmony Outcomes) confirmed kidney composite outcome effects did not vary by SGLT2 inhibitor use (RR 0.79 [95% CI 0.66–0.95]).

### Limitations
1. Baseline differences (higher BMI, etc.) may have confounded associations (confounding by indication).
2. GLP-1 RA subgroups were observational; randomization was not stratified by GLP-1 RA use (potential selection bias).
3. Limited statistical power to detect additive/synergistic effects given small combination-therapy subgroup.
4. Differences in quality of care and socioeconomic disparities may have influenced both GLP-1 RA use and CKD burden.
5. Subgroup analysis by specific GLP-1 RA agent was not possible due to small sample sizes.
