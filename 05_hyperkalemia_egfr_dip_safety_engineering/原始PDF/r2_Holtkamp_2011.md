# An acute fall in estimated glomerular filtration rate during treatment with losartan predicts a slower decrease in long-term renal function

**Frank A. Holtkamp¹, Dick de Zeeuw¹, Merlin C. Thomas², Mark E. Cooper², Pieter A. de Graeff¹, Hans J.L. Hillege³, Hans-Henrik Parving⁴, Barry M. Brenner⁵, Shahnaz Shahinfar⁶, Hiddo J. Lambers Heerspink¹**

## Abstract

Intervention in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone-system (RAAS) is associated with slowing the progressive loss of renal function. During initiation of therapy, however, there may be an acute fall in glomerular filtration rate (GFR). We tested whether this initial fall in GFR reflects a renal hemodynamic effect and whether this might result in a slower decline in long-term renal function. We performed a post hoc analysis of the Reduction of Endpoints in Non-Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus with the Angiotensin II Antagonist Losartan (RENAAL) trial. Patients assigned to losartan had a significantly greater acute fall in estimated (eGFR) during the first 3 months compared to patients assigned to placebo, but a significantly slower long-term mean decline of eGFR thereafter. A large interindividual difference, however, was noticed in the acute eGFR change. When patients were divided into tertiles of initial fall in eGFR, the long-term eGFR slope calculated from baseline was significantly higher in patients with an initial fall compared to those with an initial rise. When eGFR decline was calculated from 3 months to the final visit, excluding the initial effect, patients with a large initial fall in eGFR had a significant lower long-term eGFR slope compared to those with a moderate fall or rise. This relationship was independent of other risk markers or change in risk markers for progression of renal disease such as blood pressure and albuminuria. Thus, the greater the acute fall in eGFR, during losartan treatment, the slower the rate of long-term eGFR decline. Hence, interpretation of trial results relying on slope-based GFR outcomes should separate the initial drug-induced GFR change from the subsequent long-term effect on GFR.

---

## Introduction / Study rationale

The RENAAL trial investigated the effects of the ARB losartan versus placebo. The presence of a baseline period without RAAS inhibitor treatment and the availability of serum creatinine values on baseline and every 3 months during therapy allows study of the associations between treatment-induced short-term responses in estimated GFR (eGFR) and long-term renal function decline.

## Results

### Course of eGFR in losartan- and placebo-treated individuals

The fall in eGFR at 3 months after the start of treatment was greater in losartan-treated individuals compared with placebo: **−2.3 (95% CI −2.7 to −1.8) vs −1.6 (−2.0 to −1.1) ml/min per 1.73 m², respectively, P = 0.031**.

The initial fall in eGFR was inversely associated with the long-term eGFR slope, such that the **long-term eGFR slope in the losartan group was significantly smaller (less steep) compared with placebo: −4.2 (95% CI −3.9 to −4.6) vs −5.0 (−4.7 to −5.4) ml/min per 1.73 m² per year; P < 0.001**.

### Predictors of an acute fall in eGFR

The initial change in eGFR in the losartan group showed wide variability: **mean −2.3 ml/min per 1.73 m² (min −18.3; max +14.7)**. In univariate analysis, urinary albumin/creatinine ratio (UACR) and the month-3 change in UACR showed the strongest associations with the degree of the initial eGFR fall (Table 1). In multivariable analysis, male gender, a higher baseline eGFR, UACR, and diastolic blood pressure, a lower hemoglobin, and a larger month-3 decline in UACR were statistically significantly associated with a larger acute fall in eGFR.

### Effect of acute fall in eGFR on long-term renal function decline

Patients assigned to losartan were stratified in tertiles according to the initial change in eGFR (baseline to month 3). Baseline characteristics (Table 2): patients with a larger acute fall in eGFR had significantly higher UACR, eGFR, and systolic blood pressure at baseline, and greater reduction in UACR and systolic blood pressure after 3 months, compared with those with a moderate fall or rise in eGFR.

In losartan-allocated participants, the mean eGFR at the median time (month 33) was **lower in patients with an initial decline vs an initial rise: 28.7 (95% CI 26.3–31.0) vs 33.0 (30.9–35.2) ml/min per 1.73 m²; P = 0.007 (Figure 2)**.

When the eGFR slope was **calculated from baseline**, eGFR decline was higher (steeper) in subjects with an initial fall vs an initial rise: **−5.2 (95% CI −5.8 to −4.7) vs −3.6 (−4.1 to −3.0) ml/min per 1.73 m² per year; P < 0.001**.

A different pattern emerged when the initial eGFR effect was excluded and **long-term eGFR decline was calculated from 3 months to the final visit**. In unadjusted analyses, patients with a large initial fall in eGFR showed a more stable long-term eGFR course compared with those with a moderate fall or an increase: **−3.8 (95% CI −4.4 to −3.2) vs −4.1 (−4.7 to −3.6) vs −4.8 (−5.4 to −4.3) ml/min per 1.73 m², respectively, P = 0.0094 for tertile 1 vs 3**.

A multivariable analysis (adjusting for baseline characteristics and response parameters) demonstrated that an initial steeper fall in eGFR remained statistically significantly associated with a more stable long-term eGFR course (Figure 3). This correlation between the initial eGFR fall and long-term eGFR decline was **exclusively observed in losartan-treated patients**. No correlation was observed between the acute fall in eGFR and long-term eGFR decline in placebo-treated subjects, nor was there any association between an acute fall in eGFR and long-term eGFR slope other than defined by a fall from baseline to month 3. A sensitivity analysis using serum creatinine instead of eGFR gave similar results.

### Hard renal outcomes (doubling of serum creatinine or ESRD)

When the overall population was divided in tertiles, the rate of renal events was higher in those with an initial fall compared with those with an initial rise in eGFR. However, in patients with an initial fall in eGFR, the rate of renal events was profoundly attenuated in losartan-treated patients compared with placebo. In those with an initial rise in eGFR, renal event rate was almost similar between both treatment groups (Table 3).

## Discussion

Initiation of antihypertensive therapy with an ARB induces an acute fall in eGFR that is inversely correlated with renal function decline during long-term follow-up. The greater the acute eGFR fall, the slower the rate of long-term eGFR decline. This relationship is independent of other risk markers or changes in risk markers such as blood pressure and albuminuria.

The pharmacological effects of RAAS blockers on the GFR course are best explained by a **two-slope model**: an acute fall in GFR up to 3 months and an attenuation of the long-term GFR slope until end of treatment. The acute fall could be structural (reduced number of functioning nephrons) or hemodynamic. Studies show that after withdrawal of antihypertensive therapy, GFR increases in the majority of patients and correlates with the initial GFR fall, supporting a **hemodynamic (not structural) origin**.

Because the acute reversible hemodynamic effect makes the long-term slope (from month 3) differ from the mean slope from baseline to study end, renoprotective therapies should focus on the long-term structural decline, excluding the early hemodynamic effect. Losartan-treated subjects with a larger fall in eGFR during the first 3 months had a subsequent slower rate of long-term renal function decline. However, when slope was calculated from baseline, eGFR decline was higher in patients with an initial fall — the large initial hemodynamic effect may negate/obscure the long-term structural benefit. The authors speculate that longer follow-up may reveal a crossing of the long-term slopes (as observed for the losartan–placebo comparison), but the short follow-up precludes verification.

**Comparisons with prior literature:** Similar opposite short-term/long-term GFR effects were seen in the MDRD study (low-protein diet: faster GFR decline in first 4 months, slower thereafter — rendering the primary comparison inconclusive). Apperloo et al. showed in non-diabetic renal patients that ACE-inhibitor–treated patients with a greater initial fall in GFR had a less steep long-term slope. A systematic review of 12 randomized clinical trials showed the acute fall in eGFR (or rise in serum creatinine) was inversely related to the subsequent rate of renal function decline. This is the first large study in diabetes demonstrating this inverse association.

**On hard endpoints:** Because selecting patients by initial change in serum creatinine directly influences the doubling-of-creatinine endpoint, it is unsurprising that those with an initial rise in serum creatinine (fall in eGFR) have higher event rates. However, within the initial-fall group, the renal protective effect of losartan vs placebo is much larger than in the initial-rise group — indicating a fall in eGFR on losartan is "less worse" than on placebo with respect to hard renal outcomes.

**Proposed mechanism:** Regression to the mean is unlikely (no correlation in the placebo group; no correlation when initial change was calculated from baseline to month 6 or 9 in losartan patients). Physiologically, in diabetes and hypertension, dysfunction of afferent arteriole autoregulation increases transmission of systemic pressure into the glomerular capillary network, raising intraglomerular pressure/flow and contributing to glomerular sclerosis and proteinuria. RAAS blockade causes efferent vasodilation → reduced intraglomerular pressure, reduced filtration fraction, and an acute fall in GFR. The reduction in intraglomerular pressure may link the acute GFR reduction to long-term renal protection.

**Limitations:** Post hoc analysis; analyses by eGFR change are no longer randomized (residual confounding possible); results are hypothesis-generating. No pre-enrollment eGFR slope data, so cannot verify pre-treatment slope or distinguish therapy responders from those with progressive renal loss — a fall in eGFR could reflect treatment OR progressive loss, so it must be interpreted in clinical context. The MDRD formula (used for eGFR) suffers from bias and imprecision, likely causing an underestimation of the association strength.

**Clinical implications:**
1. A fall in eGFR after starting a RAAS inhibitor may be an indicator of responsiveness to therapy rather than a safety issue — particularly when albuminuria and blood pressure are also reduced. This encourages continuing treatment, provided other causes (renal artery stenosis, diminished arterial blood volume) or safety issues (**hyperkalemia**) are excluded.
2. The two-slope model means the assumption of a constant eGFR slope during follow-up does not hold; the initial and long-term eGFR decline should be analyzed and reported separately when determining drug effects on renal function.

**Conclusion:** An initial fall in eGFR during ARB treatment is independently inversely associated with less renal function loss during continued treatment. These opposite effects warrant caution in interpreting clinical trials using GFR slope outcomes. The authors recommend separate reporting of the drug-induced short-term and long-term effects on GFR.

## Materials and Methods

### RENAAL study design
Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial evaluating renoprotective effects of a losartan-based antihypertensive regimen vs traditional blood-pressure-lowering regimen in patients with type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and nephropathy. Type 2 diabetes defined as: >30 years old at diagnosis, no history of ketoacidosis, no insulin within 6 months of diagnosis. Inclusion: serum creatinine 1.3–3.0 mg/dl (1.5–3.0 mg/dl for males >60 kg), UACR (first morning specimen) ≥300 mg/g, HbA1c <12%, age 31–70 years. After a 6-week screening phase, patients were randomized to **losartan 50 mg (titrated to 100 mg after 4 weeks) or placebo**. Additional antihypertensives (calcium channel blockers, β-blockers, centrally acting agents, diuretics — excluding ACE inhibitors or other ARBs) permitted to reach BP goal <140/90 mm Hg. Mean follow-up 3.4 years (range 2.3–4.6 years). Conducted per Declaration of Helsinki; informed consent obtained; ethics-committee approved.

### Study visits, measurements, outcomes
Visits: screening, randomization, 1 and 3 months after randomization, then every 3 months. Serum creatinine and electrolytes measured each visit. GFR estimated by the **MDRD equation**. Losartan titrated to maximum 100 mg at the first-month visit. Acute change in eGFR assessed from baseline to month 3 (2 months after institution of maximum losartan dose). Renal events = confirmed doubling of serum creatinine from baseline, or ESRD (chronic dialysis or renal transplantation), adjudicated by an independent outcome committee.

### Statistical analyses
Short-term vs long-term eGFR change calculated from baseline to month 3, and from month 3 to month 39. Long-term eGFR slope difference between groups estimated by a linear mixed effects model with random intercepts and random slopes. Because an acute eGFR fall was only observed in losartan-assigned individuals, a multivariable model identified predictors of an initial fall during losartan therapy (covariates: age, gender, BMI, log-transformed UACR, systolic and diastolic BP, hemoglobin, total cholesterol, baseline diuretic use, and baseline-to-month-3 change in log-UACR and systolic/diastolic BP). Losartan patients were compared across tertiles of acute eGFR fall; long-term slope in each tertile estimated by linear mixed effects model. The initial fall in eGFR was controlled for baseline eGFR, diastolic BP, hemoglobin, gender, log-UACR, and month-3 change in log-UACR. Sensitivity analysis replaced eGFR with serum creatinine. Two-sided P ≤ 0.05 indicated significance. Analyses performed with SAS version 9.1 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC).
