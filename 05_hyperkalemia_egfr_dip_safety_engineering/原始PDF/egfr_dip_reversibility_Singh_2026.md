# Effect of Finerenone Treatment Discontinuation on Kidney and Cardiovascular Outcomes: A FIDELITY Analysis

## Abstract

**Introduction.** Finerenone reduced the risk of heart and kidney events in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes (T2D) in FIDELITY, a prespecified pooled analysis combining data from the phase III FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993) and FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049) trials. This FIDELITY analysis aimed to identify and assess key predictors of finerenone discontinuation and evaluate the impact of discontinuation on its efficacy in people with CKD and T2D.

**Methods.** Adults with CKD (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio 30–≤5,000 mg/g, estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] ≥25 mL/min/1.73 m²) and T2D on optimized renin-angiotensin system inhibition were randomized 1:1 to finerenone or placebo. Baseline characteristics were identified and assessed as predictors of treatment discontinuation using a multivariate Cox proportional hazards model. Stratified Cox models with treatment discontinuation as a time-varying covariate were used to assess the effect of treatment discontinuation on composite kidney and cardiovascular (CV) outcomes (kidney: kidney failure, sustained ≥57% eGFR decrease from baseline over at least 4 weeks, or kidney-related death; CV: CV death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure).

**Results.** Among 12,990 participants included in the analysis, 22.8% and 21.6% prematurely discontinued treatment in the finerenone and placebo arms, respectively. Advanced age, White/Black race, lower eGFR, higher urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio, and higher serum potassium at baseline were identified as predictors of finerenone discontinuation. Crude event rates per 100 patient-years for the composite kidney and CV outcomes were lower with finerenone versus placebo under treatment (kidney: 1.09 vs. 1.71; CV: 2.98 vs. 3.78) as well as after discontinuation (kidney: 11.95 vs. 13.67; CV: 14.07 vs. 14.73). The effect of finerenone on composite kidney and CV outcomes appeared to be reduced after discontinuation (HR = 0.82; 95% CI 0.66–1.02; HR = 0.93; 95% CI 0.79–1.09, respectively) versus the time on-treatment (HR = 0.65; 95% CI 0.54–0.78; p_interaction = 0.0959; HR = 0.79; 95% CI 0.70–0.88; p_interaction = 0.0960, respectively).

**Conclusion.** In FIDELITY, treatment discontinuation rates were similar in the finerenone and placebo arms. Finerenone demonstrated numerically higher kidney and CV benefits during treatment versus after discontinuation.

## Introduction

Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is considered the leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD), with approximately 40% of people living with the disease eventually developing CKD. Both CKD and diabetes are associated with an increased risk and severity of debilitating complications, including cardiovascular (CV) disease. Current treatment guidelines recommend multiple therapies to reduce the risk of CV events and CKD progression, including angiotensin receptor blockers (ARBs) or ACE inhibitors, SGLT-2 inhibitors (SGLT-2is), GLP-1 receptor agonists, and nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists. Although the CV and kidney benefits of these drugs are established, the impact on CV and kidney outcomes of discontinuing them is not well understood, especially for drugs approved in the last decade, such as finerenone. Most existing evidence is based on meta-analyses and cohort studies of renin-angiotensin system inhibitors (RASis) in CKD, which show that discontinuation of ARBs/ACE inhibitors is associated with increased risk of major adverse CV events (MACE), end-stage kidney disease (ESKD), and all-cause mortality.

Finerenone is a distinct, selective, nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist that inhibits mineralocorticoid receptor overactivation. It demonstrated favorable effects on kidney disease progression and CV events in FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD (adults with stage 1–4 CKD with moderately to severely increased albuminuria and concomitant T2D). Over a median follow-up of 2.6 and 3.4 years, 24.1% and 21.4% of participants in FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD, respectively, permanently discontinued finerenone treatment (excluding discontinuations due to death). This post hoc analysis used the FIDELITY dataset to explore predictors of premature finerenone treatment discontinuation and to assess the effect of treatment discontinuation on kidney and CV outcomes.

## Methods

### Study design, participants, and procedures
This post hoc analysis used FIDELITY, a prespecified pooled analysis combining individual patient-level data from FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993) and FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049) — phase III, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter trials. Eligible participants were adults (≥18 years) with CKD (UACR 30–<300 mg/g and eGFR 25–≤90 mL/min/1.73 m², or UACR 300–≤5,000 mg/g and eGFR ≥25 mL/min/1.73 m²) and T2D, on optimized renin-angiotensin system blockade, with serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L at both run-in and screening, and without symptomatic chronic HF with reduced ejection fraction.

Participants were randomized 1:1 to once-daily oral finerenone or matching placebo. Initiation dose was based on baseline eGFR (10 mg once daily if baseline eGFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m²; 20 mg if ≥60). Up-titration to target 20 mg was encouraged after 1 month if blood potassium ≤4.8 mmol/L; down-titration was allowed at any time. Sham titrations were performed in the placebo group. If blood potassium exceeded 5.5 mmol/L, study drug was withheld and restarted once potassium decreased to ≤5.0 mmol/L.

### Outcomes
To evaluate predictors of discontinuation, the analysis assessed time to premature discontinuation in the finerenone arm (discontinuations due to death excluded). Efficacy outcomes: composite kidney outcome (time to kidney failure, sustained decrease in eGFR ≥57% from baseline over ≥4 weeks, or kidney-related death); composite CV outcome (CV death, nonfatal stroke, nonfatal MI, or hospitalization for HF).

### Statistical analysis
Demographics, discontinuation reasons, and efficacy analyses used the full analysis set (all randomized participants excluding critical GCP violations). Kaplan-Meier estimates for time to end-of-treatment were based on the safety analysis set (SAS; participants taking ≥1 dose, without critical GCP violations). Predictor analyses used the SAS. Baseline characteristics (age, sex, race, region, eGFR, UACR, serum potassium, β-blocker and SGLT-2i use) were selected from clinically relevant factors and univariate Cox models, then analyzed in a multivariate Cox proportional hazards model using automatic variable selection based on the Akaike information criterion (AIC). An interaction between race and region was observed; only race was included. A random survival forest analysis ranked selected baseline characteristics by importance. The effect of discontinuation on efficacy was assessed with crude event rates per 100 patient-years (PY) under treatment and after discontinuation, and stratified Cox models with treatment discontinuation as a time-varying covariate. HRs with 95% CIs were reported. Treatment effect was compared between the primary data scope (events until end of study) and the on-treatment data scope (events up to 30 days after discontinuation). Aalen-Johansen risk differences (primary vs. on-treatment scope) and 95% CIs were calculated. Analyses used SAS v9.4; random survival forest used R v4.2.1.

## Results

### Participants and baseline characteristics
Of 12,990 participants in the full analysis set, 2,889 (22.2%) prematurely discontinued treatment (finerenone: 22.8%; placebo: 21.6%) for reasons other than death; 10,101 (77.8%) did not prematurely discontinue (either died or remained on-treatment; finerenone 77.2%; placebo 78.4%). Baseline characteristics were generally balanced between arms, but a few key differences existed between those who discontinued and those who did not: those who discontinued were more often male, White or Black/African American, and based in North America or Western Europe; Eastern Europe was underrepresented among discontinuers. More discontinuers had a history of CV disease, hypertension, heart failure, atrial fibrillation/flutter, coronary artery disease, and myocardial infarction. Mean baseline eGFR was similar between groups; those who continued had lower median baseline UACR. Loop diuretic and potassium supplement use was higher in discontinuers, while SGLT-2i and metformin use was slightly lower.

### Reasons for premature treatment discontinuation
The primary reason for premature discontinuation in both arms was adverse events (AEs): 9.0% (finerenone) vs. 8.7% (placebo). Those discontinuing due to death (finerenone 5.3% vs. placebo 6.3%) were excluded. Following AEs, withdrawal by participants (6.2% vs. 5.9%) and physician decision (4.6% vs. 3.7%) were most common.

### Adverse events
The incidence of treatment-emergent AEs leading to study drug discontinuation was slightly higher with finerenone (6.4%) versus placebo (5.4%). The rate of discontinuation due to hyperkalemia was low in both arms but slightly higher with finerenone (1.7%) versus placebo (0.6%). Other treatment-emergent AEs leading to discontinuation were similarly common in both arms.

### Time to end-of-treatment and predictors of discontinuation
Kaplan-Meier curves for time to last study drug intake were similar between arms. Based on the multivariate Cox model (finerenone arm), risk of finerenone discontinuation was increased:
- Older age vs. <65 years: age 65–74 years HR = 1.18 (95% CI 1.05–1.33); age ≥75 years HR = 1.42 (95% CI 1.21–1.65); p<0.0001.
- Black and White vs. Asian: Black HR = 1.99 (95% CI 1.55–2.56); White HR = 1.34 (95% CI 1.16–1.54); p<0.0001.
- UACR ≥1,000 mg/g vs. <300 mg/g: HR = 1.65 (95% CI 1.42–1.92); p<0.0001.
- Serum potassium ≥4.5 mmol/L vs. <4.5: 4.5–≤4.8 mmol/L HR = 1.21 (95% CI 1.07–1.36); >4.8 mmol/L HR = 1.33 (95% CI 1.14–1.55); p=0.0001.

Risk of finerenone discontinuation decreased with higher eGFR vs. eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73 m²: 45–≤60 HR = 0.71 (95% CI 0.62–0.81; p<0.0001); ≥60 HR = 0.62 (95% CI 0.53–0.72; p<0.0001).

An interaction analysis (Cox models) showed no significant heterogeneity between treatment arms for any variable analyzed. The random survival forest analysis identified **eGFR, UACR, age, and serum potassium** as the most important baseline variables associated with finerenone discontinuation.

### Effect of treatment discontinuation (time-varying covariate) on composite outcomes

**Composite kidney outcome.** Crude event rates were lower under treatment in both arms. Finerenone vs. placebo under treatment: 1.09 per 100 PY (95% CI 0.94–1.26) vs. 1.71 per 100 PY (95% CI 1.52–1.92). After discontinuation (shorter exposure times): 11.95 per 100 PY (95% CI 10.24–13.87) vs. 13.67 per 100 PY (95% CI 11.73–15.85). The effect of finerenone vs. placebo on the composite kidney outcome was numerically reduced after discontinuation (HR = 0.82; 95% CI 0.66–1.02) compared with on-treatment (HR = 0.65; 95% CI 0.54–0.78; p_interaction = 0.0959). Within the on-treatment data scope (events up to 30 days after discontinuation), the effect appeared greater (HR = 0.68; 95% CI 0.57–0.81; p<0.0001) than with the primary data scope (HR = 0.76; 95% CI 0.66–0.88; p=0.0001). Risk differences were similar in primary and on-treatment scopes.

**Composite CV outcome.** Crude event rates were lower under treatment in both arms. Finerenone vs. placebo under treatment: 2.98 per 100 PY (95% CI 2.72–3.25) vs. 3.78 per 100 PY (95% CI 3.49–4.09). After discontinuation: 14.07 per 100 PY (95% CI 12.59–15.68) vs. 14.73 per 100 PY (95% CI 13.13–16.46). A numerical decrease in the finerenone treatment effect on the composite CV outcome was observed after discontinuation (HR = 0.93; 95% CI 0.79–1.09) compared with on-treatment (HR = 0.79; 95% CI 0.70–0.88; p_interaction = 0.0960). The relative hazard reduction was slightly lower within the primary data scope (HR = 0.86; 95% CI 0.78–0.95; p=0.0018) than the on-treatment data scope (HR = 0.81; 95% CI 0.73–0.90; p=0.0001). Risk differences appeared slightly increased in the on-treatment scope, with substantial overlap between 95% CIs.

## Discussion

In this post hoc FIDELITY analysis, treatment discontinuation rates in the finerenone arm were comparable with placebo; AEs were the most common reason for stopping. Risk of finerenone discontinuation was highest among older participants, Black or White race, and those with lower eGFR, higher UACR, and higher serum potassium — with no significant heterogeneity between arms. eGFR, UACR, age, and serum potassium were the most important variables influencing discontinuation. The treatment effect of finerenone on composite kidney and CV outcomes seemed less apparent after discontinuation, but no significant heterogeneity was observed between the on-treatment period and the period after discontinuation.

Findings that low eGFR and high UACR predict discontinuation suggest that declining kidney function may increase the risk of AEs leading to discontinuation, highlighting the need for greater vigilance in patients at high risk of CKD progression. Higher baseline serum potassium was another important predictor; hyperkalemia risk increases with deteriorating kidney function, and finerenone is associated with increased hyperkalemia risk versus placebo — though finerenone-induced hyperkalemia is clinically manageable with low discontinuation rates. Clinicians should closely monitor serum potassium; if levels reach >5.5 mmol/L, finerenone can be temporarily discontinued and restarted once potassium falls to ≤5.0 mmol/L. Baseline β-blocker use and concomitant SGLT-2i use did not have a strong influence on discontinuation; a prior FIDELITY analysis reported a greater reduction in eGFR decline (baseline to month 4) and lower hyperkalemia incidence with concomitant SGLT-2i.

Older age as a predictor aligns with a prior FIDELITY age/sex analysis (elderly ≥65 years associated with increased hypotension, AEs leading to discontinuation, and death). Black and White participants were at greater risk of discontinuation than other races; prior FIDELITY analyses noted Black participants had lower baseline mean eGFR, longer diabetes duration, and greater use of insulin, β-blockers, and potassium supplements, while Asian participants had lower incidence of any AE leading to discontinuation (5.9% vs. 6.5% non-Asians). Socioeconomic and regional factors likely contribute.

In FIDELITY, finerenone reduced the risk of composite kidney and CV outcomes by 23% and 14%, respectively. Here, the treatment effect seemed less apparent after discontinuation, without significant heterogeneity between periods. Prior RASi discontinuation studies (meta-analysis; Swedish observational cohort n=10,254 — 5-year MACE and mortality risk 11.9% and 13.6%; prospective cohort n=10,400 — RASi discontinuation associated with higher MACE HR=1.27 [95% CI 1.08–1.49], HF HR=1.85 [95% CI 1.53–2.25], ESKD HR=1.30 [95% CI 1.17–1.43]) support continued therapy. Further investigation, especially real-world evidence, is warranted.

**Limitations.** Post hoc nature means findings are hypothesis-generating only. A race–region interaction during variable selection meant only one (race) could be included in the final multivariate model.

## Conclusion

In this post hoc FIDELITY analysis, older age at baseline, Black and White race, reduced baseline eGFR, increased baseline UACR, and higher baseline serum potassium were predictors of finerenone discontinuation. Of these, eGFR, UACR, age, and serum potassium were the most important. The beneficial treatment effect of finerenone on composite kidney and CV outcomes was numerically higher during the treatment period than after discontinuation, highlighting the importance of maintaining finerenone treatment in patients with CKD and T2D who are at high kidney and CV risk. While guidance for restarting finerenone after treatment interruption is available, increased clinician awareness is needed. Further studies are needed to identify strategies to avoid permanent discontinuation.
