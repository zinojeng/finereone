# Sodium Zirconium Cyclosilicate for RAASi Optimization in Patients with HFrEF: A Retrospective Analysis

**Brief Report** — Cardiol Ther (2024) 13:797–809

Received: June 7, 2024 / Accepted: October 1, 2024 / Published online: November 1, 2024. © The Author(s) 2024.

## Abstract

**Introduction:** In this retrospective analysis, the authors evaluate the effectiveness of the potassium (K⁺) binder sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (SZC) in maintaining normokalemia and facilitating the initiation, optimization, and maintenance of renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitors (RAASi) in patients with heart failure (HF) with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF).

**Methods:** A total of 44 patients with HFrEF and a history of hyperkalemia who were receiving SZC to enable the prescription of RAASi were identified from two district general hospital sites. Retrospective analysis was performed to determine biochemical response, alterations in pharmacotherapy, and subsequent HF outcomes following initiation of SZC.

**Results:** Mean K⁺ was reduced by 0.9 mmol/L within 1 month of initiation of SZC; mean K⁺ after 12 months of treatment was 4.8 mmol/L with a median (interquartile range) duration of treatment of 13 (8.4–15.1) months. Following SZC treatment, 100% of patients received an angiotensin receptor–neprilysin inhibitor (18% increase) and 93% received a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (41% increase), with 59% and 37% achieving guideline-recommended dosing, respectively. Ninety-one percent of patients were able to receive triple or quadruple therapy with the addition of a beta-blocker and a sodium glucose co-transporter 2 inhibitor. Reduced rates of hospitalization for HF (HHF) were observed with 12 episodes per 100 patient-years recorded (reduced from 21) in addition to improvements in mean left ventricular ejection fraction (29–36%) and median N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide (3458–2055 ng/L, 45% median reduction). Renal function (creatinine clearance increased from 48.4 to 49.3 ml/min) and systolic blood pressure (decreased from 124 to 122 mmHg) were similar following optimization, and no tolerability issues were identified.

**Conclusions:** Extended real-world treatment with the K⁺ binder SZC was effective at maintaining normokalemia, and was associated with a greater uptake of RAASi, a reduced rate of HHF, and improvements in cardiac biomarkers in patients with HFrEF.

**Keywords:** Heart failure; Hyperkalemia; Renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system inhibitor; Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate

## Key Summary Points

- RAASi are commonly used for treatment of HFrEF in accordance with guideline recommendations.
- Patients receiving RAASi are at increased risk of developing hyperkalemia, which can provoke life-threatening arrhythmias and often lead to interruption, cessation, or suboptimal dosing of these life-extending therapies.
- This retrospective analysis evaluates the effectiveness of the potassium binder SZC in maintaining normokalemia and facilitating the initiation, optimization, and maintenance of RAASi in patients with HFrEF.
- SZC was well tolerated in this real-world group of high-risk patients with HFrEF, was effective at maintaining normokalemia, and was associated with greater uptake of RAASi, more patients achieving guideline-recommended dosages, a reduced rate of hospitalization for HF, and improvements in cardiac biomarkers.

## Introduction

RAASi are established pharmacotherapy for the treatment of HFrEF, recommended by guidelines owing to associated improvements in patient survival, symptoms, and reduced rates of hospitalization. However, patients receiving RAASi are at increased risk of developing hyperkalemia (HK). Recent international guidelines have recommended K⁺ binders to allow initiation and optimization of RAASi in patients with HF and chronic kidney disease (CKD); stopping and down-titrating RAASi should be the last resort if the risk of HK cannot be mitigated.

HK is defined as serum or plasma K⁺ >5.0 mmol/L, with severity classed as mild (>5.0 to <5.5 mmol/L), moderate (5.5–6.0 mmol/L) or severe (>6.0 mmol/L). HK, if left untreated, can lead to fatal arrhythmias or result in cessation or down-titration of guideline-recommended RAASi therapies. Discontinuation and down-titration of RAASi have been associated with adverse outcomes in patients with HF and CKD.

Physician inertia may also prevent patients at risk of HK receiving RAASi at target doses. One approach is to induce kaliuresis by prescribing loop or thiazide diuretics; however, this is of limited value in patients with hypotension and renal impairment, often encountered in chronic HF. HF itself is a risk factor for HK, as are prevalent comorbidities such as CKD, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, and older age. A 2022 international Delphi consensus recommended that HK should be recognized as a predictable, treatable, and manageable side effect of optimal HF/CKD therapies.

Emerging strategies to overcome HK as a barrier to RAASi use include the novel K⁺ binders SZC and patiromer, which lower serum K⁺ concentrations through cation exchange of sodium and calcium, respectively, in the alimentary canal, causing increased fecal excretion of K⁺. In this retrospective analysis, the authors describe the experience of their HF service using SZC to enable patients with HFrEF with HK to receive RAASi at target or maximally tolerated doses. They studied changes in RAASi therapies, serum K⁺ levels, hospitalization for decompensated HF or HK, HF outcomes (including LVEF and NT-proBNP), and survival.

## Methods

### Study Population
Patients with HFrEF (LVEF ≤40%) who were receiving the K⁺ binder SZC between July 2020 and October 2022 were studied. Forty-nine patients were identified, of whom five were excluded from analysis because of non-adherence with SZC (n=2), in-patient prescribing only (n=2), and intermittent prescribing (n=1). All patients had multidisciplinary team input with involvement of a cardiologist specializing in HF.

### Ethical Approval
This retrospective study was submitted to Cwm Taf Morgannwg University Health Board Research & Development department. As a service evaluation using evidence-based medicines with no data collected beyond routine clinical care, formal research ethics committee approval was not required.

### Baseline Characteristics / Study Outcomes
Serum K⁺ concentrations were recorded 6 months pre-initiation, at initiation, and up to 24 months post-initiation of SZC. Prescriptions and doses of RAASi were recorded before and after SZC initiation until guideline-recommended or maximum tolerated dosages were achieved. NT-proBNP and LVEF were measured prior to K⁺ binder initiation and following optimization. HF hospitalization and admissions due to HK were recorded from electronic health records, 2 years prior to SZC initiation and until the end of the analysis period.

### Data Analyses
Statistical analysis was conducted using Prism (V10.2.0 for Mac, GraphPad Software). p<0.05 was accepted as significant. Kruskal–Wallis with Dunn's post hoc test compared pre- and post-initiation serum K⁺ levels. Two-sided Fisher's exact tests determined differences in percentage achieving guideline-recommended dosage. McNemar's test determined differences in the number of HFrEF therapies. Mann–Whitney tests determined differences in LVEF and NT-proBNP. The Wilcoxon signed-rank test determined differences in patient morbidity.

## Results

### Baseline (Table 1, n=44)
Forty-four patients were included out of 49 identified. Mean age 75.0 ± 22 years; 12 (27%) female; systolic BP 124 ± 19 mmHg; diastolic BP 70 ± 14 mmHg; mean LVEF 28.7 ± 20%; median baseline NT-proBNP 3458 ng/L (IQR 2163–6437). NYHA class: I 6 (14%), II 15 (36%), III 20 (48%), IV 1 (2%).

Comorbidities: atrial fibrillation 22 (50%), anemia 13 (30%), cancer 5 (11%), CKD 38 (86%) (Stage 2 8 [18%], Stage 3a 10 [23%], Stage 3b 15 [34%], Stage 4 11 [25%]), COPD 8 (18%), cerebrovascular disease 8 (18%), hypertension 27 (61%), ischemic heart disease 24 (55%; PCI 7 [16%], CABG 6 [14%]), LBBB 10 (23%), diabetes mellitus 17 (39%; type 1 1 [2%], type 2 16 [36%]).

Baseline medication/device use: ACEi (ramipril) 3 (7%), ARB 0, ARNi (sacubitril/valsartan) 36 (82%), ACEi/ARB/ARNi 39 (89%), MRA (spironolactone/eplerenone) 23 (52%), beta-blocker (bisoprolol/carvedilol) 35 (80%), SGLT2i (dapagliflozin) 29 (66%), loop diuretic 13 (30%), ICD 1 (2%), CRT-P 3 (6.8%).

### Serum K⁺
At initiation of SZC, mean sK⁺ was 5.7 mmol/L; 34% had mild HK (5.0–5.5 mmol/L), 36% moderate (5.5–6.0 mmol/L), and 30% severe (>6.0 mmol/L). One month following initiation, 59% achieved normokalemic status and a further 39% achieved K⁺ <5.5 mmol/L. Serum K⁺ concentration was significantly lower post-initiation (p<0.0001, Kruskal–Wallis). Mean K⁺ after 12 months was 4.8 mmol/L. Using a broader local definition of normokalemia (3.5–5.3 mmol/L), 95% of patients had controlled K⁺ levels within the first 28 days of therapy.

### RAASi Therapies (Table 2)
SZC was associated with higher prescription rates of GDMT: 93% received MRA (37% increase) and 100% received an ARNi (11% increase). The proportion receiving a guideline-recommended dose improved with a 27% increase in MRA (p<0.05) and a 43% increase in ACEi/ARB/ARNi (p<0.05). Mean MRA dose increased from 13 to 31 mg/day; mean percentage of guideline-recommended dose for ACEi/ARB/ARNi increased by 30–74%, equivalent to a mean ARNi dose of 296 mg/day.

### "Four-Pillar" Therapy
There was a two-fold increase in patients prescribed "four-pillar therapy" (32→64%; p<0.05), with 91% prescribed three or more agents. At data retrieval, 41% had SZC duration <12 months, 45% between 12 and 24 months, and 14% >24 months. Median duration of treatment was 13 months.

### HF Outcomes / Hospitalizations (Table 3)
Following RAASi optimization, NT-proBNP reduced in 79% of patients (median 2055 ng/L; p<0.05). There was a 7% increase in mean LVEF (29→36%; n=35; p<0.05). No significant changes in blood pressure or renal function. Hospitalization for decompensated HF was 21 per 100 patient-years in the 2 years preceding SZC, reduced to 12 per 100 patient-years on SZC (p<0.05). HK hospitalizations were 8 per 100 patient-years both pre- and post-initiation. Survival 40 (91%).

### Tolerability/Compliance
Tolerability of SZC was 100% during the study period. As a real-world evaluation, compliance was assumed through continued monitoring of serum K⁺ levels while receiving SZC.

## Discussion

RAASi therapy forms part of GDMT in HF and CKD, but HK can be a barrier to initiating and maintaining RAASi. Discontinuation/down-titration of RAASi has been related to adverse cardiorenal outcomes. Using K⁺ binders to control HK is now a recommended strategy to facilitate RAASi optimization. K⁺ binders have shown effectiveness in the HARMONIZE and DIAMOND trials; in this study most serum K⁺ levels were within normal range, including for patients treated for 24 months.

The SCREAM project demonstrated that 47% of patients would have MRA discontinued after a HK episode and 74% would not have therapy reinitiated within 12 months. Although discontinuing MRA reduced recurrent HK risk by 25% (HR 0.75, 95% CI 0.72–0.79), it resulted in a 10% increase in cardiovascular mortality or HF hospitalization (HR 1.10, 95% CI 1.06–1.14). The REALIZE-K study (NCT04676646) is evaluating whether SZC can enable safe long-term MRA optimization in patients with HFrEF and prevalent/high risk of hyperkalemia.

SGLT2i reduce risk of serious HK by 16% (p<0.001); DAPA-HF secondary analysis showed a 50% reduction in the incidence of moderate/severe HK (>6.0 mmol/L) in patients co-prescribed an MRA (p=0.010). SGLT2i were prescribed in 86% of patients during medicine optimization; 66% were prescribed an SGLT2i when initiated on SZC.

Following SZC initiation, a reduction in mean K⁺ was observed within the first month, with 58% achieving serum K⁺ between 4.0 and 5.0 mmol/L (ESC-defined normokalemia). Initiation of SZC and higher GDMT uptake had no overall impact on loop diuretic use (30% pre → 25% post-SZC). SZC was well tolerated (100% remained on SZC), with nearly half receiving the 10-g dose.

### Limitations
Small sample size of 44 patients reduces statistical power and may not be representative of the broader HFrEF population; results require cautious interpretation. Selection of patients treated with SZC was at the discretion of prescribing physicians, potentially introducing bias.

## Conclusion
The use of the K⁺ binder SZC was well tolerated in this real-world study of high-risk patients with HF. SZC is effective at controlling K⁺ level and enables optimization of GDMT by achieving higher doses. Ongoing trials such as REALIZE-K are further investigating the impact of K⁺ binders on MRA optimization in patients with HFrEF and with/at risk of HK. Future studies could assess the effectiveness of SZC in larger patient populations with HFrEF in a randomized setting to remove selection bias and improve statistical power.
