# Abstract

Background and hypothesis. Finerenone reduces cardiovascular events in patients with type 2 diabetes (T2D) and chronic kidney disease (CKD). The cardiovascular benefit emerges within months, before measurable kidney protection, but the early pathways carrying it are not comprehensively quantified. We hypothesized that early reductions in urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) and systolic blood pressure (SBP) jointly mediate a substantial proportion of finerenone’s long-term cardiovascular benefit, whereas changes in body weight and serum potassium do not.

# Results

At month 4, finerenone reduced UACR by 32.2% (95% CI 30.3 to 34.2), SBP by 3.6 mmHg (3.1 to 4.1), and body weight by 0.23 kg (0.13 to 0.32), and increased serum potassium by 0.19 mEq/L (0.17 to 0.20). Although all four biomarkers changed significantly, only UACR and SBP mediated cardiovascular outcomes: UACR mediated 39% (95% CI 7 to 71) and SBP mediated 21% (3 to 40); body weight and potassium did not. Jointly, UACR and SBP mediated 50% (95% CI 21 to 100) of the cardiovascular benefit.

# Conclusions

In T2D and CKD, early reductions in UACR and SBP jointly account for half of finerenone’s long-term cardiovascular benefit. Body weight and potassium changes do not mediate the benefit, supporting UACR and SBP as complementary indicators of cardiovascular efficacy.

# Introduction

Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality in people with type 2 diabetes (T2D), and the risk is amplified when chronic kidney disease (CKD) coexists. The combination of T2D and CKD defines a high-risk phenotype in which both kidney failure and cardiovascular events accrue at substantially elevated rates.1 Albuminuria, a hallmark of diabetic kidney disease, identifies the patients at greatest risk for these adverse outcomes.2

The American Diabetes Association's Standards of Care in Diabetes recommends multifactorial cardiovascular and kidney risk reduction in patients with T2D and CKD, including blood pressure optimization, glycemic control, and renin-angiotensin system blockade.3 In 2023, the Standards added the nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA) finerenone as a recommended therapy for patients with T2D, CKD, and elevated urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR) despite maximally tolerated renin-angiotensin system inhibition.3 This recommendation followed the FIDELITY pooled analysis of the FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD phase 3 trials, which demonstrated that finerenone significantly reduced both kidney and cardiovascular outcomes.4,5

Although finerenone reduces both kidney and cardiovascular events, the time courses of these benefits differ. Kidney protective effects emerge gradually, requiring approximately 18 months to become statistically apparent, whereas the cardiovascular benefit appears within months of treatment initiation.6–8 This temporal dissociation suggests that early physiological responses to finerenone—rather than slow accrual of kidney protection—drive the early cardiovascular benefit. The clinical pathways through which this early benefit is transmitted, however, have not been comprehensively quantified.

---


Two prior mediation analyses have addressed components of this question. We previously reported that finerenone-induced reduction in UACR mediated 37% of the treatment effect on cardiovascular outcomes in the FIDELITY population.⁹ In a separate time-varying analysis, systolic blood pressure (SBP) reduction was estimated to account for 12.6% of the cardiovascular benefit.¹⁰ These analyses were conducted in isolation. Whether albuminuria and SBP capture overlapping or distinct portions of the benefit—and whether other early physiological responses such as body weight reduction or serum potassium increase contribute additional mediation—remains unknown.

In this prespecified post hoc analysis, we used a causal mediation framework to quantify the proportion of finerenone's cardiovascular benefit transmitted through early changes (baseline to month 4) in four clinically modifiable parameters representing distinct physiological pathways: UACR (kidney/glomerular permeability), SBP (hemodynamic), body weight (volume), and serum potassium (mineralocorticoid receptor blockade). We then examined whether UACR and SBP, considered jointly, explain a substantial fraction of the cardiovascular benefit, and whether body weight and serum potassium contribute additional mediation despite their statistical change with treatment.

# Discussion

Among 12,143 patients with T2D and CKD, finerenone changed four early biomarkers—reducing

UACR, SBP, and body weight, and increasing serum potassium—at month 4 compared with

placebo. Despite all four changing significantly, only UACR and SBP mediated the long-term

cardiovascular benefit; changes in body weight and serum potassium did not. Considered jointly,

UACR and SBP mediated half of finerenone’s cardiovascular benefit, with the remaining half

attributable to direct effects not captured by these systemic markers.



---

The negative findings for body weight and serum potassium are as informative as the positive findings for UACR and SBP. Both biomarkers changed significantly with finerenone treatment, yet neither mediated the cardiovascular outcome. The small reduction in body weight (0.23 kg at four months) did not translate into measurable cardiovascular protection, arguing against a primarily diuretic mechanism for the cardiovascular benefit. Likewise, the 0.19 mEq/L increase in serum potassium—an expected pharmacodynamic effect of mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) blockade—did not detract from the cardiovascular benefit. Thus, this analysis does not simply identify any biomarker that changes with treatment; it isolates the specific physiological pathways through which finerenone transmits cardiovascular benefit and rules out alternatives.

Our findings extend two prior analyses of FIDELITY. We previously reported that UACR mediated 37% of finerenone's cardiovascular benefit in this dataset. The current estimate of 39% (with treatment-mediator interaction) is consistent with that finding. Ruilope and colleagues, using a time-varying SBP analysis, estimated that SBP mediated 12.6% of the cardiovascular benefit. The current estimate of 21% reflects methodological differences in the timing of mediator measurement and the modeling framework. The principal new contribution of this analysis is the joint multi-mediator framework: it quantifies the additive predictive value of these two pathways and rules out body weight and serum potassium as mediators. The joint mediation of 50% indicates that UACR and SBP capture overlapping but distinct elements of finerenone's mechanism—glomerular permeability and systemic hemodynamics, respectively.

Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonism has multiple effects on the cardiovascular system and the kidney. The diuretic effect of MR blockade through the cortical collecting duct has long been recognized, and the antihypertensive effect of MRAs in resistant hypertension is conventionally attributed to volume reduction. In our analysis, the volume effect indexed by body weight change.

---

was small and did not mediate the cardiovascular outcome, whereas SBP reduction—mediating

approximately one fifth of the cardiovascular benefit individually—appeared to operate through

pathways beyond simple volume contraction. We therefore speculate that finerenone's blood

pressure-mediated cardiovascular protection involves improvements in vascular smooth muscle

and endothelial function.²⁰

Approximately half of the cardiovascular benefit is not captured by changes in UACR or SBP.

This residual benefit is consistent with direct molecular effects of MR antagonism on cardiac and

vascular tissue. MR overactivation in cardiomyocytes and myeloid cells drives myocardial

inflammation and fibrosis; finerenone's selective high-affinity binding to the cardiomyocyte MR

produces antifibrotic and anti-inflammatory effects independent of systemic hemodynamics.²¹

Such tissue-level effects cannot be measured through the systemic biomarkers analyzed here,

which provides a plausible biological explanation for the unexplained portion of the benefit.

These findings have practical implications for clinicians managing patients with T2D and CKD.

Two simple, widely available clinical measures—UACR and clinic SBP—jointly track half of the

cardiovascular benefit attributable to finerenone. UACR and SBP should be viewed as

complementary rather than competing indicators of a beneficial response to MR antagonism.

Equally important, body weight changes and serum potassium changes are poor surrogates for

cardiovascular benefit and should not be used as response markers. The small finerenone-induced

increase in serum potassium does not detract from the cardiovascular protection, supporting the

safety-versus-benefit calculation underlying the protocol-based potassium monitoring already

incorporated into clinical use of finerenone.

# Limitations

Several limitations should be considered. First, although treatment assignment was randomized, the relationship between mediator and outcome is observational; identification of natural direct and indirect effects rests on the assumption that no unmeasured confounder of the mediator-outcome relationship exists conditional on measured baseline covariates. To quantify the robustness of the mediated effects to such unmeasured confounding, we computed E-values for the natural indirect effects on the relative mean survival scale. The E-value for the joint UACR-and-SBP indirect effect was 1.29 (1.19 for the confidence limit nearest the null); corresponding values were 1.25 (1.14) for UACR and 1.16 (1.11) for SBP. These indicate that an unmeasured mediator-outcome confounder would need to be associated with both the mediators and the cardiovascular outcome by a relative mean survival ratio of at least this magnitude, beyond the measured baseline covariates, to fully account for the mediated effect. Because these E-values are expressed on the relative mean survival scale, on which both treatment and mediator effects are modest, they should be interpreted as the minimum strength of residual confounding required rather than as evidence that no such confounding exists. This assumption is not directly testable in the current implementation of CMAverse for survival outcomes.

Second, the analysis used clinic SBP measurements. We have previously shown that finerenone produces an 8 to 10 mmHg reduction in 24-hour ambulatory SBP, considerably larger than the 1 to 4 mmHg reduction seen in clinic measurements. The true mediation by SBP is therefore likely underestimated when clinic measurements are used. Third, serum potassium was actively managed per protocol—drug was withheld at potassium >5.5 mEq/L and restarted at lower dose when potassium ≤5.0 mEq/L—so

---


the four-month potassium change reflects both pharmacology and protocol-driven titration. The

null mediation by potassium nonetheless argues against any cardiovascular detriment from the

protocol-permitted potassium increases. Fourth, important biomarkers including renin,

aldosterone, and inflammatory or fibrosis markers were not measured for the full cohort and could

not be included as mediators. Fifth, these results pertain specifically to finerenone in T2D and

CKD; they cannot be extrapolated to other populations or to other MRAs. Sixth, in post-hoc

exploratory analyses across baseline strata (albuminuria category, baseline SBP, age, sex,

cardiovascular disease history, heart-failure history, and SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 receptor

agonist use), the natural indirect effect was directionally consistent, but the proportion mediated

could not be reliably estimated in smaller subgroups owing to the instability of this metric when

direct and indirect effects are small or oppositely signed; these analyses are therefore not presented

in detail.

# Conclusion

In people with T2D and CKD, the early reduction in albuminuria and systolic blood pressure with

finerenone—two simple, clinically actionable measures—jointly mediate half of the long-term

cardiovascular benefit. The remaining half likely reflects direct tissue effects of MR antagonism

on inflammation and fibrosis. Clinically, early UACR and SBP responses can serve as

complementary indicators of finerenone's cardiovascular benefit in this high-risk population.

# Study design and participants

FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993)11,6 and FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049)12,7 were two

multinational, double-blind, randomized controlled trials of finerenone versus placebo in patients

# Study procedures

The trial designs have been published. Briefly, patients were randomly assigned 1:1 to finerenone or placebo if serum potassium was ≤4.8 mEq/L. In patients with screening eGFR &#x3C;60 mL/min/1.73m², the initial dose was 10 mg, with up-titration to 20 mg if serum potassium was ≤4.8 mEq/L. In those with eGFR ≥60 mL/min/1.73m², finerenone was started at 20 mg. Per protocol, treatment was withheld when serum potassium exceeded 5.5 mEq/L and was restarted at 10 mg if previously on 20 mg when serum potassium fell to ≤5.0 mEq/L. Visits occurred at month 1, month 4, and every 4 months thereafter until trial completion.

# Definition of mediators

Each mediator was defined as the change from baseline to month 4. Month 4 was selected a priori as the landmark for mediation. It is the earliest scheduled post-titration visit at which all four mediators were simultaneously and reliably ascertained, and it precedes the bulk of cardiovascular events, thereby preserving the temporal ordering required for causal mediation. Importantly, month 4 is the landmark used in our prior FIDELITY mediation analysis published in the Annals of Internal Medicine; retaining it keeps the present joint analysis directly comparable to that established prior work. The kidney/glomerular permeability pathway was indexed by change in log UACR. The hemodynamic pathway was indexed by change in seated systolic blood pressure (SBP) and change in body weight. The mineralocorticoid receptor blockade pathway was indexed



---


by change in serum potassium. UACR was measured by central laboratories on first morning void urine samples collected at home on three consecutive days before each visit, at baseline, month 4, and annually thereafter. Serum potassium was assessed at every visit.

# Outcome

The cardiovascular outcome was the composite of cardiovascular death, nonfatal myocardial infarction, nonfatal stroke, or hospitalization for heart failure, time-to-first event. Events were adjudicated by an independent, blinded committee.

# Mediation framework and assumptions

Causal mediation analysis was performed within a counterfactual framework, in accordance with the AGReMA (A Guideline for Reporting Mediation Analyses) statement.¹³ The directed acyclic graph in Figure 1 specifies the relationships among exposure (A: 1 for finerenone, 0 for placebo), mediator (M: change from baseline to month 4 in log UACR, SBP, body weight, or serum potassium), outcome (Y: time to cardiovascular event), and baseline confounders (C). Treatment-outcome and treatment-mediator relationships are causally identified by randomization. The mediator-outcome relationship is observational and rests on the assumption that no unmeasured confounder of this relationship exists conditional on measured baseline covariates;¹⁴ this assumption is not testable in the current implementation of CMAverse for survival outcomes.¹⁵ Temporal ordering was preserved by analyzing mediator change at month 4 and outcomes occurring after month 4; patients with cardiovascular events in the first four months were excluded.

# Statistical analysis

Baseline covariates included in all models were age, sex, race, geographic region, history of cardiovascular disease, eGFR stratum, baseline body weight, baseline systolic BP, baseline log UACR, baseline serum potassium, glycated hemoglobin, baseline use of sodium-glucose.



---

cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitor, baseline use of glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA), and trial of origin (FIDELIO-DKD or FIGARO-DKD). Change from baseline to month 4 in each mediator was estimated by least-squares regression adjusted for the baseline covariates listed above, in Stata 18.5. Mediation analyses were conducted in patients with complete baseline and month-4 mediator and covariate data; missing values were not imputed, and patients with missing baseline or month-4 values for the relevant mediator or covariates were excluded from the corresponding analysis, as enumerated above. Given that fewer than 7% of the FIDELITY population were excluded for any reason and that exclusions were balanced across treatment arms, complete-case analysis is unlikely to have introduced meaningful bias.

Mediation analysis was performed first by individual mediator (log UACR, SBP, body weight, serum potassium) and then by joint consideration of the two significant individual mediators (log UACR and SBP). Outcome models used parametric accelerated failure time (AFT) regression with a Weibull distribution, which provides flexibility for time-to-event data and is recommended for causal mediation analyses. The natural direct effect (NDE; treatment effect independent of the mediator) and natural indirect effect (NIE; treatment effect transmitted through the mediator) were estimated. Results are reported on the relative mean survival scale, where values greater than 1.0 indicate longer survival times in the finerenone group. Standard errors were estimated by the delta method, and 95% confidence intervals were derived assuming asymptotic normality. To express the total effect on an absolute scale, we estimated the difference in restricted mean survival time free of the cardiovascular outcome between treatment groups at 5 years, both model-free (Kaplan-Meier-based) and by covariate-adjusted g-computation from the Weibull model, and the 5-year absolute risk difference with the corresponding number needed to treat.

---

For the joint UACR + SBP model, point estimates were obtained by imputation, and standard errors and 95% confidence intervals were derived by bootstrapping with 200 replicates. Mediation analyses were performed in CMAverse in R.15 Sensitivity analyses for measurement error in baseline mediators were conducted in CMAverse, assuming standard deviations of 2, 4, or 6 mmHg for baseline SBP, and 10%, 20%, or 30% on the log scale for baseline UACR. Subgroup analyses excluded patients receiving SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists at baseline. To visualize mediation, cumulative incidence curves (Aalen-Johansen estimator with non-cardiovascular, non-renal death treated as a competing risk) were generated, stratifying participants by 30% UACR reduction (the threshold recommended in the ADA Standards of Care3) and by 10 mmHg SBP reduction (a clinically meaningful threshold17). Because formal sensitivity analyses for unmeasured mediator-outcome confounding are not currently implemented in CMAverse for survival outcomes, we instead quantified robustness to such confounding by computing E-values for the natural indirect effects of UACR and SBP from the corresponding point estimates and confidence limits on the relative mean survival scale.
