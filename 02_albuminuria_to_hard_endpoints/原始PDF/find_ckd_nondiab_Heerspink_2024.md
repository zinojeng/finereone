## ABSTRACT

**Background.** Finerenone, a non-steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, improved kidney and cardiovascular outcomes in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and type 2 diabetes in two phase 3 outcome trials. The FIND-CKD study investigates the effect of finerenone in adults with CKD without diabetes.

**Methods.** FIND-CKD (NCT05047263 and EU CT 2023-506897-11-00) is a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 3 trial in patients with CKD of non-diabetic aetiology. Adults with a urinary albumin:creatinine ratio (UACR) ≥200–≤3500 mg/g and an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≥25–<90 ml/min/1.73 m² receiving a maximum tolerated dose of a renin–angiotensin system inhibitor were randomized 1:1 to once-daily placebo or finerenone 10 or 20 mg depending on eGFR >60 or <60 ml/min/1.73 m². The primary efficacy outcome is total eGFR slope, defined as the mean annual rate of change in eGFR from baseline to month 32. Secondary efficacy outcomes include a combined cardiorenal composite outcome comprising time to kidney failure, sustained ≥57% decrease in eGFR, hospitalization for heart failure or cardiovascular death, as well as separate kidney and cardiovascular composite outcomes. Adverse events are recorded to assess tolerability and safety.

**Results.** Across 24 countries, 3231 patients were screened and 1584 were randomized to study treatment. The most common causes of CKD were chronic glomerulonephritis (57.0%) and hypertensive/ischaemic nephropathy (29.0%). IgA nephropathy was the most common glomerulonephritis (26.3% of the total population). At baseline, mean eGFR and median UACR were 46.7 ml/min/1.73 m² and 818.9 mg/g, respectively. Diuretics were used by 282 participants (17.8%), statins by 851 (53.7%) and calcium channel blockers by 794 (50.1%). SGLT2 inhibitors were used in 16.9% of patients; these individuals had a similar mean eGFR (45.6 versus 46.8 ml/min/1.73 m²) and a slightly higher median UACR (871.9 versus 808.3 mg/g) compared with those not using SGLT2 inhibitors at baseline.

**Conclusions.** FIND-CKD is the first phase 3 trial of finerenone in patients with CKD of non-diabetic aetiology.

**Keywords:** clinical trial, eGFR slope, finerenone, non-diabetic chronic kidney disease, immunoglobulin A nephropathy

### Key learning points

**What was known:**
- Finerenone reduced the risk of kidney failure and improved cardiovascular outcomes in patients with CKD associated with type 2 diabetes (T2D) in the phase 3 FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD outcome trials.
- Finerenone acts by targeting pathways of pathophysiological sodium retention, inflammation and fibrosis mediated by mineralocorticoid receptor overactivation in the vasculature, heart and kidney.
- Based on its properties, finerenone may also be beneficial in CKD without diabetes; tested in the ongoing FIND-CKD trial.

**This study adds:**
- FIND-CKD is the first phase 3 trial studying the efficacy of finerenone in patients with CKD of non-diabetic aetiology, including hypertension and chronic glomerulonephritis (e.g. IgA nephropathy and FSGS), who are at risk of progression.
- The primary endpoint is the eGFR slope at 32 months.
- Uses a hybrid decentralized clinical trial model integrating telemedicine and remote data collection.

**Potential impact:**
- Will determine the efficacy of finerenone for slowing kidney disease progression in CKD without diabetes, providing a potential expanded role for finerenone beyond T2D.

## INTRODUCTION

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affected ≈840 million people worldwide in 2017. CKD due to diabetes is the most common cause of kidney failure, but a substantial proportion of patients (50–80%) have CKD of other aetiologies. While the pathogenesis underlying different CKD aetiologies differs, the pathophysiological events contributing to progression of most common non-diabetic CKD aetiologies are, to an extent, similar and involve glomerular hyperfiltration, proteinuria, interstitial inflammation and, ultimately, fibrosis.

The current pharmacological strategy to reduce the rate of GFR decline involves inhibition of the renin–angiotensin system (RAS) with ACEIs and ARBs. Existing ACEI/ARB evidence is mainly derived from global trials with ARBs in patients with CKD and diabetes. There are no international clinical trials, involving a globally diverse population, on the efficacy and safety of these therapies in patients with CKD without diabetes. SGLT2 inhibitors on top of RAS inhibition have further slowed progression in CKD with and without diabetes. However, risks of kidney failure and cardiovascular complications remain very high. For example, in EMPA-KIDNEY (NCT03594110), the rate of chronic eGFR decline among patients with CKD and severe albuminuria (>300 mg/g) randomized to empagliflozin was −2.35 ml/min/1.73 m²/year, more than 3-fold higher than the average rate of decline associated with ageing.

Finerenone is a potent, selective, non-steroidal MRA that targets haemodynamic (sodium retention, blood pressure and albuminuria) and non-haemodynamic (inflammatory and fibrotic) pathways. In phase 3 trials [FIDELIO-DKD (NCT02540993) and FIGARO-DKD (NCT02545049)] enrolling patients with CKD and T2D, finerenone reduced the risks of kidney and cardiovascular events. In FIDELIO-DKD, finerenone reduced the risk of a composite kidney endpoint (57% eGFR decline, kidney failure or death from kidney causes) by 24% and eGFR decline by 0.65 ml/min/1.73 m²/year vs placebo. This difference was more pronounced in patients with elevated UACR (≥500 mg/g).

FIND-CKD tests the hypothesis that finerenone is superior to placebo in reducing eGFR decline in patients with CKD without diabetes already receiving an optimized dose of an ACEI or ARB as standard-of-care kidney protective therapy.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

### Study design
FIND-CKD is a multicentre, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group phase 3 study in patients with non-diabetic CKD of different disease aetiologies at risk of progressive eGFR decline. Primary objective: whether adding finerenone to standard of care (optimized RAS blockade) is superior to placebo in slowing progression. Conducted per Declaration of Helsinki and ICH-GCP. Registered ClinicalTrials.gov NCT05047263 and EU CTR 2023-506897-11-00. Estimated completion: February 2026.

### Study patients
Patients ≥18 years with clinical diagnosis of CKD eligible if either:
- eGFR ≥25–<60 ml/min/1.73 m² and UACR ≥200–<500 mg/g, OR
- eGFR ≥25–<90 ml/min/1.73 m² and UACR ≥500–≤3500 mg/g.

For inclusion: on stable and maximum tolerated dose of ACEI or ARB for ≥4 weeks; serum potassium ≤4.8 mmol/l.

Excluded: type 1 diabetes, T2D or HbA1c ≥6.5%; systolic BP ≥160 or diastolic BP ≥100 mmHg; symptomatic HF with reduced ejection fraction with class 1A indication for steroidal MRAs; autosomal dominant or recessive polycystic kidney disease; lupus nephritis or ANCA-associated vasculitis or any kidney disease requiring immunosuppressive therapy within 6 months prior to enrolment.

The number of participants with eGFR ≥25–<60 ml/min/1.73 m² and UACR ≥200–<500 mg/g planned to be capped at ≈10% of the total population.

### Randomization and study treatment
Randomized 1:1 to finerenone (10 or 20 mg) or matching placebo once daily on top of stable standard-of-care therapy. Stratified by screening UACR (≤1000 vs >1000 mg/g) and baseline SGLT2 inhibitor use (yes/no). Starting dose: 10 mg OD if eGFR ≥25–<60 ml/min/1.73 m²; 20 mg OD (target) if eGFR ≥60 ml/min/1.73 m². Up-/down-titrated based on potassium and eGFR.

### Study follow-up
Visits at screening, baseline (day 1), month 1, month 3, then every 3 months up to month 12; thereafter 4-monthly until study end. Minimum planned treatment duration per patient is 32 months; maximum ≈49 months for the first randomized participant. Additional visit 4 weeks after study drug discontinuation to assess off-drug effects.

### Efficacy and safety assessments
eGFR assessed at all study visits (including follow-up) by central laboratory using the 2009 CKD-EPI creatinine equation (age, sex, race). Prespecified sensitivity analyses using 2021 equations; cystatin C collected at all creatinine time points. UACR measured at screening; day 1; months 1, 3, 6, 12 and 24; end of treatment; follow-up visit; using first morning void urine on consecutive days (3 days before screening and day 1; 2 days before other visits). Urinary protein:creatinine ratio at same time points. BP at all visits. Health status via EQ-5D-5L on day 1 then every 12 months. Safety: vital signs, adverse events; 12-lead ECG on day 1 and end of treatment. Hyperkalaemia is an adverse event of special interest; serum potassium monitored, with counts of patients >5.5 mmol/l and >6.0 mmol/l.

### Statistical analysis

**Sample size:** Total 1500 patients (750 per arm) provides >90% power to demonstrate superiority of finerenone with respect to total eGFR slope from day 1 to month 32 at two-sided α=5%. Power calc assumes a 0.7 ml/min/1.73 m²/year total eGFR slope difference at month 32. Assumption based on findings that a yearly eGFR slope reduction of 0.5–1.0 ml/min/1.73 m² is associated with HR ≈0.7 for clinical outcomes in moderate–large trials. Screening ≈3160 patients planned to achieve 1580 randomized (allows ≈5% overall discontinuation, ≈40 per arm).

**Primary outcome:** Total eGFR slope = mean annual rate of change in eGFR from baseline to month 32. Serial change modelled using two-slope linear spline mixed effects model with a fixed change point defining acute and chronic eGFR slope at month 3 (finerenone had acute eGFR effects differing from long-term effects in FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD). Fixed effects: treatment, time (continuous), treatment-by-time interaction, baseline eGFR (continuous); adjusted for stratification factors (baseline SGLT2 inhibitor use; screening UACR ≤1000 vs >1000 mg/g). Random effects for intercept, acute slope (baseline–month 3) and chronic slope (month 3–end). Total slope difference estimated at month 32. Success if P<.05. Primary analysis in full analysis set (all randomized patients). Chronic eGFR slope and change in eGFR from baseline to 4 weeks after end of treatment supplement the primary analysis.

**Secondary outcomes:** Combined cardiorenal endpoint comprising onset of kidney failure (ESKD or sustained decrease in eGFR <15 ml/min/1.73 m²), sustained ≥57% decrease in eGFR from baseline, hospitalization for heart failure, or cardiovascular death. Additional: kidney composite (kidney failure or sustained ≥57% eGFR decrease) and cardiovascular composite (HF hospitalization or CV death). Tested via stratified logrank test at two-sided 5%; stratified Cox model for HRs with 95% CI. Confirmatory hierarchical testing order: annual eGFR slope → composite cardiorenal → composite kidney → composite cardiovascular.

**Exploratory endpoints:** Change from baseline in UACR; change in EQ-5D-5L health status scores.

**Safety:** Safety analysis set = all randomized patients taking ≥1 dose. Hyperkalaemia adverse event of special interest; assessments include change in serum potassium, incidences >5.5 and >6.0 mmol/l, hospitalization for hyperkalaemia and permanent discontinuation due to hyperkalaemia.

**Study oversight:** Steering committee of external nephrology/cardiology experts; independent data monitoring committee. Funded by Bayer AG (sponsor); sponsor responsible for data collection and analysis in collaboration with authors.

## RESULTS

First patient enrolled September 2021; enrolment completed May 2023. In total, 3231 patients screened and 1584 randomized at 283 sites across 24 countries. Most screen failures did not meet eGFR and/or UACR entry criteria. Of the 1584, 84 were randomized to the decentralized clinical trial (DCT) model (available in eight countries across North and Latin America, Asia, Europe and Australia).

By region: Asia 844 (53.3%), Europe and Oceania 535 (33.8%), North America 132 (8.3%), Latin America 73 (4.6%). Mean age 54.7 years (SD 14.3); 1049 (66.2%) male; most patients Asian [n=866 (54.7%)]. Baseline mean eGFR 46.7 ml/min/1.73 m² (SD 16.1); mean serum potassium 4.5 mmol/l (SD 0.4); median UACR 818.9 mg/g (IQR 577.4–1244.0). eGFR <45 ml/min/1.73 m² in 844 (53.3%); eGFR ≥60 in 317 (20.0%); UACR >1000 mg/g in 592 (37.4%).

ACEIs or ARBs at baseline: 1581 (99.8%). Statins 851 (53.7%) and calcium channel blockers 794 (50.1%) most frequent after RAS inhibitors. Diuretics 282 (17.8%). SGLT2 inhibitors 267 (16.9%). Patients on SGLT2 inhibitors had slightly higher median UACR [871.9 mg/g (IQR 619.2–1409.1) vs 808.3 mg/g (IQR 566.6–1218.5)]; otherwise largely comparable. ARBs prescribed more frequently in Asia; ACEIs, diuretics, beta blockers and SGLT2 inhibitors less frequently in Asia.

CKD attributed to hypertensive/ischaemic nephropathy 460 (29.0%), IgA nephropathy (IgAN) 417 (26.3%) and FSGS 215 (13.6%). Hypertensive/ischaemic nephropathy dominant in Europe/Oceania, Latin America and North America (36–41%); IgAN more predominant in Asia (35%). CKD aetiology confirmed by kidney biopsy in 787 (49.7%). At baseline hypertension 1396 (88.1%), atherosclerotic CVD 189 (11.9%), heart failure 35 (2.2%). Atherosclerotic CVD less prevalent in Asia.

## DISCUSSION

The efficacy and safety of the non-steroidal MRA finerenone was demonstrated in FIDELIO-DKD and FIGARO-DKD; guidelines recommend finerenone to prevent kidney disease progression and CV events in CKD patients with T2D. Both trials excluded CKD without diabetes. FIND-CKD addresses this gap.

Mineralocorticoid receptor activation contributes to sodium/water retention, inflammation and fibrosis. Small studies showed steroidal MRAs (spironolactone, eplerenone) reduced albuminuria and stabilized eGFR decline over 1 year but increase potassium and hyperkalaemia risk, particularly in more severe CKD; neither is approved for CKD treatment. Finerenone's pharmacological properties differ. In the FIDELITY pooled analysis, permanent drug discontinuation due to hyperkalaemia was low (1.7%). In an indirect comparison of FIDELITY and the AMBER trial (NCT03071263), finerenone had lower hyperkalaemia/discontinuation risk than spironolactone; a head-to-head comparison in ≈400 patients with HF and CKD supports lower hyperkalaemia risk with finerenone. Lower hyperkalaemia risk may be attributed to physicochemical, pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic properties including short half-life (2–3 hours) and distinct receptor binding mode.

**Primary endpoint rationale:** eGFR slope over 32 months. A meta-analysis of 66 RCTs showed a strong association between treatment effects on GFR slope and on an established kidney outcome of 57% GFR decline or kidney failure, consistent across CKD subgroups and severity. A treatment effect of 0.75 ml/min/1.73 m²/year in a trial of 1600 participants translates into a reduction in incidence of 57% GFR decline or kidney failure with very high confidence [median HR 0.74 (95% Bayesian prediction intervals 0.60, 0.89)]. Association strongest for total slope vs chronic slope, supporting total slope as primary endpoint. Composite of ≥57% GFR decline or kidney failure used as key secondary (trial not powered for it). A larger GFR decline threshold used in FIND-CKD (≥57% vs ≥40% in FIDELIO-DKD) because finerenone may cause acute haemodynamically-mediated GFR reduction. Minimum treatment duration of 32 months chosen from post hoc FIDELIO-DKD total slope difference at 32 months.

**Population comparison:** Broad population with different CKD aetiologies. Mean age, sex distribution and CVD history (excluding HF) similar to non-diabetic participants in DAPA-CKD and EMPA-KIDNEY. Median UACR higher in FIND-CKD (819 mg/g) than EMPA-KIDNEY (461 mg/g) but slightly lower than DAPA-CKD (861 mg/g). Mean eGFR highest in FIND-CKD (46.7 vs 38.7 EMPA-KIDNEY, 41.7 DAPA-CKD). Proportion with glomerulonephritis higher in FIND-CKD (reflecting larger Asian population); IgAN highest in FIND-CKD (26.3%); 417 participants with IgAN, similar to recently completed phase 3 IgAN trials.

**Concomitant therapy:** Nearly all participants using ACEI or ARB. 17% using SGLT2 inhibitor for >4 weeks prior to enrolment, reflecting the ongoing approval/reimbursement process and dapagliflozin being the only SGLT2i with proven kidney benefits during recruitment. SGLT2i users had similar eGFR/UACR, indicating persistent high risk. Prior finerenone phase 3 post hoc analyses suggested similar efficacy in patients using/not using SGLT2 inhibitors. The ongoing CONFIDENCE trial (NCT05254002) prospectively assesses albuminuria-lowering effects of empagliflozin, finerenone and combination in 807 adults with T2D and CKD. Diuretics used in 18% — lower than other recent CKD trials, likely reflecting high proportion of Asian patients (where diuretics less frequently used), exclusion of symptomatic HFrEF with class 1A MRA indication, and differences in mean eGFR.

**Decentralized model:** A novel aspect — participants offered opportunity to participate in a DCT model where study procedures are performed remotely at home, leveraging digital technologies and wearable devices.

**Conclusion:** FIND-CKD is designed to evaluate the efficacy, safety and tolerability of finerenone vs placebo in patients with CKD without diabetes. A beneficial effect on slowing GFR decline would expand finerenone's role for kidney protection beyond T2D.
