# An acute fall in estimated glomerular filtration rate during treatment with losartan predicts a slower decrease in long-term renal function

**Authors:** Frank A. Holtkamp, Dick de Zeeuw, Merlin C. Thomas, Mark E. Cooper, Pieter A. de Graeff, Hans J.L. Hillege, Hans-Henrik Parving, Barry M. Brenner, Shahnaz Shahinfar, Hiddo J. Lambers Heerspink

**Journal:** Kidney International (2011) 80, 282–287; doi:10.1038/ki.2011.79; published online 30 March 2011

## Abstract

Intervention in the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone-system (RAAS) is associated with slowing the progressive loss of renal function. During initiation of therapy, however, there may be an acute fall in glomerular filtration rate (GFR). The authors tested whether this initial fall in GFR reflects a renal hemodynamic effect and whether this might result in a slower decline in long-term renal function. They performed a **post hoc analysis of the RENAAL trial** (Reduction of Endpoints in Non-Insulin-Dependent Diabetes Mellitus with the Angiotensin II Antagonist Losartan).

Key findings:
- Patients assigned to losartan had a significantly greater acute fall in estimated GFR (eGFR) during the first 3 months compared to placebo, but a significantly slower long-term mean decline of eGFR thereafter.
- A large interindividual difference was noticed in the acute eGFR change.
- When patients were divided into tertiles of initial fall in eGFR, the long-term eGFR slope calculated from baseline was significantly higher (steeper decline) in patients with an initial fall compared to those with an initial rise.
- When eGFR decline was calculated from 3 months to the final visit (excluding the initial effect), patients with a large initial fall in eGFR had a significantly **lower** long-term eGFR slope (more stable) compared to those with a moderate fall or rise.
- This relationship was independent of other risk markers or change in risk markers such as blood pressure and albuminuria.

**Conclusion:** The greater the acute fall in eGFR during losartan treatment, the slower the rate of long-term eGFR decline. Interpretation of trial results relying on slope-based GFR outcomes should separate the initial drug-induced GFR change from the subsequent long-term effect on GFR.

**Keywords:** angiotensin receptor blocker; chronic kidney disease; diabetic nephropathy; glomerular filtration rate; renal insufficiency; serum creatinine

## Introduction (key points)

- Diabetic nephropathy is the most common cause of ESRD. RAAS-blocking agents can prevent onset/progression of nephropathy, attenuate deterioration of kidney function, and improve survival in patients with diabetes (refs 1–4).
- Blood pressure-lowering medication (ACE inhibitors or ARBs) is associated with an initial fall in GFR or increase in serum creatinine, most likely resulting from a reduction in intraglomerular pressure.
- A systematic review showed that a rise in serum creatinine of up to 30% of baseline is no reason for concern, provided serum electrolytes (principally potassium) remain within normal limits (ref 8).
- A small-scale study (Apperloo et al., ref 9) indicated that the magnitude of initial fall in GFR is inversely related to the long-term slope of GFR decline and is reversible after termination of RAAS blockade — suggesting the initial fall is hemodynamic rather than structural and can serve as an early marker of subsequent slower long-term decline. Large-scale placebo-controlled evidence was lacking.
- The RENAAL trial (ARB losartan vs placebo) provided a baseline period without RAAS inhibitor treatment plus serum creatinine values at baseline and every 3 months, allowing study of the associations between treatment-induced short-term eGFR responses and long-term renal function decline.

## Results

### Course of eGFR in losartan- and placebo-treated individuals

- **Acute fall in eGFR at 3 months** was greater in losartan vs placebo: **−2.3 (95% CI −2.7 to −1.8) vs −1.6 (−2.0 to −1.1) ml/min per 1.73 m² , P = 0.031**.
- **Long-term eGFR slope** (calculated from month 3) was significantly smaller (less steep decline) in the losartan group vs placebo: **−4.2 (95% CI −3.9 to −4.6) vs −5.0 (−4.7 to −5.4) ml/min per 1.73 m² per year; P < 0.001**.
- (Figure 1: mean eGFR levels through 39 months; long-term slopes calculated from month 3.)

### Predictors of an acute fall in eGFR

- The initial change in eGFR in the losartan group showed wide variability: mean −2.3 ml/min per 1.73 m² (min −18.3; max +14.7).
- **Univariate:** UACR (urinary albumin/creatinine ratio) and month 3 change in UACR showed the strongest associations with degree of initial eGFR fall.
- **Multivariable (significant covariates):** male gender, higher baseline eGFR, UACR, and diastolic blood pressure, lower hemoglobin, and larger month 3 decline in UACR were significantly associated with a larger acute fall in eGFR.

### Effect of acute fall in eGFR on long-term renal function decline

Patients assigned to losartan were stratified in tertiles according to initial change in eGFR. Patients with a larger acute fall in eGFR had significantly higher UACR, eGFR, and systolic BP at baseline, and greater reduction in UACR and systolic BP after 3 months.

**Slope calculated FROM BASELINE (includes initial effect):**
- Mean eGFR at median time (month 33): lower in initial-decline vs initial-rise patients: **28.7 (95% CI 26.3–31.0) vs 33.0 (30.9–35.2) ml/min per 1.73 m²; P = 0.007** (Figure 2).
- eGFR slope from baseline: steeper decline in initial-fall vs initial-rise: **−5.2 (95% CI −5.8 to −4.7) vs −3.6 (−4.1 to −3.0) ml/min per 1.73 m² per year; P < 0.001**.

**Slope calculated FROM MONTH 3 to final visit (excludes initial effect):**
- Unadjusted, by tertile (large fall / moderate fall / rise): **−3.8 (95% CI −4.4 to −3.2) vs −4.1 (−4.7 to −3.6) vs −4.8 (−5.4 to −4.3) ml/min per 1.73 m²; P = 0.0094 for tertile 1 vs 3.**
- Multivariable analysis: an initial steeper fall in eGFR remained significantly associated with a more stable long-term eGFR course (Figure 3: P = 0.0089 unadjusted; P = 0.0497 adjusted).
- This correlation was **exclusively observed in losartan-treated patients**; no correlation in placebo-treated subjects.
- Sensitivity analysis using serum creatinine instead of eGFR gave similar results.

### Tertile characteristics (Table 2 — baseline characteristics by tertiles of initial change in eGFR)

Tertile 1 (ΔeGFR −8.6 ± 4.0, N=239): Age 59.1, 64.4% male, eGFR 41.1, SBP 153.6, DBP 82.7, SCr 1.8, UACR median 1525 (587–3417), Hb 12.4, chol 232.3, HbA1c 8.4%.
Tertile 2 (ΔeGFR −2.4 ± 1.3, N=240): Age 60.3, 58.3% male, eGFR 38.0, SBP 152.6, UACR median 1359 (627–2900).
Tertile 3 (ΔeGFR +4.2 ± 4.9, N=240): Age 60.6, 60.8% male, eGFR 39.7, SBP 149.2, UACR median 893 (480–1692).

### Hard outcomes (Table 3 — renal events: doubling of serum creatinine or ESRD)

- When the overall population was divided into tertiles, the rate of renal events was higher in those with an initial fall compared with those with an initial rise in eGFR.
- However, in patients with an initial fall in eGFR, the rate of renal events was **profoundly attenuated in losartan-treated patients compared with placebo**.
- In those with an initial rise in eGFR, renal event rate was almost similar between both treatment groups.

---

## Discussion (key points)

- Initiation of antihypertensive therapy with an ARB induces an acute fall in eGFR that is inversely correlated with renal function decline during long-term follow-up: the greater the acute eGFR fall, the slower the rate of long-term eGFR decline.
- This relationship is independent of other risk markers or changes in risk markers (blood pressure, albuminuria).
- The initial fall in GFR reflects a renal hemodynamic effect (reduction in intraglomerular pressure) rather than structural damage, consistent with the intraglomerular hypertension hypothesis (Brenner/Anderson).
- Clinical implication: a rise in serum creatinine / acute eGFR fall at treatment initiation should not automatically raise safety concerns leading to discontinuation of RAAS inhibitors.
- Methodological implication: interpretation of trial results relying on slope-based GFR outcomes should separate the initial drug-induced GFR change from the subsequent long-term effect on GFR.
