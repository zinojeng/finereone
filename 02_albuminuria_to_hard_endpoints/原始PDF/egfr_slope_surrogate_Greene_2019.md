# Performance of GFR Slope as a Surrogate End Point for Kidney Disease Progression in Clinical Trials: A Statistical Simulation

**Authors:** Tom Greene, Jian Ying, Edward F. Vonesh, Hocine Tighiouart, Andrew S. Levey, Josef Coresh, Jennifer S. Herrick, Enyu Imai, Tazeen H. Jafar, Bart D. Maes, Ronald D. Perrone, Lucia del Vecchio, Jack F.M. Wetzels, Hiddo J.L. Heerspink, Lesley A. Inker

**Journal:** JASN 30: 1756–1769, 2019. doi: https://doi.org/10.1681/ASN.2019010009 · PMID 31292198

**Correspondence:** Dr. Lesley A. Inker, Division of Nephrology, Tufts Medical Center, Boston, MA. Email: LInker@tuftsmedicalcenter.org

## ABSTRACT

**Background.** Randomized trials of CKD treatments traditionally use clinical events late in CKD progression as end points. This requires costly studies with large sample sizes and long follow-up. Surrogate end points like GFR slope may speed up the evaluation of new therapies by enabling smaller studies with shorter follow-up.

**Methods.** We used statistical simulations to identify trial situations where GFR slope provides increased statistical power compared with the clinical end point of doubling of serum creatinine or kidney failure. We simulated GFR trajectories based on data from 47 randomized treatment comparisons. We evaluated the sample size required for adequate statistical power based on GFR slopes calculated from baseline and from 3 months follow-up.

**Results.** In most scenarios where the treatment has no acute effect, analyses of GFR slope provided similar or improved statistical power compared with the clinical end point, often allowing investigators to shorten follow-up by at least half while simultaneously reducing sample size. When patients' GFRs are higher, the power advantages of GFR slope increase. However, acute treatment effects within several months of randomization can increase the risk of false conclusions about therapies based on GFR slope. Care is needed in study design and analysis to avoid such false conclusions.

**Conclusions.** Use of GFR slope can substantially increase statistical power compared with the clinical end point, particularly when baseline GFR is high and there is no acute effect. The optimum GFR-based end point depends on multiple factors including the rate of GFR decline, type of treatment effect and study design.

---

## Significance Statement

Randomized controlled trials of CKD traditionally use clinical events that happen late in the course of CKD progression as study end points. Doing this requires large sample sizes and long follow-up periods that can make the studies costly. The authors use statistical simulations to investigate circumstances in which using the change in GFR over time or GFR slope as a study end point reduces the required sample size or trial duration compared with clinical end points. They found that GFR slope performs better than clinical end points when patients' initial GFRs are high and the treatment has no acute effect on GFR. The results along with other recent studies suggest GFR is a valid surrogate end point for CKD clinical trials that may allow for more efficient trials and help speed the development of new CKD therapies.

## INTRODUCTION / BACKGROUND

CKD is common and harmful, causing kidney failure in its late stages, but with few therapies. Pivotal RCTs traditionally use ESKD, eGFR <15 ml/min per 1.73 m², or doubling of serum creatinine as clinical end points. However, these are late events in CKD progression, leading to costly requirements for large sample sizes and long follow-up.

In March 2018, the National Kidney Foundation (NKF), in collaboration with the FDA and the European Medicines Agency (EMA), sponsored a scientific workshop "Change in Albuminuria and GFR as End Points for Clinical Trials in Early Stages of CKD" to evaluate surrogate end points for trials of CKD progression. This manuscript is part of a series reporting analyses undertaken with the workshop. Other manuscripts in the series demonstrate consistent epidemiologic association between GFR slope and subsequent clinical end points, as well as strong agreement between treatment effects on the clinical end point and on GFR slope in previous CKD RCTs. This manuscript defines the circumstances in which application of GFR slope can improve statistical power to allow smaller sample size or shorter duration while controlling the risk of false conclusions.

Kidney failure, defined by uremic symptoms signifying a need for kidney replacement therapy, occurs when GFR reaches a level of about 5–15 ml/min per 1.73 m², a narrow range relative to normal GFR.

## METHODS

### Simulation Strategy
Simulations were based on the same models for GFR trajectories used in a previous simulation study evaluating end points based on 30% or 40% GFR decline, with updates to input parameters reflecting distributions in an expanded database with 47 randomized treatment comparisons.

### GFR Trajectories in the Control Group
Simulated on the basis of a growth curve model in which each subject's GFR measurements varied randomly about subject-specific linear trajectories defined by random slopes and intercepts.

### Long-Term Treatment Effects on GFR Slope
Three types of long-term treatment effects were simulated:
1. **Uniform effect** — the same treatment effect for all patients irrespective of progression rate.
2. **Proportional effect** — the treatment effect is proportional to the rate of GFR decline among patients with negative slopes but has no effect among patients whose slopes would have been >0 without the treatment.
3. **Intermediate model** — halfway between the uniform and proportional effect models.

### Short-Term (Acute) Treatment Effects on GFR
The initial mean slopes during the first 3 months after randomization were allowed to differ from long-term mean slopes after 3 months. Simulations considered both negative acute effects (early GFR decline) and positive acute effects (early GFR increase). Acute effect attenuation was modeled as linearly related to GFR level, with the acute effect declining to zero when GFR reaches 15 ml/min per 1.73 m². Normalized to a GFR of 42.5 ml/min per 1.73 m²:

Normalized acute effect = [(42.5 − 15)/(GFR value − 15)] × actual acute effect.

Analyses of prior CKD trials indicate both negative and positive acute effects are common, with an overall mean acute effect of 0.15 ml/min per 1.73 m² and an SD of 1.01 ml/min per 1.73 m² after normalization. Normalized acute effects were estimated to fall between −1.25 and 1.25 ml/min per 1.73 m² for 74% of trials, and between −2.50 and 2.50 ml/min per 1.73 m² for 98% of trials.

### Relationships of GFR with Kidney Failure and Death
The mortality hazard rate was assumed to be linearly related to predicted GFR, with higher death rates at lower GFR. ESKD was assumed to occur when the GFR trajectory first declined below a patient-specific threshold uniformly distributed between 6 and 15 ml/min per 1.73 m².

### Outcomes
Two surrogate end points based on GFR slope:
1. **Chronic GFR slope** — mean rate of change in GFR after 3 months.
2. **Total GFR slope** — mean rate of change in GFR from baseline to a designated time late in follow-up (over 2, 3, or 4 years for RCTs with follow-up periods of 2, 2.5–4, and 4–6 years respectively), incorporating both acute and chronic slopes.

Two alternative time-to-event end points:
1. Time to a confirmed **30% GFR decline** or kidney failure.
2. Time to a confirmed **40% GFR decline** or kidney failure.

The clinical end point was approximated as time to a confirmed **57% GFR decline** or kidney failure (a 57% GFR decline closely approximates doubling of serum creatinine).

### Analyses of Simulated Data
For each scenario, 800 independent data sets were simulated, each with 500 subjects (250 treatment / 250 control). GFR slope was analyzed using mixed effects models; time-to-event outcomes using Cox proportional hazards models. A confirmatory GFR measurement 1 month after each initial qualifying measurement was generated.

### Key Input Parameters (Table 1 highlights)
- **Mean long-term slope:** −1.5, −3.25, or −5 ml/min per 1.73 m²/yr
- **SD of long-term slope:** 3.0, 3.5, 4.0, or 4.5 ml/min per year/1.73 m²
- **Mean acute effect:** −2.5, −1.25, 0, +1.25, or +2.5 ml/min per 1.73 m² at baseline GFR 42.5
- **Size of long-term treatment effect:** 0% or 25% reduction in slope
- **Mean baseline GFR:** 27.5, 42.5, or 67.5 ml/min per 1.73 m²
- **Accrual/follow-up:** Short (1 yr accrual + 1.5 yr follow-up), Medium (1.5 yr accrual + 2.5 yr further), Long (2 yr accrual + 4 yr further)
- **Measurement frequency:** Months 3, 6, and every 6 months thereafter
- **Permanent loss to follow-up:** 2%/yr; Intermittent missing GFRs: 5%

## RESULTS

### No Acute Effect and Uniform Long-Term Effect
Figure 1 displays sample sizes required for 90% power with two-sided α=0.05 for the slope and time-to-event end points when the treatment reduces mean chronic slope by 25%, effect is uniform, and there is no acute effect. In this idealized situation, GFR slope end points consistently support much smaller sample sizes than the clinical end point.

### No Acute Effect, Intermediate or Proportional Long-Term Effect
The superior power of slope end points versus the clinical end point for uniform treatment effects is reduced when the long-term effect is proportional (Figure 2). Analysis of previous CKD trials (Supplemental Appendix 3) suggests long-term treatment effects are usually intermediate between uniform and proportional. When the long-term effect is intermediate, relative efficiencies of the slope end points remain consistently greater than one compared with the clinical end point, with highest relative efficiencies when baseline GFR is high or follow-up is short.

Using the total GFR slope usually allows both sample size and trial duration to be reduced while maintaining the same power as any of the time-to-event end points. When there is no acute effect and mean progression is intermediate or fast, substituting the total slope for the clinical end point allows follow-up to be reduced from 4–6 years to 2 years while simultaneously improving efficiency by 17%–63% (sample size savings of 14%–39%), including a 29% reduction for the intermediate case with baseline GFR 42.5 ml/min and moderate GFR decline.

### Nonzero Acute Effects
Figure 4: relative efficiency of candidate surrogates versus clinical end point when baseline GFR is intermediate and normalized acute effects between −2.5 and +2.5 ml/min per 1.73 m² attenuate linearly to zero as GFR declines to 15. The influence of the acute effect on the total slope is greatest when mean progression is slow and/or follow-up is short. For acute effects between −1.25 and +1.25 ml/min per 1.73 m², relative efficiency of the total slope is consistently higher than that of time-to-event end points when mean GFR decline is fast, but is lower when the acute effect is negative and mean GFR decline is slow. The relative efficiency of the chronic slope exceeds that of the other end points when the acute effect is negative, and is lower than the other end points when the acute effect is positive.

### Risk of Bias and Type 1 Error (Figure 5)
When there is no long-term treatment effect and the acute effect fully attenuates before ESKD (medium 2.5–4 yr follow-up), any nonzero estimated treatment effect represents a bias. When the acute effect attenuates as GFR declines, negative acute effects lead to substantial biases *against* the treatment for the total slope, and somewhat smaller biases *in favor* of the treatment for the chronic slope (reversed pattern for positive acute effects). The bias in the total slope from acute effects is greater when follow-up is shorter or GFR decline is slower; the bias in the chronic slope is greater when average GFR decline is faster but unrelated to length of follow-up.

## DISCUSSION

The simulations provide the first systematic comparison of the performance of slope and time-to-event end points in CKD RCTs, extending the companion manuscripts that evaluate validity of total and chronic slope as surrogate end points.

**Two sets of findings emphasized:**
1. The relative performance of slope end points compared with the clinical end point depends on the type of long-term treatment effect, with slope end points often providing superior performance when treatment effects are uniform, and the clinical end point performing better when effects are proportional. Empirical analyses of previous CKD RCTs suggest long-term treatment effects tend to be intermediate rather than fully uniform or fully proportional. When the long-term effect is intermediate and there is no acute effect, slope end points can usually achieve the same power as the clinical end point or confirmed 30%/40% GFR declines while substantially reducing both sample size and follow-up. Efficiency gains are greatest for trials with higher baseline GFR. For example, substituting the total slope for the clinical end point can reduce follow-up from 4–6 years to 2 years while reducing sample size by 14%–39% across the no-acute-effect scenarios with moderate to fast GFR progression. The majority of interventions under consideration for new RCTs are not expected to produce acute effects (e.g., chemokine receptor antagonists, DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, bicarbonate supplementation, metformin, anti-IgG4 monoclonal antibodies, NADPH oxidase 2 inhibitors).

2. Slope-based analyses are more complicated when the treatment has an acute effect. A negative acute effect can reduce or eliminate the power advantages of the total slope. In contrast, the chronic slope usually provides greater power than either the clinical end point or alternative time-to-event end points when acute effects are negative. However, this advantage comes with a risk of false conclusions (Figure 5) because the chronic slope evaluates GFR change from a post-randomization time point after treatment has already modified GFR. Attenuation of a negative acute effect as GFR declines can increase risk of falsely concluding treatment benefit.

**Three suggested strategies when a negative acute effect is expected:**
1. If the acute effect is relatively small (≤1.25 ml/min per 1.73 m²), enrichment of the study cohort with fast progressors combined with sufficiently long follow-up often provides reasonable power for the total slope, exceeding that of the clinical end point.
2. Analyzing the treatment effect on total GFR change from baseline to poststudy GFR measurements obtained after discontinuing the treatment should retain most of the power advantages of the chronic slope while minimizing risk of falsely concluding benefit.
3. Artificial censoring of GFRs after treatment discontinuation while using weighting or imputation to mitigate selection bias can limit bias due to reversal of acute effects after discontinuation.

When a positive acute effect may be spurious, analyses of time-to-event outcomes or of the total slope which uses a prerandomization baseline GFR may inflate risk of falsely concluding treatment benefit; in this situation the chronic slope represents the more conservative end point.

**Limitations:** (1) GFR trajectories are highly multifactorial and conclusions extend only to the specific input parameters considered; (2) simulations limited to a simplified GFR model with a short acute phase followed by a long linear chronic phase; (3) focus primarily on scenarios where the acute effect depends on GFR level and attenuates to zero as GFR declines to 15 ml/min per 1.73 m² per year.

**Conclusion.** For treatments without acute effects, slope-based end points can substantially reduce required sample size and duration of follow-up compared with the clinical end point and alternative time-to-event end points based on 30% or 40% GFR decline, particularly in study populations with elevated baseline GFR. Application of slope-based end points remains useful under certain conditions in the presence of acute effects but requires considerable care. The choice of the optimum end point and study design needs to be evaluated based on detailed power calculations under the conditions of each randomized trial.
