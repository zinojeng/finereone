# Change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for progression of kidney disease: a meta-analysis of treatment effects in randomised clinical trials

**Heerspink HJL, Greene T, Tighiouart H, Gansevoort RT, Coresh J, Simon AL, Chan TM, Hou FF, Lewis JB, Locatelli F, Praga M, Schena FP, Levey AS, Inker LA; for the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration**
*Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol* 2019;7(2):128-139. DOI: 10.1016/S2213-8587(18)30314-0. PMID: 30635226. Funding: US National Kidney Foundation.

---

## Summary

**Background** — Change in albuminuria has strong biological plausibility as a surrogate endpoint for progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD), but empirical evidence supporting its validity is lacking. The authors aimed to determine the association between treatment effects on early changes in albuminuria and treatment effects on clinical (hard) endpoints, to inform use of albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint in future randomised controlled trials.

**Methods** — Meta-analysis. Searched PubMed for English-language publications (Jan 1, 1946 to Dec 15, 2016) using terms "chronic kidney disease," "chronic renal insufficiency," "albuminuria," "proteinuria," "randomized controlled trial." Key inclusion: quantifiable measurements of albuminuria or proteinuria at baseline and within 12 months of follow-up, plus information on incidence of end-stage kidney disease. Requested individual patient data (IPD) from authors of eligible studies. Estimated treatment effects on 6-month change in albuminuria and on the composite clinical endpoint of treated end-stage kidney disease, estimated GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 m², or doubling of serum creatinine. Used a **Bayesian mixed-effects meta-regression** to relate treatment effects on albuminuria to treatment effects on the clinical endpoint across studies, and built a prediction model.

**Findings** — 41 eligible treatment comparisons (referred to as "studies") from randomised trials with sufficient patient-level data on **29,979 participants (21,206 [71%] with diabetes)**. Over median follow-up of **3.4 years (IQR 2.3–4.2)**, **3,935 (13%)** participants reached the composite clinical endpoint.
- Across all studies, meta-regression **slope of 0.89 (95% Bayesian credible interval [BCI] 0.13–1.70)** — each 30% decrease in geometric mean albuminuria by treatment was associated on average with **27% lower hazard for the clinical endpoint (95% BCI 5–45%)**.
- **Median R² 0.47 (95% BCI 0.02–0.96)**.
- Association strengthened after restricting analyses to patients with baseline albuminuria of more than 30 mg/g (ie, 3.4 mg/mmol): **R² 0.72 (95% BCI 0.05–0.99)**.
- Model prediction: treatments that decrease geometric mean albuminuria to 0.7 (ie, 30% decrease) relative to control provide an average **HR of 0.68** for the clinical endpoint, and 95% of sufficiently large studies would have HRs between **0.47 and 0.95**.

**Interpretation** — Results support a role for change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for progression of CKD, particularly in patients with high baseline albuminuria; for patients with low baseline levels of albuminuria the association is less certain.

## Key methods detail

- **Trial-level association strength categories:** R² <0.49 = weak; R² 0.49–0.72 = moderate; R² >0.72 = strong.
- **Positive predictive value (PPV)** used to describe uncertainty in predicting the clinical endpoint. Threshold treatment effect on ACR assuring high probability of benefit defined as PPV ≥97.5%.
- Sample-size definitions: **large trial** = SE on ACR effect within 0.05 (n≈1090, minimum detectable geometric mean ratio 0.849); **modest trial** = SE 0.12 (n≈190, minimum detectable geometric mean ratio 0.675).
- ACR = urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio. Treatment effect on albuminuria expressed as geometric mean ratio of ACR; percentage decrease = (1 − geometric mean ratio) × 100.
- Analyses in SAS 9.4 and R 3.16.1.

## Study selection (Figure 1)

- 6752 studies identified (6744 MEDLINE, 8 other sources) for May 16, 2007–Dec 15, 2016, plus 35 studies from previous National Kidney Foundation/FDA conference papers, REASSURE and IgA nephropathy meta-analysis → 6787 analysed for eligibility.
- After exclusions and IPD requests: 52 studies supplied data → 49 in pooled database → **41 treatment comparisons in the primary analysis** (43 and 44 in sensitivity analyses 1 and 2).

## Population characteristics (Table 1)

- Overall: 41 studies, 29,979 participants; mean age 58.2 (SD 12.6); 66.8% male; 87.2% non-black; 21,206 (70.7%) with diabetes; mean baseline eGFR 58.2 mL/min per 1.73 m² (SD 25.0); median baseline ACR 272 mg/g (IQR 30–1134); 3,935 (13.1%) reached clinical endpoint.
- Diabetes subset: 10 studies, 21,102 participants. Glomerular disease: 9 studies, 1,325. Other CKD: 22 studies, 7,552.

## Results (patient-level and trial-level)

- Over median 6 months (IQR 6–6.4): geometric mean ratio of ACR was 16% (95% CI 8–24) in control groups and 34% (27–40) in treatment groups → **22% decrease in ACR due to treatment (geometric mean ratio 0.78, 95% CI 0.74–0.82)**.
- Larger effects at baseline ACR >30 mg/g (geometric mean ratio 0.76, 95% CI 0.72–0.81) vs ACR <30 mg/g (0.92, 0.89–0.96).
- Across all interventions, active treatment led to decreased clinical endpoint risk: **HR 0.73 (95% CI 0.67–0.81)**.
- Trial-level (overall): slope 0.89 (95% BCI 0.13–1.70); intercept −0.07 (95% BCI −0.29 to 0.14); median R² 0.47 (95% BCI 0.02–0.96); root mean square error 0.14 (95% BCI 0.03 to 0.27). Bayesian probabilities: 53% weak, 28% moderate, 19% strong association.
- Restricted to baseline ACR >30 mg/g: R² 0.72 (95% BCI 0.05–0.99); slope 0.91 (0.19–1.67); intercept −0.04. Association probabilities: 27% weak, 24% moderate, 49% strong.
- Among participants with baseline ACR <30 mg/g, event rate low (180 [4.6%] of 3935) so estimates unreliable.
- **EMPA-REG OUTCOME identified as an outlier** (weaker association between treatment effects on surrogate and clinical endpoint; baseline albuminuria substantially lower than most studies). Excluding it increased association strength from R² 0.47 to R² 0.72 (95% BCI 0.08–0.99), though not in the baseline ACR >30 mg/g subgroup.
- **Hou et al. study** identified with unusually small variability in serum creatinine measurements; excluding it gave consistent results (R² 0.42, 95% BCI 0.01–0.95 after exclusion).

## Predicted treatment effects by trial size (Table 3)

- All participants — 30% ACR decrease (geometric mean ratio 0.7): HR 0.68 (infinite sample size). Threshold ACR decrease to ensure PPV ≥97.5%: 25% (infinite), 26% (large trial), 31% (modest trial).
- Participants with baseline ACR ≥30 mg/g: thresholds 20% (infinite sample), 21% (large trial), 27% (modest trial).

## Discussion highlights

- In studies of interventions where albuminuria has biological plausibility as a surrogate, treatment effect on albuminuria had moderately strong association with treatment effects on clinical endpoints; stronger for baseline ACR >30 mg/g.
- Companion meta-analysis of observational studies (~700,000 individuals): after adjustment for measurement error, a 30% decrease in albuminuria over 2 years was associated with a 22% reduction in end-stage kidney disease risk (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.66–0.92) — similarity gives reassurance.
- Prior meta-analyses reported conflicting results; one large meta-analysis (9,088 participants, 37 trials) showed no clear association but had insufficient variation/power. Authors attribute differing conclusions to differences in included trials and analytical methodology (their Bayesian meta-regression accounts for correlation among sampling errors).
- **Interpretation:** inferring clinical benefit of albuminuria reduction does NOT seem possible for interventions producing only moderate decreases in albuminuria (ie, <20–30%). Results are particularly useful for diabetic kidney disease with high baseline albuminuria.
- **Limitations:** modest number of studies with available data; some trialists declined participation; imprecision in subgroup trial-level associations; lower proportion of women; inconsistent race/ethnicity categorisation; only clinical endpoints during trial follow-up considered; wide prediction intervals; most included studies tested RAAS blockers.
