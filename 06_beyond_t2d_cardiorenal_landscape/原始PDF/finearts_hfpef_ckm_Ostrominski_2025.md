ABSTRACT
BACKGROUND  Obesity is a core pathophysiologic contributor to cardiovascular, kidney, and  metabolic (CKM) 
conditions. However, the association between  different adiposity-related anthropometrics and  cardiovascular outcomes  
in persons with CKM  conditions has not been  rigorously explored.
OBJECTIVES  In this study, the authors sought  to examine  cardiovascular outcomes  and  treatment effects of 
finerenone according to different adiposity-related anthropometrics.
METHODS  In this prespecified participant-level pooled  analysis of FIDELIO-DKD, FIGARO-DKD,  and  FINEARTS-HF 
(FINE-HEART), cardiovascular outcomes  and  treatment effects of finerenone according to baseline body  mass  index 
(BMI), waist circumference (WC), waist-height ratio (WHtR), and  waist-hip ratio (WHR) were  evaluated with the use of 
multivariable-adjusted Cox proportional hazards regression and  Poisson regression.
RESULTS  Of 18,759 participants with available data for all anthropometrics, 52%  had  a BMI  $ 30  kg/m 2 and  98% had  
any excess adiposity. Among  those with BMI  <30  kg/m 2 , 95% had  increased abdominal  adiposity, especially women  and  
older individuals. Higher BMI, WC, WHtR,  and  WHR  were  each significantly associated with a wide  range of cardiovas-
cular outcomes, including cardiovascular death or heart failure (HF) hospitalization and  its individual components,  major 
adverse cardiovascular events, new-onset  atrial fibrillation, and  incident HF hospitalization. BMI-adjusted WHtR  and  
WHtR-adjusted  BMI  were  each associated with a higher rate of cardiovascular death or HF hospitalization, and  
participants with elevated BMI  and  WHtR  experienced a higher rate of cardiovascular death or HF hospitalization 
compared  with those with elevated BMI  or WHtR  alone (P < 0.001).  Benefits of finerenone on  cardiovascular death or 
HF hospitalization were  consistent regardless of baseline BMI  (P interaction = 0.27) or WHtR  (P interaction = 0.26), and  
absolute benefits appeared  to be  greater among  participants with higher adiposity. Serious adverse events were  less 
common  with finerenone vs placebo, regardless of baseline BMI  category (P interaction = 0.08).
CONCLUSIONS  These  findings suggest that assessment  of anthropometrics capturing abdominal  adiposity,
in addition to BMI, may  enhance  obesity identification and  risk stratification among  individuals with CKM  conditions. 
Finerenone consistently reduced  adverse cardiovascular outcomes  across a wide  range of adiposity. (FINE-HEART:

METHODS
THE  INTEGRATED  FINE-HEART  PROGRAM,  PATIENT  
POPULATION,  AND  TRIAL  CHARACTERISTICS.  The
rationale, design, and  primary  results of 
FINE-HEART  have  been  previously  described.  20  
Briefly, FINE-HEART  is a prespecified  participant-
level pooled  analysis of 3 global, multicenter, 
double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized  clin-
ical trials evaluating  the  safety and  efficacy of finer-
enone  in adults with  CKD  and  type  2 diabetes  (T2D) or 
heart  failure with  mildly  reduced  or preserved  ejec-
tion fraction (HFmrEF/HFpEF)  with  or without  T2D.  
The  design, baseline  characteristics, and  primary  re-
sults of each  of the  trials included  in FINE-HEART  
have  been  previously  reported.  13,21-26
The  FIDELIO-DKD  and  FIGARO-DKD  trials enrolled  
adults (aged  $ 18  years) with  T2D  and  CKD  across  48  
countries.  FIDELIO-DKD  (Efficacy and  Safety  of 
Finerenone  in Subjects  With  Type  2 Diabetes  Mellitus 
and  Diabetic Kidney  Disease; NCT02540993 ) required  
a urinary  albumin-to-creatinine  ratio of 30  
to <300  mg/g, an  estimated  glomerular  filtration rate 
(eGFR)  of 25  to <60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 , and  a history of 
diabetic retinopathy  or a urine  albumin-to-creatinine  
ratio (UACR) of 300  to 5,000  mg/g  and  eGFR  of 
25  to <75 mL/min/1.73  m  2 . FIGARO-DKD  required  
either a UACR  of 30  to <300  mg/g  with  an  eGFR  of 
25  to 90  mL/min/1.73  m  2 or a UACR  of 300  to
5,000  mg/g  with  an  eGFR  of $ 60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 .
Both  trials required  a serum  potassium  
of # 4.8  mmol/L  for enrollment.  Use of renin-
angiotensin  system  inhibitors and  dosing  was  opti-
mized  before  randomization  during  run-in phases  
(lasting 4-16  weeks)  in both  trials. Patients with  a 
diagnosis  of symptomatic  (NYHA  functional  class II-IV) 
HF  with  reduced  ejection fraction were  excluded,  but  
those  with  HF  and  higher  left ventricular ejection 
fraction were  eligible.
FINEARTS-HF  enrolled  adults (aged  $ 40  years) 
with  symptomatic  HFmrEF/HFpEF  across  37 coun -
tries. Key  inclusion  criteria included  left ventricular 
ejection fraction $ 40%,  elevated  natriuretic peptides  
(adjusted  based  on  atrial fibrillation [AF]  status and  
clinical setting of screening), structural heart  disease,
recent  diuretic use  for $ 30  days, and  BMI  # 50  kg/m  2 .
Participants were  required  to have  an  eGFR  
of $ 25  mL/min/1.73  m  2 and  a serum  potassium  level
of # 5.0  mmol/L  for enrollment.  Participants were  
eligible for enrollment  regardless  of T2D  status, 
UACR  level, and  clinical care setting (whether  hos-
pitalized, recently hospitalized, or in ambulatory  
care).
TRIAL  PROCEDURES.  Participants in each  of the  
3 trials were  randomly  allocated to finerenone  or 
placebo.  The  initial dose  of study  medication  was  
10  mg  once  daily for participants with  baseline  
eGFR  <60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 (FIDELIO-DKD  and  
FIGARO-DKD)  or # 60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 (FINEARTS -
HF), titrated to a target dose  of 20  mg  once  daily as 
tolerated. For  participants with  baseline  
eGFR  $ 60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 (FIDELIO-DKD  and  
FIGARO-DKD)  or >60  mL/min/1.73  m  2 (FINEARTS -
HF), study  medication  was  initiated at a dose  of 
20  mg  once  daily, but  further titration to a target dose  
of 40  mg  once  daily occurred  only  in FINEARTS-HF.  
The  trial protocols  were  approved  by  the  ethics 
committee  or Institutional Review  Board  at every  
participating site, and  every  participant provided  
written  informed  consent.

DISCUSSION
In this prespecified  FINE-HEART  analysis, excess  
adiposity  was  nearly  universally present  among  per-
sons  with  CKD  and  T2D  or HF  enrolled  in the  
FIDELIO-DKD,  FIGARO-DKD,  and  FINEARTS-HF  tri-

pometric  measure  were  associated  with  a wide  range
of morbidity  and  mortality  outcomes,  with  WC  and  
WHtR  exhibiting  more  linear associations  compared  
with  BMI.  BMI  and  WHtR  exhibited  independent  and  
complementary  associations  with  adverse  outcomes,  
with  participants with  elevated  BMI  and  WHtR  
experiencing  a higher  rate of cardiovascular  death  of 
HF  hospitalization compared  with  those  with  
elevated  BMI  or WHtR  alone.  Finally, benefits of 
finerenone  on  a wide  range  of adverse  clinical out-
comes  appeared  to be  consistent  across  the  spectrum  
of adiposity  in these  studies.

CLINICAL  IMPLICATIONS.  How  should health
professionals use W HtR  or W C alongside BMI ? 
These  findings suggest  that WHtR  or WC  should  be  
obtained  alongside  BMI  to reduce  underrecognition  
of obesity  among  persons  with  CKM  diseases,
especially among  those  with  BMI  <30  kg/m  2 .
Beyond  obesity  recognition, these  findings suggest  
that WHtR  or WC  measurement  may  enhance  risk 
stratification for cardiovascular  events, regardless  
of BMI.
How  do  these  findings influence patient counseling  or 
management  decisions? The  presence  and  extent  of 
excess  adiposity  may  inform  the  timing  and  intensity 
of management  efforts, including  obesity-focused  
treatment  decisions.  Among  eligible persons  with  
CKM  diseases, finerenone  should  be  considered  to 
prevent  or delay  cardiovascular  events, regardless  of 
adiposity  status.
W hat should future guidelines consider? Future  
guidelines  targeting persons  with  CKM  diseases  
should  consider  whether  to incorporate  explicit
recommendations  for WHtR  or WC  measurement  as 
part of individualized  risk stratification efforts.
STUD Y  STRENGTHS  AND  LIMITATIONS.  To our  knowl -
edge, this study  represents  one  of the  largest and  
most  comprehensive  assessments  of the  interplay 
between  adiposity-related anthropometrics  and  
clinical outcomes  among  individuals  with  CKM  con-
ditions. Key  strengths  of the  analysis include  the  
large number  of well phenotyped  trial participants, 
the  simultaneous  measurement  of several adiposity-
related anthropometrics  beyond  BMI, and  the  wide  
range  of morbidity  and  mortality  outcomes  captured  
(including  incident  AF, HF, and  MACE).  In addition, 
the  systematic  investigation of independent  and  
combined  phenotypes  of BMI  and  WHtR  provides  
unique  insights into their complementary  value  in 
this population.
However,  some  limitations should  be  emphasized.  
First, despite  sensitivity analyses  showing  consistent  
findings after extensive  covariate  adjustment,  there  
is potential for residual confounding,  including  con-
founding  by  indication. Indeed, individuals  with  
higher  BMI  at baseline  were  more  often  treated with  
key  pharmacotherapies,  which  may  reflect differen-
tial risk. Furthermore,  some  important  covariates, 
such  as health  behaviors, structural and  social de-
terminants  of health, cardiac biomarkers,  and  frailty 
status, were  not  systematically  measured  in FINE -
HEART.  However,  approximately  linear adiposity-
outcome  associations  were  observed  among  in-
dividuals  with  the  highest  number  of CKM  condi-
tions, among  whom  frailty may  be  greatest. Similarly, 
although  landmark  analyses  revealed  similar findings 
compared  with  the  primary  analysis, reverse  causal-
ity may  have  contributed  to some  of these  study  
findings, especially among  persons  with  lower  BMI.  
Second,  some  endpoints,  such  as outpatient  wors -
ening  and  recurrent  HF  events  (part of the  primary  
composite  endpoint  in FINEARTS-HF),  could  not  be  
harmonized  across  the  3 trials. Third, the  BMI-and  
natriuretic peptideŌĆōbased  exclusion  criteria in 
FINEARTS-HF  may  limit generalizability of these  
findings to persons  with  HF  at extremes  of BMI  (eg, 
>50  kg/m  2 ). Fourth,  owing  to the  absence  of an  in-
ternational measurement  standard, 52 WC  assessment  
may  have  varied  across  trials, investigators, sites, and  
global regions.  However,  WC  appears  to be  strongly  
associated  with  adverse  outcomes  regardless  of the  
measurement  technique.  53 Fifth, because  the  primary  
endpoint  of cardiovascular  death  (exclusive from  un-
determined  death)  was  narrowly  missed  in the  overall

CONCLUSIONS
These  findings suggest  assessment  of anthropomet -
rics capturing  abdominal  adiposity, in addition  to BMI, 
may  enhance  obesity  identification and  risk stratifi-
cation among  individuals  with  CKM  conditions.  
Finerenone  consistently  reduced  adverse  cardiovas-
cular outcomes  across  a wide  range  of adiposity.
