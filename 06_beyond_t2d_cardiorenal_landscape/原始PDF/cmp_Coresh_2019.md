# Change in albuminuria and subsequent risk of end-stage kidney disease: an individual participant-level consortium meta-analysis of observational studies

**Attribution:** According to PubMed / PubMed Central. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/S2213-8587(18)30313-9

## Abstract

**Background:** There is strong biologic plausibility to support change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD), but empirical evidence to support its validity in epidemiologic studies is lacking.

**Methods:** Analyzed 28 cohorts including 693,816 individuals (80% with diabetes) and 7,461 end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) events, defined as initiation of kidney replacement therapy. Percent change in albuminuria was quantified during a baseline period of 1, 2 and 3 years using linear regression. Associations with subsequent ESKD quantified using Cox regression in each cohort, followed by random-effects meta-analysis. Further adjustment for regression dilution was used to take into account the high variability of albuminuria.

**Findings:** Change in urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) was consistently related to subsequent risk of ESKD. The adjusted hazard ratio of ESKD following a **30% decrease in ACR during a 2-year baseline period was 0.83 (95% CI 0.74–0.94)**; after further adjustment for regression dilution it was **0.78 (95% CI 0.66–0.92)**. Adjusted hazard ratios were relatively consistent across cohorts and subgroups but were somewhat stronger at higher ACR (p-interaction<0.05). In a group of persons with baseline ACR >30 mg/g, an average reduction in ACR of 30% over 2-years was estimated to confer >1% absolute reduction in 10-year ESKD risk, even at earlier stages of CKD. Results were generally similar when using change in urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (PCR), or when study populations from clinical trials were evaluated. Limitations include potential residual confounding, modeling assumptions, and heterogeneity.

**Interpretation:** Change in albuminuria was consistently associated with subsequent risk of ESKD across a wide range of cohorts, lending support to using average change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint in clinical trials of CKD progression in the setting of elevated albuminuria.

## Introduction

A recent scientific workshop sponsored by the US National Kidney Foundation, in collaboration with the US Food and Drug Administration and European Medicines Agency (NKF-FDA-EMA Workshop) evaluated candidate surrogate endpoints for clinical trials of drugs to slow kidney disease progression, particularly among participants with early stages of CKD. Change in albuminuria has been used for decades in some clinical trials for developing therapies to slow progression of CKD. However, substantial controversy exists about whether change in albuminuria meets the criteria for a surrogate outcome for ESKD risk in phase 3 clinical trials. Criteria for surrogacy include biological plausibility, correlation with clinical trial estimates of clinical endpoints, and observation of consistent risk associations with clinical endpoints across a wide range of settings. This study focuses on the latter aspect by examining the relationship of change in albuminuria to ESKD risk in a large individual-level meta-analysis, complementing parallel investigations in clinical trials.

Previous meta-analyses of clinical trials conflict with respect to the strength of associations between change in albuminuria and risk of ESKD. Noted limitations were selected populations, small sample size, and relatively short duration of follow up. Observational studies can overcome these limitations. Imprecision in estimating albuminuria at the individual level is substantial and leads to regression dilution, which weakens empirical associations. The current investigation is published together with a companion meta-analysis of clinical trials.

## Methods

### Study selection criteria
The CKD Prognosis Consortium (CKD-PC) consists of more than 70 cohorts with at least 1,000 participants across over 40 countries, with data on serum creatinine and albuminuria and 50+ events on outcomes of interest. All cohorts with appropriate data, a repeated measure of albuminuria during an elapsed period of 8 months–4 years, and subsequent ESKD or mortality follow-up opted into this study. Included **28 cohorts** with 20 having ESKD follow-up and 27 having mortality follow-up. Restricted to participants aged ≥18 years. Data transfer and analysis July 2015–June 2018. Same analysis performed in 14 clinical trials from the CKD-EPI clinical trials database (including 9 not in CKD-PC). Approved by IRB at Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health.

### Procedures
Albuminuria measured as ACR (or AER converted to ACR) and proteinuria measured as PCR (or PER converted to PCR) analyzed separately. Changes examined on the log scale to focus on relative changes. A change of +/−0.515 on the log (base 2) scale corresponds to a 30% decrease and 43% increase in albuminuria (symmetric relative changes: 1/0.70=1.43). Three baseline periods (1, 2, 3 years) defined. eGFR calculated using CKD-EPI 2009 creatinine equation. Diabetes defined as fasting glucose ≥7.0 mmol/L (126 mg/dl), non-fasting glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dl), HbA1c ≥6.5%, glucose-lowering drug use, or self-reported diabetes.

Primary outcome: ESKD developing after the baseline period, defined as initiation of kidney replacement therapy. Also repeated for cardiovascular, all-cause, and non-cardiovascular mortality.

### Statistical analysis
Two-stage approach: each cohort analyzed separately, followed by random-effects meta-analysis. Adjusted HRs modeled as a spline function of change in albuminuria (five knots: 1-fold [no change, reference], 2-fold and ½-fold, 4-fold and ¼-fold). Cox models adjusted for baseline age, sex, race/ethnicity (black vs. non-black), systolic blood pressure, total cholesterol, diabetes, history of CVD, current smoking, former smoking, first eGFR and albuminuria. HRs adjusted for regression dilution by dividing the log hazard ratio by the reliability coefficient of change in albuminuria. Absolute risk of ESKD estimated up to 10 years using competing risk (Weibull) models. Stata/MP 14 and 15.

## Results

Study population: **28 cohorts, total 693,816 participants** in the 2-year baseline period analysis. 20 cohorts had ESKD follow-up, 27 mortality, 11 cardiovascular mortality. Albuminuria quantified with ACR in 16 cohorts, PCR in 3, both in 9. Total **ESKD events after 2-year baseline period: 7,461**. Mean age 63 years (cohort IQR 58–66). Mean baseline eGFR 78 ml/min/1.73m². Sixteen cohorts had mean eGFR >60 (low median baseline ACR 2–18 mg/g); twelve had mean eGFR <60 (wide range of median albuminuria 18–1118 mg/g). Median fold change in albuminuria close to 1.0 (IQR 0.61 to 2.17).

**Key ESKD associations (2-year baseline period, 30% reduction in ACR):**
- Adjusted HR = **0.83 (95% CI 0.74–0.94)** (adjusted for age, sex, race/ethnicity, SBP, total cholesterol, diabetes, history of CVD, current/former smoking, first eGFR and albuminuria)
- Reliability of albuminuria change in median cohort = **0.721** (2-year baseline period)
- After adjustment for regression dilution: HR = **0.78 (95% CI 0.66–0.92)**
- Using 25th/75th percentile reliability coefficients: HR **0.76 (0.63–0.91)** and **0.80 (0.70–0.93)**
- Consistent across baseline periods and ACR/PCR (20–32% risk reduction across analyses)

A **43% increase in albuminuria** was opposite in direction but similar in magnitude to a 30% decrease. Notable interaction: baseline albuminuria — a 30% decrease in ACR associated with stronger relative hazard reduction at higher baseline levels (p-interaction <0.001 for ACR, 0.006 for PCR). No interaction of baseline albuminuria with albuminuria increase.

**Mortality:** All-cause and cardiovascular mortality each related to change in ACR and PCR, but weaker than for ESKD and sometimes non-linear. A 43% increase in ACR associated with adjusted HR of cardiovascular mortality of **1.14 (1.06–1.22)** while a 30% decrease had a weaker HR of **0.94 (0.87–1.02)**. A 43% increase in ACR over 2-years had an adjusted HR of all-cause mortality of **1.07 (1.05–1.09)**; a 30% decrease not significantly associated with all-cause mortality.

**Clinical trials (CKD-EPI):** 9 of 13 trials (11,272 participants) not in the CKD-PC main analysis. Change in ACR had strong linear association (log scale) with ESKD. Adjusted relative hazard of ESKD after 2-year baseline period = **0.83 (95% CI 0.69–1.00)**, and **0.79 (0.63–1.00)** after regression dilution adjustment.

**Absolute risk:** The apparent impact of a 30% reduction in albuminuria accentuated with longer follow-up, exceeding 1% at 10 years if baseline albuminuria high (e.g., ACR 300 mg/g or PCR 500 mg/g).

## Discussion

Change in albuminuria was strongly and robustly associated with subsequent risk of ESKD across a wide range of settings. The adjusted HR of ESKD following a 30% decrease in ACR during a 2-year baseline period was 0.83 (95% CI 0.74–0.94); after regression dilution adjustment, 0.78 (95% CI 0.66–0.92) — a **22% ESKD risk reduction**, consistent whether albuminuria was quantified using albumin or protein and whether change was measured over 1, 2, or 3 years (20–32% across analyses). Greater relative and absolute risk reduction at higher albuminuria levels, but no difference with RAAS inhibitor use. One-third to one-half of ESKD cases developed without any increase in albuminuria during the baseline period.

The 20–32% ESKD risk reduction (regression-dilution adjusted) for a 30% reduction in albuminuria is similar in magnitude to the ~30% ESKD reduction found in the companion meta-regression of clinical trials. Both papers indicate change in albuminuria is more informative as a surrogate of ESKD at higher baseline albuminuria (higher absolute risk reductions). The companion meta-regression suggests a minimum threshold average reduction of >20–30% in albuminuria is needed to infer clinical benefit.

**Limitations:** possible residual confounding, uncaptured untreated ESKD cases, limited non-white representation, unexplained heterogeneity, non-standardized albuminuria measurement across cohorts, median reliability estimate, varying follow-up length, linearity assumptions in interaction tests.

**Conclusion:** Even modest true changes in albuminuria (e.g., 30% decrease) were associated with a significant reduction of ESKD risk when baseline albuminuria was elevated, providing a complementary line of evidence to intention-to-treat analyses in clinical trials. Taken together, results from the two very different analyses agree both qualitatively and quantitatively after adjustment for regression dilution.

## Research in Context

**Added value:** Comprehensive individual-level meta-analysis of the association between change in albuminuria with subsequent ESKD risk. Analyzed 28 cohorts including 693,816 individuals and 7,461 ESKD events. Adjusted HR of ESKD following a 30% decrease in ACR during a 2-year baseline period 0.83 (95% CI 0.74–0.94); 0.78 (95% CI 0.66–0.92) after regression dilution adjustment. Among persons with ACR >30 mg/g, a 30% ACR reduction over 2 years estimated to confer >1% absolute reduction in 10-year ESKD risk.

**Implications:** Change in albuminuria is consistently associated with subsequent ESKD risk across cohorts and subgroups, lending support to use of average change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for clinical trials including in early CKD.
