## Abstract

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) decline is causally associated with kidney failure and is a candidate surrogate endpoint for clinical trials of chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression. Analyses across a diverse spectrum of interventions and populations is required for acceptance of GFR decline as an endpoint. In an analysis of individual participant data, for each of 66 studies (total N participants, 186,312), we estimated treatment effects on the total GFR slope, computed from baseline to 3 years, and chronic slope, starting at 3 months after randomization, and on the clinical endpoint (doubling of serum creatinine, GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 mÂ² or kidney failure with replacement therapy). We used a Bayesian mixed effects meta-regression model to relate treatment effects on GFR slope with those on the clinical endpoint across all studies and by disease groups (diabetes, glomerular diseases, CKD, or cardiovascular diseases). Treatment effects on the clinical endpoint were strongly associated with treatment effects on total slope (median RÂ² = 0.97 [95% Bayesian credible interval [BCI] 0.82 to 1.00]), and moderately associated with those on chronic slope (RÂ² = 0.55 [95% BCI 0.25 to 0.77]). There was no evidence of heterogeneity across disease. Our results support use of total slope as a primary endpoint for clinical trials of CKD progression.

## Introduction

Global prevalence of CKD is common, estimated at 9%, and is associated with increased risk of kidney failure, cardiovascular disease and mortality. A critical challenge in evaluation of therapies for CKD is that randomized controlled trials (RCTs) traditionally use kidney failure and doubling of serum creatinine as clinical endpoints, which are often late events in the progression of CKD. In order to obtain sufficient clinical events to achieve adequate statistical power, RCTs often require substantial follow-up or are restricted to patients with rapidly progressive or late-stage disease, yet some interventions may have a greater effect when applied earlier in the disease course.

For all kidney diseases, patients must have a decline in GFR to reach kidney failure, providing strong biological plausibility for GFR decline as a surrogate endpoint for CKD progression in RCTs. There is also robust epidemiological evidence that both a single measurement of GFR and GFR decline over 1-to-3-year periods are strongly associated with subsequent kidney failure across subgroups of age, sex, and diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease status. Using a smaller number of RCTs, the investigators previously demonstrated that treatment effects on the GFR slope were highly predictive of treatment effects on clinical endpoints in hypothetical future RCTs. On the basis of this evidence, GFR slope has been used to evaluate efficacy of treatments in less common conditions such as glomerular diseases or when treatments with established efficacy and safety in one population are expanded to another population. However, uncertainty remained as to the use of GFR slope in more common causes of kidney disease and across severity of kidney disease.

RCTs in populations with earlier stages of CKD or at risk for CKD demonstrate the efficacy of novel agents for prevention of adverse outcomes including mortality, heart failure events and CKD progression. These studies provide the opportunity to evaluate if the strong associations between treatment effects on GFR slope with those on the clinical endpoint persist with inclusion of more diverse interventions and populations, strengthening the evidence for the validity of GFR slope as a surrogate endpoint for kidney failure.

## Results

### Study description

Using a systematic search to identify RCTs evaluating interventions for CKD progression, the prior publication included 47 treatment comparisons spanning 49 RCTs. The updated search allowed identification of 19 new RCTs, leading to a total of 66 treatment comparisons (herein referred to as studies) for the current analysis. These 66 studies included **186,312 participants** and represented **17 interventions** across four broad disease groups. The 17 interventions included four trials of SGLT-2 inhibitors as well as 16 studies of seven treatments not previously included, such as endothelin receptor antagonists, glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonist (GLP-1), and mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRA).

Studies of patients with CKD from other causes or cause not specified had lower mean eGFR than the studies of patients with diabetes, glomerular diseases (GN), or cardiovascular disease (CVD); studies of patients with CVD had lower levels of urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) and slower progression compared to the other disease groups. Compared to the prior set of studies, the current studies include populations with higher mean levels of GFR and at high risk for CVD.

### Treatment effects on the GFR slope and the clinical endpoint

Patterns of change in GFR after initiation of an intervention are often nonlinear, with possibly differing direction and rates of change in early follow-up (acute effect) versus longer-term follow-up (chronic slope). The average rate of decline from beginning to end of the study incorporates both (total slope). A shared parameter mixed-effects model estimated the effects of treatment on the total GFR slope at three years (3-year total slope; the approximate mean length of the included studies) and on chronic slope, computed from three months following randomization. Across the full collection of studies and for studies in diabetes, CKD and GN, but not for CVD, there was an average benefit of the active treatments on the 3-year total slope and on the chronic slope compared with control.

Across the 66 studies, the median follow-up for the clinical endpoint was **35 months (25thâ€“75th percentile 22 to 52)**, with shorter follow-up for CVD studies. Over the full study duration, a total of **11,396 (6.1%)** patients reached the primary clinical endpoint, defined as the composite of kidney failure with replacement therapy (KFRT; initiation of chronic dialysis or kidney transplantation), sustained GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 mÂ², or doubling of serum creatinine (equivalent to 57% decline in GFR). A benefit on the clinical endpoint was indicated by a hazard ratio less than 1 estimated within each study using Cox regression. Across all studies and for diabetes, CKD and GN, but not CVD, an average benefit of treatment was observed. The secondary clinical endpoint (composite of KFRT or sustained GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 mÂ²) showed an average benefit of treatment overall and across diabetes, CKD, GN, and CVD.

For the total slope, the median RÂ² decreased from 0.97 (0.82, 1.00) to **0.92 (0.56, 0.99)** relative to the primary clinical endpoint. ## Discussion

CKD is a slowly progressive disease, often taking 10 years or longer from initiation to kidney failure, and therapies initiated earlier are often more effective. Validated surrogate endpoints are needed to assess efficacy in early stages. Evidence for validity derives from three sources: biological plausibility, epidemiologic data, and trial-level analyses (regarded as the most important and most difficult criterion). Treatment effects on the total slope computed at 3 years accounted for an estimated **97% of the variation between studies** in treatment effects on the clinical endpoint, with similar results across CKD severity and diverse disease groups. This falls in the range for a **strong surrogate** (RÂ² > 0.72) and compares favorably with widely used surrogate endpoints such as progression-free survival for metastatic breast cancer or LDL cholesterol for major cardiovascular events.

The results indicate potential concerns with the use of the chronic slope, which ignores the acute effect of a drug on GFR slope. The trial-level associations for chronic slope were considerably less favorable than in earlier analyses (which had shown chronic slope accounting for a median 96% [95% BCI 63% to 100%] of variation). Because total slope includes both acute and chronic components, its superior performance suggests the acute effect might be correlated with a component of the primary clinical endpoint (which includes doubling of serum creatinine). Consistent with this, treatment effects on chronic slope predicted the secondary clinical endpoint (excluding doubling of serum creatinine) more accurately than the primary. Chronic slope performance nonetheless falls within the RÂ² range (0.49 to 0.72) proposed for moderate surrogate performance, and may serve as the more conservative endpoint when a positive acute effect may not indicate a true lasting benefit.

The trial-level model predicts a modest benefit on the primary clinical endpoint even when there is no effect on the 2-year total slope, which may reflect the average negative direction of acute effects across the 66 RCTs. For future trials, computation of total GFR slope over shorter durations for interventions without a negative acute effect could be considered.

The weaker chronic-slope associations in the CVD group may explain the less favorable current results, since CVD studies were not designed to evaluate CKD progression specifically and included lower-risk populations (higher GFR, lower ACR).

**Implications:** (1) These data provide high confidence in the validity of the 3-year total GFR slope as a surrogate endpoint for kidney failure, allowing it as a primary endpoint across disease subgroups and CKD severity (lower limit of 95% CI for trial-level RÂ² of 0.82, up from 0.78 in prior results, exceeding the 0.72 threshold). (2) The consistency across disease severity and disease suggests total slope can be used to explore heterogeneous effects among subgroups within an RCT, and may be appropriate for subsequent studies after initial RCTs showed benefit on the clinical endpoint.

**Strengths:** a large collection of RCTs with diverse disease severity, diseases and interventions; a robust GFR-slope method accounting for informative censoring and multiple sources of measurement variability; individual patient-level data allowing adjustment for spurious sampling-error correlations so meta-regression parameters refer to true latent treatment effects.

**Limitations:** associations could only be evaluated over each trial's follow-up period; differing follow-up times across studies/disease groups may cause variation; prediction intervals assume similar relationships between future and prior trials (caution for interventions with especially large acute effects); GFR was estimated from creatinine, which can be affected by non-GFR factors (caution for interventions affecting body composition or dietary intake). Cystatin C is a more robust alternative filtration marker that should be evaluated before widespread use in trials.

**Conclusion:** GFR slope can be used as a validated surrogate endpoint for CKD progression. Selection of total versus chronic GFR slope, and the duration over which total GFR slope is computed, should consider study design, population, intervention, and the specific drug development program.

## Methods (summary)

- **Ethics:** Protocols of individual trials were reviewed by participating centers' IRBs; the current analyses were deemed exempt by the Tufts Medical Center IRB.
- **Overview (seven steps):** (1) systematic search/identification of RCTs; (2) obtain data-use agreement and transfer to the Data Coordinating Center at Tufts Medical Center; (3) within each RCT, intent-to-treat estimation of treatment effect on GFR slope (shared parameter mixed effects model) and on clinical endpoint (Cox proportional hazards); (4) trial-level Bayesian meta-regression; (5) leave-one-out outlier/model-adequacy assessment; (6) partial-pooling meta-regression for disease subgroups; (7) 95% Bayesian prediction intervals and trial-level positive predictive values for a hypothetical future trial.
- **Datasets:** systematic review updated August 2018 to April 2020 added 19 new RCTs (three SGLT-2 inhibitor trials + 16 studies of seven novel treatments) to the prior 47 comparisons/49 RCTs â†’ 66 treatment comparisons. Interventions categorized (RASB, RASB vs CCB, RASB+CCB, intensive BP control, low protein diet, antiplatelet, allopurinol, nurse-coordinated care, albuminuria-targeted therapy, endothelin receptor antagonist, MRA, DPP-4, GLP-1, SGLT2, intensive glucose, statin+ezetimibe, immunosuppressive therapy). Disease groups: diabetes, glomerular diseases (GN), CKD (other/unspecified), CVD.
- **Clinical endpoint:** composite of KFRT, sustained GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 mÂ² (baseline GFR â‰¥25), or doubling of serum creatinine, over full study duration. Secondary endpoint: composite of KFRT or GFR <15 mL/min per 1.73 mÂ² (excludes doubling of serum creatinine).
- **GFR:** estimated using the CKD-EPI 2009 creatinine equation; creatinine standardized to IDMS-traceable methods (or reduced 5% from non-IDMS methods).
- **GFR slope model:** shared parameter mixed-effects model assuming a linear mean GFR slope from 3 months post-randomization; treatment effects on acute, chronic, and total slopes defined by group differences at 3 months, in slopes from 3 months onward, and in changes from baseline to 2- or 3-year follow-up (factored by duration). Random slopes/intercepts; power-of-the-mean residual variance model; allows non-uniform treatment effects and accounts for informative censoring (in studies with â‰¥15 deaths/KFRT). Fit using SAS 9.4 NLMIXED.
- **Bayesian meta-regression:** 2-stage mixed-effects model relating estimated to true latent treatment effects (bivariate normal, robust sandwich correlation estimates), then variation in true latent effects across trials. Fit via MCMC (RStan 2.21.3) with diffuse priors (uniform âˆ’5 to 5 for the meta-regression slope; N(0, 10,000) for mean treatment effects; inverse-gamma priors for variances). Validity supported when: (1) meta-regression slope large with BCI not crossing 0; (2) intercept near 0 with BCI crossing 0; (3) RÂ² large (>0.72).
- **Software:** SAS 9.4; R 4.1.0; RStan 2.21.3; metafor 3.0-2 (rma.uni).
- **Statistics & reproducibility:** all included studies were RCTs (mostly double-blind placebo-controlled; BP/dietary/lifestyle trials were unblinded). No sample-size calculation was performed for the number of trials; all studies meeting entry criteria with data-use agreement were included.
