# Change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for progression of kidney disease

**Heerspink HJL, Greene T, Tighiouart H, Gansevoort RT, Coresh J, Simon AL, Chan TM, Hou FF, Lewis JB, Locatelli F, Praga M, Schena FP, Levey AS, Inker LA, for the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration.** *Lancet Diabetes & Endocrinology* 2019; 7(2): 128-139. doi:10.1016/S2213-8587(18)30314-0. PMID: 30635226.

## Background
Change in albuminuria has strong biological plausibility as a surrogate endpoint for progression of chronic kidney disease (CKD), but empirical evidence to support its validity is lacking. The authors aimed to determine the association between treatment effects on early changes in albuminuria and treatment effects on clinical endpoints, to inform the use of albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint in future randomised controlled trials.

## Methods
- **Design:** Meta-analysis using individual patient data from randomised controlled trials.
- **Search:** PubMed for English-language publications from Jan 1, 1946, to Dec 15, 2016; search terms included "chronic kidney disease", "chronic renal insufficiency", "albuminuria", "proteinuria", and "randomized controlled trial".
- **Key inclusion criteria:** Quantifiable measurements of albuminuria or proteinuria at baseline and within 12 months of follow-up, plus information on the incidence of end-stage kidney disease.
- **Endpoints estimated:** Treatment effect on 6-month change in albuminuria, and the composite clinical endpoint of treated end-stage kidney disease, eGFR <15 mL/min per 1·73 m², or doubling of serum creatinine.
- **Analysis:** Bayesian mixed-effects meta-regression relating treatment effects on albuminuria to those on the clinical endpoint across studies; a prediction model was developed for the clinical-endpoint treatment effect on the basis of the albuminuria treatment effect.

## Findings (key numbers)
- **41 eligible treatment comparisons** (referred to as "studies") from randomised trials.
- **29 979 participants**; **21 206 (71%) with diabetes**.
- Median follow-up **3·4 years (IQR 2·3–4·2)**.
- **3935 (13%) participants** reached the composite clinical endpoint.
- **Meta-regression slope 0·89 (95% Bayesian credible interval [BCI] 0·13–1·70).**
- Each **30% decrease in geometric mean albuminuria** (treatment vs control) was associated with an average **27% lower hazard** for the clinical endpoint (**95% BCI 5–45%**; median **R² 0·47, 95% BCI 0·02–0·96**).
- Association strengthened when restricted to patients with **baseline albuminuria >30 mg/g** (i.e., 3·4 mg/mmol): **R² 0·72 (95% BCI 0·05–0·99)**.
- **Prediction for future trials:** treatments that decrease geometric mean albuminuria to **0·7 (30% decrease)** relative to control give an average **HR 0·68** for the clinical endpoint; 95% of sufficiently large studies would have **HRs between 0·47 and 0·95**.

## Interpretation
The results support a role for change in albuminuria as a surrogate endpoint for progression of CKD, particularly in patients with high baseline albuminuria; for patients with low baseline levels of albuminuria this association is less certain.
