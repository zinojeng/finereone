## Abstract (verbatim, from PubMed)

**Background.** In the Treatment of Preserved Cardiac Function Heart Failure With an Aldosterone Antagonist (TOPCAT) trial, patients with heart failure and preserved left ventricular ejection fraction assigned to spironolactone did not achieve a significant reduction in the primary composite outcome (time to cardiovascular death, aborted cardiac arrest, or hospitalization for management of heart failure) compared with patients receiving placebo. A post hoc analysis revealed approximately a 4-fold difference in composite event rates between the 1678 patients randomized in Russia and Georgia versus the 1767 randomized in the Americas (United States, Canada, Brazil, and Argentina).

**Methods and Results.** Demographic characteristics and treatment responses were compared across regions. Patients randomized in Russia/Georgia were younger, with less atrial fibrillation and diabetes but more prior myocardial infarction and heart failure hospitalizations. They had lower left ventricular ejection fraction and serum creatinine but higher diastolic blood pressure (P<0.001 for each). Hyperkalemia and doubling of creatinine were more likely, and hypokalemia was less likely, in patients receiving spironolactone in the Americas, with no significant treatment effects on these laboratory measures in Russia/Georgia. In the Americas, spironolactone significantly reduced the primary composite outcome, cardiovascular death, and the hospitalization rate for heart failure. Event rates were markedly lower in Russia/Georgia, with no detectable impact of spironolactone.

**Conclusions.** This post hoc analysis demonstrated greater potassium and creatinine changes and possible clinical benefits with spironolactone in patients with heart failure and preserved ejection fraction randomized in the Americas, raising concerns about the appropriateness of pooling data across these disparate regions.
