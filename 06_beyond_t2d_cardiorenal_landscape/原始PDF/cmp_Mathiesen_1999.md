## Design
- **8-year prospective, randomised controlled trial.**
- **44 normotensive patients** with insulin dependent (type I) diabetes and **persistent microalbuminuria (30–300 mg/24 h)**.
- **Treatment group (n=21):** captopril **100 mg/24 h** + bendrofluazide **2.5 mg/24 h**.
- **Control group (n=23):** left untreated.
- Institution: Steno Diabetes Center, Gentofte / University Hospital of Copenhagen (Rigshospitalet), Denmark.
- **Diabetic nephropathy** endpoint defined as albuminuria persistently **>300 mg/24 h**.
- Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) measured with **⁵¹Cr-EDTA plasma clearance over 4 hours**.
- After 4 years, two patients in each group were excluded (did not attend follow-up sessions).
- Four control patients started antihypertensive treatment (diuretics, β blockers, or a calcium channel blocker); three treatment-group patients had bendrofluazide changed to frusemide (oedema or diastolic BP >95 mm Hg).
- After 8 years, 16 of 21 treatment-group and 2 control-group patients were investigated after a **treatment pause of 2 months**.

## Key results
- **Progression to diabetic nephropathy:** 40% (9/23) in control group vs 10% (2/21) in captopril group; **survival analysis P=0.019**.
- During the treatment pause in the captopril group there was a significant increase in urinary albumin excretion (**P<0.001**). In **6 (38%) of the 16** patients albuminuria exceeded **300 mg/24 h**.
- **GFR (captopril group):** declined from **126 (23) ml/min at baseline to 114 (23) after 8 years**, then rose again to **126 (21)** during the treatment pause.
- **Decline in mean GFR over 8 years:** control group **11.8 ml/min (95% CI 1.2 to 22.0; t test P=0.03)**; captopril group **1.4 ml/min (95% CI −4.9 to 7.7; P=0.65)**; **P=0.09 between groups**.
- Among the **8 control patients who developed nephropathy**, GFR fall was **27.3 ml/min (95% CI 3.7 to 51.0; P=0.03)**; in the **6 captopril patients** whose albumin excretion rose >300 mg/24 h during the treatment pause, GFR **increased 3.8 ml/min (95% CI −3.5 to 11.0)** (**P=0.02 between groups**).
- **HbA1c and blood pressure did not differ** between the two groups at any time during the study.

## Conclusions (authors)
- The beneficial effect of ACE inhibition in preventing diabetic nephropathy is **long lasting and associated with preservation of normal GFR**.
- The temporary fall in GFR in the intervention group during pause was regarded as a **reversible haemodynamic phenomenon** (patients with persistent microalbuminuria at follow-up had stable normal GFR).
- The observation period exceeded 2 years and GFR was measured with good accuracy/precision, addressing limitations of previous shorter studies.
