# ABSTRACT

# BACKGROUND

The nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist finerenone reduces clinical events in heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction/preserved ejection fraction; however, the implications of treatment-related hypotension are unknown.

# OBJECTIVES

The authors investigated predictors of systolic blood pressure (SBP) ≤ 100 mm Hg and investigator reported hypotension and their associations with randomized treatment and clinical outcomes in the FINEARTS-HF (Study to Evaluate the Efficacy [Effect on Disease] and Safety of Finerenone in Participants With Heart Failure and Left Ventricular Ejection Fraction [Proportion of Blood Expelled Per Heart Stroke]) trial.

# METHODS

FINEARTS-HF was a randomized, placebo-controlled trial of finerenone in symptomatic patients with chronic heart failure (left ventricular ejection fraction ≥ 40%). Predictors of SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg and hypotension were identified using Cox models. Associations between SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg and hypotension, treatment, and clinical outcomes were evaluated using time-updated Cox models. The primary outcome was a composite of total heart failure events and cardiovascular death.

# RESULTS

Among the 5,815 participants with available data, post-baseline SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg occurred in 899 (538 with finerenone vs 361 with placebo; odds ratio: 1.60; 95% CI: 1.38-1.85) and investigator-reported hypotension in 364 patients (225 with finerenone vs 139 with placebo; odds ratio: 1.67; 95% CI: 1.34-2.08). Participants experiencing SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg had lower baseline SBP, were older, had higher N-terminal pro–B-type natriuretic peptide levels, a history of smoking, and no diabetes. Treatment-related risk of the primary endpoint was reduced in patients with no/before SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg (rate ratio: 0.78; 95% CI: 0.67-0.90) and appeared to attenuate afterwards (rate ratio: 0.99; 95% CI: 0.70-1.39), although no formal statistical interaction was observed (Pinteraction = 0.33).

# CONCLUSIONS

In this prespecified analysis of the FINEARTS-HF trial, finerenone led to higher rates of post-baseline SBP ≤ 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension. Although hypotension should not prompt automatic treatment discontinuation, these patients should be carefully monitored. # METHODS

The study design and the primary results of the FINEARTS-HF trial have been previously described.¹¹,12 The trial protocol was approved by the ethics committee at all participating institutions, and all participants provided written informed consent. Briefly, FINEARTS-HF was an international, multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial testing the efficacy and safety of finerenone in patients with symptomatic HFmrEF/HFpEF. Key inclusion criteria were ≥40 years of age, NYHA functional class II to IV, diuretic treatment within 30 days before randomization, an LVEF ≥40%, evidence of structural heart disease, and elevated natriuretic peptides with different cutoffs based on atrial fibrillation status. Key exclusion criteria included: estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) &#x3C;25 mL/min/1.73 m², potassium levels >5.0 mmol/L, recent myocardial infarction, coronary artery bypass grafting or percutaneous coronary intervention, and history of cardiomyopathies or inflammatory heart disease. Additionally, patients with symptomatic hypotension with mean systolic blood pressure (SBP) &#x3C;90 mm Hg at screening or at randomization were excluded. Eligible patients were randomized in a double-blind 1:1 fashion to either finerenone or matching placebo. Participants with an eGFR ≤60 mL/min/1.73 m² started with 10 mg once daily with a maximum maintenance dose of 20 mg, whereas participants with an eGFR >60 mL/min/1.73 m² started with 20 mg with a maximum maintenance dose of 40 mg.

# CLINICAL OUTCOMES

The primary outcome of the FINEARTS-HF trial was a composite of total (first and recurrent) HF events (HF hospitalization or HF urgent visit) and CV death. Other outcomes included in this analysis were the individual components of the primary endpoint, all-cause mortality, CV hospitalizations, and all-cause hospitalizations.

# STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

Analyses were performed based on the first occurrence of SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension. Risk of SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension was expressed with odds ratios. Baseline characteristics were summarized as mean ± SD, median (Q1-Q3), or frequencies (%). Baseline characteristics were compared between participants who did and did not develop SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension using Student’s t-tests or Wilcoxon rank-sum tests for comparison of continuous variables, as appropriate, and chi-square tests for comparison of categorical variables. Predictors of hypotension were assessed using multivariable Cox models using a stepwise forward selection adding variables with a value of P &#x3C; 0.05. The association between baseline SBP as a continuous variable, treatment, and incidence rates of SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension was assessed with Poisson regression models using restricted cubic splines. Associations between both SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg and investigator-reported hypotension and clinical outcomes were evaluated using time-updated Cox proportional hazard models with and without adjustment for age, region, sex, randomized treatment, LVEF, baseline SBP, heart rate, cigarette smoking, type 2 diabetes, eGFR, body mass index, beta blockers, angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor.

# RESULTS

# BASELINE CHARACTERISTICS

Among 6,001 participants in the FINEARTS-HF trial, data regarding post-baseline SBP measurements were missing in 186 patients who were excluded, leaving 5,815 participants who were analyzed in this study. Randomization to finerenone was well balanced among patients with available data (50.1%) and those without post-baseline SBP measurements (49.5%). Baseline characteristics in patients with available post-baseline SBP vs those with missing values are summarized in Supplemental Table 1.

At 3-months post-randomization, finerenone lowered the mean SBP by 3.1 mm Hg (95% CI: 2.5-3.7 mm Hg). Over a median follow-up of 32 months, 899 of the 5,815 (15.5%) patients included in the analysis experienced SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg, more frequently in the finerenone study arm (538 [18.5%] vs 361 [12.4%]; OR: 1.60 [95% CI: 1.38-1.85]; P &#x3C; 0.001). The cumulative incidence of post-randomization SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg gradually accrued over time and fewer participants (364 [6.3%]) experienced investigator-reported hypotension.

In the primary ITT analysis, finerenone-related benefits on the primary outcome appeared more evident in participants not experiencing/before SBP 100 mm Hg (RR: 0.78; 95% CI: 0.67-0.90) and attenuated after SBP 100 mm Hg (RR: 0.99; 95% CI: 0.70-1.39); however, no significant interaction was observed (Pinteraction = 0.33). In the 30 days following SBP 100 mm Hg, an estimated 4% of patients permanently discontinued study drug (5% of finerenone patients, 3% of placebo patients) but not necessarily due to hypotension.

In the on-treatment analysis among participants adherent to randomized treatment, the benefits of finerenone on the primary outcome appeared to be maintained in people before/without SBP 100 mm Hg (RR: 0.66; 95% CI: 0.57-0.77) and after SBP 100 mm Hg occurrence (RR: 0.62 [95% CI: 0.42-0.90]; Pinteraction = 0.45). Similar findings were observed when analyzing total HF events (Supplemental Table 5).

Following investigator-reported hypotension, the incidence rates of permanent study drug discontinuation increased to 22.3 per 100 patient-years, with no differences between study arms. In the 30 days following hypotension, an estimated 7% of patients permanently discontinued study drug (7% of finerenone patients, 8% of placebo patients). # Study Design

FINEARTS-HF was a randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial of finerenone in symptomatic patients with chronic HF and LVEF ≥40%. Only patients with available data on post-randomization SBP values were included in this analysis (N = 5,815).

Two definitions of treatment-emergent hypotension were included:

- SBP &#x3C;100 mm Hg at any scheduled study visit, regardless of symptoms
- Investigator-reported hypotension not associated to a specific SBP value

# CONCLUSIONS

In this prespecified analysis of the FINEARTS-HF trial, we found that finerenone increased rates of SBP &#x3C;100 mm Hg. However, only a fraction of hypotension was reported by investigators as adverse events, few hospitalizations related to hypotension occurred, and similar rates of drug discontinuation were observed after hypotensive events in finerenone and placebo arms. Following hypotension, finerenone benefits were attenuated but appeared consistent.

# Finerenone-Related Risk of Hypotension in HFpEF

# PERSPECTIVES

COMPETENCY IN MEDICAL KNOWLEDGE: In HFmrEF/HFpEF patients treated with finerenone, the occurrence of SBP &#x3C; 100 mm Hg is common whereas hypotension reported as an adverse event is less frequent, rarely leading to hospitalizations. Among patients who were adherent to treatment, clinical benefits of finerenone appear consistent irrespective of the occurrence of hypotension.

TRANSLATIONAL OUTLOOK: The pathophysiology of blood pressure lowering and hypotension in patients with HFmrEF/HFpEF is multifactorial as these people typically have several comorbidities often requiring polypharmacy. In patients treated with finerenone, the occurrence of hypotensive events should not automatically prompt drug interruption as people may still derive long-term clinical benefits.
