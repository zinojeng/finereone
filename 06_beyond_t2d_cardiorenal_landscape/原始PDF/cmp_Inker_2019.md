# GFR Slope as a Surrogate End Point for Kidney Disease Progression in Clinical Trials

**Inker LA, Heerspink HJL, Tighiouart H, Levey AS, Coresh J, Gansevoort RT, Simon AL, Ying J, Beck GJ, Wanner C, Floege J, Li PK-T, Perkovic V, Vonesh EF, Greene T.** JASN 2019;30(9):1735–1745. doi:10.1681/ASN.2019010007. PMID:31292197.

## Abstract

**Background.** Surrogate end points are needed to assess whether treatments are effective in the early stages of CKD. GFR decline leads to kidney failure, but regulators have not approved using differences in the change in GFR from the beginning to the end of a randomized controlled trial as an end point in CKD because it is not clear whether small changes in the GFR slope will translate to clinical benefits.

**Methods.** To assess the use of GFR slope as a surrogate end point for CKD progression, a meta-analysis of **47 RCTs** that tested **12 interventions** in **60,620 subjects** was performed. Treatment effects were estimated on GFR slope (mean difference in GFR slope between the randomized groups) for the total slope starting at baseline, the chronic slope starting at 3 months after randomization, and on the clinical end point (doubling of serum creatinine, GFR <15 ml/min per 1.73 m2, or ESKD) for each study. Bayesian mixed-effects analyses were used to describe the association of treatment effects on GFR slope with the clinical end point and to test how well the GFR slope predicts a treatment's effect on the clinical end point.

**Results.** Across all studies, the treatment effect on 3-year total GFR slope (median R2 = 0.97; 95% Bayesian credible interval [BCI], 0.78 to 1.00) and on the chronic slope (R2 = 0.96; 95% BCI, 0.63 to 1.00) accurately predicted treatment effects on the clinical end point. With a sufficient sample size, a treatment effect of **0.75 ml/min per 1.73 m2/yr or greater** on total slope over 3 years or chronic slope predicts a clinical benefit on CKD progression with at least **96% probability**.

**Conclusions.** With large enough sample sizes, GFR slope may be a viable surrogate for clinical end points in CKD RCTs.

## Key figures / notes (verified from full text)

- Data set: 47 RCTs, 12 interventions, 60,620 subjects.
- Total slope: assessed from baseline; chronic slope: assessed from 3 months after randomization.
- Clinical end point composite: doubling of serum creatinine, GFR <15 ml/min per 1.73 m2, or ESKD.
- Method: Bayesian mixed-effects meta-regression relating treatment effect on GFR slope to treatment effect on the clinical end point.
- 3-year total GFR slope: median R2 = 0.97 (95% BCI 0.78–1.00).
- Chronic slope: R2 = 0.96 (95% BCI 0.63–1.00).
- Surrogacy threshold: treatment effect >=0.75 ml/min/1.73 m2/yr predicts clinical benefit with >=96% probability (with adequate sample size).
