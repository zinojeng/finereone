# EMPEROR-Preserved Risk Model and Outcomes in the FINEARTS-HF Trial

> Attribution: Full text retrieved from PubMed / PubMed Central (open access). DOI: https://doi.org/10.1001/jamacardio.2026.1049

## Key Points

**Question**
Does the EMPEROR-Preserved prognostic model accurately stratify risk in patients with heart failure with mildly reduced or preserved ejection fraction enrolled in the FINEARTS-HF trial, and does baseline risk modify the treatment effect of finerenone?

**Findings**
In this secondary analysis of 6001 patients from the FINEARTS-HF trial, the risk model showed good discrimination and generally preserved calibration across mortality. The relative treatment effect of finerenone was consistent across all risk categories, while absolute risk reductions were greater among patients at higher baseline risk.

**Meaning**
In this study, the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model provided a practical framework for risk stratification and estimation of absolute treatment benefit in patients with heart failure and mildly reduced or preserved ejection fraction and may support patient selection and enrichment in future clinical trials.

## Abstract

**Importance**
Patients with heart failure (HF) and mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF) or preserved EF (HFpEF) show substantial heterogeneity in prognosis.

**Objectives**
To evaluate the performance of biomarker-driven prognostic models derived from the Empagliflozin Outcome Trial in Patients With Chronic Heart Failure With Preserved Ejection Fraction (EMPEROR-Preserved) Trial in the Finerenone Trial to Investigate Efficacy and Safety Superior to Placebo in Patients With Heart Failure (FINEARTS-HF) and to examine whether baseline risk modified the therapeutic effect of finerenone.

**Design, Setting, and Participants**
This is a prespecified secondary analysis of the FINEARTS-HF trial, which was conducted across 653 sites in 37 countries among adults aged 40 years and older with symptomatic HF and left ventricular EF (LVEF) of 40% or greater. Patients were randomized between September 2020 and January 2023, and data analysis for this study was conducted from September to October 2025. The median (IQR) follow-up period was 32 (23-37) months.

**Intervention**
Finerenone (titrated to 20 mg or 40 mg) or placebo.

**Main Outcomes and Measures**
EMPEROR-Preserved risk scores for the outcomes of first HF hospitalization or cardiovascular death, cardiovascular death, and all-cause death were calculated in FINEARTS-HF using models incorporating N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide, high-sensitivity cardiac troponin T, New York Heart Association functional class, history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and diabetes, insulin use, and—depending on outcome—age, hemoglobin and albumin levels, HF duration, time from prior HF hospitalization, and sodium-glucose transporter 2 inhibitor use. Estimated risks were compared with observed event rates, and model performance was assessed using Harrell C statistic. Treatment effects were evaluated across risk quintiles (Q1 to Q5) and across the continuous risk distribution.

**Results**
Among 6001 patients (mean [SD] age, 72.0 [9.6] years; 2732 [45.5%] women; 3003 randomized to finerenone and 2998 randomized to placebo), the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model estimated risk of outcomes, with Q5 vs Q1 hazard ratios (HRs) of 10.49 (95% CI, 8.14-13.52) for the composite of HF hospitalization or cardiovascular death and 13.47 (95% CI, 8.79-20.64) for cardiovascular death. The model demonstrated good discrimination. The treatment effect of finerenone was consistent across risk quintiles for first HF hospitalization or cardiovascular death (Q1: HR, 0.93 [95% CI, 0.58-1.49]; Q2: HR, 1.04 [95% CI, 0.76-1.43]; Q3: HR, 0.82 [95% CI, 0.62-1.07]; Q4: HR, 0.81 [95% CI, 0.65-1.01]; and Q5: HR, 0.88 [95% CI, 0.74-1.05]; P for interaction = .68) and remained uniform across the continuous risk spectrum.

**Conclusions and Relevance**
The EMPEROR-Preserved risk models demonstrated good performance in FINEARTS-HF. Baseline risk did not modify the relative treatment effect of finerenone.

**Trial Registration**
ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT04435626

## Introduction

Over the past decade, the need for accurate risk prediction in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) has grown substantially, driven by the global rise in HFpEF prevalence. Beyond the risk of mortality, frequent heart failure (HF) hospitalization imposes a substantial health care and economic burden. The identification of patients at highest risk is essential for optimizing resource allocation, guiding intensity of outpatient surveillance (including use of implantable monitors), and potentially, informing personalized therapeutic strategies in this heterogeneous syndrome.

Several prognostic models for HFpEF have recently been proposed, including the HF-DANAS and ApHAC scores; however, these models are limited by the lack of adequate external validation, geographic and ethnic variability in predictive performance, and reliance on clinical variables that may not fully reflect contemporary HF therapy. General HF risk models, such as the Meta-Analysis Global Group in Chronic (MAGGIC) heart failure risk score, have also shown suboptimal accuracy when applied to patients with HFpEF.

In this context, the Empagliflozin Outcome Trial in Patients With Chronic Heart Failure With Preserved Ejection Fraction (EMPEROR-Preserved) risk model, developed from a large, multinational randomized clinical trial, represents a contemporary tool that incorporates established prognostic biomarkers, such as N-terminal pro–B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-proBNP) and high-sensitivity cardiac troponin T (hs-cTnT), as well as information related to sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitor therapy—features that distinguish it from prior models. This study aimed to evaluate the performance of the EMPEROR-Preserved model in the Finerenone Trial to Investigate Efficacy and Safety Superior to Placebo in Patients With Heart Failure (FINEARTS-HF), the most recent and largest global trial in patients with HFpEF, and to assess whether baseline risk modifies the treatment effect of finerenone.

## Methods

### FINEARTS-HF Trial: Design and Objectives

This is a prespecified secondary analysis of FINEARTS-HF, a prospective, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, event-driven trial that examined the efficacy and safety of finerenone compared with placebo in patients with HF with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF) or HFpEF. The design, baseline characteristics, and primary results are published. Ethics committees for the 653 participating institutions in 37 countries approved the protocol, and all patients gave written informed consent. The trial followed the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT) reporting guideline.

### Study Patients and Treatment

Key inclusion criteria were age 40 years or older, symptomatic HF in New York Heart Association (NYHA) functional class II to IV, treatment with a diuretic for more than 30 days before randomization, and a left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) of 40% or greater with evidence of structural heart disease (either left atrial enlargement or left ventricular hypertrophy) measured within 12 months of screening. Patients were also required to have elevated natriuretic peptide levels (NT-proBNP >300 pg/mL [or BNP >100 pg/mL] for patients in sinus rhythm or NT-proBNP >900 pg/mL [or BNP >300 pg/mL] for patients in atrial fibrillation), measured within 90 days in those with a recent worsening HF event within 90 days of randomization or measured 30 days before randomization in those without a recent worsening HF event. Both ambulatory and hospitalized patients were eligible for enrollment. Patients with prior LVEF of less than 40% with subsequent improvement to 40% or greater were also eligible, provided ongoing HF symptoms were present and all other inclusion criteria were satisfied. Key exclusion criteria were estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of less than 25 mL/min/1.73 m², serum or plasma potassium greater than 5.0 mEq/L at screening or randomization, or symptomatic hypotension with mean systolic blood pressure less than 90 mm Hg at screening or randomization.

Race was included in the analysis for reporting transparency and for readers to assess generalizability of results. Race was determined by the participant based on fixed categories. Participants who selected American Indian or Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian or Other Pacific Islander, and multiple races as well as those who did not report their racial identity were classified as belonging to additional groups.

Eligible participants were randomized in a 1:1 ratio to finerenone or matching placebo. The starting dose was 10 mg once daily in participants with an eGFR of 60 mL/min/1.73 m² or less, with a maximum maintenance dose of 20 mg once daily, whereas the starting dose was 20 mg once daily if the eGFR was greater than 60 mL/min/1.73 m², with a maximum maintenance dose of 40 mg once daily.

### EMPEROR-Preserved Risk Score

Analysis of outcomes according to the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score was prespecified in the FINEARTS-HF academic statistical analysis plan. The EMPEROR-Preserved risk calculator was developed using data from 5988 participants in the EMPEROR-Preserved trial. It represents a biomarker-driven prognostic model for patients with HFpEF, based on NT-proBNP and hs-cTnT measurements. The model aims to predict the composite end point of first HF hospitalization or cardiovascular (CV) death, as well as CV death and all-cause death individually. External validation was conducted in 1251 participants of the PARAGON-HF trial in whom NT-proBNP and hs-cTnT levels had been measured at randomization. For the outcomes of first HF hospitalization or CV death, 8 independent factors were identified; similarly, 8 factors were identified for CV and for all-cause death. The EMPEROR-Preserved risk score was calculated as the sum of values obtained by multiplying each variable included in the model by its respective β coefficient. Baseline survival rates were estimated using constants derived from the EMPEROR-Preserved trial, which served as the derivation cohort. In the present analysis, the model was applied to estimate risk over a broader time to first composite outcome comprising CV death or worsening HF events, including urgent HF visits as well as HF hospitalization. The association between this model and the primary outcome of total (first and recurrent) HF events and CV death as well as the secondary outcome of total HF events was examined. Using the risk model for time to first HF hospitalization or CV death, prespecified safety outcomes were evaluated, including hyperkalemia, hypokalemia, elevation of serum creatinine, and hypotension.

### Statistical Analysis

In the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model, albumin was the only variable that was completely missing; median values from the EMPEROR-Preserved trial cohort were used for imputation. Patients were categorized into quintiles according to the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score. Baseline characteristics were summarized by quintiles using frequencies and percentages for categorical variables and means and SDs or medians and IQRs for continuous variables.

The incremental discrimination of the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model compared with NT-proBNP–only or hs-cTnT–only models was evaluated using Harrell C statistic and the continuous net reclassification improvement (NRI). Model calibration was assessed by comparing observed and estimated 2-year event risks across quintiles of predicted risk.

For each quintile of the EMPEROR-Preserved score, crude incidence rates for each outcome were calculated as events per 100 patient-years of follow-up. Time-to-first events were evaluated using Kaplan-Meier curves and Cox proportional hazards models, stratified by region and baseline LVEF (<60% or ≥60%) and adjusted for treatment assignment. Hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% CIs were reported. Total (first and recurrent) events were evaluated using Nelson-Aalen cumulative hazard curves and rate ratios (RRs) with 95% CIs from semiparametric proportional rates models. Incidence rates for each outcome across the spectrum of the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score were examined using a Poisson regression model with restricted cubic splines with 3 knots.

The relative effect of finerenone compared with placebo by baseline EMPEROR-Preserved risk categories was calculated as an RR with 95% CI derived from semiparametric proportional rates models for total events or as a HR with 95% CI from Cox proportional hazards models for time-to–first events. All models were stratified by geographic region and baseline LVEF. The effect of finerenone on each outcome across the full spectrum of the risk score was modeled using restricted cubic splines, with knots placed at points that resulted in the model with the lowest Akaike information criterion value. Absolute rate differences were estimated using a Poisson regression model with the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score included as a continuous variable, modeled using a restricted cubic spline with 3 knots. Absolute benefits of finerenone were calculated for (1) the composite outcome of total HF events and CV death and (2) total HF events, under the assumption of a constant treatment effect across the range of the baseline risk score.

All statistical analyses were performed using Stata version 18 (StataCorp). A 2-sided P < .05 was considered statistically significant. Data analysis was conducted from September to October 2025.

## Results

Of 6001 patients enrolled in FINEARTS-HF, a total of 3003 were randomly assigned to finerenone and 2998 to placebo. The mean (SD) age was 72.0 (9.6) years, and 2732 patients (45.5%) were women. The mean (SD) LVEF was 52.6% (7.8). A total of 1854 patients (37.0%) were classified as NYHA functional class III or IV, and the median (IQR) NT-proBNP level was 1041 (449-1946) pg/mL. The median (IQR) follow-up period was 32 (23-37) months.

### Baseline EMPEROR-Preserved Score

The median (IQR) EMPEROR-Preserved score for the composite outcome of first HF hospitalization or CV death was 5.4 (4.8-6.1), whereas the median (IQR) scores for CV death and all-cause death were 4.5 (4.0-5.0) and 4.2 (3.7-4.8), respectively.

### Patient Characteristics According to EMPEROR-Preserved Risk Score

Among baseline characteristics not included in the risk score, patients at the highest estimated risk were older and had lower LVEF, eGFR, body mass index, and Kansas City Cardiomyopathy Questionnaire (KCCQ) total symptom scores as well as a higher prevalence of stroke and atrial fibrillation. Regarding background therapy, the use of β-blockers, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or ARNIs was less frequent, whereas the use of diuretics was more common in those with the highest estimated risk. Trends in patient characteristics across risk categories for CV death and all-cause death were generally consistent with those observed for the quintiles of risk for first HF hospitalization or CV death.

### Outcomes Related to EMPEROR-Preserved Risk Scores

For the composite end point of first HF hospitalization or CV death, the incidence rate was 2.2 (95% CI, 1.7-2.8) per 100 patient-years in the lowest estimated-risk quintile and 25.0 (95% CI, 23.0-27.3) per 100 patient-years in the highest quintile, corresponding to a HR of 10.49 (95% CI, 8.14-13.52). When modeled as a continuous variable, the risk score demonstrated a clear, graded association with higher event risk. A similar stepwise gradient was observed for expanded outcomes—including total HF events and CV death, total HF events alone, and first HF event or CV death.

### EMPEROR-Preserved Risk Model Discrimination and Calibration

The composite end point of first HF hospitalization or CV death occurred in 599 patients (19.9%) in the finerenone group and 664 patients (22.1%) in the placebo group. At 2 years, the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model demonstrated superior discrimination for this composite end point compared with NT-proBNP alone, with an improvement in the C statistic and a continuous NRI. The model also outperformed hs-cTnT alone. When stratified by risk quintiles, both observed and model-estimated risks showed a clear gradient across quintiles. Calibration was generally acceptable in the lower to middle quintiles, whereas estimated 2-year risk modestly exceeded observed risk in the highest quintile. In a sensitivity analysis excluding the SGLT2 inhibitor term from the linear predictor, model performance was materially unchanged.

At 2 years, the C statistics and continuous NRI values for CV death and all-cause death were likewise higher than those obtained using NT-proBNP alone, and the model demonstrated superior discrimination compared with hs-cTnT alone. For both CV death and all-cause death, observed 2-year risks closely aligned with model-estimated risks across all quintiles, demonstrating good calibration.

### Effect of Finerenone Across the Spectrum of Baseline Risk

For the composite outcome of first HF hospitalization or CV death, the relative effect of finerenone was consistent across quintiles of the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score, with hazard ratios of 0.93 (95% CI, 0.58-1.49), 1.04 (95% CI, 0.76-1.43), 0.82 (95% CI, 0.62-1.07), 0.81 (95% CI, 0.65-1.01), and 0.88 (95% CI, 0.74-1.05) from the lowest to the highest quintile (P for interaction = .68). Similar consistency in relative risk reduction across risk-score quintiles was observed for other outcomes, including the primary trial outcome. Applying the overall relative risk reduction for the primary composite outcome (time to first worsening HF event or CV death) to the lowest risk quintile gave an absolute reduction in risk of 3.7 per 1000 person-years (number needed to treat, 272); by contrast, these numbers were 43.0 per 1000 person-years (number needed to treat, 23) in the highest quintile of risk.

When analyzed as a continuous variable, the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score showed no evidence of statistical heterogeneity in the relative treatment effect of finerenone across the entire risk spectrum for any outcome. With respect to absolute risk reductions, patients at higher baseline risk derived greater absolute benefit.

### Safety Outcomes Across the Spectrum of Baseline Risk

In the placebo group, the incidences of hyperkalemia and elevated creatinine increased across higher EMPEROR-Preserved risk score quintiles. Overall, the incidences of hypotension, elevated creatinine, and hyperkalemia were higher with finerenone than with placebo, whereas the incidence of hypokalemia was lower with finerenone. However, the magnitude of these differences between finerenone and placebo groups was consistent across all risk-score quintiles.

## Discussion

One of the principal findings is that the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model demonstrated robust performance in FINEARTS-HF, showing good discrimination and generally preserved calibration except within the highest-risk quintile for first HF hospitalization or CV death. High discriminative ability was maintained for conventional outcomes—all-cause death, CV death (more than 13-fold difference in risk between highest and lowest quintiles), and first HF hospitalization or CV death—and for extended end points incorporating total HF events, including the primary end point of FINEARTS-HF (12-fold difference in risk between highest and lowest quintiles).

Patients in FINEARTS-HF were at higher risk than those in EMPEROR-Preserved because of lower mean LVEF, higher NT-proBNP levels, and a greater prevalence of NYHA class III. Despite this, model performance for CV and all-cause death was preserved across the risk distribution, likely reflecting substantial overlap between the factors driving elevated risk in FINEARTS-HF and the core prognostic variables incorporated into the mortality models (NT-proBNP, LVEF, and NYHA class).

In contrast, outcomes involving HF hospitalization showed a modest overestimation of risk in the highest-risk quintile. The model incorporates time since the most recent HF hospitalization (<3 months or 3-6 months) as a strong prognostic factor; however, in FINEARTS-HF, approximately 80% of patients in the highest-risk quintile met these criteria, resulting in a uniform addition of risk across this group and disproportionately inflated estimated risk in patients with already advanced clinical profiles.

Comparison with the PREDICT-HFpEF model (developed using DELIVER, externally validated in PARAGON-HF and I-Preserve, and subsequently evaluated in FINEARTS-HF) offers additional perspective. Although PREDICT-HFpEF demonstrated good discrimination for first HF hospitalization or CV death in FINEARTS-HF—comparable with the EMPEROR-Preserved model—its ability to predict CV and all-cause death was lower (C statistics for CV death: 0.68 vs 0.73; all-cause death: 0.69 vs 0.71). A key distinction is the inclusion of hs-cTnT in the EMPEROR-Preserved model but not in PREDICT-HFpEF. In EMPEROR-Preserved, hs-cTnT had the strongest association with all-cause death and was second only to NT-proBNP for CV death. The PARAGON-HF trial demonstrated that elevated hs-cTnT was an independent predictor of CV death.

In the placebo group of EMPEROR-Preserved, event rates for first HF hospitalization or CV death differed more than 11-fold between the highest and lowest quintiles of the risk score—far exceeding the discriminatory capacity of traditional univariable markers such as age, sex, NT-proBNP, NYHA class, or diabetes.

Another important finding is that the relative risk reduction with finerenone was consistent across the full spectrum of baseline risk, whereas the absolute risk reduction was greater among patients at higher risk. These observations are consistent with post hoc analyses from DELIVER, PARAGON-HF, and other major HFpEF trials.

The inclusion of a term for SGLT2 inhibitor use in the original risk model warrants careful consideration, particularly given the distinction between causal treatment effects observed in randomized clinical trials and prescribing patterns in routine clinical practice. In EMPEROR-Preserved, treatment allocation was randomized, and the corresponding coefficient reflects a causal reduction in risk; in observational settings, SGLT2 inhibitor use may be associated with higher baseline risk because clinicians preferentially prescribe these agents to higher-risk patients. In sensitivity analyses excluding the SGLT2 inhibitor term, discrimination, calibration, and risk gradients were materially unchanged. Taken together, the EMPEROR-Preserved risk model may serve as a valuable clinical tool—supporting estimation of absolute treatment benefit with finerenone, identification of patients with HFmrEF or HFpEF requiring intensified management and follow-up, and optimization of patient selection in future HFmrEF and HFpEF clinical trials.

### Limitations

First, the possibility of residual confounding or unmeasured heterogeneity across subgroups cannot be excluded, and subgroup interaction analyses were underpowered. Second, study patients were derived from a randomized clinical trial with stringent inclusion and exclusion criteria, which may limit generalizability to the broader HFmrEF and HFpEF population. Finally, one laboratory variable required for the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score (albumin) was unavailable in FINEARTS-HF and was imputed using the median value from EMPEROR-Preserved, which may reduce variability and attenuate true associations.

## Conclusions

In the FINEARTS-HF trial, the EMPEROR-Preserved risk score demonstrated broad applicability across all evaluated outcomes. Baseline risk did not modify the relative treatment effect of finerenone; however, patients at higher risk experienced substantially greater absolute benefit. These findings highlight the value of the EMPEROR-Preserved score as a pragmatic tool for individualized risk stratification and estimation of absolute treatment benefit. The score may also facilitate targeted patient enrichment in future HFmrEF and HFpEF clinical trials.
