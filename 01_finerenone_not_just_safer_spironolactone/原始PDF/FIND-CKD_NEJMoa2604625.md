### ABSTRACT

**BACKGROUND**

In randomized trials, finerenone, a nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, improved kidney and cardiovascular outcomes in patients with type 2 diabetes and chronic kidney disease (CKD). Whether finerenone has similar effects in patients without diabetes who have CKD is unknown.

**METHODS**

We randomly assigned adults without diabetes who had CKD (estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR], 25 to <90 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> of body-surface area) and albuminuria (urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio, 200 to ≤3500, with albumin in milligrams and creatinine in grams) and were using a renin–angiotensin system inhibitor to receive finerenone (10 or 20 mg daily) or placebo. The primary outcome was the total eGFR slope (mean annual rate of change in the eGFR from baseline to month 32), assessed with a two-slope linear spline mixed-effects model. Secondary outcomes included a composite of kidney or cardiovascular events (reduction from baseline of at least 57% in the eGFR, kidney failure, hospitalization for heart failure, or death from cardiovascular causes), a composite of the two kidney events, and a composite of the two cardiovascular events.

**RESULTS**

A total of 1584 participants underwent randomization — 793 were assigned to the finerenone group, and 791 to the placebo group. The mean (±SD) baseline eGFR was 46.8±16.2 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> with finerenone and 46.6±16.0 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> with placebo. The mean annual rate of change in the eGFR from baseline to month 32 was −3.3 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> (95% confidence interval [CI], −3.6 to −3.1) with finerenone and −4.0 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> (95% CI, −4.3 to −3.8) with placebo (difference, 0.7; 95% CI, 0.3 to 1.1; P<0.001). Prespecified hierarchical testing showed that the risk of a composite kidney or cardiovascular outcome event was lower with finerenone than with placebo (hazard ratio, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.60 to 0.99; P=0.04); the hazard ratio was 0.78 (95% CI, 0.60 to 1.01) for the composite of the two kidney events and 0.60 (95% CI, 0.27 to 1.33) for the composite of the two cardiovascular events. The most common adverse event was hyperkalemia (135 participants [17.0%] with finerenone and 105 [13.3%] with placebo); hyperkalemia events led to discontinuation of the trial regimen in 12 participants (1.5%) and 1 participant (0.1%), respectively, and to hospitalization in 7 (0.9%) and 5 (0.6%).

**CONCLUSIONS**

Among adults with CKD who did not have diabetes, finerenone led to a slower decrease in the eGFR than placebo over 32 months. (Funded by Bayer; FIND-CKD ClinicalTrials.gov number, NCT05047263.)

T HE PREVALENCE OF CHRONIC KIDNEY disease (CKD) has more than doubled since 1990, with approximately 800 million adults affected globally in 2023.<sup>1</sup> Although type 2 diabetes is the most common cause of CKD,<sup>2</sup> more than half the cases are in persons without diabetes.<sup>3,4</sup> Renin–angiotensin system (RAS) inhibitors and sodium–glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors reduce the loss of kidney function and risk of kidney failure in adults with CKD, regardless of whether they have diabetes.<sup>5-9</sup> Despite the use of these guideline-recommended treatments,<sup>10</sup> progressive loss of kidney function and adverse cardiovascular outcomes occur in many persons with CKD, particularly those with severely increased albuminuria levels.<sup>11</sup> Thus, new therapies are needed.

Overactivation of the mineralocorticoid receptor is implicated in the pathophysiology of CKD through increased sodium and fluid retention and activation of proinflammatory and profibrotic processes.<sup>12</sup> Finerenone is a selective nonsteroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist that has been shown to reduce the loss of kidney function and the risk of adverse kidney and cardiovascular outcomes in persons with CKD and type 2 diabetes.<sup>13-15</sup> The current trial, FIND-CKD (Finerenone in Non-Diabetic CKD), examined the efficacy and safety of finerenone in adults without diabetes who had CKD.

# METHODS

## TRIAL DESIGN AND OVERSIGHT

We conducted a phase 3, international, prospective, double-blind, randomized trial of finerenone as compared with placebo in adults without diabetes who had CKD. The trial design was published previously<sup>16</sup>; the protocol is available with the full text of this article at NEJM.org. The trial was designed by the steering committee and Bayer (the sponsor). The trial followed the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and the Council for International Organizations of Medical Sciences as well as the International Council for Harmonisation guidelines for Good Clinical Practice. An independent data and safety monitoring committee oversaw the trial throughout. Data were collected by the investigators, analyzed by the sponsor, and independently confirmed by staff at the University Medical Center Groningen using the original data. The first author prepared the

initial draft of the manuscript and had full access to the data. All the authors reviewed and revised subsequent drafts, approved the final version, and, together with the sponsor, agreed to submit the manuscript for publication. The first and last authors vouch for the accuracy and completeness of the data; the sponsor and the investigators vouch for the fidelity of the trial to the protocol.

## PARTICIPANTS

Eligible participants had a clinical diagnosis of CKD and an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of at least 25 but less than 60 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> of body-surface area and a urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio between 200 and 500 (with the albumin level measured in milligrams and the creatinine level measured in grams), or an eGFR of at least 25 but less than 90 ml per minute per 1.73 m<sup>2</sup> and a urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio between 500 and 3500. Eligibility criteria included a serum potassium level of 4.8 mmol per liter or less and receipt of a stable dose of an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor or an angiotensin-receptor blocker therapy for at least 4 weeks before screening. Patients were excluded if they had received a diagnosis of type 1 or type 2 diabetes or had a glycated hemoglobin level of at least 6.5%. Patients were also excluded if they had an indication for a steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist; had polycystic kidney disease, lupus nephritis, or antineutrophil cytoplasmic autoantibody–associated vasculitis; or had received immunosuppressive therapy within 6 months before screening. Full eligibility criteria are provided in Table S1 in the Supplementary Appendix, available at NEJM.org). All the participants provided written informed consent before trial entry.

## TRIAL VISITS

Trial visits were prespecified to occur at screening, baseline, and months 1, 3, 6, 9, and 12. Thereafter, visits were prespecified to occur every 4 months until the end of the trial. Participants were to be treated for at least 32 months. An additional visit occurred 4 weeks after discontinuation of the trial regimen to assess the effects of finerenone as compared with placebo after treatment. ## TRIAL PROCEDURES

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to receive oral finerenone or matching placebo. Randomization was conducted with the use of interactive response technology and included baseline SGLT2 inhibitor use (yes vs. no) and screening urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio (≤1000 vs. >1000) as stratification factors. Finerenone therapy was initiated at a dose of 20 mg per day if the screening eGFR was at least 60 ml per minute per 1.73 m² and at 10 mg per day if the screening eGFR was 25 to less than 60 ml per minute per 1.73 m². An increase in the dose of finerenone from 10 mg per day to the target dose of 20 mg per day was permitted from month 1 onward in participants with a serum potassium level of 4.8 mmol per liter or less and an eGFR that was no more than 30% less the value at the last visit. A decrease in the dose was allowed at any time for safety reasons. If the serum potassium level exceeded 5.5 mmol per liter, finerenone or placebo was withheld for 72 hours and restarted at a dose of 10 mg per day once the serum potassium level decreased to 5.0 mmol per liter or less. All the investigators, treating physicians, participants, and trial personnel were unaware of the trial-group assignments throughout.

## OUTCOMES

The primary efficacy outcome was the total eGFR slope, defined as the mean annual rate of change in the eGFR from baseline to month 32. Exploratory eGFR outcomes included the mean annual rate of change in the eGFR from month 3 until discontinuation of the trial regimen (i.e., chronic eGFR slope) and the mean eGFR change from baseline to week 4 after discontinuation of finerenone or placebo. In exploratory analyses, the primary outcome was assessed in prespecified participant subgroups, which included subgroups defined according to stratification factors and baseline eGFR. Secondary outcomes, assessed according to a prespecified hierarchical testing strategy, were as follows: a composite of kidney or cardiovascular events (a sustained decrease of at least 57% in the eGFR [defined by a decrease from baseline that was sustained for ≥4 weeks], kidney failure [defined by an eGFR of <15 ml per minute per 1.73 m² that was confirmed ≥4 weeks

after the initial assessment, the need for long-term dialysis for ≥30 days, or kidney transplantation], hospitalization for heart failure, or death from cardiovascular causes), a composite of a sustained decrease from baseline of at least 57% in the eGFR or kidney failure as defined above, and a composite of hospitalization for heart failure or death from cardiovascular causes.

Additional exploratory outcomes included the change in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio from baseline to month 6 and a composite of a sustained decrease of at least 40% in the eGFR (defined by a decrease from baseline that was sustained for ≥4 weeks) or kidney failure. Safety outcomes were adverse events and serious adverse events that began or worsened during the period between the first dose of finerenone or placebo and up to 3 days after any temporary or permanent interruption. Prespecified safety outcomes included a serum potassium level of more than 5.5 mmol per liter or more than 6.0 mmol per liter, hospitalization for serious hyperkalemia, and permanent discontinuation of the trial regimen because of hyperkalemia.

## STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

We calculated that a sample size of 1500 participants would provide the trial with more than 90% power at a two-sided significance level of 5% to detect a mean annual rate of decline in the eGFR between baseline and month 32 that was 0.7 ml per minute per 1.73 m² less with finerenone than with placebo. This calculation assumed that 5% of the trial participants would have an intercurrent event (an event due to kidney failure or death from any cause) and that 5% would discontinue the trial.

Continuous data are expressed as means with standard deviations or medians with interquartile ranges, and categorical data are expressed as numbers and percentages. Efficacy analyses were conducted in the full analysis population, which included all the participants who underwent randomization. The primary efficacy outcome was analyzed with the use of a two-slope linear spline mixed-effects model that included trial group–specific fixed effects (finerenone vs. placebo) for the intercept, time from randomization, time since month 3 (change point), and the randomization stratification factors and their interaction with the time components. # RESULTS

**PARTICIPANTS**

Between September 21, 2021, and May 12, 2023, we assessed 3231 persons for eligibility. A total of 1584 participants underwent randomization — 793 were assigned to the finerenone group, and 791 to the placebo group. During a median treatment period of 36.6 months, 4 participants (0.5%) in the finerenone group and 2 (0.3%) in the placebo group discontinued the trial, and 157 (19.8%) and 177 (22.4%), respectively, discontinued the trial regimen (Fig. S1). Baseline characteristics and concomitant medications were balanced between the trial groups (Table 1 and Table S2). The mean age was 54.7 years; 535 participants (33.8%) were women, and the median urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio was 818.9. Overall, 1580 participants (99.7%) were receiving a RAS inhibitor, and 270 (17.0%) were receiving an SGLT2 inhibitor. The representativeness of the trial population is shown in Table S3.

**PRIMARY OUTCOME**

At baseline, the mean (±SD) eGFR was 46.8±16.2 ml per minute per 1.73 m² in the finerenone group and 46.6±16.0 ml per minute per 1.73 m² in the placebo group. Figure 1A shows the mean change in the eGFR from baseline over time in the trial groups. The mean rate of change in the eGFR from baseline to month 3 was 1.2 ml per minute per 1.73 m² greater with finerenone than with placebo (Fig. 1B). After month 3, the rate of eGFR decline was slower with finerenone.

At month 32, the mean eGFR was 39.4±18.2 ml per minute per 1.73 m² with finerenone and 38.1±18.4 ml per minute per 1.73 m² with placebo. In the analysis of the primary outcome, the mean annual rate of change in the eGFR from baseline to month 32 (total eGFR slope) was −3.3 ml per minute per 1.73 m² (95% CI, −3.6 to −3.1) with finerenone and −4.0 ml per minute per 1.73 m² (95% CI, −4.3 to −3.8) with placebo, corresponding to a decline that was 0.7 ml per minute per 1.73 m² (95% CI, 0.3 to 1.1) slower with finerenone (P<0.001) (Fig. 1B and Table 2). From the end of treatment to the end of follow-up, the mean eGFR increased with finerenone and decreased with placebo (Fig. 1A).

## SECONDARY OUTCOMES

Kidney or cardiovascular events (composite kidney–cardiovascular outcome) occurred in 110 participants (13.9%) in the finerenone group (4.7 events per 100 person-years; 95% CI, 3.9 to 5.6) and 134 participants (16.9%) in the placebo group (5.8 events per 100 person-years; 95% CI, 4.9 to 6.9), for a hazard ratio of 0.77 (95% CI, 0.60 to 0.99; P=0.04) (Table 2, Fig. 3, and Fig. S5). The hazard ratio (finerenone vs. placebo) for the composite of a sustained decrease from baseline of at least 57% in the eGFR or kidney failure was 0.78 (95% CI, 0.60 to 1.01; P=0.06), and the hazard ratio for the composite of hospitalization for heart failure or death from cardiovascular causes was 0.60 (95% CI, 0.27 to 1.33) (Table 2). The proportional-hazards assumption was assessed and was not violated.

## EXPLORATORY EFFICACY OUTCOMES

The mean change in the eGFR from baseline to week 4 after the end of the treatment period was −8.7 ml per minute per 1.73 m² (95% CI, −9.7 to −7.8) with finerenone and −11.1 ml per minute per 1.73 m² (95% CI, −12.0 to −10.1) with placebo (difference, 2.4 ml per minute per 1.73 m²; 95% CI, 1.2 to 3.5). The relative change in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio from baseline to month 6 was −41.3% (95% CI, −44.2 to −38.3) with finerenone and −9.1% (95% CI, −13.1 to −5.0) with placebo (relative difference, 35.4%; 95% CI, 30.9 to 39.6); this apparent difference was sustained over time. A reduction of 30% or more in the urinary albumin-to-creatinine ratio from baseline to month 6 occurred in 444 participants (56.0%) in the finerenone group and 193 participants (24.4%) in the placebo group (odds ra-

tio, 3.99; 95% CI, 3.22 to 4.95) (Fig. S6). The composite of a sustained decrease of at least 40% in the eGFR or kidney failure occurred in 168 participants (21.2%) in the finerenone group (7.4 events per 100 person-years; 95% CI, 6.3 to 8.5) and 206 participants (26.0%) in the placebo group (9.3 events per 100 person-years; 95% CI, 8.1 to 10.6), for a hazard ratio of 0.76 (95% CI, 0.62 to 0.93).

Adverse events were reported in 542 participants (68.3%) who received finerenone and 517 participants (65.4%) who received placebo (Table 2). ### DISCUSSION

In this double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled clinical trial, finerenone resulted in a slower annual rate of eGFR decline in participants without diabetes who had CKD and were receiving an angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitor or angiotensin-receptor blocker.<sup>10</sup> The incidence of the composite of kidney or cardiovascular events was lower with finerenone than with placebo. Hyperkalemia events were more frequent with finerenone than with placebo, but serious hyperkalemia events were infrequent.

The primary outcome was the change in the eGFR over time, a surrogate end point that is increasingly being used to assess drug efficacy in CKD trials.<sup>21</sup> Although our trial was not powered to assess the effects of the trial regimens on clinical outcomes, it showed that, in addition to the observed reduction in the annual rate of eGFR decline with finerenone, the risk of the composite of kidney or cardiovascular events was lower in the finerenone group than in the placebo group. This finding is similar to the results of a meta-analysis of 66 randomized controlled trials, which found that an attenuation of 0.75 ml per minute per 1.73 m² in the eGFR decline as compared with placebo predicted a reduction of 23% in the risk of adverse kidney outcomes.<sup>21</sup>

During trial recruitment, updated clinical practice guidelines recommended the use of SGLT2 inhibitors as part of evidence-based therapy in patients with CKD.<sup>10,22</sup> In our trial, 17% of the participants were using SGLT2 inhibitors at baseline,<sup>16</sup> and the efficacy of finerenone in participants who were using SGLT2 inhibitors was similar to that in participants who were not using SGLT2 inhibitors. Although we hypothesize that the use of both finerenone and SGLT2 inhibitors may provide complementary effects, this was not directly tested in the present trial.

Use of the steroidal mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists spironolactone and eplerenone in persons with CKD can be limited by the side-effect profile of these drugs and safety concerns, particularly hyperkalemia.<sup>23</sup> Of the approximately 1400 participants with CKD in the BARACK-D (Benefits of Aldosterone Receptor Antagonism in Chronic Kidney Disease) trial, more than half discontinued spironolactone within 6 months after randomization owing to safety criteria (primarily related to acute kidney injury) that were specified in the protocol; hyperkalemia accounted for more than 10% of the discontinuations.<sup>24</sup> No unanticipated safety signals were observed with finerenone in the current trial. Hyperkalemia was the most common adverse event, but increases in the serum potassium level were modest, leading to discontinuation of the trial regimen in few participants and no hospitalizations. The incidence rates of acute kidney injury and symptomatic hypotension were also low and similar in the finerenone and placebo groups.

This trial has certain limitations. Although we recruited a geographically diverse population, most of the participants were men and had advanced CKD with severe albuminuria, and few were Black. Furthermore, participants with kidney diseases such as autosomal dominant or autosomal recessive polycystic kidney disease, lupus nephritis, or antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody–associated vasculitis were excluded, as were those with an extremely low eGFR or low-level albuminuria. Thus, the findings here are applicable to persons with characteristics similar to those of the trial participants and cannot be generalized.

In the current trial involving adults without diabetes who had CKD, finerenone resulted in a slower rate of eGFR decline over 32 months than placebo.
