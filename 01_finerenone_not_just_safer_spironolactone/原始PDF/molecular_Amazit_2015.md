## Abstract (verbatim, Europe PMC)

Aldosterone regulates sodium homeostasis by activating the mineralocorticoid receptor (MR), a member of the nuclear receptor superfamily. Hyperaldosteronism leads to deleterious effects on the kidney, blood vessels, and heart. Although steroidal antagonists such as spironolactone and eplerenone are clinically useful for the treatment of cardiovascular diseases, they are associated with several side effects. Finerenone, a novel nonsteroidal MR antagonist, is presently being evaluated in two clinical phase IIb trials. Here, we characterized the molecular mechanisms of action of finerenone and spironolactone at several key steps of the MR signaling pathway. Molecular modeling and mutagenesis approaches allowed identification of Ser-810 and Ala-773 as key residues for the high MR selectivity of finerenone. Moreover, we showed that, in contrast to spironolactone, which activates the S810L mutant MR responsible for a severe form of early onset hypertension, finerenone displays strict antagonistic properties. Aldosterone-dependent phosphorylation and degradation of MR are inhibited by both finerenone and spironolactone. However, automated quantification of MR subcellular distribution demonstrated that finerenone delays aldosterone-induced nuclear accumulation of MR more efficiently than spironolactone. Finally, chromatin immunoprecipitation assays revealed that, as opposed to spironolactone, finerenone inhibits MR, steroid receptor coactivator-1, and RNA polymerase II binding at the regulatory sequence of the SCNN1A gene and also remarkably reduces basal MR and steroid receptor coactivator-1 recruitment, unraveling a specific and unrecognized inactivating mechanism on MR signaling. Overall, our data demonstrate that the highly potent and selective MR antagonist finerenone specifically impairs several critical steps of the MR signaling pathway and therefore represents a promising new generation MR antagonist.

## Introduction (extracted from PMC full text)

The mineralocorticoid receptor (MR) and its physiological ligand aldosterone are main effectors of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. Pathophysiological overactivation of MR signaling may induce disorders such as heart failure and chronic kidney disease. Spironolactone is described as the first generation of potent MR antagonists but lacks selectivity because it antagonizes the androgen receptor and acts as a progesterone receptor agonist. Finerenone is a novel nonsteroidal MR antagonist that combines potency and high selectivity and has demonstrated more efficient protection from cardiorenal injuries.

## Experimental Procedures / Methods (extracted from PMC full text)

**Cell models:**
- HK-GFP-MR cells (human kidney cells stably expressing GFP-MR, clone 20) — primary renal model for nuclear translocation studies.
- Murine cortical collecting duct KC3AC1 cells — used for endogenous target gene / ChIP analyses.
- HEK 293T cells — used for transactivation assays with transient receptor expression.

**MR variants constructed (structure–function / mutagenesis):** MRS810L (familial hypertension mutation), MRA773G, MRQ776A, MRS810A, MRR817A, MRM852A, MRC942A, MRT945A, MRS810M, MRN770A. The S810L mutant is clinically relevant as it is responsible for a severe form of early-onset familial hypertension.

Methods included: equilibrium/kinetic ligand-binding assays with in vitro translated full-length MR; molecular modeling of the MR ligand-binding pocket; automated quantitative microscopy of GFP-MR subcellular distribution (translocation index); Western blot of MR phosphorylation/degradation; transactivation reporter assays; and chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) on the SCNN1A gene promoter.

## Results (extracted from PMC full text; quantitative values copied)

### Finerenone is a potent mineralocorticoid antagonist
- IC50 values of **58 ± 9 nM** (finerenone) and **74.0 ± 15 nM** (spironolactone) were determined.
- Finerenone binds in vitro translated full-length MR with high affinity: **Kd = 1.52 ± 0.01 nM**.
- Kinetic experiments gave a dissociation **half-life of ~50 min** for the finerenone–MR complex.

### Ligand-induced nuclear import
- Aldosterone induced a maximal **translocation index of 3.11 ± 0.02** (GFP-hMR).
- Antagonists alone (at 1 h) produced only moderate increases: values of **1.84 ± 0.01** (spironolactone) and **1.76 ± 0.01** (finerenone) (additional conditions **1.75 ± 0.01** and **1.78 ± 0.01**).
- After 24 h, translocation index values rose to **2.71 ± 0.01, 2.71 ± 0.01, 2.82 ± 0.01, and 2.82 ± 0.02**.
- Interpretation: finerenone delays / impedes aldosterone-induced nuclear import kinetics more effectively than spironolactone.

### MR degradation and phosphorylation
- Western blots showed aldosterone caused receptor degradation at 4 h and 24 h, whereas the antagonists did not promote such receptor degradation.
- Both finerenone and spironolactone blocked aldosterone-induced MR phosphorylation and subsequent degradation.

### Transcriptional cofactor recruitment (ChIP on SCNN1A)
- Aldosterone induced significant recruitment of MR, SRC-1, and RNA polymerase II to the SCNN1A regulatory sequence.
- Finerenone alone not only impaired MR, SRC-1, and RNA Pol II recruitment but also **remarkably reduced basal MR and SRC-1 binding** — consistent with inverse-agonist behavior, a property distinct from spironolactone.

### S810L mutant MR (early-onset hypertension)
- Aldosterone activates MRS810L with **EC50 = 4.2 ± 0.4 × 10⁻¹⁰ M**.
- Spironolactone activates MRS810L with **EC50 = 25 ± 2 × 10⁻¹¹ M** (i.e., spironolactone behaves as an agonist on this mutant).
- Finerenone did NOT activate MRS810L; instead it inhibited aldosterone-induced activity with **IC50 = 16.1 ± 3.1 × 10⁻⁶ M** — i.e., strict antagonism.

### Structural determinants of selectivity/potency (mutagenesis)
Fold reductions in finerenone potency upon mutation:
- N770A: **33.2-fold**
- S810A: **9.0-fold**
- A773G: **43.1-fold**
- S810M: **160.4-fold**

Ser-810 and Ala-773 were identified as key residues for the high MR selectivity and potency of finerenone.

## Discussion (extracted from PMC full text)

Finerenone has a higher antagonistic potency for MR than spironolactone and alters nuclear translocation of the receptor more efficiently. It totally blocked aldosterone-induced MR phosphorylation and degradation. Notably, RNA Pol II recruitment onto the regulatory sequence within the SCNN1A promoter was not only impaired by finerenone in the presence of aldosterone but also markedly diminished under basal conditions, supporting a specific inactivating (inverse-agonist-like) mechanism on MR signaling. The findings position finerenone as a promising new-generation MR antagonist with superior selectivity and a distinct mechanism relative to steroidal antagonists.
